# Selected Bibliography and Notes on Sources

This book is built on the work of many writers and researchers. The bibliography below is organized by volume and by theme within each volume. It is not exhaustive. It is a working list for the reader who wants to go deeper into any of the arguments the book engages. Editions listed are current or standard; earlier editions exist for most.

## A Note on Method and Sources

A book this broad cannot pretend to originality on every point. Almost every argument the book makes has been made before, somewhere, by someone more specialized than we are. What the book attempts is integration — bringing together arguments from traditions that rarely speak to each other and building a usable synthesis. The reader who wants specialist treatment of any particular argument has an enormous literature to go to, much of it listed above.

We have relied especially on five authors whose work forms something like a load-bearing wall of the book. Hannah Arendt and Eric Hoffer for the mass-movement diagnosis. Robert Putnam for the empirical ground. Yuval Levin for the institutional analysis. And Michael Sandel for the philosophical frame in which individual rights and civic virtue are both honored without either being dissolved into the other. Readers who want a shorter reading list than the one above would do well to start with those five.

Two collaborative efforts deserve a specific note of gratitude. The Haidt–Bail collaborative Google document on social media and political dysfunction is a model of how intellectual disagreement ought to work in our moment. The Civic Health Project's maintenance of the depolarization-organization database has made the empirical work of Chapter 16 possible for non-specialist writers. Both are ongoing and publicly accessible.

Finally: all errors in this book are ours. The sources above are better than we are, and where we have misread or misapplied them, the fault is not theirs.
