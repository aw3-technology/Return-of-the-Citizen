# Conclusion
## What We Owe Each Other

The question this book opened with was the oldest in political philosophy: what does a human being owe the political community, and what does the community owe the human being? It is the question Aristotle asked, and Cicero, and Locke, and Madison, and Arendt, and every serious writer since. It has never been finally settled. It will not be settled here. But it can be answered well enough, in any given moment, for the persons in that moment to live by. That is what we have tried to do.

Our answer, stripped to its smallest form, has three parts.

*

We owe each other our attention.

Attention is the first civic good because it is the one the current arrangement is most efficiently stripping from us. An attention captured by the outrage loop, by the feed, by the algorithm, by the ambient performance economy, is an attention that cannot do what a citizen's attention must do — attend to the concrete person across the table, the concrete proposal on the table, the concrete work at hand. The recovery of attention is not a therapeutic virtue with incidental political consequences. It is the foundational political discipline of our age. A population that cannot attend will be ruled by those who can.

The attention we owe each other is not passive. It is active, willed, patient. It is the attention the juror owes the witness, the parent owes the child, the physician owes the patient, the friend owes the friend. It is the attention we have generally been brought up to give in private life and that we have, for reasons the book has tried to map, largely ceased to give in public life. The return of the citizen begins — not ends, begins — with the decision to attend.

*

We owe each other the presumption of good faith.

Not the certainty of it. Bad faith exists; the book has not pretended otherwise. But the default. The presumption that the person across the table is, like us, a fellow citizen trying to understand a hard situation in the world as she has found it. This presumption is falsifiable — if a person proves to be acting in bad faith, the presumption is properly retired for her — but it is the default from which a civic life proceeds. The opposite default, which is now the operating default of most American public life, is that the person across the table is acting in bad faith until proven otherwise. Under that default, every interaction becomes adversarial, every political argument becomes a morality play, and every fellow citizen becomes a candidate for the enemy list. We have tried it; it does not work.

The presumption of good faith is not a sentimental disposition. It is the cognitive habit of the republican citizen, who understands that a self-governing people has to talk with itself about hard things, and cannot do so if its default assumption is that the speaker is lying or malign. It is also — we have argued throughout — empirically justified. More in Common's research establishes, repeatedly, that members of each American tribe are systematically mistaken about the views of the other tribe, and that the mistake is not random. It points in the direction of vilification. The other side is, generally, less extreme than we have been taught to assume. To act on that evidence, by default, is not naive. It is accurate.

*

We owe each other the work of the common.

The common is what we build together and share. The road, the school, the water system, the courthouse, the park, the library, the hospital, the congregation, the neighborhood, the polity. The common is what the modern economy disaggregates and the modern culture privatizes. Its repair is the task of our generation.

The work of the common is not heroic. It is not glorious. It is mostly boring — the planning committee, the audit, the election recount, the zoning debate, the budget negotiation, the shift at the food bank, the snow day on the PTA phone tree. It is the work the civic generation of 1910–1940 did in such quantity that it became invisible to those who followed and assumed they had inherited it by nature rather than by labor. We did not inherit it by nature. We inherited it, and we stopped doing it. It stopped.

What we owe each other is the resumption of this work. Not in grand gestures; in the ordinary texture of ordinary weeks. The reader who will close the book and attend a school board meeting this quarter has done more for the Return of the Citizen than the reader who will close the book and post about the Return of the Citizen. This is not a criticism of the second reader — we are also, it must be admitted, writers of posts — but a clarification of the priority. The work is at the small scale, in the slow time, with the actual neighbors.

*

There is a strong version of what the book has argued, and a weak version. The strong version is that a country doing these things at scale would become a country no longer at risk of the pathologies Volume II described. We believe this version, with appropriate humility. The weak version is that a country doing some of these things, unevenly, is better off than a country doing none of them. The weak version is undeniable, and it is the version the book asks its reader to act on even if the strong version is in doubt. The civic practices Volume III described are goods whether or not they change the country. They change the person doing them, and they contribute to whatever change is possible in a polity whose future has not been decided.

The future has not been decided. This is, in the end, the most important sentence in this book. The diagnosis of Volume II was grim — we are in a bad time for the American citizen, for a set of reasons that cannot be wished away. But the diagnosis is not a prophecy. What happens next depends on what is done, and what is done depends on who does it, and who does it is mostly the ordinary persons who will read a book like this one, do some of what it suggests, and continue doing so for long enough that the doing becomes a life.

*

The two of us who wrote this book began it in disagreement and end it in a qualified agreement that is closer to how disagreement is supposed to work among citizens than how it usually now works among Americans. We have argued through, not around, the tension between our starting framings. We have done so in the belief that a book that pretended to synthesis would be less useful than a book that enacted one.

The citizen is an individual who belongs. The individual needs the community to be an individual at all, and the community needs the individual to be a community rather than a mass. The liberal tradition is correct that each person is an end in herself; the republican tradition is correct that persons are formed in participation with equals; the communitarian tradition is correct that the modern self has been hollowed by abstraction; the post-liberal tradition is correct that liberal societies have been eating their own preconditions. None of these traditions is sufficient alone. All of them are necessary together. The older name for the arrangement is — has been, will be — the republic. The older name for its bearers is — has been, will be — the citizens.

*

We owe each other attention, the presumption of good faith, and the work of the common. That is the answer. It is not the last word. It is the first word of the next part of the argument, which is not written down. The next part will be written in the choices of the persons who finish this book and then — and this is the meaningful moment — do something with what they have read.

The citizen has not disappeared. The citizen is in the reader, waiting for the right conditions to return. We have tried to describe the conditions.

Return, then.
