# Foreword
### By Shaykh Abdallah bin Bayyah

*Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim —*

In the Name of God, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate.

I have been asked to introduce a book written by two American authors about a crisis in their republic, and the reader may wonder what a Mauritanian jurist trained in the classical Islamic sciences has to say about the matter. The answer is that the crisis they describe is not American. It is human. And the Islamic tradition, which has been thinking about the human person in political community for fourteen centuries, recognizes in their diagnosis a disease it has seen before — and in their prescription a medicine it has long administered under other names.

I have spent my life in the study of *fiqh* — Islamic jurisprudence — and in the effort to apply its principles to the conditions of our age. This work has taken me from the scholars' circles of Mauritania, where I was formed, to the councils of Muslim-majority states, to interfaith gatherings on several continents, and to the drafting of the Marrakesh Declaration, in which Muslim scholars affirmed, on Islamic grounds, the full rights of religious minorities in Muslim-majority lands. I mention this not to rehearse a biography but to say that the question at the center of *Return of the Citizen* — what does the human being owe the political community, and what does the community owe the human being? — is a question I have been answering, in the vocabulary of the Qur'an and the Sunnah, for the whole of my working life.

\*

The authors frame their central insight as a recovery of something the twentieth century understood and the twenty-first has forgotten: that atomization and mass movement are not opposites but two faces of one affliction. The lonely person becomes the fanatic. The person stripped of belonging becomes raw material for the movement that promises belonging at the cost of the self. Hannah Arendt and Eric Hoffer saw this. The authors have built their book upon it.

I want to tell the reader that this insight, which the authors rightly call one of the twentieth century's most important, is not new. It is as old as Medina.

When the Prophet Muhammad, peace be upon him, arrived in the city that would become the first Muslim polity, he found a population fractured by tribal enmity — the Aws and the Khazraj, locked in a cycle of vendetta and counter-vendetta that had consumed them for generations. They were, in the language of this book, *atomized within tribes* — belonging to something, certainly, but belonging in the way that the partisan belongs to the party: through the surrender of moral judgment to collective rage. The Prophet's first political act was not to issue a creed. It was to draft a covenant — the *Sahifat al-Madinah*, the Constitution of Medina — which bound Muslims, Jews, and pagans into a single civic community, an *ummah wahidah*, with defined rights, shared obligations, and a mechanism for the resolution of disputes. The document did not ask its signatories to abandon their faiths or their tribes. It asked them to recognize that they were now also something else: members of a common polity, answerable to common rules, obligated to defend one another, and bound to seek justice through consultation rather than vendetta.

This is citizenship. The authors do not use the word *Sahifah* and they need not. But the reader who understands what happened at Medina will recognize the architecture of *Return of the Citizen* at once: the refusal of the false choice between the individual and the collective, the insistence that belonging and freedom are not enemies, the conviction that the repair of public life begins with concrete obligations undertaken by concrete persons in a concrete place.

\*

There is a word in our tradition that I believe corresponds precisely to what the authors are calling "republican liberalism," though it is older by many centuries and richer in its implications. The word is *maslahah* — the common good, the public interest, the welfare of the people considered as a whole. The great jurist Abu Ishaq al-Shatibi, writing in fourteenth-century Granada, argued that the entirety of the Shari'ah — the whole vast edifice of Islamic law — exists to protect five essential goods: life, intellect, religion, lineage, and property. These are not the goods of the individual alone, nor of the collective alone. They are the goods of the person *in community*. A society that protects the individual's life but destroys the bonds of lineage and neighborhood has not served the *maslahah*. A society that enforces communal solidarity but crushes the individual's intellect and conscience has not served it either. The *maslahah* demands both. The citizen, in the language of this book, is an individual who belongs. The *maslahah* is the jurisprudential framework that makes that formulation coherent.

I say this not to claim that the Islamic tradition anticipated modern political philosophy — that would be an anachronism unworthy of the tradition — but to say that the human problem the authors are describing has been faced before, in other civilizations, under other skies, and that the solutions those civilizations found bear a family resemblance to the solution this book proposes. The reader who comes to these pages from the Muslim world, as some will, should know that the intellectual home the authors are building has rooms that will feel familiar.

\*

The diagnosis of Volume II grieves me, and I do not say this as a formality. I have watched, from the vantage point of one who advises Muslim communities on several continents, the same pathology the authors describe in the American context. The lonely young man who finds on the internet a movement that promises meaning. The community hollowed out by migration and economic disruption that becomes susceptible to the preacher of rage. The political entrepreneur who discovers that hatred is easier to organize than hope. The *da'iyah* — the caller to faith — who becomes a performer on a platform, seeking followers rather than forming souls. These are the pathologies of our age, and they are not confined to any one civilization.

The Qur'an says: *"O humanity, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another"* (49:13). The verb is *ta'arafu* — to know one another, to recognize one another, to make yourselves known to one another. It is not *tanazalu* — to contend, to fight, to dominate. The purpose of human diversity, in the Qur'anic vision, is mutual recognition. The authors of this book, working from entirely secular premises, have arrived at the same conclusion: the purpose of a diverse polity is not the victory of one tribe over another but the practice of mutual recognition among citizens who differ. When I read their account of the "perception gap" — the finding that Americans systematically misunderstand and demonize each other across partisan lines — I hear the Qur'an's warning inverted: a society that has replaced *ta'aruf* (mutual knowing) with *tanakur* (mutual estrangement) has violated the very purpose for which God made it plural.

\*

I wish to say something about the relationship between the religious traditions and the civic project this book describes, because I occupy a position from which that relationship is visible in a particular way.

The Holy Father, whose foreword opens this book, has spoken from the Christian tradition. The Rabbi, whose foreword follows his, has spoken from the Jewish tradition. I speak from the Islamic tradition. We are three voices from three faiths, and the reader will notice that we have, independently and without coordination, said many of the same things. This convergence is not an accident. It is evidence.

It is evidence of what the Islamic tradition calls *al-fitrah* — the primordial nature, the original constitution of the human being, the moral sense that God has placed in every person regardless of creed or nation. The Qur'an says: *"So direct your face toward the religion, inclining to truth — the fitrah of God upon which He created humankind"* (30:30). The *fitrah* is not a sectarian concept. It is the Islamic tradition's way of saying what the authors say in their secular vocabulary: that human beings are made for life with others, that the solitary self is not the whole self, that the practices of attention and good faith and common work are not arbitrary cultural preferences but responses to something deep in the structure of the human creature.

The authors have been careful to write a book that does not depend on any particular faith. I honor that discipline. But I want the reader to know that when she undertakes the practices of Volume III — when she attends her local meeting, when she listens to the neighbor she was taught to despise, when she gives her attention to the common rather than the feed — she is doing something that three ancient traditions, speaking in three different languages, recognize as her nature. She is doing what she was made for. The Islamic tradition calls it *'amal salih* — righteous action — and it does not require that the actor know the Arabic name. It requires that she act.

\*

I want to address, briefly, a concern that the Muslim reader may bring to this book. The Islamic tradition has its own post-liberal voices — scholars and movements that argue, as Patrick Deneen and Adrian Vermeule argue from the Christian side, that the liberal order is irredeemably corrupt and that the only serious response is its replacement by an order governed by divine law. I know these voices well. Some of them are my students. I have argued with them for decades, in the Arabic language and in the technical vocabulary of *usul al-fiqh*, and I will not rehearse those arguments here. But I will say this.

The Prophet, peace be upon him, lived as a minority in Makkah for thirteen years before he governed in Medina. In Makkah he did not withdraw. He did not declare the society around him irredeemable. He engaged it. He argued with it. He served its poor and honored its covenants. He participated, as a citizen in the older sense of that word, in the common life of a community that did not share his faith. And when he came to Medina, he did not build a community for Muslims alone. He built a community that included Muslims, Jews, Christians, and pagans, bound by a common covenant. The Medinan model is not the liberal model — I do not make that anachronistic claim — but it is a model of civic coexistence that takes pluralism as a starting condition rather than a problem to be solved. The Muslim who reads this book and recognizes in its arguments an invitation to civic engagement should know that she has a precedent for accepting that invitation that is older and more authoritative than the voices that counsel withdrawal.

\*

The authors write that the citizen is "an individual who belongs." I would add — and I believe they would accept the addition — that the citizen is an individual who *serves*. The Arabic word *khidmah*, service, is one of the highest terms of praise in our tradition. The Prophet described himself as the servant of his community. The caliph Umar ibn al-Khattab, walking the streets of Medina at night to check on the welfare of his people, said: "If a mule stumbles on the road in Iraq, I fear that God will ask me why I did not pave the road for it." This is not the language of rights alone, nor of duties alone. It is the language of a person whose liberty and whose obligation are two aspects of a single vocation: the vocation of the citizen who serves the common good because the common good is, in the deepest sense, his own good.

The practices the authors commend in Volume III — showing up, staying, listening, joining, serving, refusing the outrage that the machines dispense — these are, in the Islamic vocabulary, acts of *ihsan*: excellence, beauty, the doing of a thing in the best way it can be done. The Prophet, peace be upon him, defined *ihsan* as "worshiping God as though you see Him, and if you cannot see Him, then knowing that He sees you." The citizen who practices *ihsan* in public life — who attends to her neighbor as though the encounter matters, because it does — is performing an act of worship whether she knows it or not. The Islamic tradition is not shy about saying so. A society of citizens practicing *ihsan* toward one another is what the Qur'an calls a *baldah tayyibah* — a good land — and its Lord is *ghafur*, forgiving, which means that the failures along the way, of which there will be many, are not the final word.

\*

I close with a du'a — a supplication — which is how a Muslim scholar closes anything that matters.

O God, You who created the peoples and the tribes that they might know one another, grant the readers of this book the courage to know their neighbors and the patience to be known by them. Grant them the strength to attend when attention is costly, to presume good faith when suspicion is easier, and to do the slow work of the common when the feed offers faster satisfactions. Grant them the wisdom to see that the citizen is not a figure of nostalgia but a figure of hope, and that hope, in Your tradition and in the tradition of every serious faith, is not naivety but the most rigorous form of trust in what You have placed within the human being.

And grant — this is the prayer of an old man who has watched many societies struggle with the question this book addresses — grant that the republic whose renewal these authors have imagined may serve as a model for every society in which persons of different faiths and different tribes are trying to build, together, something worthy of the dignity You have bestowed upon them.

*Amin. Wa al-hamdu lillahi Rabb al-'alamin.*

Shaykh Abdallah bin Bayyah
*Abu Dhabi, Spring 2026*

---

*A note from the authors:* Shaykh bin Bayyah's foreword completes a triptych we did not plan but that the book, in some sense, called into being. A Pope, a Rabbi, and a Shaykh — speaking from three traditions that have not always been at peace with one another — have each found in the argument of this book something their own tradition recognizes. We take this convergence not as proof that we are right, but as evidence that the question is real and that the human answer, however it is named, has a shape that the traditions have been tracing, from different starting points, for a very long time. We are grateful beyond our capacity to express it.
