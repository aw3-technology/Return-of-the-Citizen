# Foreword
### By Rabbi Kalman Meir Ber

There is a passage in the Talmud — Sanhedrin 37a — that the authors of this book do not cite but that sits beneath their argument like bedrock. The Mishnah teaches that Adam was created alone so that no person could say to another, "My father was greater than your father." The sages understood something the modern world has labored to rediscover: that equality is not a policy position. It is a condition of the human creature. And the political community that forgets it does not merely become unjust. It becomes unintelligible to itself.

I have been asked to introduce *Return of the Citizen* because the authors believe, correctly, that the Jewish tradition has something to say about what it means to be a citizen — and because they are generous enough to want that voice heard alongside the voice of the Holy Father, whose own foreword precedes mine in these pages. I am honored to stand in such company, and I want to say at the outset that the Pope and I, who disagree about a great many things that matter, agree about the thing this book is about: the human person is made for life with others, and a society that strips away the conditions of that life is committing an act of slow violence against its own members.

I write as a rabbi, which means I write as a teacher. For thirty years I have taught Torah in communities where the questions this book raises — loneliness, the collapse of trust, the seductions of the tribe — are not abstractions. They are the questions that walk into my study on a Tuesday afternoon wearing the face of a congregant who has lost his sense of who he is.

\*

The Jewish tradition does not have a single political theory. It has an argument — one that has been running for three thousand years and shows no signs of resolution. But within that argument, certain things are settled, or as close to settled as Jews ever get, and two of them bear directly on the thesis of this book.

The first is that the individual and the community are not opposites. They are conditions of each other. The Torah was given not to a single prophet but to a people assembled at the foot of a mountain — *kol Yisrael*, all of Israel, together. And yet the commandments are addressed in the singular: *you* shall not murder, *you* shall not steal, *you* shall love your neighbor as yourself. The covenant is collective; the obligation is personal. The authors call their synthesis "republican liberalism." A rabbi might call it *brit* — covenant — which is the older word for the same insight: that a person becomes fully a person only within a bond of mutual obligation, and that the bond is only real if the persons within it are free to say yes or no.

The second settled thing is that argument is not a defect of communal life. It is the form communal life takes when it is healthy. The Talmud preserves the minority opinion alongside the majority, not out of scholarly politeness but out of a conviction that the truth is too large for any single mind and that the defeated argument may one day be needed. The phrase the tradition uses is *machloket l'shem shamayim* — argument for the sake of heaven. Such argument, the Mishnah says in Pirkei Avot, endures. Argument that is not for the sake of heaven — argument whose purpose is victory, humiliation, the annihilation of the other — does not endure. It consumes.

I read Volume II of this book with the particular pain of recognition. The affective polarization the authors describe, the style of public speech that treats the opponent not as a fellow citizen but as a disease to be eradicated, the tribal sorting that makes every disagreement existential — this is argument not for the sake of heaven. It is the argument that consumes. And the Jewish tradition knows, from long and bitter experience, where it leads.

\*

Let me say something about loneliness, which is the wound at the center of this book's diagnosis.

The first thing the Torah declares to be *lo tov* — not good — is not idolatry, not murder, not theft. It is loneliness. "It is not good for the human to be alone." This is said before the giving of the law, before the building of any community, before the entry into any covenant. It is the foundational moral observation of the entire tradition: the solitary self is an incomplete self. God Himself, if one may say so, looked at the solitary human and said: this is not yet what I meant.

The authors have marshaled the empirical evidence — Putnam's data, the Surgeon General's findings, the studies on social capital and its collapse — and the evidence is devastating. But the rabbi in me wants to say that we did not need the data. We have known this since Bereishit. The lonely person is the person available for capture, as Arendt saw. The lonely person is the person who will hand himself over to any movement that promises to end the loneliness, as Hoffer saw. And the lonely person is the person whom the Holy One, blessed be He, looked at in the garden and said: I will make for him a counterpart. Not a subordinate. Not a mirror. A counterpart — an *ezer k'negdo*, a help who stands opposite, who is both with him and against him, who completes him precisely by being other than him.

This is what citizenship is supposed to be. The citizen is the *ezer k'negdo* of every other citizen: a person who stands with you in the common project and against you in the honest argument, who helps you precisely by refusing to be your echo. A republic of echoes is not a republic. It is a hall of mirrors, and the Jewish tradition has a word for what happens in halls of mirrors. It is called *avodah zarah* — idol worship — the worship of one's own image projected onto the cosmos.

\*

The authors are careful, in this book, to maintain what they call "symmetry of the mass analysis" — applying the diagnosis of cult formation equally to left and right, to secular ideologies and religious ones. I want to affirm this symmetry from within my own tradition, and I want to do so with the honesty the book deserves.

Jews are not exempt. We who have been, across the centuries, the quintessential victims of mass movements have also been, in certain times and places, their participants and even their architects. The Jewish commitment to justice — *tzedek, tzedek tirdof*, justice, justice you shall pursue — has sometimes curdled, in modern hands, into the very ideological rigidity the prophets warned against. And the Jewish commitment to peoplehood — the fierce attachment to the survival of the community — has sometimes hardened into the tribalism that mistakes loyalty for virtue and dissent for betrayal. I do not say this to give ammunition to the enemies of my people, of whom there are always enough. I say it because the book in front of you has earned, by its own honesty, the right to be met with honesty in return. No tradition is a vaccine against the mass. Every tradition must do its own work.

\*

I want to speak now to Volume III, which is the volume the authors say costs something. It costs something because it asks the reader to do things — not to think different thoughts, but to live differently. To join. To show up. To stay. To listen to the person she was trained to despise. To practice the disciplines of attention in an economy of distraction.

The Jewish tradition has a word for this kind of practice. It is *mitzvah* — commandment. Not suggestion. Not lifestyle recommendation. Commandment. The rabbis counted 613 of them, and while a modern Jew may observe fewer than that, the underlying architecture is the same: the moral life is not a feeling. It is a practice. It is something you do with your body, in a place, on a specific day of the week, with other people. You light candles on Friday evening not because you feel spiritual but because it is Friday evening and the candles are there and the obligation is there and the community that shares the obligation is there. The feeling, if it comes, comes after the practice, not before it.

This is exactly what the authors are arguing in their secular vocabulary. The citizen does not wait until she feels civic. She shows up at the school board meeting, and the feeling follows the action. She joins the association, and the belonging follows the joining. She listens to the neighbor across the political line, and the recognition of the neighbor's humanity follows the listening. Practice first. Disposition second. This is not a secular insight dressed up in civic clothing. It is one of the oldest insights of the religious life, and the authors are right to build their program on it, whatever vocabulary they use.

\*

I want to close with a story, as rabbis do.

There is a famous tale about Hillel the Elder, who was asked by a man standing on one foot to summarize the entire Torah. Hillel said: "What is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow. That is the whole Torah. The rest is commentary — now go and learn."

The skeptic reads this and thinks Hillel was being reductive. He was not. He was being radical. He was saying that the entire vast architecture of Jewish law — the 613 commandments, the Talmud's oceans of argument, the millennia of commentary and counter-commentary — rests on a single foundation: the recognition that the other person is real. That his pain is as real as yours. That her dignity is as non-negotiable as yours. That the person you are arguing with at the school board meeting, the person you are sitting next to on the citizens' panel, the person whose politics make your blood boil — that person is created, as you are, *b'tselem Elohim*, in the image of God, and is therefore owed the kind of attention you would want to receive.

This is what I hear the authors of *Return of the Citizen* saying, in their own language and from their own starting point. They are saying: the other citizen is real. Not a bot, not a caricature, not a symptom, not a deplorable, not an enemy of the people. A person. A neighbor. A fellow-bearer of the weight of self-government.

The Jewish tradition adds one thing the authors, in the discipline of their secular project, do not say, and I will say it for them because it is mine to say. The work they are describing — the patient, undramatic, Tuesday-evening work of showing up and staying and listening and arguing honestly and refusing to hate — this work is not only civic. It is holy. In Hebrew the word is *kadosh*. It means set apart. Set apart not in the sense of withdrawn from the world, but in the sense of dedicated to something beyond the self. The citizen who does this work may not know she is doing holy work. She may never use the word. But the tradition I serve says she is, and I believe it, and I commend this book to every reader who is willing to find out what it feels like to try.

*Shalom aleichem* — peace be upon you. And upon the republic whose renewal these authors have labored to imagine.

Rabbi Kalman Meir Ber
*Spring 5786*

*A note from the authors:* Rabbi Ber's foreword, like that of Pope Leo XIV, speaks from a particular tradition. We are grateful that he has allowed his tradition to illuminate our argument, and we note, with the kind of pleasure two secular writers rarely get to feel, that the Pope and the Rabbi have, independently and in their own voices, arrived at many of the same conclusions. The reader who belongs to neither tradition, or to a different one, will find, we hope, that the convergence is itself an argument for the thesis of this book.
