# Introduction
## The Citizen Missing from the Room

There is a figure who used to stand at the center of political life in the countries that call themselves republics. He — and, belatedly, she — appears under many names in many traditions: *polites*, *civis*, *burgher*, *citoyen*, *citizen*. The figure is always roughly the same. The citizen is a person who belongs to a political community of equals, who participates in its common life, who is bound by its laws, who bears responsibility for its character, and whose personhood is in some deep sense completed by the participation rather than merely exercised through it. The word does not simply mean *resident* or *voter* or *taxpayer*. It means, at root, *one who shares in the polis* — one who is a partner in the common thing.

This figure has gone missing from the room.

You can feel the absence in the texture of public life. We talk as consumers talking to other consumers, through platforms designed to measure attention and extract it. We talk as partisans to enemies we have never met, through systems that reward contempt and punish sympathy. We talk as therapy clients to therapists who never answer, pouring our grievances into feeds that never reply. What we almost never do is *address each other as citizens* — as people who share a polity, who owe each other an explanation, who are jointly responsible for the common thing we are building or wrecking.

This is a book about why the citizen disappeared, what the disappearance has cost us, and how — concretely, patiently, with the tools we already have — the citizen might return.

## The Puzzle

We live at what is, by almost every measurable standard, the best moment in the history of our species. Global extreme poverty has fallen by more than a billion people in a generation. Childhood mortality has collapsed. Information that a medieval monarch could not have bought at any price is free to anyone with a phone. Violent crime in most rich countries is a fraction of what it was in the 1970s. The great killers of the twentieth century — famine, war between great powers, smallpox — are either extinct or in retreat.

And yet.

Suicide rates among young Americans have climbed relentlessly for two decades. The U.S. Surgeon General has declared an "epidemic of loneliness." The percentage of Americans who trust their neighbors to return a lost wallet has fallen. The percentage who know their neighbors' names has fallen. The percentage who believe democracy is essential to a good life has fallen. Trust in nearly every major American institution — Congress, the press, the church, the university, the criminal justice system, the corporation — has fallen, in most cases by half or more, over the past fifty years. Each of the two largest political parties now regards the other, in survey after survey, not as rival but as existential threat. And the movements attracting the most intense loyalties — on the illiberal left and the illiberal right, in the identity crusades and the technological eschatologies — are movements whose core promise is that the regime which produced this abundance is corrupt beyond repair and must be torn down.

This is the paradox that has to be explained. A society that has delivered unprecedented material goods has produced a citizenry that is unhappy, isolated, furious, and drawn to political movements built on the premise that the society is irredeemable.

Every serious book in the last ten years has tried to explain this puzzle. Patrick Deneen says it is because liberalism succeeded. Robert Putnam says it is because the civic associations collapsed. Yuval Levin says it is because the institutions became platforms. Jonathan Haidt says it is because the phones rewired the brains. Michael Sandel says it is because the merit regime humiliated the losers. Chris Bail says it is because the algorithms distorted the public mirror. Ezra Klein says it is because partisan identity ate every other identity. Each of these explanations has empirical weight behind it. None of them, alone, is enough.

This book's answer is both simpler and older. Those accounts are describing, from different angles, a single phenomenon the twentieth century was the first to name clearly: the hollowing-out of the person and the rise of the mass.

## The Two Halves of a Single Problem

Hannah Arendt and Eric Hoffer, writing independently and publishing in the same year, 1951, saw the same thing. The totalitarian movements of their century had not recruited from the ranks of the politically engaged and the associationally thick. They had recruited from among the atomized — the unemployed, the dispossessed, the lonely, the people who had lost their place in a world. Arendt put it with characteristic bluntness: loneliness is "the common ground for terror." Hoffer, in plainer American prose, observed that the true believer is running *from himself* as much as toward a cause — "losing one's individual distinctness in a compact collective whole" — and that mass movements are therefore interchangeable at their psychological core. Whether the movement is fascist, communist, nationalist, religious, or revolutionary is a matter of local accident. The human raw material is the same.

This was one of the twentieth century's most important insights, and it has been largely forgotten. Most contemporary analysis treats the rise of mass politics and the collapse of civic life as two different problems. They are one problem. The person stripped of family, neighborhood, congregation, union, club, and vocation is the person available for capture by the tribe. You do not get cults in a society with thick local life. You get cults in a society where the local life has been stripped away and replaced with nothing.

The implication cuts in both directions.

If you take only the first half of the argument — the individual side — you get what a great deal of well-meaning contemporary writing looks like. You urge people to think critically, open their minds, seek diverse viewpoints, resist the extremes. This is not wrong, but it is radically incomplete. It is advice given to a lonely man standing in a parking lot. The advice does not reach him, because the conditions that made him susceptible in the first place — the absence of anyone in his life to think with, to argue with, to be accountable to — have not changed. Critical thinking is not a cognitive style you can download. It is a practice that requires a community of other minds.

If you take only the second half — the civic side — you get the kind of pious communitarian writing that sends every reader to plant a garden with his neighbors. This, too, is not wrong, and in the last chapter of this book we will ask something not far removed from it. But the civic recommendation, offered alone, misses that the person who will have to do the planting is a modern individual, formed by modern freedom, and that any proposal to rebuild civic life on ancient foundations has to reckon with the fact that the ancient foundations were made of inequalities — of sex, of class, of religion, of race — that we rightly will not resurrect. The modern citizen has to be a citizen *and* an individual. The tradition that understood this best, intermittently and without the name, is the one this book tries to recover.

## Four Traditions and What Each One Gets Right

Before we can recover that tradition, we have to walk through the company it keeps. Four major intellectual traditions have laid serious claim to the word *citizen* in the last two centuries, and the first volume of this book is a tour through each. The very short version:

**Classical liberalism** — from Locke through Kant and Mill to Rawls and Fukuyama — begins with the individual. The person comes before the polity; rights are held against the state; the purpose of political order is to secure those rights so that each person can pursue his own conception of the good. Classical liberalism's central achievement is moral: the recognition that every human being is an end in himself, that no one is merely a subject, that the political community exists for its members rather than the other way around. Its central danger is that, in bracketing the question of the good, it can leave its adherents without a shared language for anything that matters most to them, and thus without the resources to resist the tribes that do offer such a language.

**Civic republicanism** — from Aristotle and Cicero through Machiavelli and Harrington to Arendt and Pettit — begins with the polity. The person is completed by participation in public life; liberty is best understood not as absence of restraint but as the condition of not being at the mercy of an arbitrary power; a self-governing people requires a certain kind of character, which is what "virtue" meant before the word turned moralistic. Civic republicanism's central achievement is its account of freedom as *non-domination*, which is indispensable for understanding the unfreedom of the modern worker, the modern debtor, and the modern user. Its central danger is that, in emphasizing the common good, it can slide into the imposition of a *particular* good, and thus into a soft or hard authoritarianism.

**Communitarianism** — Sandel, MacIntyre, Taylor, Walzer, Bellah — arose in the 1980s as a direct response to Rawls. Its claim is that the liberal self, the one choosing its values from behind a veil of ignorance, is a philosophical fiction and a sociological catastrophe. Real selves are constituted by traditions, communities, and relationships they did not choose. A politics that pretends otherwise produces the alienated, atomized individual who ends up in a mass movement. Communitarianism's central achievement is its diagnosis of why liberal societies generate their own worst enemies. Its central danger is that, taken too far, it loses the capacity to criticize the communities it reveres.

**Post-liberalism** — Deneen, Vermeule, Ahmari, Hazony — is the most recent and most aggressive of the critiques. Its claim is that liberalism has not gone wrong but rather succeeded: what it set out to do, it has done, and what it has done is the wreckage. The post-liberals do not want to reform liberalism; they want to replace it. The most honest thing we can say about post-liberalism is that its diagnosis is often piercing and its prescriptions are often frightening. We engage it in this book because a generation of readers will engage it, and the book that pretends otherwise will be talking past its audience.

Our own position, built across Volume I, is neither a fifth tradition nor a synthesis by committee. It is closest to what Richard Dagger has called "republican liberalism" — the thesis that the liberal achievement and the republican achievement are not rivals but complements, and that the citizen as a type requires both. A citizen is an individual, with rights against the state and a conscience that is his own, *and* a member of a polity, bound in a common life with equals whose good is also his good. Neither half without the other. That is the thesis this book defends.

## What the Argument Asks of the Reader

We are going to ask three things.

First, we are going to ask you to read past your own camp. If you begin the book as a libertarian individualist, we will ask you to read Sandel and MacIntyre charitably. If you begin as a communitarian, we will ask you to take Mill and Berlin at their strongest. If you begin as a post-liberal, we will ask you to read Fukuyama's *Liberalism and Its Discontents* without preparing rebuttals. If you begin as a progressive, we will ask you to take the populist diagnosis seriously — not its prescriptions, but its underlying grievance. If you begin as a conservative, we will ask the same about the left critique of economic power. This is not a plea for centrism. It is a plea for attention. The book that wins its arguments by ignoring the strongest version of the opposing case has nothing to teach anyone who already held that opposing case, which is to say it has nothing to teach most of its potential readers.

Second, we are going to ask you to entertain the possibility that the problem is at once larger and smaller than you think. Larger, because it is not a matter of which party wins the next election or which policy passes the next congress. Smaller, because the answer — whatever shape it eventually takes at scale — has to begin in lives no bigger than yours and ours, in institutions no more glamorous than the school board and the neighborhood association and the bowling league. Volume III will argue that there is no other scale at which the repair can start.

Third, we are going to ask you to consider that the mood of the moment — the cynicism, the defeatism, the belief that the system is beyond fixing, the secret or not-so-secret hope that someone will come along and burn it all down — is itself a symptom of the disease rather than an accurate perception of the prognosis. A people that believes itself to be doomed will be doomed; a people that believes itself capable of common action will, eventually, act. The single most important political act, in a moment like ours, is to refuse the story that nothing can be done.

## A Brief Tour of the Structure

**Volume I — The Citizen.** Seven chapters on the philosophical foundation. The oldest question; the four traditions in turn; the American quarrel between the Federalists and the Anti-Federalists as the first great local version of the same argument; and, in the concluding chapter, our own reconstruction — republican liberalism — as a usable answer that carries forward into the rest of the book.

**Volume II — Broken Systems in an Age of Abundance.** Seven chapters on what has actually happened. The paradox of plenty; the return of mass-movement psychology; the collapse of social capital; the institutional decay; the polarization findings; the platform public sphere; and the hollowing of work and merit. We engage each of the major explanations on offer, note where they disagree, and argue for a synthesis in which the loneliness thesis — Arendt's and Hoffer's — is the connective tissue.

**Volume III — The Citizen Revitalized.** Seven chapters on what to do. Why most solutions fail. The surprisingly large empirical literature on what actually works to depolarize and rebuild. Critical thinking as civic discipline. Institutional renewal from the inside out. Deliberative democracy in practice. The personal practices of a public life. And, finally, a picture of what a country of returning citizens would look like — not as utopia, but as a place we could plausibly build from here.

And a conclusion, which is the book's moral argument in its barest form: *what we owe each other*.

## A Personal Word

The two of us who wrote this book did not start from the same place. The book itself is an argument we have been having with each other for several years. The reader will be able to feel, in some chapters, one of our voices more than the other. We have not tried to erase this. A book about the citizen, written by two citizens, arguing in public toward a common thesis, is closer to the thing it argues for than a book of seamless corporate prose would have been.

The citizen is not a nostalgic figure. The citizen is a figure of the future, or of nothing at all. This is a book about how to choose the first.

Let us begin.
