# Preface

This book began as an argument between friends.

We were talking, one of us from an individualist starting point and the other from a civic-republican one, about a question neither of us could stop asking: what has happened to the American citizen? Not the American voter, who shows up every four years and is endlessly surveyed and tracked. Not the American consumer, whose preferences are the object of the largest data-gathering apparatus in human history. The citizen — the person who inhabits a public world with equals, who can still be addressed in the second person plural, who owes something to that world and is owed something by it in return. We agreed that the citizen had gone missing. We agreed that the disappearance was a catastrophe. We did not agree on why.

One of us tends to describe the problem in the language of the individual: people have been absorbed into ideological cults, severed from the reasoning minds that make them free, captured by mass movements whose grammar is the grammar of the mob. The fix is the recovery of the single person, standing on his or her own reason, thinking openly and arguing honestly with others who do the same.

The other tends to describe the problem in the language of the polity: people have been stripped of the associations that form character, of the institutions that tell them who they are, of the public space in which the virtues are practiced. The fix is the recovery of the civic — the rebuilding, from the ground up, of the shared life without which the individual is only a rumor of itself.

These two framings do not obviously agree. They have been arguing with each other for forty years in the academy and for longer than that in American life. The classical liberal who reads the civic republican hears the faint footfalls of Vermeule and the administrative theocracy. The civic republican who reads the classical liberal hears the hum of Amazon and the loneliness epidemic. Each camp can produce a respectable library of books demonstrating that the other is the real danger.

We have concluded that both of us are right and that the apparent disagreement is the book. The citizen is degraded by atomization and by mass movement at once — because they are the same phenomenon seen from different angles. Hannah Arendt and Eric Hoffer saw this in 1951. The lonely person, the person stripped of a world, becomes raw material for the movement that promises a world. You cannot save the individual without restoring the polity, and you cannot restore the polity without respecting the individual. The tradition that understood this best is the one whose name is on the cover of this book. The citizen is an individual who belongs.

We have tried to write the book that sees both halves at once. We have also tried, as much as two writers can, to earn the right to the arguments we make. In practice this has meant spending a long time with thinkers neither of us would naturally cite. We have read Patrick Deneen carefully enough to recognize what is true in his diagnosis and what is dangerous in his prescription. We have read John Rawls carefully enough to see why his critics are not bad-faith traditionalists. We have read Robert Putnam's data closely enough to feel their weight. We have read Eric Hoffer and Hannah Arendt end-to-end, which is not the same as quoting them, and it has changed what we think about what is happening now. We have tried to treat every camp we engage with the way we would want our own position treated if it were the one on the other side of an argument.

A few notes before we begin.

On the word *cult*. We use it, in the pages that follow, to describe a specific kind of ideological formation whose features are well-documented — by Robert Lifton, Margaret Singer, and Steven Hassan, and by Hoffer and Arendt before them. We do not use it to describe every political movement we disagree with. We apply it, when we apply it, symmetrically: to the far left and to the far right, to secular ideologies and to religious ones, to the "woke" tribe and to the populist tribe, to identity radicalisms and to tech eschatologies alike. The point of the analysis is not that the other side is a cult and our side is fine. The point is that the conditions of modern life produce the kind of person who is susceptible to mass formations, and that this susceptibility is the common property of everyone reading this book, including us.

On partisanship. We are citizens of no party. This is not a claim of neutrality — we have political opinions and the attentive reader will be able to infer some of them — but a claim about what we think this book is for. A book that scores points against a named political figure makes a smaller claim on the future than a book that tries to say something durable about human beings in public life. We have tried to write the latter.

On three volumes. This is a long book, and we do not apologize for it. A question this old and this hard is not going to yield to a fast argument. The three-volume structure — foundation, diagnosis, renewal — is the only honest architecture we could find. If the reader picks up only one volume, we hope it will be Volume III, which is the one that costs something to write and, we think, something to do.

On who this book is for. It is for the citizen in the old sense: the person who reads seriously, thinks for herself, and is still open to being wrong. It is not for the partisan who wants ammunition, and it is not for the cynic who has already concluded that the public world is over. We believe it is not over. We believe most Americans, across every line that currently separates them, want something better than what our public life has become. This book is our attempt to say what that something could be, and how a person might begin to build it.

We have made every argument we could make as honestly as we know how. The rest is with the reader.

*Will Schulz and Mike Suppes*
*April 2026*
