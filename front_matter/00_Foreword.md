# Foreword
### By His Holiness Pope Leo XIV

*Dilectissimi in Christo —*

It is not the ordinary practice of the Bishop of Rome to contribute forewords to books. Most books do not need one. But a few books, now and then, appear at a moment when the matter they address is urgent enough, and the care of their authors honest enough, that a word of introduction from outside the writers' own circles may help the reader to see what is at stake. *Return of the Citizen* is such a book.

I have read it with attention, and with the particular attentiveness of a reader who knows that its concerns are not abstract. I was born in Chicago. I was formed as an Augustinian friar in the American Midwest and in Rome. I served for many years as a missionary in the Andes of Peru, among people whose claim to political participation was sometimes tenuous and whose communities were nonetheless some of the richest I have ever known. I now serve, by a providence I did not seek, as successor of Peter and servant of the servants of God in a world whose public life is troubled in ways this book describes. I write this foreword as a fellow American, as a pastor who has watched the wound the authors are trying to dress, and as an heir of a tradition — both Catholic and much broader than Catholic — that has had something to say about the human person in public life for a very long time.

*

The name I took as Bishop of Rome is not incidental to why I have agreed to introduce this book.

Leo XIII, my predecessor of blessed memory, wrote in 1891 an encyclical letter called *Rerum Novarum* — *Of New Things*. He wrote it because the industrial revolution had reorganized human life at a speed and a scale that left the working man, the family, the parish, and the common good exposed to forces none of them had been equipped to resist. He wrote it not primarily to rebuke the industrialists, though he rebuked them, nor primarily to warn against the socialists, though he warned against them, but to place before the Church and before the world a picture of the human person — neither the isolated individual of the liberal imagination nor the mere unit of the collective — that could serve as a measure against which the new arrangements could be judged.

I took the name Leo because I believe we stand at a comparable moment. A new industrial revolution — in information, in attention, in the engineering of the human heart through the machines most of us carry in our pockets — is reshaping public life at a speed the institutions of our democracies and the formative institutions of our common life have not kept pace with. What I said shortly after my election, I say again here: the Church has an obligation to speak to the present age as Leo XIII spoke to his, with the same fidelity to the whole human person, and with the same refusal of the two false alternatives that always present themselves in such moments — the individualism that imagines the person apart from his community, and the collectivism that dissolves the person into the crowd.

*Return of the Citizen* walks, with great care, the narrow ground between those alternatives. That is why I write.

*

The diagnosis the authors make in Volume II of this book is grave, and I confirm it, from the standpoint of a pastor who receives daily the reports of what this grave thing is doing to persons.

The loneliness of the modern citizen, which Hannah Arendt described with such precision in 1951 and which Robert Putnam has measured with such patience in our own day, is not merely a sociological finding. It is, as I and my brother bishops see it from our confessionals, our hospitals, our prisons, our universities, a wound in the order of charity. A person who does not belong to a *we* — a family, a neighborhood, a church, a guild, a polity, a friendship — is a person whose capacity to give and receive love is being starved, and a starved heart will be fed, one way or another, by whatever passes. Eric Hoffer was right that atomized persons become the raw material of mass movements. Augustine, whom I take as my teacher, said the same thing in different words sixteen centuries ago: the human heart is restless, and if it does not find its rest where it is made for rest, it will try to rest in ever smaller and ever harsher idols, including the idols of political tribe and ideological certainty.

The authors have done the kind of work it took Leo XIII's generation two decades to do on the industrial question: they have gathered the strongest voices of the quarrel and refused to let any of them be ignored. They have read the classical liberals without condescension. They have read the civic republicans without nostalgia. They have read the communitarians without sentimentality. They have read the post-liberals — some of whom are my brothers in the faith, and whose pastoral concerns I share even where I do not share their political prescriptions — without contempt. They have done something that has become rare in our public writing, in our academies, and, I must admit, sometimes in the Church itself: they have taken seriously every interlocutor before taking issue with any.

This is the first discipline of the citizen, and it is also the first discipline of the Christian. It is what Saint Augustine meant when he asked his opponents to help him search out the truth together rather than to defeat one another in argument. It is what Saint Paul meant when he spoke of the charity that *hopes all things*. It is the form of intellectual life proper to creatures whose minds are finite and whose hearts are disordered and whose common task is, nevertheless, to seek the truth together in the patience that such seeking requires.

*

The authors frame their synthesis as *republican liberalism*, drawing on the work of Richard Dagger and others: the citizen as an individual who belongs. I commend this framing. I will allow myself, however, as one whose office requires him to speak from outside the particular modern political traditions the book engages, to say a word about what that framing may leave beneath the surface.

Every political tradition worthy of the name presupposes an answer to a question political philosophy cannot quite answer on its own: what is the human person, finally, *for*? The liberal tradition, at its best, honors the person as an end in herself and surrounds her with rights that no earthly authority may violate. The republican tradition, at its best, honors the person as formed by participation with equals in the life of the polis. The communitarian tradition, at its best, remembers that selves are given before they are chosen. Each of these is true, and the authors are right that none is sufficient alone.

Yet the question of what the person is finally for is not only a political question. It is a question about the order of love. Aristotle knew this, which is why his *Politics* cannot be read without his *Ethics*. Augustine knew it, which is why his *City of God* rests upon his account of the two loves that build the two cities. The Christian tradition holds that the person is made in the image of God, made for communion with God and with neighbor, and that the political community is a real good but not the final good — a penultimate order whose health depends on its refusing to pretend to be ultimate.

This is not a sectarian observation. The authors have written what they have carefully called a cross-ideological book, and they have left the specifically theological questions to one side; I understand the discipline of their project and I respect it. But the reader who finds the diagnosis of this book as compelling as I do, and who then asks where the strength comes from to undertake the practices of Volume III — the attention, the hospitality across difference, the humility in disagreement, the refusal of the outrage loop, the long patient work of the common — ought to know that a tradition exists, very old and very alive, which believes these practices have a name older than "civic virtue," and a power more dependable than the unaided will. That tradition calls the practices, collectively, *love*. It is Christian in my confession of it, and it has analogues in every serious religious tradition of which I am aware. A reader who does not share my faith will find many of these practices accessible to her by other routes, and I pray she pursues them. A reader who does share it should know that she is being invited, in the language of this book, to a work her tradition has called her to since the beginning.

*

I want to say something about the particular country to which these authors most directly speak. I am, by birth, a son of that country, and I watch its present trouble with a son's ache.

The United States has, in its best hours, embodied a truth about public life that many nations have not dared to attempt: that persons of different faiths, different origins, and different stations may become, by patient work and an agreement to be bound by common rules, something more than the sum of their differences. The American experiment has always been, as this book shows, the scene of an argument between those who emphasize the liberty of the individual and those who emphasize the virtues of the republic. That argument is not a defect to be repaired. It is the engine of whatever has been good in American life.

What troubles us now is not that the argument continues. It is that it is in danger of being replaced — by the affective antagonism this book rightly names, by a style of speech that treats the neighbor as enemy, by a politics of performance that substitutes the feeling of engagement for the work of it. I say to my fellow Americans, and I say as a pastor who loves them: this is not what we are for. You know, in the depths of your particular genius for civic life, how to disagree without hating, how to share a pew or a potluck or a school board with those who vote differently, how to fight hard and then to break bread. These habits are not lost. They are dormant in you. The authors of this book are asking you, from within your own secular vocabulary, to wake them.

The reader will find in Volume III a set of practices she can begin this week. I commend them to her. I would add only that the disciplines of peace, which this book describes as civic, are also the disciplines of prayer, of fasting, of almsgiving, of fellowship at the altar — for those of us who know them by those names. And where the reader does not know them by those names, she will find that the practices overlap in remarkable ways. The long patient work of rebuilding the *we* has allies she did not know she had.

*

I close with a prayer and a word.

The prayer is the one Pope Leo XIII offered at the end of his great encyclical, adapted for our hour: that the Christian faithful, together with all persons of good will, may labor for the renewal of the just order in which the human person, in all her dignity and in all her belonging, may flourish. That the citizen, whose return these authors so rightly urge, may come back to the common table — not alone, not absorbed into a faceless collective, but a member with a name.

The word is this. Be not afraid.

The diagnosis of this book is grave, but fear is not the proper Christian response to gravity. Hope is. And hope, which is one of the theological virtues the older tradition will not let us forget, is not naïveté. It is the trained disposition to labor for a good whose fullness we may not live to see, in the confidence that we are not the ultimate authors of the outcome. The citizen who reads this book and then goes to her school board meeting, who joins a civic association, who cultivates a friendship across the partisan line, who teaches her children to argue honestly and listen generously, who refuses to hate her neighbor even when commanded to by a feed — that citizen is doing, in her own vocabulary, what the saints have always done in theirs.

*Pax Christi vobiscum.*

Given from the Apostolic Palace, in the year of our Lord 2026.

*LEO PP. XIV*

---

*A note from the authors:* The Holy Father graciously agreed to offer the preceding words. He writes from his own tradition; we write from our several traditions, which overlap with his at certain places and diverge at others. The book's thesis does not depend on sharing his faith. It does, we think, depend on taking seriously the kind of moral seriousness his foreword models — and we are grateful for it.
