# Chapter 2
## The Republican Inheritance

The word *citizen* has never been a neutral word. It sounds neutral to us — bureaucratic, almost — because we meet it mostly on passport applications and jury summonses, in the language of paperwork. But its lineage is not neutral at all. *Citizen* is a republican word. It was coined to name a particular kind of human being in a particular kind of political community, and the tradition that coined it had commitments most of its present users would be surprised to discover they have inherited. To say "I am a citizen" is, whether one knows it or not, to speak a line from a very old play. The play has many acts and many authors, and some of its scenes we rightly refuse to stage again. But modern politics in the countries that call themselves republics is written in its grammar, and a book about the disappearance of the citizen has to begin by saying what was there when the citizen was present.

This chapter is that saying. It is a walk through the civic-republican tradition — the oldest continuous strand in Western political thought and the one most directly evoked by the noun on the cover of this book. We take the tradition in order, from Aristotle to the present, and we try to let each of its major voices speak in its own register before we judge. At the end we extract the tradition's core commitments, we make the strongest case we know how to make for it, and we name the dangers we think it carries. Our claim, to show our hand at the start, is not that civic republicanism is the tradition we should now revive in pure form. No serious person should want Athens back. Our claim is that the tradition named something real about what it is to be a human being in public, and that without recovering what it named, the word *citizen* loses its weight and becomes a synonym for *resident with documents*. The disappearance of the citizen, which this whole book is about, begins with the forgetting of what the word was originally for.

***

The tradition has two branches, in productive argument with each other for about twenty-five centuries, worth keeping distinct at the start. The first we will call, following the conventional labels, *neo-Athenian* or *civic humanist*. It runs from Aristotle through the Florentine humanists to Hannah Arendt, and its central claim is that direct participation in public life is not merely instrumental but intrinsically valuable — that to act and speak among equals in the open space of a political community is a fundamental human good, maybe the highest of the properly human goods, and that a life which never rises to such action is missing something private life cannot supply. The second branch we will call *neo-Roman*, after the label that Quentin Skinner and Philip Pettit have made canonical. It runs from the Roman republican writers through Machiavelli and Harrington to Pettit himself, and its central claim is subtler and in some ways more portable: that freedom consists not in the absence of interference but in the absence of *domination* — in not living at the mercy of an arbitrary power that can, if it wishes, interfere with you, whether or not it chooses to do so on any given day. The benevolent slave-owner who lets his slave do as he pleases does not thereby make the slave free. The slave is unfree because he lives at the master's discretion, and discretion is what non-domination refuses to accept. Both branches insist that self-government is the political form in which freedom is possible; both insist that self-government requires a certain kind of citizen, formed in certain kinds of institutions. But the first branch is more heroic and the second more juridical, and modern republicans differ sharply on which emphasis to carry forward.

We will carry both, because both are true. What follows is the lineage.

***

Aristotle is the beginning, and everything that follows is in some sense a footnote to him, often a footnote disagreeing with the text above the line. The *Politics* opens with the claim for which Aristotle is most often quoted and most often misread — that man is by nature a political animal, *zoon politikon*. The phrase is not a sociological observation that humans like to hang around in groups. It is a metaphysical claim about what a human being is. The *polis*, for Aristotle, is the natural completion of human nature, in the sense that an acorn's completion is an oak. Outside the polis, he says in a famous passage, a man is either a beast or a god — something below the properly human or something above it, but not the thing itself. The human being comes into what it is by participating with other human beings in the shared project of governing a common life. That is the aperture through which the characteristic human capacities — reason, speech, judgment, the capacity to give an account of the good and argue about it — actually open. Alone, we cannot do what we are for.

This is a large claim, and a modern reader has to resist two opposite temptations. The first is to dismiss it as quaint because it assumes a political community that no longer exists, rested on slavery and on the exclusion of women, and operated at a scale we cannot reproduce. The second is to embrace it uncritically as nostalgia, as though one could reconstitute the Athenian assembly with a Zoom account and a civics pamphlet. Aristotle deserves neither treatment. What we should take from him is the structural insight: human flourishing is not a private matter; it has a public face; and the public face is not an optional decoration on the private good but an ingredient of it. If you cannot act in public with equals, something in you does not get to grow. That claim is separable from fifth-century Greek institutions. It survives them.

Aristotle's second contribution is his account of virtue. A virtue, for him, is not a feeling and not a moral sentiment but a *disposition* — a stable, habituated way of responding to the world, acquired over time through repeated practice in an environment that rewards the right responses and punishes the wrong ones. You become just by doing just things in a community that knows the difference. You become courageous by doing courageous things among people who recognize courage. The virtues are not downloaded; they are formed, and formation is a social process, carried out in institutions — the household, the school, the army, the assembly. Take away the institutions of formation and you do not get an unformed but innocent human being. You get a human being formed by whatever default environment remains, which is unlikely to produce the dispositions a self-governing community requires. This is a claim whose force the twentieth century made painfully concrete and the twenty-first has done nothing to refute. The virtues have to be cultivated, and cultivation has conditions, and the conditions can be destroyed.

There is a third Aristotelian contribution, mentioned less often but as important as the other two. *Politics*, he says, is the master science — not in the sense that it gives orders to the other sciences, but in the sense that it sets the ends within which the others operate. Medicine is for health; rhetoric is for persuasion; economics is for household management; but what is health for, and what is persuasion for, and what is a well-managed household for? Only a political community can answer. The question of the good human life is a political question, because it is the question of what a community is trying to sustain. The idea that politics is just one domain among many, or an unfortunate distraction from the real business of private flourishing, would have seemed to Aristotle a category error. We mention this because the book we are writing is, in a sense, a book about the damage done when this hierarchy is inverted.

***

Cicero is the bridge. If Aristotle is the ancestor of every subsequent argument about virtue and the polis, Cicero is the ancestor of every subsequent Western argument about law, office, and the public thing. His enormous achievement, extended across the *De Re Publica*, the *De Legibus*, and the *De Officiis*, was to translate the Greek inheritance into a Roman vocabulary of law and institutions — and, in so doing, to put the republican tradition into a form that could survive the transition from the city-state to the large, legally organized polity. His formula, in one of the most consequential sentences in the history of political thought, defines the *res publica* as a partnership — *res populi*, the thing of the people — bound together not by common blood or common place but by common agreement about right and by shared interest. A republic, for Cicero, is not a nation in the ethnic sense and not a multitude in the statistical sense. It is a *populus* — a people constituted by its agreement to live under law and to pursue its good in common.

Several things follow from this definition that the tradition would never let go of. First, the republic is an artifact of *justice*. A regime that does not rest on a shared commitment to right is not merely a bad republic but not a republic at all; Cicero is willing to follow the logic to the point of denying, against the tyrant, that the tyrant's state is a state in the proper sense. Second, the republic is a *partnership* — *societas*, a term that carries legal and almost commercial weight. To be a citizen is to be a partner in a joint undertaking whose burdens are shared and whose benefits are enjoyed as of right rather than doled out as charity. Third, the republic is held together by *institutions* — law, office, custom, religion — more than by personal virtue alone. Cicero was a practicing politician, and he knew that no community could be sustained on private virtue unsupported by institutional scaffolding. That is why the Roman contribution to the republican tradition, over and above the Greek, is the contribution of *constitutionalism* in the deep sense: the idea that the public thing is held in place by a structure of offices and laws, checked against one another, rotating in the hands of citizens who serve for a time and then return to private life.

The American founders read Cicero as closely as they read any pagan writer. They absorbed the vocabulary and they absorbed the architecture. If Aristotle gives us the anthropology of the citizen — what a citizen is — Cicero gives us the institutional grammar: what a republic looks like from the inside, what offices it fills, what it demands of those who hold them, how it names the public thing it is trying to preserve.

***

Machiavelli is the figure most distorted by his reputation, and the recovery of the republican Machiavelli — the author of the *Discourses on Livy* rather than the author of *The Prince* — is one of the genuine scholarly achievements of the last half-century. What the *Discourses* give us is not the cynical advisor to tyrants of popular caricature but a passionate partisan of free cities, a close reader of the Roman republic, and the thinker who adds to the Ciceronian inheritance a set of claims that the tradition had not yet fully articulated and that would prove enormously consequential.

Three claims stand out. The first is that a free city requires armed citizens. Machiavelli was obsessed with this, partly because of his experience watching Italian city-states hire mercenaries and pay the price, and partly because of what he took from his reading of Rome. A people that will not defend itself cannot remain free, because it places its freedom in the hands of someone else, and that someone else will sooner or later discover that he has the power to take the freedom away. There is a modern reading of this point — the gun-rights reading — that is neither wrong nor sufficient. Machiavelli's deeper claim is about civic muscle in a more general sense: a republic of citizens who have delegated to specialists every demanding public function, from fighting to deliberating to governing, has already lost the thing it was trying to preserve. Freedom is not a commodity that one can buy from professionals.

The second claim is that republics survive by *institutions that channel conflict*, not by institutions that suppress it. This is Machiavelli at his most modern. The Roman republic, he insists, grew strong not in spite of its social conflicts — patrician against plebeian, Senate against tribunate — but because of them, because those conflicts were contained within legal and institutional forms that turned them into productive contests rather than civil wars. A polity that tries to eliminate faction eliminates the energy that makes self-government possible. A polity that lets faction run without institutional channels destroys itself. The republican art is the art of designing the channels — of building the assemblies and courts and offices in which disagreement becomes deliberation rather than violence. Madison will echo this exactly in *Federalist* 10 and 51, and anyone who has read the *Discourses* will recognize the family resemblance.

The third claim, the one that troubles most modern readers, is that republics require *public virtue* — *virtù*, in Machiavelli's untranslatable term — and that when a citizenry ceases to have it, the game is over. Virtue here is not the moralistic feeling the word now conjures. It is closer to civic muscle: the disposition to prefer the common good to private advantage when the two conflict, to stand up for one's institutions, to do one's share in the joint work of the city. The classical concept of *corruption*, which the tradition carries forward into the eighteenth century and beyond, refers not primarily to graft — though it includes graft — but to the loss of this disposition. A corrupt citizenry is not one whose politicians take bribes. It is one that has stopped being willing to do its part. Once that has happened, no constitutional mechanism can save the city, because constitutional mechanisms are operated by citizens, and corrupt citizens will operate even good mechanisms badly.

One does not have to sign every Machiavellian claim to see that this vocabulary — armed citizens, institutional channels for conflict, the disease of corruption as the loss of public-spiritedness — has been the operating vocabulary of every subsequent republican argument up to our own moment.

***

James Harrington, writing in 1656 in the wake of the English civil wars, matters less for the particular institutions he proposed than for the structural argument he made. His *Commonwealth of Oceana* is the first modern work to argue, with full philosophical seriousness, that the distribution of *property* is the foundation of the distribution of *power*, and that no constitution can be stable that is not fitted to the property structure of the society under it. A republic of free citizens, Harrington argued, requires a broad distribution of property — above all, of land — because citizens who do not control the means of their own subsistence are structurally dependent on those who do, and dependent men cannot be free in the republican sense. Give one man enough land to buy the bread of all the others, and you have already given him the power to command them, regardless of what the written constitution says.

This is a proto-modern argument, and its influence on the American founders was enormous. Thomas Jefferson's obsession with the yeoman farmer and the homestead, John Adams's writings on the necessity of a middling sort of landowner, the whole Jeffersonian suspicion of great concentrations of wealth — these are not agricultural nostalgia. They are Harrington, domesticated for a new continent. The argument also gives the tradition a handle for thinking about economic unfreedom in a way that liberal rights-talk, with its focus on formal non-interference, often struggles to grip. If the structural distribution of property determines whether citizens are dependent or independent, then the question of who owns what is a republican question of the first rank, not a separable economic question that politics addresses only at the margins. We will come back to this in the context of Pettit, and again in the context of Sandel and Ahmari. The point for now is that the republican tradition, from Harrington forward, has never had the luxury of treating economic organization as external to the question of political freedom.

***

Rousseau is the figure we have to handle honestly, because he is the figure the tradition's critics most often hold up as the proof that the whole enterprise slides into tyranny. The charge is that Rousseau's concept of the *general will* — the will of the people as a moral body, distinct from the aggregated wills of the individuals who compose it — opens the door to the claim that people must be "forced to be free," that a political authority speaking in the name of the general will can override any particular will that disagrees with it, and that this is the road to the terror. Eric Hoffer and a long tradition of cold-war liberals read Rousseau this way. The reading is not baseless.

But Rousseau deserves better than the caricature. What he was trying to articulate, in the *Social Contract* and more fully in the later political writings, is a problem that no republican thinker can evade: how can a people be genuinely self-governing without either collapsing into a sum of private interests (in which case the "public" is a fiction) or empowering some part of itself to speak for the whole (in which case the self-government is fraudulent)? The general will is his answer, and whatever one thinks of the answer, the question is real. A republic whose politics is only the arithmetic of private preferences is not a republic at all; a republic in which some faction claims to speak for the public against the public is a tyranny in republican drag. Rousseau is trying to locate, between these two failures, the conceptual space in which a people can genuinely will something in common that is not reducible to what its individual members happened to want when the survey was taken.

We think this problem is real, we think Rousseau's solution contains dangers the tradition must guard against, and we think the best response is to keep the problem alive while rejecting the formulation that "the people" can be represented so completely by any institution that dissent from that institution becomes dissent from the people itself. The liberal revolution, which we turn to next chapter, will insist on protecting the dissenter against exactly that kind of claim, and it will be right to do so. Rousseau at his best — in the concern with civic religion, in the insistence that citizens must be formed to their freedom, in the refusal to settle for a politics that is only the management of private appetites — belongs in the republican line. Rousseau at his worst belongs in the warning signs the line has to read.

***

Tocqueville is the figure through whom the tradition meets America directly, and if there is one text in the republican lineage that every reader of this book should spend a month with, it is the *Democracy in America* — the two volumes, slowly, in a good translation, with the attention one would give to a long poem. What Tocqueville saw in the young United States in 1831 was a republican society operating at a scale that no classical republican had thought possible, and operating by means that no classical republican had theorized. He saw it with astonishment and with a certain measured alarm, and his book is still the fullest account we have of what a working civic culture looks like from the inside.

The claim most often quoted from Tocqueville — that the Americans of his time were constantly forming *associations* for every imaginable public purpose, from building roads to founding schools to promoting temperance — is the claim most central to his republican argument. Voluntary associations, he wrote, are the "schools of self-government." They are the institutions in which the habits of democratic citizenship are actually formed, because they are the institutions in which ordinary people acquire the skills of meeting, proposing, disagreeing, deciding, and acting together. A democracy that consists only of a relationship between isolated individuals and a centralized state has, Tocqueville thought, no mechanism for producing the citizens it requires. The associations are the mechanism. Without them, even good constitutional forms become empty. With them, even imperfect forms can become schools in which a people practices the arts a free people has to have.

Tocqueville also gives the tradition two warnings whose relevance to our moment is almost painful. The first is the *tyranny of the majority*, which he distinguishes carefully from the tyranny of a despot. A majority that has made up its mind, and that controls not only the ballot box but the schools and the newspapers and the pulpits and the manners of the country, can crush dissent more thoroughly than any king, because there is nowhere outside its reach to stand. The second warning is *soft despotism* — his name for the future he thought democracy was drifting toward. A soft despotism does not oppress its subjects; it infantilizes them, taking over one by one the functions of self-government in exchange for comfort and security, until the people it rules is a flock of well-tended animals that has forgotten what it would mean to govern itself. Tocqueville thought the remedies for both dangers were the same: robust associational life, local self-government, a healthy civil religion, and the cultivation in the citizenry of the kind of pride that refuses to be administered.

We quote Tocqueville so often in this book because we believe he was right about what he saw and because we believe the dynamic he feared is the one we now inhabit.

***

Hannah Arendt carries the tradition across the twentieth century's darkest passage, and she does it with a force and originality that makes every subsequent civic republican either explicitly her student or unconsciously her debtor. Two of her books matter most for this chapter. *The Human Condition*, published in 1958, is the philosophical anthropology of politics that the tradition had not previously received at this level of rigor. *On Revolution*, published in 1963, is her reading of the American founding as the closest a modern polity has come to recovering the republican possibility.

Arendt's distinctive contribution is her account of *action* — a term she uses with great precision. Action, for Arendt, is what human beings do when they speak and deed in the open space of a political community of equals, initiating something that was not there before, taking responsibility in public for what they do, and being seen by others who are free to agree or disagree. Action is categorically different from *labor*, the activity that sustains biological life, and from *work*, the activity that makes durable things. Labor and work have their own dignity, but they are not the specifically political activities, and a human life that contains only labor and work — even a very prosperous life so composed — is a life that has not yet entered the distinctively public dimension. To act is to appear, to take up a place in what Arendt calls the *space of appearance*, the common world in which human beings are visible to one another as agents rather than merely as bodies or as functionaries.

This sounds abstract until one notices that Arendt's analysis of totalitarianism, which was the intellectual project of her life, rests on it directly. What totalitarian movements destroy, she argued, is exactly the public space in which action is possible. They destroy it by destroying the plurality — the simultaneous presence of many different persons with their many different perspectives — that the public space requires. They replace plurality with ideology, which gives every citizen the same "correct" perspective, and they replace action with conformity, which performs agreement rather than initiating anything. The lonely, atomized individuals she describes in *The Origins of Totalitarianism* — the ones susceptible to the movement because they have no world of their own — are lonely in a quite specific sense: they have lost access to the space in which action is possible, because the associations and institutions that maintained it have been destroyed. The movement offers them a fraudulent public in exchange for the real one they have lost, and they take the offer because they have no other.

This is the deepest version of the claim that structures our whole book. The atomized individual and the mass movement are one phenomenon. The mass movement is what happens when the public space has been hollowed out and a counterfeit offered in its place. To restore the citizen is therefore to restore the public space — the genuine one, in which plurality is real and action is possible — and this is not a job for individual heroes but for institutions and associations, for the slow work of rebuilding the molds within which public life occurs.

***

Philip Pettit, writing at the end of the twentieth century, gives the tradition its sharpest modern formulation, and his central concept — *liberty as non-domination* — is the one we are going to carry forward most explicitly into the rest of this book. Pettit's 1997 *Republicanism* and his 2012 *On the People's Terms* are the canonical sources, and their argument deserves to be laid out carefully, because it corrects a mistake in the standard liberal account of freedom that has real consequences for how we think about the unfreedoms of our own moment.

Isaiah Berlin, in his famous 1958 essay "Two Concepts of Liberty," divided the family of freedoms into two: *negative* liberty, the absence of interference by others, and *positive* liberty, the presence of the conditions that enable one to govern oneself. The distinction has dominated Anglophone political theory for two generations, and Berlin used it, among other things, to warn against the positive-liberty tradition's tendency to slide into the tyranny that claims to be forcing people to be free. Pettit's move is to insist that there is a third concept Berlin missed, and that it is the one the republican tradition had always been about. Freedom, on the republican account, is neither merely the absence of interference nor the possession of the capacities for self-rule. It is the absence of *domination* — the condition of not living at the mercy of another agent's arbitrary power.

The difference matters, and Pettit illustrates it with an image the tradition had used for centuries. Consider a slave with a benevolent master. The master chooses never to interfere with what the slave does. He is kind, inattentive, or on holiday. By the negative-liberty standard, the slave is free: there is no interference. By the republican standard, the slave is emphatically unfree, and obviously so, because his non-interference is a gift the master can withdraw at any time. He lives in the shadow of another person's will. He must shape himself, cultivate deference, avoid offense, read the master's moods — all the behaviors of the unfree — precisely because the master's forbearance is contingent and could end. A freedom that depends on the good graces of a power one cannot check is not freedom but permission.

This concept changes what one can see when one looks at the modern world. A debtor who owes more than he can pay is not free in the republican sense, even if his creditor chooses to be lenient, because his creditor can at any moment choose otherwise. A worker whose employer can fire him at will for any reason or none, in a labor market where the next employer has the same power, is not free in the republican sense, even if his current boss is decent. A user of a digital platform whose terms can be changed unilaterally, whose account can be suspended without appeal, and whose livelihood depends on the platform's continuing willingness to host him is not free in the republican sense. Each of these figures lives at the discretion of a power he cannot check, and the tradition classifies each as dominated. Non-domination names a real unfreedom that the negative-liberty tradition often struggles to see.

The policy implication is not that these forms of economic life should be abolished but that the republican standard adds a dimension to our assessment of them. A free society is one in which citizens stand, as much as politics can manage, on ground that is not owed them at someone else's pleasure — one in which power, private as well as public, is checked by institutions that make arbitrary interference impossible or costly. This is not a new demand. It is the oldest republican demand, now articulated for a world in which domination comes as often from an algorithm or a balance sheet as from a prince.

***

Michael Sandel's *Democracy's Discontent*, published in 1996 and expanded in 2022, is the book that put the civic-republican diagnosis in the hands of a wide American audience, and it is the contemporary book closest in spirit to the one we are writing. Sandel's central historical claim is that the American political tradition, through most of its history, was not merely liberal in the modern rights-procedural sense but civic-republican in a substantive sense — concerned with the formation of citizens, with the dispersal of economic power, with the cultivation of self-governing habits in ordinary life — and that American public philosophy, over the course of the twentieth century, quietly abandoned the civic-republican side of its inheritance in favor of what he calls the *procedural republic*. The procedural republic understands its task as the neutral umpire of citizens' freely chosen ends; it brackets questions about the good life, about virtue, about what kind of people a polity needs; it confines itself to rights and procedures, and it hopes that everything else will take care of itself.

Sandel's argument is that everything else did not take care of itself. A polity that brackets every substantive question about the good discovers that its citizens are left without a shared language for the things that matter most to them, and that into the vacuum rush ideologies — market fundamentalism, identity tribalism, technocratic expertise — that are not in any serious sense neutral, but that present themselves as such because the political language of the good has been abandoned. Sandel's later book *The Tyranny of Merit* extends the argument into the meritocratic regime that has come to dominate elite politics, and it makes the case that this regime's inability to speak honestly about dignity, contribution, and the common good is a direct cause of the populism now rising against it. We think Sandel is substantially right, and Volume II of this book will develop several strands of his analysis at length. For now, what we want to register is that his diagnosis is a civic-republican diagnosis — it rests on the tradition we are sketching in this chapter — and that the recovery he recommends is a recovery of exactly the inheritance we are trying to make visible.

Alongside Sandel, two contemporary theorists have done the synthesizing work that makes the tradition usable in present-day terms. Iseult Honohan's *Civic Republicanism*, published in 2002, is the clearest one-volume statement of republicanism as a live political philosophy, distinguished from both liberalism and communitarianism. Richard Dagger's *Civic Virtues*, published in 1997, gave us the phrase *republican liberalism* which we have borrowed, with some modification, to describe our own position. Dagger's project was to show that the best version of the civic-republican tradition and the best version of the liberal tradition can be reconciled — that one does not have to choose between rights against the state and participation in the state, that the autonomous individual and the engaged citizen can be, and must be, the same person. That is our thesis too, and we acknowledge the debt openly.

***

If one reads this lineage carefully, a cluster of commitments emerges that constitutes the tradition's core, distinct from any particular thinker's particular emphasis. Six of them are worth naming.

The first is *liberty as non-domination*, in the Pettit formulation: freedom is the condition of not living at the mercy of an arbitrary power, and a free society is one whose institutions secure this condition for all its citizens. This is the tradition's master concept of freedom, distinct from the negative-liberty concept of the absence of interference and from the positive-liberty concept of self-realization, and it is the concept that gives republicanism its analytical edge on questions of economic and social power that other traditions often struggle to grip.

The second is the *rule of law*, in the full Ciceronian sense: that a republic is constituted by its laws rather than by the will of any person or faction, that the laws bind officials as well as subjects, and that the path to every legitimate political end runs through lawful institutions rather than around them. The rule of law is the institutional form of non-domination; a society in which law itself is the discretionary instrument of whoever holds power is a society in which no one is secure against arbitrary interference.

The third is *anti-corruption*, in the classical sense. Corruption here is not graft, though it includes graft. It is the loss of public-spiritedness — the disposition in a citizenry to prefer private advantage to the common good, to let the public thing deteriorate because tending to it is someone else's problem, to treat political office as a private asset rather than a shared trust. A corrupt citizenry cannot be saved by good institutions, because institutions are operated by citizens. This is the tradition's explanation for why it cares so much about virtue: without it, the rest collapses.

The fourth is *mixed government* — the idea, running from Polybius through the American founders, that stable free government requires the institutional balancing of different principles, interests, and orders against one another, so that no single faction can capture the whole. The principle is older than any particular constitutional form, and it underwrites everything from the Roman Senate's relation to the tribunes to the American separation of powers.

The fifth is *civic virtue*, understood as a disposition rather than a moralistic feeling. The virtues the tradition prizes — courage, justice, prudence, public-spiritedness, the willingness to serve and the willingness to listen — are habits formed in institutions and practiced in action. They are not private moral achievements. They are the operating equipment of the citizen, and they are cultivated in the associations, offices, and daily practices of a working republic.

The sixth is *popular sovereignty*, understood not as the raw rule of a numerical majority but as the commitment that the political community is the people's own, constituted by its members, answerable to them, and legitimate only insofar as it tracks their genuine common interest. The tradition has always struggled with how to institutionalize this commitment without sliding either into the tyranny of the majority or into the fiction that some elite speaks for the people against the people. The struggle is perpetual and the commitment does not lapse with it.

These six commitments, taken together, are what it means to speak the word *citizen* in the republican key.

***

It is time to make, before we criticize the tradition, the strongest case we know how to make for it. Here is that case.

The liberal account of freedom, on its own, cannot explain why the benevolent master's slave is unfree. The liberal, holding the negative-liberty concept, has to say either that the slave *is* free, because there is no interference, which is absurd on its face, or that the slave is free in the negative sense but lacks something else we also care about, which is a retreat rather than an answer. The republican does not have to dodge. The slave is unfree because he lives at another's discretion, and discretion is itself a form of unfreedom. This is not a marginal case. It is the structural form of unfreedom, and it recurs wherever one agent's well-being depends on another agent's arbitrary choice. The republican diagnosis, once seen, is hard to unsee, and it illuminates a vast terrain of modern life — the precarious employee, the indebted household, the tenant in an unregulated market, the small-town resident dependent on a single employer, the citizen of a company town, the platform creator whose audience is borrowed — that the liberal framework tends to represent as a collection of private choices freely made. Sometimes they are that. Sometimes they are dominations dressed as contracts.

The tradition also names something the other traditions often miss: that freedom has a public face. A human being who is formally unmolested but cannot appear in public as an agent, cannot take part in the deliberations that shape the world she lives in, cannot see herself reflected in the institutions that govern her, is not simply a private person enjoying her private liberties. She is a diminished version of a human being, and her diminishment is political. The tradition does not accept the modern bargain in which public life is outsourced to specialists and the citizen is reduced to a consumer of political services, because it does not accept that such a reduction leaves the citizen intact. Something essential is lost in the outsourcing, and the tradition insists on saying so.

Finally, the tradition is right that the individual and the community are not the opposed terms of a zero-sum contest. The citizen, as the republican tradition understood the word, is an individual *and* a member, and the membership is constitutive of the individuality rather than in competition with it. The person who belongs to a functioning public world is a larger person than the person who does not. This is the core republican claim, and we think it is true. It is the claim that makes the word *citizen* mean something that neither the word *individual* nor the word *subject* can mean on its own.

***

Having made the case, we owe the reader the dangers. A full critique of civic republicanism belongs to the chapters on the liberal revolution and on the post-liberal turn, where the tradition meets its most serious modern challenges. But three hazards are worth flagging here, because they are internal to the tradition and a reader ought to see them now.

The first is the slide into *authoritarian communitarianism*. A tradition that insists on the primacy of the public good, on the formation of citizens by institutions, and on the dispositional requirements of a self-governing people is always one short step from the claim that the polity should use its power to form its citizens in the right way, by force if necessary. Some republican voices have taken that step, and the revolutionary career of the Rousseauian tradition is the cautionary tale. The danger is real, and the liberal insistence on rights against the polity — which we take up in the next chapter — is the necessary corrective. A republicanism that forgets this corrective is not a republicanism worth reviving.

The second is the *exclusion of the non-citizen*. Historical republicanism has almost always been the republicanism of *some* among the inhabitants of a territory, and the "some" has usually been defined by criteria we now rightly reject — sex, property, religion, race. The Athenian citizen was a male property-holder in a society sustained by slaves. The Roman citizen was a category defined against a much larger category of non-citizens and foreigners within the empire. Even the American republican tradition, which expanded the category more broadly than its predecessors, did so slowly and against fierce resistance, and the full extension of citizenship to all adult inhabitants is barely a human lifetime old. The tradition's critics are correct that the old citizenship was built on exclusions, and a revival of the tradition has to be a revival *without* those exclusions — which means the revival is not, strictly, a revival but a completion.

The third is *nostalgia*, the deepest hazard of a tradition this old. A writer who loves the polis can easily find himself in the posture of mourning a world that was built on inequalities he does not want back and operated at scales that cannot be reproduced. Nostalgia is a comfortable posture, because it relieves the nostalgic of the harder task of translating the tradition's insights into a form usable in present conditions. We have tried, in this chapter, to resist the nostalgia while preserving the insights. Whether we have succeeded the reader will judge; our conviction is that the insights are portable, and that a republican tradition with its exclusions stripped, its scales rethought, and its vocabulary retranslated is still the tradition most capable of naming what has been lost.

***

The last task of this chapter is to point ahead. The civic-republican inheritance, taken alone, is not sufficient for the modern citizen, and we do not want to be read as saying it is. The tradition we have just walked through has a blind spot, and the blind spot is serious.

The blind spot is that classical republicanism never developed a worked-out theory of *individual rights against the political community*. It could describe, with great subtlety, the conditions under which a citizen participates freely in the common life of the polis. It could specify the virtues a citizen must have and the institutions that form them. It could articulate non-domination as the structure of free life among equals. What it could not quite do — not in Aristotle, not in Cicero, not even, for different reasons, in Rousseau or in the earlier American republicans — was say clearly what the polity *may not do to its members*, no matter how general the will that commanded it, no matter how virtuous the citizenry that endorsed it, no matter how pressing the common good that seemed to require it. The tradition treated the well-formed citizen as someone whose good would largely align with the good of the polity, and it did not build, into its vocabulary, a strong fence around the individual that the polity could not cross.

The liberal revolution, which we take up in the next chapter, is the story of that fence being built. Its great names — Locke, Kant, Mill, Berlin, Rawls — are the names of a philosophical tradition whose central achievement was the articulation of rights that are held *against* the state, of zones of conscience and choice that no political community may legitimately invade, of a conception of the person as an end in himself who cannot be sacrificed to the good of the whole, no matter how good the whole. This is moral progress, and we are going to say so in just those terms. A recovered civic republicanism without the liberal fence is a republicanism that can, in principle, crush its dissidents in the name of the people; history is full of examples. We do not want that recovery.

But the liberal revolution came with its own blind spot, which is the opposite of the republican one. In putting the fence around the individual, it too often forgot that the individual, fenced in from the polity, is not yet a citizen — that a world in which rights are secure but no one has any particular reason to participate in the public life of the community is a world in which the citizen, in the republican sense, has already begun to disappear. The liberal tradition, at its best, knew this; at its worst, it forgot it, and its forgetting is one of the things that has produced our present trouble. The work of the rest of Volume I is to hold the two traditions together — to honor what each saw, to name what each missed, and to argue that a responsible politics in our century requires both.

For now, we have done what this chapter set out to do. We have walked through the republican inheritance, from Aristotle's polis to Pettit's non-domination, and we have tried to show why the word *citizen* carries a weight that no mere modern usage can dissolve. The word comes with a history. The history comes with commitments. A person who wants to claim the word honestly — and the argument of this book is that we must — has to know the weight she is picking up. Now we turn to the liberal revolution, which both challenged the inheritance and completed it, and which a serious contemporary civic republicanism cannot afford to refuse.
