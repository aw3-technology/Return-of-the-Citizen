# Chapter 6
## The American Synthesis

An American book on citizenship cannot be written without American sources. We say this not because America is the pattern for the world. It is not. We say it because the American experiment, whatever else one thinks of it, had to confront the exact tension this book is about — individual rights against civic virtue, the person against the polity, commerce against community — under conditions that did not permit the luxury of remaining abstract. The European philosophers whose quarrel we have traced over the last four chapters had the leisure, for the most part, of argument. The Americans had to write a constitution that would work. They had to design institutions that would hold; they had to give reasons their fellow citizens would accept; they had to persuade a suspicious, armed, recently revolutionary population to agree on a frame of government that would bind their grandchildren. They fought the philosophical quarrel first, and in many ways more practically than the Europeans who followed them. We go to them now not because they settled the question but because they tested it.

We also go to them because, whatever readers of this book's cover may expect, the American founding is not a monolith. It is a debate. The Constitution that governs us was produced by one party to that debate; the Bill of Rights was the price paid to the other; and the country that emerged from ratification has been carrying both sides of the argument ever since. The genius of the American system, to the degree that it has any, lies in the fact that neither side won cleanly. The Federalist vision of a large commercial republic with a strong national government and a professional representative class coexists, uneasily and productively, with the Anti-Federalist vision of intimate local polities in which citizens know one another and deliberate face to face. When either side wins decisively, the system degrades. When the tension is maintained, something recognizable as self-government survives.

We will not treat the Founders as secular saints. They were political actors with real limits — on slavery, on the franchise, on who counted as a citizen in the first place — and this book is not going to pretend otherwise. The question we are asking of them is not "were they perfect?" It is "what did the argument they had give us?" The answer, we think, is a great deal.

***

Begin with Madison. *Federalist 10* is probably the single most studied essay in American political thought, and most of what has been written about it flatters the modern reader into missing its central claim. Madison's problem is the problem that had wrecked republics from antiquity to his own century: faction. A free people, he observes, will inevitably divide against itself. The causes of faction are sown in the nature of man — differences of opinion, differences of passion, differences of interest — and the most common and durable source is the unequal distribution of property. One cannot remove faction without removing liberty, and the cure of removing liberty is worse than the disease.

The usual remedy, in the republican tradition as Madison inherited it, was to make the polity small enough that factions could be reasoned into submission by a shared civic culture — the classical assumption, running from Aristotle through Montesquieu, that self-government required a compact, homogeneous citizenry with a common sense of the good. Madison proposes the opposite. Extend the sphere, he writes, and you take in a greater variety of parties and interests; you make it less probable that a majority of the whole will have a common motive to invade the rights of other citizens; and where such a common motive exists, you make it harder for those who feel it to discover their own strength and act in unison. The large republic, not the small one, will be the refuge of liberty.

This is counterintuitive and powerful, and it has been vindicated by more than two centuries of American experience. But note — and this is where our argument cuts against the conventional reading — Madison's cure depends on a very particular kind of citizenry. It depends on citizens whose disagreements run along multiple, crosscutting lines. The Virginia planter and the Massachusetts merchant disagree about tariffs; they agree about religious liberty; they disagree about slavery; they agree about trial by jury; they disagree about a national bank; they agree about militia service. Each citizen belongs to several overlapping, non-congruent factions, and the factions check one another precisely because no single axis of disagreement captures the whole of political identity. A citizen can be outvoted on tariffs by the very neighbor with whom he stands on religion.

When that condition holds, Madison's mechanism works. Factions multiply, collide, and cancel. When that condition fails — when every disagreement has collapsed onto a single axis, so that knowing a citizen's view on one question lets you predict his view on all the others — the mechanism does the opposite of what Madison designed. Instead of a plurality of factions checking one another, two great super-factions face off across every question at once, and each experiences the other not as a rival to be outvoted but as an existential threat to be defeated. The large republic, which Madison expected to dilute faction, now concentrates it. The multiplication of factions that was supposed to be a solvent has been replaced by the fusion of factions into a binary.

This is what the polarization literature we will examine in Chapter 12 calls affective polarization. We flag it here because it is the precise failure mode of the Madisonian design. When contemporary Americans ask why the Constitution no longer seems to prevent the tyranny of majorities it was designed to prevent, the answer is not that the Constitution has been amended into impotence. The answer is that the sociological substrate on which it depended — cross-cutting identities, pluralities of loyalty, disagreements that did not all correlate — has been steadily replaced by something closer to tribal politics, and tribal politics is the condition under which *Federalist 10* was never designed to work. Madison's argument was never that structure alone could save a free people. His argument was that the right structure, paired with the right kind of people, could do the work. We have the structure. The people are the question.

*Federalist 51* is the essay read alongside 10, and it makes a complementary point about institutional design. If men were angels, Madison writes, no government would be necessary; if angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary. In framing a government which is to be administered by men over men, the great difficulty is this: you must first enable the government to control the governed, and in the next place oblige it to control itself. Ambition must be made to counteract ambition. The interest of the man must be connected with the constitutional rights of the place. By so contriving the interior structure of the government that its several constituent parts may, by their mutual relations, be the means of keeping each other in their proper places, you can harness the very passions you cannot abolish.

This is the design logic of the separation of powers — legislative against executive, federal against state, Senate against House, House against Supreme Court. It is not a matter of trusting the officials. It is a matter of arranging things so that one ambitious man's grasp for power is checked by another ambitious man's grasp for his. You do not need virtuous rulers. You need a machine whose parts cancel one another.

We pause here to register what the argument concedes. Madison is betting, in *Federalist 51*, that structure can substitute for virtue. He is betting that you can run a republic on ambition if you arrange the ambition cleverly enough. This is one of the great Federalist bets, and it is closely related to the Federalists' relative comfort with commerce — their willingness to accept, and even to welcome, a society in which most citizens most of the time are pursuing their own interests in a large market rather than practicing civic virtue in a small assembly. The representation solves the participation problem. The citizen does not have to be in the room; he has to be able to vote for someone who will be in the room on his behalf. The large commercial republic can work because the citizen is a voter and a consumer and a private person, not a full-time participant in public affairs.

This is an enormous concession to modernity, and it is why the Federalists have been the natural party of the American system ever since — of capitalism, of the national government, of the professional political class. The bet they made has, on balance, paid off. The republic has survived. The commercial order they defended became the wealthiest the world has ever seen. Representation has substituted, most of the time, for participation.

But it has not substituted cleanly, and the question of what was lost in the substitution is the question the Anti-Federalists asked.

***

They lost. The Constitution was ratified. Their names — Brutus, the Federal Farmer, Patrick Henry, George Mason, Melancton Smith — are familiar today only to specialists. The Federalist has been reprinted in every edition a publisher could devise; most Americans have never read a word of the Anti-Federalist writings. History, as the saying goes, is written by the winners, and the Anti-Federalists had the double misfortune of losing the ratification fight and of writing prose that lacked the polish of Madison and Hamilton. They are often treated, when they are treated at all, as the losers of a debate that history has retrospectively settled.

We want to argue, carefully, that they were right about a great deal of what would be lost. The republic they warned against is, in important respects, the republic we now inhabit. Their arguments have not been refuted. They have been eclipsed.

Consider Brutus, the pseudonymous New York essayist whose papers ran in the New York Journal through the winter of 1787–88 and who is the sharpest of the Anti-Federalist writers. Brutus looks at the proposed Constitution and asks a simple question: can a republic of this size, this commercial complexity, this distance between rulers and ruled, actually sustain the civic life on which free government depends? He concludes it cannot. The representatives in a distant capital will not know the people they represent; the people will not know them; what will substitute for knowledge will be faction, party, and the manipulations of a professional political class. The federal judiciary, armed with the power to declare the meaning of a written constitution, will grow over time into an unaccountable oracle whose rulings bind generations that had no hand in its appointment. A standing army, however modest at the outset, will become the instrument of executive power against the states and the people. A national bank and a national debt will tie the commercial class to the central government and give the central government a permanent interest in expansion. The states will be absorbed; the localities will decline; and the civic virtue that once animated the small republics of New England and the yeoman counties of Virginia will be dissolved in the solvent of commercial mobility and national administration.

Every one of these predictions has come true. This is not a partisan observation. It is a descriptive one. The federal judiciary does, in fact, exercise enormous power over questions the Constitution does not obviously settle, and its rulings do, in fact, bind generations that had no hand in the appointment of the justices. The standing army is real and is the instrument of a global military commitment the Founders would have found unrecognizable. The national government has absorbed, through the spending power and the commerce clause, authorities the original Constitution reserved to the states. The professional political class exists, is well-paid, and is in many respects unaccountable to the citizens of any particular place. The states, once meaningful polities with distinct legal and civic cultures, have become administrative subdivisions of a single national order, and the localities below them have in many cases ceased to be arenas of serious self-government at all. Brutus would recognize the country we live in. Madison, if we are honest, would not.

The Federal Farmer, whose *Letters* ran through the same winter and whose authorship remains disputed, pressed a different but related point. His worry was about representation itself. A legislature of a few hundred men, drawn from an eventual population of tens of millions, cannot represent anything meaningful in the older republican sense of the term. The representative ceases to be a fellow citizen who carries his neighbors' concerns to the assembly and becomes a remote professional whose life is more like that of the national political class than like that of the people who elected him. His interests converge with those of his colleagues and of the administrative state he helps staff, and diverge from those of the constituents he nominally serves. The Federal Farmer anticipated, in 1787, the dynamic that contemporary political scientists call the "cartel" structure of two-party systems: the elected officials of both parties have more in common with each other than any of them has with the voters they putatively represent, and the contests between them mask a deeper agreement on the terms of elite reproduction.

Patrick Henry, whose speeches at the Virginia ratifying convention are the most electric American political rhetoric before Lincoln, put the civic-republican case in its barest form. "The genius of democracy," he told his countrymen, "lies in a frequent appeal to the body of the people." A free people must know itself; a free people must assemble; a free people must retain the habit of ruling, or the habit will be lost, and with it the thing itself. Henry saw in the proposed Constitution the construction of an order in which the body of the people would cease to appear as such, and would be replaced by a remote aggregation of voters periodically consulted on choices pre-selected by others. He was, on this point, correct. The plebiscitary democracy of the modern American republic — in which the citizen is, for most practical purposes, a voter rather than a participant — is the democracy Henry feared, and it is also the democracy Madison was, within the limits of what he would say plainly, willing to accept.

The Anti-Federalists were the first great American civic republicans. Their intellectual legacy is alive in every serious argument about federalism, about subsidiarity, about the decline of local self-government, about the right scale for a citizen to have real purchase on the decisions that shape her life. Their argument was not, as the Federalist victors sometimes implied, a sentimental preference for small places over large ones. It was a philosophical claim about the conditions of freedom: that freedom as non-domination, to use Pettit's later phrase, is only possible when the rulers and the ruled are close enough to one another that accountability is real, when the polity is small enough that the citizen can actually know what he is ruling and being ruled by, when the habits of participation are practiced often enough to be preserved as habits.

We do not pretend, with some of our contemporaries, that the Anti-Federalists would have built a better republic. They would not have. The government they favored — or rather, the multiplicity of small republics loosely confederated in something like the Articles of Confederation — would in all likelihood not have survived the early nineteenth century. It would have fractured under the pressure of regional interests, been picked apart by European powers, and left the continent to some other political form entirely. The republic as we know it is a Federalist republic; that was the price of the republic surviving at all. What we are saying is more specific. The Anti-Federalists lost the fight over how to construct the republic, and they were right to lose it. But they were often clearer than Madison about what the republic they could not prevent would become. They did not stop the commercial, continental, administrative state. But they described it, in advance, with an accuracy Madison could not match, because Madison was invested in not seeing it, and they had no comparable reason to look away.

This is the first and deepest lesson of the American synthesis. The Federalists were right about what had to be done to make the republic possible. The Anti-Federalists were right about what the republic would cost. The country we inherit has been, for two and a half centuries, the ongoing negotiation between the possibility and the cost.

***

Forty years after ratification, a young French aristocrat arrived on American shores and began taking the notes that would become the single most penetrating book ever written about the United States. Alexis de Tocqueville traveled through the country for nine months in 1831 and 1832, ostensibly to study the American prison system. What he produced, in the two volumes of *Democracy in America* published in 1835 and 1840, is the essential nineteenth-century text on the civic life of the American republic and, after Hannah Arendt, the single most important interlocutor in the book you are reading.

Tocqueville's great question was not whether democracy would spread — he considered that already settled — but whether the democratic form of life could be reconciled with human excellence and human freedom. He came to the United States because it was the oldest and most successful democracy he could study, and because he wanted to understand what had made it work. Much of what he found he admired. Some of what he found he feared. The most original thing in the book is that the admiration and the fear are not about different things. They are about the same thing, seen from different angles.

Start with what he admired. "Americans of all ages, all stations of life, and all types of disposition," he wrote, "are forever forming associations." Religious associations, moral associations, serious associations, futile associations, general associations, restricted associations, enormous associations, minuscule associations — the Americans formed them by the thousand for every conceivable purpose. They built hospitals, prisons, schools, libraries, churches, lyceums, literary societies, temperance leagues, antislavery conventions, improvement societies, mutual-aid societies. Where a Frenchman in the same situation would call on the government, where an Englishman would call on a great lord, the American called a meeting of his neighbors.

This capacity, Tocqueville thought, was the central fact of American freedom. The associations were not ornamental. They were what made democracy possible. They were, in his famous phrase, "schools of self-government": the places where ordinary people, without any training, learned the skills a self-governing polity requires — how to propose a motion, how to amend it, how to lose a vote and accept the loss, how to persuade a neighbor who disagreed, how to submit one's private preferences to the procedures of a common decision, how to speak in public and how to listen. These are not natural skills. They are learned skills. They are learned, if they are learned at all, in the small arenas where the stakes are low enough that mistakes can be corrected and the participants care enough that they show up.

The township was, for Tocqueville, the foundational arena. His long chapters on the New England town meeting argue that the national freedom of the United States is parasitic on local self-government, because only at the local level do citizens actually practice ruling and being ruled. The citizen who has debated the location of the schoolhouse with his neighbors, who has served his term as constable or selectman, who has voted on the assessment of his own taxes, knows in his body what self-government is. He carries that knowledge up to the state and the nation, where it ripens into a capacity for informed national citizenship. The citizen who has never practiced self-government at any scale he can see cannot do it at scales he cannot. You cannot produce a free national politics by exhortation; you can only produce it by growing free citizens, and free citizens are grown locally or not at all.

This is the Anti-Federalist argument, vindicated in empirical form by a visiting Frenchman fifty years after ratification. Tocqueville does not cite Brutus. He did not need to. He describes what he sees, and what he sees is that the national republic is working because, and only because, beneath it there persists a layer of small, intimate polities in which citizens know each other and transact the ordinary business of common life face to face. Strip away that layer, Tocqueville implies — and he is explicit that democratic societies tend, over time, to strip it away — and the national republic loses its foundation. It will continue to operate, formally, as a democracy. It will cease to contain citizens.

Now the fears. Tocqueville identified two distinctively democratic pathologies that have haunted the tradition ever since. The first is the tyranny of the majority. The European versions of tyranny, he observed, operate on the body: a king tortures a heretic; a tyrant confiscates an opponent's property; a regime censors a dissenting book. American tyranny, he thought, would operate on the mind. In a society of formally equal individuals, where the only source of legitimate authority is the aggregate of opinion, dissenting opinion becomes something worse than illegal. It becomes shameful. You are not imprisoned for holding an unpopular view. You are ignored; you are shunned; you are denied the small social exchanges that make a life livable; you are left in a formal freedom that is actually a social silence. "The master no longer says: You shall think as I do or you shall die. He says: You are free not to think as I do; your life, your property, everything remains to you; but from this day on you are a stranger among us." This is not a theoretical prediction. It is a description of a mechanism we now see at work on every social platform in the country, on every university campus, in every professional circle whose members must protect their standing. The tyranny of the majority in its subtler American form is not a mob at the door. It is a silence at the dinner table.

The second pathology is the one Tocqueville calls soft despotism, and the passage in which he describes it — Volume II, Book IV, Chapter VI of *Democracy in America* — is in our opinion the single most important paragraph written about modern democratic life, and every contemporary reader of this book should read it once a year, the way one reads a physical examination. Tocqueville is imagining the future of democratic societies. He is trying to describe the kind of despotism to which such societies are vulnerable. It is, he says, unlike any despotism that has ever existed. It will not be violent. It will not be cruel. It will not even be a despotism its victims will recognize as such.

"I see an innumerable multitude of men, alike and equal, constantly circling around in pursuit of the petty and banal pleasures with which they glut their souls. Each of them, withdrawn into himself, is almost unaware of the fate of the rest. Mankind, for him, consists of his children and his personal friends. As for the rest of his fellow citizens, they are near enough, but he does not notice them. He touches them but does not feel them; he exists only in himself and for himself alone. And if, on these terms, there remains in his mind a sense of family, there no longer remains a sense of society."

Above this multitude, Tocqueville continues, rises an immense tutelary power. It is mild. It is provident. It is watchful. It does not tyrannize. It does not break wills; it softens them. It does not destroy; it prevents existence. It reduces each nation to nothing more than a herd of timid and industrious animals, of whom the government is the shepherd.

This is 1840. If that passage does not describe something, nothing does. It is the most accurate forecast of the condition of a mature liberal democracy ever written, and the fact that it was written before the invention of the telephone, before the welfare state, before television, before the internet, before the smartphone, is one of the intellectual miracles of the nineteenth century. What Tocqueville saw was that the combination of democratic equality and commercial prosperity and administrative centralization, without any strong countervailing layer of associational life, would produce a population that was formally free, materially comfortable, and incapable of governing itself. He was right. We are his forecast.

The reason Tocqueville matters so much to this book is that he is the writer who sees both halves of the problem we are trying to describe. He sees the value of the individual — he is no enemy of modern liberty, and his defense of religious pluralism and private property is unambiguous. He sees the necessity of the polity — he understands that free individuals are produced only by the associations and townships and deliberative habits that turn formal freedom into practiced freedom. And he sees, with a clarity no subsequent writer has matched, that the collapse of the second produces the degradation of the first. The soft despotism passage is not a prediction about the state. It is a prediction about what happens to souls when the state becomes the only reliable public actor in their lives.

Every argument this book makes, at some level, is an elaboration of a passage Tocqueville wrote in 1840.

***

If Tocqueville is the visiting diagnostician, Lincoln is the American voice the argument relies on most for its specifically civic-republican register. We do not invoke him as a rhetorical convenience. We invoke him because the problem he faced — the problem of how a free people can preserve the institutions of their freedom against the passions that would dissolve them — is our problem, and because the answers he worked out, in a political vocabulary that was recognizably American and recognizably classical at once, are answers we have not improved upon.

The Lyceum Address, delivered in January of 1838 to the Young Men's Lyceum of Springfield, Illinois, is the text. Lincoln was twenty-eight. He was speaking to a local audience of small-town Illinoisans. The topic was announced as "The Perpetuation of Our Political Institutions." What he produced is one of the strangest and most penetrating speeches in the American canon.

Lincoln begins by observing that the danger to American institutions will not come from abroad. No foreign power could cross the Atlantic and the Appalachians and conquer a free people in arms. "If destruction be our lot, we must ourselves be its author and finisher. As a nation of freemen, we must live through all time, or die by suicide." The danger, he says, is internal, and it is specifically the danger of mob violence and the disregard for law that mob violence breeds. He cites recent lynchings, recent riots, recent summary killings by crowds who had decided that justice was too slow and the law too weak. In each case, Lincoln says, the crime against the particular victim was bad enough. But the deeper crime was against the law itself — against the habit of submitting grievances to courts, of arguing disputes through institutions, of accepting procedural outcomes one did not personally favor because the procedure was the thing being defended.

Lincoln's remedy is a phrase he uses with no apology and that has scandalized some of his subsequent interpreters. He calls for what he names a "political religion" of reverence for the laws. Let every American, he says, let every lover of liberty, let every mother pledge her child, let every schoolmaster teach his pupils, let every pulpit and bar and legislature speak of reverence for the Constitution and the laws. Let the bad laws, he adds, be repealed as soon as possible — but while they continue in force, let them be religiously observed, because the habit of lawful observance is the habit that preserves the institutions themselves.

The word "religion," for Lincoln, is doing precise work. He does not mean piety in the sectarian sense. He means what Cicero meant by *religio* and what Tocqueville meant when he wrote that "despotism may govern without faith, but liberty cannot." A free polity requires a shared reverence — a set of pre-rational commitments — that citizens carry into public life as a kind of moral substrate. Without that substrate, every institution becomes renegotiable in the moment of any particular citizen's dissatisfaction with its outcome, and institutions that are renegotiable in the moment of dissatisfaction are not institutions at all. They are provisional conveniences, and they will be burned when the inconvenience passes a threshold.

The political religion of the Lyceum Address is the civic-republican claim that a free people has to love the institutions that make their freedom possible, not merely use them. It is the claim against which every subsequent American argument about reform and preservation has had to measure itself. We quote Lincoln on this point because the twenty-first-century American, on both the left and the right, has largely abandoned it. Contemporary political discourse treats institutions instrumentally — as means to partisan ends, to be preserved when one's own side controls them and torn apart when the other side does. This is the posture Lincoln identified as suicidal, in 1838, before any of the specific institutional stresses of our own time had appeared. He was right then; he is right now; and a country that cannot recover what he meant by political religion is a country that will not last as a republic, whatever it continues to call itself.

The Gettysburg Address, twenty-five years later, is the other text, and its 272 words are, in our view, the most compact civic-republican statement in the American tradition. Lincoln is dedicating a battlefield cemetery; he is speaking over the bodies of men who have just died in the largest battle ever fought on American soil; and he is telling the surviving citizens of the republic what those men died for. He does not say they died to defeat the South. He does not say they died to free the slaves, though freeing the slaves is the action that will now follow. He says they died so that "government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth."

That formula — of the people, by the people, for the people — is not a liberal slogan. It is a republican one. Liberal government is government that protects the rights of the people. Republican government is government that is *done by* the people — that has no existence apart from their participation, their judgment, their responsibility, their sacrifice when the sacrifice is required. Lincoln is not abandoning the liberal inheritance; he is adapting it. The Declaration's "all men are created equal" is preserved and radicalized. But the frame in which it is preserved is the civic-republican frame: the nation is the people acting together, and the people acting together is what the dead at Gettysburg were defending. A republic whose citizens have ceased to act together has nothing for its soldiers to die for, whatever the formal rights on its books.

Lincoln is the American bridge between the Founders' generation and the mass-democratic twentieth century. He is also, and this is the deeper point, the figure who shows that the Federalist/Anti-Federalist argument can be held together by someone of sufficient moral and political seriousness. Lincoln is a constitutional nationalist; he is also a small-town Illinoisan who carried the habits of the New England town meeting into the White House. He defends the large republic; he also understands that the large republic lives only on the moral capital generated by the small ones. He is Madison and Brutus at once. He is the American synthesis in a person.

***

The last figure we need for this chapter is the one who tried, in the full daylight of the twentieth century, to rescue the thing Lincoln had defended. John Dewey's *The Public and Its Problems*, published in 1927, is the most serious American attempt of its century to revive democratic citizenship in the conditions of mass society. Dewey is writing against Walter Lippmann, whose *Phantom Public* of 1925 had argued that democratic citizenship in a modern industrial society was essentially impossible — that ordinary people could not possibly know enough about the complex technical questions of modern governance to make informed judgments, and that democracy would therefore have to be replaced, in practice if not in name, by expert administration with periodic plebiscitary consent.

Dewey's response is the key move. Democracy, he says, is not a set of procedures. It is "a way of life." It is a mode of associated living, of communicated experience, in which every member of the community participates in shaping the conditions of his own life and in learning from the participation of others. The procedures — voting, legislatures, courts — are the outward form. The substance is the continuous practice of communication, inquiry, and shared decision among citizens who have learned to take one another seriously. A society in which the procedures operate but the substance has drained away is not a democracy. It is a husk of one.

Dewey's positive program is pedagogical all the way down. Every institution, he argues, is also a school of citizenship, or it is not fit for its place in a democratic polity. A workplace either teaches its workers the habits of cooperation, deliberation, and responsibility, or it trains them in subordination and resentment. A school either forms its students into the kind of people who can participate in self-government, or it produces credentialed consumers of expert opinion. A neighborhood either teaches its residents the arts of public life, or it is a sorting mechanism for privately lived lives. The question one asks of any institution, from a union to a church to a family to a baseball league, is not only "does it do its stated job?" It is also "does it produce citizens?" And if it does not, Dewey insists, it undermines the polity it inhabits, no matter how well it performs its formal function.

This is a profound argument and, in our view, substantially correct. It is also the argument with the clearest lineage to our own. When we argue in Volume III that institutional renewal is not a question of reforming institutions from the outside but of the conduct of persons within them, we are restating a Deweyan claim.

Dewey has weaknesses, and we will not hide them. His optimism about expert-guided publics — the faith that science, adequately communicated, would in time produce a competent democratic citizenry — underestimated something he could not yet see: the twentieth-century discovery that expertise can be a disguise for ideology, that the sciences of man are especially vulnerable to capture by partisan programs, and that a citizenry conditioned to defer to experts is a citizenry that has lost something Dewey himself valued, namely the practice of independent judgment. His book was written in 1927, before Stalinism, before Nazism, before the century had shown what ideological mass movements could do to the kind of "public" Dewey was trying to reconstruct. He did not see, because he could not yet see, how fragile the conditions for democratic inquiry are and how readily they dissolve into the conditions for democratic capture. Arendt, writing twenty-four years later with that experience behind her, understood something Dewey did not.

But the central Deweyan insight stands, and we rest on it. Democracy is pedagogical all the way down. A society that wants to remain self-governing has to treat every institution that forms its citizens as part of the constitutional order, and has to judge those institutions, at least in part, by whether they form the kind of citizens the constitutional order requires. A country that has stopped doing this — that treats schools, workplaces, media, families, and churches as matters indifferent to the political order, to be arranged on any grounds whatever — is a country that will discover, over time, that the political order is unsupported by the people it was supposed to contain.

***

Pull the threads. The Federalists built an institutional architecture capable of housing a free people across a continent. Their bet — that ambition could be harnessed, that representation could substitute for participation, that a large commercial republic could sustain liberty where small republics had failed — was largely vindicated. The Anti-Federalists warned that the architecture they could not prevent would, over time, erode the civic substrate on which free government depends. That warning, too, was largely vindicated. Tocqueville, arriving fifty years later, saw both at once: a republic whose national freedom rested on a layer of local associations whose decline, when it came, would leave the formal republic intact and the substance hollowed out. Lincoln, in the middle of the century, tried to name the political religion by which a free people could love the institutions that sheltered their freedom, and died in the attempt to preserve them. Dewey, in the next century, tried to translate that project into an industrial democracy and produced, for all his blind spots, the theoretical language in which we still argue.

The American tradition does not resolve the Federalist/Anti-Federalist argument. It keeps it alive. The Constitution is Federalist. The Bill of Rights is Anti-Federalist. The national government is Federalist. The federal system is Anti-Federalist. The Supreme Court is Federalist. Trial by jury is Anti-Federalist. The standing army is Federalist. The Second Amendment is, in its original civic-republican reading, Anti-Federalist. Madison and Hamilton gave us the frame. Brutus and the Federal Farmer shadowed it with a critique we still read when we want to know what was given up for the frame to stand. American political life has been, from the start, an unstable compromise between the commercial republic and the civic republic, and the instability has been its greatest strength. When the Federalist side wins decisively — when the national government absorbs everything, when representation fully displaces participation, when commerce eats civic life — the system degrades toward the soft despotism Tocqueville forecast. When the Anti-Federalist side wins decisively — when local particularism becomes absolute, when the national order fractures into the sectional passions the Constitution was designed to manage — the system degrades toward disunion, which in our history has meant civil war.

Health, for an American polity, is the maintenance of the tension. It is not the splitting of the difference; it is not centrism; it is not "both sides have a point." It is the preservation, in the institutional architecture and the cultural habits of the people, of both halves of an argument that cannot be won and should not be. The national commercial republic needs the local civic republic to supply it with citizens. The local civic republic needs the national commercial republic to defend it against aggression and to secure rights its own particularisms cannot reliably protect. Each depends on the other; each is the other's corrective; each degrades without the other. The American genius, when the American order has displayed any, has been the refusal to collapse the tension into a single principle.

We began this chapter by saying that the American experiment addressed, under real pressure, the tension this book is about. That tension is the tension between the individual and the polity, between rights and virtue, between the person who holds claims against the community and the person whose very capacity to hold claims is generated by the community he belongs to. The Federalists, for the most part, spoke for the first. The Anti-Federalists, for the most part, spoke for the second. Neither spoke for both; neither needed to, because their argument with each other did the speaking. What the American tradition has to teach is that the rights-and-virtue problem is not a problem to be solved once, by a philosopher, in a book. It is a problem to be managed, continuously, by a people who have learned to hold both terms in their hands at once. Rights without virtue decay into the soft despotism of equal and solitary consumers. Virtue without rights decays into the hard despotism of the community that permits no dissent. The American order, at its best, has insisted on both, imperfectly, unevenly, with terrible failures along the way — slavery chief among them, and the failures are part of the record — but with a persistence that has kept the tension alive for two and a half centuries, which is longer than most political orders have lasted.

The claim we want to land this chapter on is the claim that matters for what follows. The American tradition already contains what this book calls republican liberalism. It has always held rights and virtue together — unevenly, imperfectly, but in practice. It has always insisted that the citizen is an individual, with rights no majority can vote away, and a member, with obligations no private pursuit can dissolve. It has always known, in its institutions more than in its explicit theory, that the two terms are not rivals but conditions of each other. The Constitution protects rights; the Bill of Rights limits the government; the federal system preserves the local arenas in which virtue is practiced; the political religion Lincoln described is the habit of loving an order in which both hold. The American synthesis is not a formula. It is a living practice, often failing, periodically renewed.

The intellectual task of the next chapter is to make explicit what has been implicit in this inheritance and to extend it. We will argue, with Richard Dagger but for more ambitious purposes, that republican liberalism — rights against the state and non-domination in the polity, individual conscience and civic virtue, the person and the citizen as a single figure — is the usable answer to the question this book has been circling. It is not new. It is old. The Americans have been half-practicing it from the start. What we can do, at this hour, is name what they have been doing, say why it is worth defending, and begin to imagine what a renewed version of it would look like in the twenty-first century. For that we need a chapter of our own. We turn to it now.
