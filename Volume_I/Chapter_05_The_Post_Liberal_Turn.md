# Chapter 5
## The Post-Liberal Turn

Every generation produces a book whose function is to say out loud what a great many people have begun to suspect in private. In 2018, the book was Patrick Deneen's *Why Liberalism Failed*. It was not the first book to say that liberalism was in trouble, and it was not, strictly speaking, the most careful. But it was the book that arrived at the right moment in the right register, and it landed. Barack Obama listed it on his end-of-year reading list and called it thought-provoking. David Brooks wrote about it twice in the *New York Times* and called it valuable even while disagreeing with it. Ross Douthat praised it; R. R. Reno, at *First Things*, received it as a confirmation of a long-running editorial project. Within eighteen months of its publication it had been translated into a dozen languages and become the reference text for a whole cohort of younger intellectuals — some academic, some journalistic, some now in political staff jobs and think-tank presidencies — whose self-understanding is built partly on the premise that Deneen was right. A serious book about the condition of the citizen, written in the late 2020s, cannot walk past this. There are bad reasons to engage post-liberalism — to score points, to perform moderation, to notice in its prescriptions a convenient enemy — and there are good reasons, and the good reasons are simpler. The post-liberal argument is not stupid. It is, in its diagnostic mode, often uncomfortably sharp. A defender of the liberal tradition, or of anything in the neighborhood of it, who cannot say where the post-liberals are right has not actually defended anything; he has repeated his catechism at a louder volume. We intend to do better than that, though we will not pretend the effort is easy.

We will take Deneen as our starting point, because he is the figure the reader will have heard of, and because his book is the most accessible gateway into the wider post-liberal constellation. Then we will widen the frame to include Adrian Vermeule, whose prescriptions are more specific and more alarming; Sohrab Ahmari, whose economic turn is the movement's most interesting and underrated move; Yoram Hazony, whose conservative-nationalist version of the critique has become the unofficial platform of a particular kind of American and Israeli right; and, in an older register, Christopher Lasch, whose left-communitarian prescience makes it impossible to read post-liberalism as a purely right-wing phenomenon. We will then try to steelman the case as honestly as we can, naming the places where the diagnosis is piercing and the critics are evasive. And finally — this is the difficult passage — we will say where we think the post-liberal move goes wrong, and why its error is not a small one. The book is not post-liberal. It is friendly to much of the diagnosis and hostile to most of the prescription. We would rather say so than leave the reader to infer it.

Deneen's argument, in its simplest form, has two axes. The first is anthropological: liberalism, he argues, rests on a particular picture of the human being, a picture in which the self is conceived as prior to its attachments, choosing its identity and its values out of a kind of sovereign inwardness. This is the self Sandel had already called the unencumbered self and MacIntyre had called the emotivist self; Deneen's contribution is to press the picture back through the tradition — not only to Rawls, but to Locke, and past Locke to Hobbes, and from Hobbes back to a nominalist current in late-medieval thought in which the will, rather than the reasoning intellect, becomes the organ of freedom. The second axis is ethical and political: liberalism understands freedom as the absence of constraint, the removal of whatever stands between the choosing self and the objects of its choice. What stands between the self and its choices, in concrete human life, is mostly other people — family, clan, congregation, guild, neighborhood, local authority — and the accumulated norms through which those forms of belonging bind their members. Liberal freedom therefore operates, over historical time, as a solvent. It dissolves the mediating institutions in the name of liberating the individuals who inhabited them. And as those institutions go, the capacity of human beings to form one another through them goes with them. What remains is the individual, increasingly alone, and the state, increasingly large, because an atomized population requires a managerial apparatus of a kind that a thickly associated population does not.

It is worth pausing to note how ambitious this genealogy is, because the ambition accounts for both the power and the vulnerability of the argument. Deneen is not simply diagnosing a modern malaise. He is reaching back through four centuries of Western thought to claim that a wrong turn was taken somewhere between late-medieval nominalism and early-modern political theory, that every subsequent refinement of the liberal project has amplified the original deviation rather than corrected it, and that even the most humane and communitarian liberals — the Mills and the Deweys and the Rawlses of a more sociable reading — are operating within a frame whose metaphysical foundations are incompatible with human flourishing at its root. This is a theological move as much as a philosophical one, and it is advanced with the calm of a thinker who believes he has traced the condition of the modern citizen back to its Ockhamist origin and does not much mind how startling that sounds. There is a version of this argument that is rigorous and a version that is merely imposing, and the difference lies in the willingness to test the genealogy against the historical record. We will return to that test shortly. For now, we record that the scope of Deneen's indictment is the first thing a careful reader should note about the book, because the scope is what makes the prescription follow so relentlessly from the diagnosis, and what makes the diagnosis, once accepted, so difficult to confine.

The most striking move in *Why Liberalism Failed* — and the one that distinguishes it from earlier critiques in the same family — is Deneen's insistence that what we are living through is not liberalism's failure but its success. The usual story is that liberalism began well, promising rights and tolerance and the rule of law, and then somehow went off the rails in the late twentieth century, when it was captured by technocrats or by identity politics or by globalized capital. On that telling, reform is possible; we need to return to the original liberal project. Deneen refuses the consolation of that story. The pathologies we observe — the loneliness, the atomization, the managerial state, the dissolution of the intermediate institutions, the consumer citizen who cannot articulate a common good — are not deviations from the liberal project. They are the project, now fully worked out. What liberalism set out to produce it has produced. The disaster is the fulfillment. If you find this dark, Deneen would say, you should not blame the messenger; you should consider the possibility that the tradition itself is the source of the darkness, and that the only honest response is to look for what comes next.

This is a powerful argument and it deserves to be heard at its strongest, so we will not paraphrase further. The point we want to make now is a structural one. Deneen's thesis is not reformist. It is not a plea to recover early Locke from late Rawls, or to pair the liberal tradition with a richer account of civic virtue in the way Dagger or Sandel would suggest. It is a claim that the whole tradition, from its metaphysical foundations outward, is misconceived, and that the humane conservatives and liberal communitarians who have spent forty years trying to reform it are wasting their energy on a corpse. That claim sets the tone for the wider movement, and it is what makes the prescription, when we get to it, so difficult to contain.

The wider movement is not uniform, and the book would be doing the reader a disservice to pretend it is. Adrian Vermeule, the Harvard legal scholar, is the most intellectually rigorous and the most politically aggressive of the post-liberal figures. His doctrine, which he calls "common-good constitutionalism," is a direct assault on the originalist and libertarian forms of conservative legal thought that have dominated the American right for forty years. The central contention is simple. Constitutional law should not be read as a neutral framework for adjudicating competing private interests; it should be read as an instrument by which public authority directs the polity toward substantive goods — peace, justice, the flourishing of families, the moral ecology of communities. Neutrality, on this view, is a myth, and indeed a dishonest myth, because the current liberal order is not neutral at all; it simply privileges a particular substantive good, namely the maximization of individual choice, and pretends to be procedural while enforcing its substantive preferences through the administrative state. If the state is already in the business of forming persons — and Vermeule argues, with force, that it is — then the honest question is not whether it should form them but toward what. And his answer, argued most openly in his book *Common Good Constitutionalism* and more provocatively in shorter essays, is a tradition with a recognizable name: the classical natural-law tradition as preserved and elaborated within the Catholic Church.

Here the wider movement's religious texture becomes unavoidable. Vermeule is a Catholic convert and a self-described integralist — one who holds, in the strong sense, that the political community is rightly subordinated to the ends defined by the true religion, and that the distinction between the secular and the spiritual is an accommodation rather than a permanent feature of political order. The integralist proposal, stated plainly, is a confessional state: a polity whose law openly acknowledges the authority of the Church in matters the Church claims competence over, and whose neutrality toward competing conceptions of the good is finally abandoned. This is not a caricature; it is the explicit position of a serious body of recent writing in *First Things*, *The Postliberal Order* newsletter, and elsewhere. It is not the universal position of every figure we are engaging with in this chapter — Deneen is more reticent, Ahmari explicitly demurs — but it is a live and defended position at the movement's intellectual core, and a book that declines to notice it is not engaging the actual thing.

We should add one further observation about Vermeule before moving on, because it illuminates something about the movement as a whole. The writing is lucid, the legal scholarship is serious, and the historical knowledge is deep; none of the cheap dismissals work. What the reader notices, after spending time with the corpus, is that the argument for common-good constitutionalism is advanced without any of the hesitations an observer of twentieth-century regimes might reasonably be expected to express. The possibility that the capture of the administrative apparatus by a substantive moral vision could produce the kinds of catastrophes the twentieth century produced is considered, when it is considered, as a remote contingency rather than as the operative caution. The historical record, when cited, is cited selectively — the medieval synthesis at its high points, the Dominican schools at their most sophisticated, the Catholic political thought of the nineteenth century at its least entangled with the throne — and the intervals at which these same ideas were put to work in the machinery of states are passed over. We are not accusing Vermeule of bad faith; we are saying that the argument has a blind spot, and that the blind spot is precisely where the liberal tradition, for all its thinness, has historically done its best work. The liberal constitutional achievement was always the response to a lived experience of what it was like to inhabit a polity that did know what the good was and did not hesitate to direct the population toward it. The post-liberal case against liberal neutrality proceeds as though that lived experience had no evidentiary weight.

Sohrab Ahmari is the post-liberal figure we find most interesting, and we say this without irony. His 2023 book *Tyranny, Inc.* — subtitled *How Private Power Crushed American Liberty, and What to Do About It* — is distinct within the movement because it takes economic power seriously. The standard post-liberal target is what Ahmari calls cultural liberalism: the regime of expressive individualism, no-fault divorce, consumer-grade identity, the whole apparatus of a society that cannot name the good. Ahmari does not spare that target, but his argument is that the cultural complaint alone is insufficient, and indeed that much of what cultural conservatives lament is downstream of an economic order in which ordinary Americans do not have the standing to form durable families, communities, or attachments of any kind. The worker without predictable hours, the debtor whose financial life is dictated by the small print, the tenant whose housing is contingent on the yield requirements of a distant institutional investor — these are not just economic victims; they are people whose standing as free actors has been destroyed by concentrations of private power that classical liberalism, in its contemporary libertarian form, has treated as no concern of politics. Ahmari's rhetorical move is to steal the civic-republican vocabulary of non-domination from the academic left and deploy it against private employers, landlords, and creditors, rather than only against the state. That is a move classical liberals do not know how to respond to, because it uses their own tradition's idioms — coercion, unfreedom, the wrongness of being subject to another's arbitrary will — in a domain they had long since conceded to "the market." We will return to this in Volume II, when we engage Sandel on meritocracy and the hollowing of work. For now, we register that Ahmari is the post-liberal figure most worth argument, because his critique reaches parts of liberal practice the others leave untouched.

There is a further reason Ahmari matters to this book's argument, and it is a reason worth making explicit. The standard American conservative response to a critique of private economic power is to reply that the alternative is a still-larger state, and that the cure is worse than the disease. The reply has been made so often and in so many venues that it has acquired the texture of a reflex rather than an argument. What Ahmari's work forces a careful reader to notice is that the choice is not binary. The mediating institutions — the unions, the fraternal orders, the mutual-aid societies, the parish and the synagogue credit cooperatives, the guild-like professional associations — were for most of American history the third term. They restrained both the overreach of the state and the overreach of concentrated private power, and they did so because they were the standing organizations of citizens with both civic and economic lives, capable of acting for themselves at a scale smaller than the state and larger than the nuclear family. When those institutions collapsed, the binary between state power and private power became inescapable, and the political argument narrowed to an unwinnable choice between two kinds of domination. Ahmari is, on this reading, recovering a specifically civic-republican insight about the architecture of unfreedom: that a free person is one not subject to the arbitrary will of another, and that in modern conditions the arbitrary will of an employer or a landlord or a creditor can be as constraining as the arbitrary will of a magistrate. We think this is right, and we will come back to it.

Yoram Hazony's version of the post-liberal argument is the one with the broadest political reach.

Yoram Hazony's version of the post-liberal argument is the one with the broadest political reach. His 2018 *The Virtue of Nationalism* and his 2022 *Conservatism: A Rediscovery* have done something none of the other post-liberal texts have managed: they have supplied an organizing vocabulary for an international conservative movement that stretches from Washington to Jerusalem to Budapest. The argument is conservative and nationalist in a specific historical sense. Hazony places himself in an Anglo-American and Hebraic tradition that predates Enlightenment liberalism and to which, in his telling, liberalism's universalism has been the great solvent. The true sources of political order are not universal principles derived from abstract reason but the concrete inheritances of family, congregation, tribe, and nation, and the moral loyalties these inheritances produce. The appeal to Scripture runs through both books and is not decorative; Hazony is making a theological argument that a certain kind of political loyalty is what human beings are made for, and that liberal universalism has attempted to substitute an alien principle — the abstract person, bearer of rights — for the actual people in their actual communities. One does not have to agree with him to notice that he is operating at a level of historical depth that most of his critics have not matched. One also does not have to agree with him to notice that his conservatism is compatible with a range of political arrangements, including ones the reader may find difficult to endorse.

And then there is Christopher Lasch, who died in 1994 and whose name is rarely grouped with the others, but whose work is an indispensable bridge. Lasch was a historian of the American left who ended up writing some of the sharpest American conservative criticism of the twentieth century without ever becoming a conservative. *The Culture of Narcissism*, published in 1979, is a book about the psychological consequences of the dissolution of the family, the trade, the neighborhood, and the traditional moral authorities. Its argument is that the therapeutic self, reared without the discipline of genuine attachments, will present to clinical observation as a narcissist — incapable of love, unable to endure solitude, dependent on the validation of others even while pretending to self-sufficiency — and that this psychological type is not an aberration but the product of an entire civilization's institutional collapse. *The Revolt of the Elites*, published posthumously in 1995, extends the argument upward into the professional class, whose separation from the common life of the country had, in Lasch's view, destroyed the last cultural precondition of American democracy. Lasch is indispensable to the post-liberal genealogy because he says most of what Deneen says, two decades earlier, with almost none of the theological register. He is not a Catholic, he is not an integralist, he does not propose a confessional state or a return to the natural-law tradition. He proposes, insofar as he proposes anything, a populist democratic recovery that the left could have claimed and did not. Anyone who wants to insist that the post-liberal diagnosis is a sectarian right-wing phenomenon has to explain Lasch, and also has to explain why the concerns Lasch raised from within the left have been taken up by almost no one on the contemporary left except, in fragmentary form, Ahmari and a small circle around him.

We dwell on this breadth because the easy version of this chapter — the version in which post-liberalism is a confessional Catholic reaction to secular modernity, and can be quarantined as such — is not the honest version. The diagnostic argument circulates across religious, national, and ideological lines because it is seeing something real. We turn to what it is seeing.

***

We owe the post-liberals a steelman, and we will try to deliver one in the voice they themselves would recognize as their own.

They are right, first, that liberal societies have hollowed out the intermediate institutions. This is not a contested claim; Putnam's forty years of data make it nearly unanswerable at the descriptive level. The families are smaller and more fragile. The congregations are less numerous and emptier. The unions are a third of their mid-century size. The civic associations have thinned. The local newspapers have disappeared. The fraternal orders have aged into irrelevance. Whatever one thinks of the causes, the effect is a society in which the formation of human beings is no longer carried out, in any robust way, by the institutions to which liberal political theory had historically delegated that labor. The state, which in liberal theory was supposed to be neutral among conceptions of the good, has expanded to fill the vacuum, and the market, which in liberal theory was supposed to be an instrument of individual choice, has expanded to colonize the domains the associations once filled. The post-liberal claim that liberal theory did not predict this, and cannot address it from within its own resources, is a claim that deserves an answer.

They are right, second, that neutrality as a regulative ideal is often a cover story. The state that claims to bracket the question of the good life is a state that, in practice, selects for a specific cluster of substantive goods — individual mobility, marketable credential, expressive freedom, consumer choice, the authority of credentialed expertise — and builds its institutions around them. The citizen whose life is organized around a different cluster of goods — rootedness, religious observance, unchosen obligation, the dignity of skilled manual work, the authority of a parish or a place — experiences the neutral state not as a neutral arbiter but as a quiet partisan whose thumb has been on the other side of the scale for the whole of his political life. Vermeule is not inventing this complaint; he is articulating something that has been legible in the experience of many Americans for a generation, and the liberal response — "the state is neutral, you are just noticing that it doesn't share your preferences" — is not adequate to what is actually being complained about.

They are right, third, that the growth of the administrative state is not causally independent of the decline of local self-government. This is the structural claim Deneen makes most insistently, and we have not seen a satisfying rebuttal. When the parish stops running the school, someone has to run the school, and that someone is, by default, an apparatus accountable not to the people whose children attend it but to a bureaucracy. When the mutual-aid society stops providing sick benefits, someone has to provide them, and that someone is, by default, an insurance regime administered at a scale at which the recipient has no standing. The mediating institutions did work that was invisible until it stopped being done, and the state has, in many domains, absorbed that work at a cost — in legitimacy, in local attachment, in the sheer intelligibility of the arrangement to the people subject to it — that liberal theory had not budgeted for. It is fair to observe that the post-liberals rarely calculate what the alternative arrangements cost when they were actually tried; but it is also fair to observe that the liberal reply, which is often simply to cite the ways those older arrangements were unjust, has the structure of changing the subject.

They are right, fourth — and this is the point we feel most strongly, because it is the one this book has to reckon with — that a public philosophy that cannot name the good is at a severe disadvantage against public movements that can. A citizenry asked to defend a political order on the grounds that it is procedurally fair and maximally neutral among competing conceptions of the good has been given, in effect, an accountant's defense of its own way of life, and it should not surprise us when the accountant's defense proves weaker than the prophet's attack. The liberal tradition at its best has resources here — Tocqueville, Dewey, Martin Luther King, the tradition of public religion that Robert Bellah reconstructed, the civic-republican current we will make central in Chapter 7 — but the mainline liberalism of the last half-century has largely lost access to those resources. It has defended procedure against substance and thereby conceded substance to its enemies. The post-liberals have not so much invented a new argument as noticed an opening.

If this is the steelman, and we believe it is, then the question for our book is not whether the diagnosis is right in broad strokes. The diagnosis is right in broad strokes. The question is what to do with it.

***

Here the post-liberal turn goes wrong, and the chapter must now say where.

The first problem is what we can only call the diagnostic slide into unfalsifiability. When every bad outcome in a liberal society is attributed to liberalism, the theory has no empirical traction. If the family is fragile, liberalism did it. If the church is empty, liberalism did it. If the administrative state is large, liberalism did it. If the market is rapacious, liberalism did it. If the culture is thin, if the public is inattentive, if the young are lonely, if the elites are contemptuous, if the workers are disposable — liberalism did it. This is no longer an explanation; it is a theodicy in reverse, in which a metaphysical principle is assigned credit for every phenomenon within its historical vicinity. And the difficulty, which deserves more attention than the post-liberals have given it, is that the empirical pattern does not line up. Putnam's documentation of the collapse of civic associations is the richest body of evidence we have on this question. The collapse has been more severe in non-American liberal orders — Britain, France, the Netherlands — whose political traditions are substantially less Lockean, less individualist, less founded on the specific anthropological commitments Deneen identifies as the pathogen. The French Third Republic was a secular-statist project, not a Lockean one, and yet the social capital metrics in France track the American ones. The United Kingdom did not have a Lockean founding in the American sense, and yet the collapse of civic life in the UK has been, if anything, faster. The Netherlands, the exemplar of pillarized confessional society through the middle of the twentieth century, is now among the most atomized of the advanced economies. If liberalism is the solvent, the timing and geography have to be explained. A theory that explains American dysfunction by way of American first principles has a problem when the same dysfunction shows up, on the same curve, in societies whose first principles are different. At the very least, the post-liberals owe us an account of why their master variable is doing less work than they claim.

The second problem is the prescription. Here we have to be specific, because the charge against the post-liberals that bothers us most is the one they have least addressed. Deneen, famously, is vague. The closing chapters of *Why Liberalism Failed* gesture at what comes after liberalism without specifying it, and his 2023 book *Regime Change* is clearer on what must be overthrown than on what should replace it. Vermeule is not vague, and his specifics are what should alarm. A regime in which the state is understood to direct its population toward substantive goods defined by a particular religious and legal tradition, with constitutional interpretation transformed into an instrument of that direction, and with the administrative apparatus turned from neutral regulator into spiritual formator — this is not a hypothetical. It has been tried. The historical examples — the confessional states of early modern Europe, the throne-and-altar regimes of the nineteenth century, the various integralist experiments of the twentieth — are not advertisements for the proposal. They are cautions. The post-liberal response, when pressed on these precedents, tends to distinguish the classical ideal from its historical corruptions, which is exactly the move post-liberals refuse to allow liberals when the liberals distinguish their own ideals from the historical corruptions of liberal politics. What is sauce for the Lockean goose is sauce for the Thomist gander. If we are permitted to judge liberalism by its twentieth-century outcomes, we are permitted to judge integralism by its seventeenth-century ones.

The third problem is one Francis Fukuyama identified in his 2022 response, *Liberalism and Its Discontents*, and we think Fukuyama is largely right. The post-liberal critique attacks a caricature of liberalism — liberalism as Lockean possessive individualism, full stop — and does not engage the fuller tradition. The fuller tradition includes Tocqueville, whose analysis of American democracy was from the beginning an analysis of the associations that made liberal self-government possible, and who was not in any meaningful sense a Lockean. It includes Mill, whose *On Liberty* is usually read as an argument for the removal of social restraint and, when read more carefully, is an argument about the conditions under which individuals can be educated toward a richer and more demanding freedom — not less constraint in general, but the specific discipline of a communal practice of reasoning. It includes Dewey, whose whole democratic-communitarian project was an attempt to reconstitute the shared life that industrial modernity had hollowed out. It includes the American civil-rights tradition, which articulated universal liberal principles in the idiom of a prophetic religious community and thereby refuted, by practice, the claim that liberalism has no resources for a thick public ethics. Post-liberals are, by and large, silent about these figures, and when they are not silent, they tend to classify them as deviations from the real liberal project. But they cannot be deviations from the real liberal project and also not liberals. The move by which the opponent's best arguments are read out of the tradition whose failures the opponent is held responsible for is a rhetorical trick, not an argument. The liberal tradition, taken in its full historical spread, is not the reductive thing post-liberalism needs it to be in order to be the pathogen post-liberalism needs it to be. A more generous liberalism is available — has always been available — and the post-liberals have won their argument in part by declining to engage it.

The fourth problem is one the hostile reviewers have been sharpest on. Robert Kuttner, writing in the *New York Review of Books*, called *Why Liberalism Failed* "an apocalyptic cri-de-coeur disguised as political theory." The description is not fair to the whole book, but it is fair to the mode. The apocalyptic register is a feature, not an accident, of the post-liberal corpus. It licenses a politics that rejects deliberation in advance. If the regime is not merely failing but has succeeded in producing a catastrophe, and if the catastrophe is civilizational, then the ordinary instruments of reform — argument, persuasion, electoral politics, the slow adjustment of public opinion — are not merely inadequate but complicit. Only rupture will do. This is the logic by which the post-liberal movement has slid, in its more militant corners, from a critique of liberal neutrality into advocacy of the kind of forceful remaking of public life that liberal constitutional forms were designed to prevent. The apocalyptic tone is in this sense not a rhetorical flourish but a structural commitment. And it is worth naming, because the civic tradition this book defends is the tradition of deliberation — the slow, patient, public reasoning of equals with each other — and an intellectual posture that rules deliberation out on the front end is a posture the civic tradition has to disagree with before it disagrees with anything else.

There is a further difficulty with the Fukuyama reply's implications that is worth stating plainly, because it bears on the structure of the debate. The post-liberal writer who refuses to engage the fuller tradition has, in effect, arranged the argument so that he cannot lose. Any liberal who responds with Tocqueville is told that Tocqueville is not really a liberal; any liberal who responds with Mill on character is told that this is not the real Mill; any liberal who points to Dewey's thick account of democratic community is told that Dewey is a transitional figure who did not see where his premises led. The result is that the "liberalism" the post-liberal attacks is by definition the minimal, thin, procedural version, and the tradition's richer resources are read out of the tradition in advance. This is a perfectly understandable rhetorical strategy, but it is not an honest engagement. The liberal tradition is, and has always been, a conversation rather than a single doctrine, and the conversation has included within it almost every insight the post-liberals now present as external corrections to it. The move by which that conversation is flattened into a caricature is the move that permits the replacement program; without the flattening, the natural next step would be not replacement but recovery, and recovery is the step we believe the evidence actually warrants.

The fifth problem is the most important one and the one the book will take up most directly. The post-liberal move collapses the distinction between the rejection of Rawlsian neutrality and the rejection of liberal rights. It treats them as a package. If you want a polity that can name substantive goods, you are told, you must abandon the framework of rights, procedures, and limits on state authority that the liberal tradition built. If you want rights, procedures, and limits, you must accept that your polity cannot name any substantive good and is therefore disarmed against the movements that can. This is an argumentatively convenient binary, and it is wrong. It is the central claim of this book, pressed across every subsequent chapter, that the binary is false. You can reject the liberal-philosophical thesis that all conceptions of the good must be bracketed at the level of public reason and still hold that the state must be constrained by rights, by due process, by limits on its own reach into conscience. You can recover a thick civic language of the common good and still refuse to dismantle habeas corpus. You can insist that a republic requires citizens of a certain formed character and still deny that the state is rightly the primary agent of that formation. The civic republican tradition, at its best, held precisely this combination: substantive about the good, constrained about the means, insistent on both liberty and self-government. It is the tradition Vermeule and Deneen have, in their different ways, misread as requiring the full post-liberal package. The package is optional. The move from "the liberal picture of the self is thin" to "therefore we must build a confessional administrative state" is not a deduction; it is a leap. The second move does not follow from the first, and we intend, across the remaining chapters of this volume, to show why.

It is worth noting, because it is easy to miss, that the religious texture of the post-liberal movement is both explanatory and limiting. The movement is overwhelmingly Catholic: Deneen, Vermeule, Ahmari, Reno, the roster at *First Things*, the younger writers at *Compact* and *The Lamp* and *The Postliberal Order*. This is not an accident and it is not discreditable. The Catholic intellectual tradition has, for reasons rooted in its natural-law inheritance and its memory of Christendom, the resources to articulate a thick and ordered account of the common good against which the liberal order can be measured and found wanting. A similar critique can be made from within Protestant or Jewish or secular traditions — Hazony makes it from a Hebraic position, Lasch made it from a secular populist one, and MacIntyre works from a Thomist-inflected but philosophically framed argument — but the institutional density of the Catholic version, its journals and its fellowships and its academic perches, is what has given the post-liberal movement its current visibility. The limit, which should be named with the same candor, is that a movement whose intellectual energy depends on a particular religious tradition is not well-positioned to become a general public philosophy in a society as religiously plural as ours. The post-liberal project, taken at its own word, is a proposal about the ordering of a society most of whose members do not share its foundational commitments, and the question of how such an ordering is to be achieved — except by the imposition the movement's rhetoric sometimes makes too available — has not been adequately answered. We note this not to dismiss the movement but to describe its reach. A post-liberal political program that does not confront the religious pluralism of its society is a program for the society that does not exist. The society that does exist will require a different set of answers.

***

The uncomfortable recognition is that the diagnosis we have just defended — the one the post-liberals have articulated with such force — is largely the same diagnosis the communitarians made in the 1980s, which we have already engaged in the last chapter. Sandel on the unencumbered self, MacIntyre on the fragmentation of moral vocabulary, Taylor on the malaise of modernity, Bellah on the habits of the heart: all of these are, in their different idioms, accounts of the same civilizational hollowing-out that Deneen and his circle describe. The post-liberals sometimes acknowledge this genealogy and sometimes efface it, but the intellectual continuity is undeniable, and the reader attentive to that continuity will have noticed that the diagnostic chapter of *Why Liberalism Failed* could have been written by a communitarian of the 1980s without major alteration.

What distinguishes Sandel from Deneen, then, is not the diagnosis. It is the program. Sandel wants a civic-republican *supplement* to the liberal tradition — a recovery of the older American idiom of public virtue, local self-government, and a formative politics, installed alongside the apparatus of rights and procedural justice rather than instead of it. Deneen wants a *replacement*. The polity Sandel would build has the Bill of Rights in it; the polity Deneen gestures toward does not, or has it in some unrecognizable form. This is the fault line this book sits astride, and we want to name it plainly, because a reader who has followed the argument to this point deserves to know where we come down.

We come down on the supplement side, without apology. We believe the American civic-republican and liberal traditions are not rivals but complements — that the achievement of rights against the state is a moral acquisition no thinking person should give up, and that the achievement of a formed public virtue is a civic acquisition no thinking person should abandon. We believe the fault the post-liberals correctly identify in liberalism-as-practiced is the narrowing of the tradition to its procedural skeleton, not the tradition itself. We believe the prescription they offer, taken at its word, would produce forms of unfreedom more severe than the ones they are trying to cure. And we believe — and this is the conviction that carries the rest of Volume I — that the American tradition contains, in its own founding quarrel, a richer set of resources for thinking through this question than either the communitarians or the post-liberals have drawn on.

That quarrel is the subject of the next chapter. The Federalists and the Anti-Federalists did not use the vocabulary we have been using in this one, but they were arguing about the same question. Could a commercial republic, extended across a continental scale, sustain the civic virtue on which free government depends? Madison thought it could, and built a constitutional machine to make up in structure what the polity would inevitably lose in shared character. Brutus and the Federal Farmer thought it could not, and warned that the scale would erode the associations and habits that made the whole project possible. They lost the argument in 1788. It is possible to hold, as we do, both that they were right to lose and that they were right about what would be lost. The synthesis we are building, and the American tradition we are trying to recover, is not the victory of one side over the other. It is the retrieval of the argument itself — an argument about what a citizen is and what it takes to keep one — that the Americans conducted at a depth neither our communitarians nor our post-liberals have matched. We turn to it now.
