# Chapter 4
## The Communitarian Reply

The previous chapter ended with the liberal tradition at the height of its powers. Rawls had given liberalism its most rigorous modern form; Berlin had given it its most humane philosophical voice; and the liberal order, in the decades after the Second World War, had delivered more freedom to more people, across more lines of sex and race and class and religion, than any rival had managed in the whole history of the species. If the tradition has critics — and it does, and they are serious — we owe those critics the same charity we just paid to liberalism itself. We owe them, in particular, an honest statement of what they actually believe, unflattened by caricature, before we can begin to say where we agree and where we do not.

This chapter is about the first wave of those critics, the ones who arose in the 1980s in direct philosophical combat with *A Theory of Justice* and who have collectively come to be called the communitarians. We use the label because it has stuck and because the figures themselves, with varying degrees of comfort, accepted it; but the reader should know from the outset that "communitarian" is a family resemblance rather than a doctrine. Alasdair MacIntyre and Charles Taylor and Michael Sandel and Michael Walzer would not all sign the same manifesto, and at least one of them would decline to sign anything. What unites them is not a shared program but a shared diagnosis. They looked at the liberal self — the unencumbered chooser behind the veil of ignorance — and they said, in their different voices, that this self cannot exist, that the political philosophy built upon it is therefore built on a fiction, and that the fiction is not harmless. The person the fiction conjures into being, if pursued long enough by the institutions that rely on it, becomes a real person, stripped and hollow and available for any ideology that offers him a world. That, the communitarians said, is the society we are building. We are building it not out of malice but out of a philosophical default that cannot articulate the value of the thick communal life it is quietly dissolving.

This is the argument we need to reconstruct carefully, because it is the hinge of the whole book. If Arendt and Hoffer are right that loneliness is the raw material of mass movement — and the second volume will argue that they are — then the communitarians are the tradition that explains *why liberal societies produce so much loneliness*. They do not explain it as a moral failing of liberals. They explain it as an unintended consequence of a philosophical self-understanding that has leaked from the seminar rooms into the culture, and from the culture into the institutions, until the dominant vocabulary for describing what a person is has lost the power to describe most of what a person actually is.

***

To understand why communitarianism had to arise when it did, one has to understand what Rawls had done. Before *A Theory of Justice* was published in 1971, political philosophy in the English-speaking world had been, for a generation, in a state of polite suspended animation. The great question — what makes a political order just — was being treated as, at best, a historical question (what did Locke mean? what did Kant mean?) or, at worst, a matter of linguistic analysis (what do we mean when we say *just*?). Rawls did something that almost no one had dared since Sidgwick. He offered a full constructive theory of justice, with intuitions specified, principles derived, and a method — the original position, the veil of ignorance, reflective equilibrium — that allowed the argument to be tested rather than merely admired. The book was an event. It did what only a handful of books in any generation of philosophy do: it reset the conversation.

And because it reset the conversation, every critic who came after had to engage it. One could not reply to Rawls by waving in the general direction of Aristotle. One had to show, in Rawls's own terms, where the argument went wrong. This is the task the communitarians took on. Their books are not populist jeremiads. They are patient, technical, sometimes dry, always respectful engagements with the master text of their generation. MacIntyre's *After Virtue* appeared in 1981, Sandel's *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice* in 1982, Walzer's *Spheres of Justice* in 1983, Taylor's *Sources of the Self* in 1989, Bellah and his collaborators' *Habits of the Heart* in 1985. Five books in eight years, from five authors working largely in isolation from one another, arriving at a convergent verdict. The period is one of the most intellectually fertile in twentieth-century moral and political thought, and it is worth honoring as such before we take up the individual arguments.

***

The most philosophically radical of the five is probably MacIntyre, and we begin with him because his argument operates at a level of depth beneath which the others largely assume. *After Virtue* opens with what has become one of the most arresting images in modern moral philosophy. MacIntyre asks us to imagine a catastrophe — a natural science catastrophe rather than a political one. Imagine that a backlash against the sciences destroys the laboratories, burns the books, executes the scientists. Generations pass. Then a revival begins. People dig up fragments of the old texts. They find a bit of a periodic table here, a half-equation there, a lecture note from a seminar whose context is gone. They start using the words — *mass*, *neutrino*, *gene*, *valence* — but they no longer stand in the web of practices and tests and shared inquiries that gave those words their meaning. They argue furiously with each other using these terms. The arguments cannot be resolved, because the vocabulary has been severed from the form of life that made it intelligible. A sophisticated outside observer would see that what looked like science was a kind of ritual mimicry of science, the shell without the animal.

That, MacIntyre says, is the state of modern moral discourse. We use the old words — *good*, *right*, *justice*, *virtue*, *rights*, *duty* — with every appearance of confidence, but the words are fragments taken from incompatible traditions whose contexts we have forgotten. When a Kantian and a utilitarian and a rights-talk liberal and a residual Aristotelian argue about abortion or war or punishment, they are not disagreeing in the way scientists disagree, on a single question within a shared framework. They are shouting across a rubble-field of broken vocabularies, each invoking the authority of a tradition most of them could not reconstruct if pressed. This is why, MacIntyre says, modern moral argument is "interminable." It is not that we lack good will. It is that the conceptual resources have been shattered, and we are fighting over the pieces.

This is where MacIntyre makes his most radical move, the one that still scandalizes liberal readers a generation later. There is, he argues, no such thing as ethics "in general." Ethics is always ethics *within a tradition of practice* — a tradition of the kind the Aristotelian Nicomachean Ethics took for granted, in which the virtues are defined in relation to goods internal to practices, the practices are situated within a form of communal life, and the form of life is carried by a narrative that each member of the community is in some sense living through. The Enlightenment project, he says, made the audacious attempt to derive an ethics without any of this — to deduce moral principles from the premises of unencumbered rationality alone — and the attempt failed because it could not have succeeded. The Enlightenment bequeathed to us, not a finished ethics, but the debris of one, and our moral life now consists in arranging that debris in patterns we find temporarily pleasing.

The reader new to MacIntyre often thinks at this point that he is reading a reactionary — that the argument is meant to return us to an imagined Aristotelian polis with its hierarchies and exclusions intact. This is a serious misreading. MacIntyre is not a political theorist offering a model. He is a moral philosopher making a diagnostic claim: that we cannot get from where we are to anywhere good unless we first admit that our moral vocabulary is not functioning as we think it is. The positive program he eventually offers — most fully worked out in his later books on dependent rational animals and on the recovery of practices within subcommunities — is monkish rather than imperial. It asks us to find or build small communities within which moral meaning can again be transmitted across generations, and to wait. It is a philosophy of the dark ages, as he himself has more than once acknowledged.

We will come back to the question of whether this is adequate. For now, notice what MacIntyre has added to the conversation that was not there before. He has argued that the modern liberal citizen — the one who announces his "values" as if choosing items from a catalogue — is not exercising autonomy in any deep sense. He is arranging fragments he does not fully understand. And the reason radical ideologies are able to recruit him so effectively is that they offer him something the fragment-vocabulary cannot: a total, coherent, narratively unified account of what he is for. The mass movement is, among other things, a counterfeit tradition. It mimics the structure MacIntyre says every intelligible moral life requires. And in the absence of real traditions to inhabit, the counterfeit will do.

***

Sandel's contribution is more surgical. *Liberalism and the Limits of Justice*, published when its author was twenty-nine, is one of the great feats of close reading in late twentieth-century political philosophy. Sandel does what nobody had quite done before: he takes the metaphysical assumptions lurking underneath Rawls's political theory and pulls them, one by one, into the light.

Rawls had tried to sidestep metaphysics. His theory of justice, he insisted, was "political, not metaphysical." The veil of ignorance was a device of representation; the original position was a thought experiment, not a description of any actual self. But Sandel shows, patiently and relentlessly, that the device cannot do the work Rawls asks of it unless some picture of the self is already in place. To choose principles of justice from behind the veil — to bracket one's particular commitments, relationships, religious convictions, attachments — the self doing the choosing must be the kind of self for which those commitments are, in principle, separable. The self must be, as Sandel puts it in the book's most quoted phrase, "prior to its ends." It must be a bare unity of will, able to step back from everything that is normally called its "identity" and evaluate those attachments as if from outside.

Sandel's question is whether any such self is coherent. On one hand, the Rawlsian self must be prior to its ends, or else the whole contractualist apparatus collapses: if the self is constituted by its particular attachments, then it cannot meaningfully choose principles of justice while bracketing them. On the other hand, the self must be the *author* of its ends, the agent whose autonomous choice is the moral source of those ends' authority — this is the Kantian inheritance Rawls explicitly honors. But if the self is prior to its ends in the strong sense the argument requires, it cannot also be the author of them, because a self stripped of all particulars is not an author of anything. It is a point, a bare locus of consent, with no resources to generate any particular choice rather than another. And if the self is the author of its ends in the robust sense Kantian autonomy requires, then the ends it authors must belong to it, must be constitutive of its moral personality in a way that makes their bracketing incoherent. The deontological self must be both prior to its ends and the author of its ends, and it cannot be both.

This is, we think, one of the great philosophical moves of the late twentieth century, and it has aged well. Sandel is not saying that liberalism is wrong to prize autonomy. He is saying that the particular conception of autonomy Rawls needs — autonomy as the bare chooser, untouched by attachments — is incoherent. Any real human autonomy is exercised from within a thicket of constitutive commitments. A person deliberating about whether to keep a promise to his mother is not bracketing his relationship with his mother in order to evaluate the promise "from nowhere"; he is deliberating as the person whose mother that is. A citizen deliberating about justice in his country is not bracketing his citizenship; he is deliberating as a citizen of that country, with its particular history, obligations, shames, and loves. To bracket these is not to reach a purer moral standpoint. It is to reach no standpoint at all.

Sandel's later work extends this founding insight into a sustained critique of what he calls the "procedural republic" — the polity that has organized itself around the liberal ideal of bracketing substantive questions about the good, and that finds itself, having so organized itself, unable to generate the civic ties, loyalties, and shared purposes that a democracy needs to sustain itself. *Democracy's Discontent* traces the American version of this story: how a republic founded on a civic-republican tradition of self-government has, over a century and a half, reorganized itself around procedural neutrality and now discovers that the procedural neutrality cannot answer the questions its citizens are actually asking. *The Tyranny of Merit* turns the same argument on the meritocratic ideology that has become the procedural republic's implicit theodicy — the story that tells the winners they deserve their winnings and, more corrosively, tells the losers they deserve their losses. We will come back to Sandel's merit argument in Volume II, where it does important diagnostic work. For now it is enough to note that Sandel has been, for forty years, the most consistent and philosophically disciplined voice arguing that the liberal settlement, taken as a total account of political life, is inadequate to the political life of the liberal societies that have tried to live by it.

***

If MacIntyre is the most radical and Sandel the most surgical, Charles Taylor is the most philosophically capacious. Taylor's achievement is hard to summarize because it is vast. *Sources of the Self* (1989) is nearly six hundred pages of intellectual history tracing the construction of the modern self through Augustine, Montaigne, Descartes, Locke, the Romantics, and the twentieth-century critics — a book which, when one finally closes it, has changed the way one reads almost everything else. *The Ethics of Authenticity* is a short, almost popular book that distills one strand of the longer argument into a meditation on the modern ideal of being true to oneself, its real moral weight, and the ways in which it has been degraded into self-expression severed from any horizon of significance beyond the self. *A Secular Age* (2007) is the long book about the modern religious condition — about what it means that we now inhabit, as Taylor puts it, an "immanent frame" in which transcendence is no longer the default, and what it costs and offers the self who lives within it.

The point of reading Taylor, for our purposes, is to absorb a single liberating observation: the modern self is *a historical achievement*. It is not the universal human condition — not what humans always were, released from ignorance, when the fog of superstition finally cleared. It is a specific configuration of self-understanding, painstakingly assembled over five centuries of Western moral and religious and intellectual life, and it carries within it both real goods we should not want to surrender and real pathologies that its architects did not foresee. Understanding how this self was built — the particular inwardness of Augustine, the disengaged reason of Descartes, the affirmation of ordinary life in the Reformers, the expressive self of Rousseau and the Romantics, the buffered self of the modern secular — is prerequisite to understanding its pathologies. We cannot diagnose what we cannot see, and for most moderns the modern self is invisible precisely because we are inside it.

Taylor's coinage of the "buffered self" is especially useful for our argument. The premodern self, he says, was *porous*. Its boundary was not fixed; it could be penetrated by spirits, by gods, by the force of a relic, by the enchantment of a place, by the ancestral dead. To be a person was to be continuous in certain ways with the world around you, including its unseen dimensions. The modern self, by contrast, is *buffered*. It has a sharp boundary; meaning arises inside the boundary and is a product of the self's own reflective activity; the world outside is mute matter, available for manipulation. The buffered self is, among other things, the precondition of modern freedom. It is what allows you to walk through an enchanted landscape unaffected. It is also, Taylor notes, the condition of a specific kind of loneliness — a sense of being sealed off, of having to generate meaning from within because the outside will not supply it — that is a distinctive modern ache. The "nova effect," his name for the explosion of plural spiritual options within the immanent frame, is one of his names for the disorientation that follows when the sources of meaning multiply past the point where any of them can be firmly inhabited.

This is not a sermon against the modern self. Taylor is a Catholic, and he is not above making spiritual arguments, but *A Secular Age* is resolutely philosophical and descriptive, and it takes pains to honor the moral gains of the modern condition: the affirmation of ordinary life, the end of theologically sanctioned cruelty, the seriousness with which modernity takes the inner life of the individual. The book is not a case for going back. It is a case for knowing where we are. The communitarian pay-off of Taylor's work, for our purposes, is this: the self that shows up in Rawls's original position is not a neutral abstraction. It is a particular historical type, the buffered self at a late stage, taking itself to be the universal form of rational agency because it has forgotten how it came to be. A political philosophy built on this self will, without intending to, push the institutions that surround it in the direction of the self's own assumptions — toward contract rather than covenant, toward choice rather than inheritance, toward procedure rather than substance — and the institutions so pushed will gradually cease to form any other kind of self. This is not a conspiracy. It is a feedback loop, and its end state is the hollowed-out individual our book is trying to understand.

***

Walzer's *Spheres of Justice* comes at the problem from a different angle, and it is important to include him because he makes a move the more metaphysical communitarians do not. Walzer is, by temperament and politics, a social democrat of the mid-twentieth-century variety, and his communitarianism is the least nostalgic in the tradition. His argument is not that the self is constituted by a tradition. His argument is about *goods* — the things we distribute — and about the meanings those goods have within particular communities.

Rawls had tried to derive principles of just distribution from a single standpoint, abstracted from any particular community. Walzer asks whether this is even the right ambition. Goods, he observes, are not fungible units of welfare; they have meanings, and the meanings are social. What counts as a fair distribution of medicine is different in kind from what counts as a fair distribution of political office, and both are different from what counts as a fair distribution of love, or honor, or grace, or a place in a college class. Each of these spheres has its own internal logic, which arises from the way the good in question is understood within the community that distributes it. Justice, Walzer says, is "complex" — a matter of keeping each sphere in its proper domain, preventing the kinds of good that should dominate only within their own sphere from colonizing others. (The dominance of money over medicine, or of pedigree over political office, is a characteristic form of injustice precisely because it violates spheres rather than internal rules.)

The deep point, for us, is the argument against what Rawls and his tradition had tried to do. There is no "view from nowhere" in political philosophy, Walzer says. Justice is not a theorem derivable from principles outside history. It is a practice worked out by particular peoples with particular histories of struggle over particular goods, and the philosopher's job is not to replace that practice with a theorem but to participate in it as a thoughtful citizen — to reason about the meanings of the goods his community is fighting over, to draw out the implications of those meanings for distribution, and to submit his reasoning to the same public deliberation as anyone else's. This is a communitarian claim about the method of political philosophy itself, and it is one of the most widely accepted of the communitarian positions even among readers who have no other communitarian sympathies. The view from nowhere, it turns out, was always a view from somewhere that had forgotten it was somewhere.

***

Etzioni's project is the most practical of the five, which is why it is both the most accessible and, in a certain way, the most constrained. Amitai Etzioni — a sociologist rather than a philosopher, and a veteran of Israeli and American public life — did not try to win the argument with Rawls in metaphysics. He tried to build a movement. *The Spirit of Community* (1993) and the associated platform of what he called "responsive communitarianism" set out a middle way between the libertarian and the authoritarian: rights without responsibilities are adolescent, responsibilities without rights are authoritarian, and a decent public philosophy has to affirm both in the same breath. The communitarian network he organized produced a platform in 1991, signed by an unlikely cross-ideological roster, arguing for specific policy positions — on family, on schools, on public safety, on campaign finance — that would restore the balance between the autonomy of the individual and the order of the community.

Etzioni is not as philosophically powerful as Sandel or Taylor, and he knew it. What he offered instead was a bridge between the theoretical critique and the practical life of a liberal democracy — a set of positions a working politician could plausibly run on, a set of concerns a local school board could plausibly address, a set of sensibilities that could organize a PTA meeting or a zoning hearing as easily as a philosophy seminar. The responsive communitarian movement made real headway in the 1990s, with echoes in the Clinton-era "third way" and in some of the Blair-era British reformism, before it stalled. The reasons it stalled are instructive and we will return to them at the end of this chapter, but it is worth noting that Etzioni made a serious attempt to turn the communitarian diagnosis into a livable civic practice, and the attempt has not been surpassed in the decades since.

***

The sixth figure, or rather the sixth voice — since the book is collectively authored — is *Habits of the Heart*, the 1985 study by Robert Bellah, Richard Madsen, William Sullivan, Ann Swidler, and Steven Tipton. If MacIntyre and Taylor give us the philosophical and historical bones of the communitarian argument, *Habits of the Heart* gives it the empirical flesh. The book is an interview study — two hundred middle-class Americans talking at length about love, work, community, and the good life — and its great achievement is to listen closely enough to hear the gap between what its subjects say and what they do.

The Americans in the study live, on the whole, in dense webs of commitment. They are embedded in marriages, churches, neighborhoods, civic organizations, voluntary associations. Their lives are structured by obligations they did not choose and could not easily shed without becoming unrecognizable to themselves. But when the authors ask them to describe what they are doing, the vocabulary available to them is almost entirely the vocabulary of expressive individualism. They describe their marriages as opportunities for personal growth. They describe their churches as places where they are supported in their individual spiritual journeys. They describe their civic commitments in terms of the satisfaction they derive from them. The *language* is the language of the unencumbered self, choosing values. The *life* is the life of the encumbered self, held together by ties the speaker cannot articulate.

Bellah's most famous example has entered the English language: "Sheilaism." A young nurse named Sheila, asked about her religious beliefs, describes her faith as a private, personal system — "my own Sheilaism," she calls it — with a god who wants her to love herself, be gentle with herself, pursue her own fulfillment. She is not alienated; she is a kind and generous person. She is not unreligious; she is sincere. But she has no vocabulary for the tradition that would connect her inner life to any community of others, no practice that would hold her to any standard outside her own sense of flourishing, no narrative that would link her life to anyone else's. If asked to articulate what she believes, all she can do is point back at herself.

Bellah's point is not to mock Sheila. It is to observe that the language available to her has no other shape. American public vocabulary, in the years the study was conducted, had been so thoroughly colonized by the idioms of expressive individualism that even the most community-minded person had to reach for an individualist phrasing to describe what she was doing. The ties were still there — marriages, churches, kids, neighbors, work — but the ability to *say* what those ties meant, to name the substance of the life they supported, had been largely lost. And once a practice cannot be named, it cannot be transmitted; and once it cannot be transmitted, the next generation may inherit the practice only as a weakened residue, or not inherit it at all.

*Habits of the Heart* is the book that shows, in the voices of ordinary Americans, what MacIntyre and Taylor are talking about in the abstract. It is also, for that reason, the most politically portable of the communitarian texts. You can hand a copy to a reader who does not know what *telos* means and has no interest in finding out, and the reader will recognize himself and his neighbors in the pages. This recognition is a necessary condition for the communitarian argument to do any practical work in the culture, and Bellah and his collaborators earned it.

***

What, then, is the common core? If one stands back from these five or six bodies of work, what is the communitarian argument in its strongest form?

It is not, despite what its enemies sometimes say, an argument against individualism as such. Every one of these writers affirms the moral seriousness of the individual — MacIntyre in his insistence on the rational agency involved in inhabiting a tradition, Sandel in his insistence that the self must be the author of its ends in some real sense, Taylor in his sustained admiration for the modern ideal of authenticity, Walzer in his social-democratic commitment to the equal standing of each citizen, Etzioni in his explicit defense of rights as the ground on which responsibilities are to be exercised, Bellah in his careful respect for each interviewee's account of his own life. The communitarians are not anti-individualist. They are anti-*atomist*. The distinction matters, and it is the distinction the whole tradition is trying to press into the American conversation.

The argument, stated compactly, runs in three parts. First, the individual conceived as *prior to* his community is a fiction — not a useful simplification, not a methodological starting point, but a picture so distorted of what a human being is that building a political philosophy on it guarantees that the philosophy will miss most of what political life is about. Second, the institutions a liberal order depends on — family, church, school, neighborhood, profession, guild, association — are the institutions that liberal rhetoric systematically devalues by treating them as mere voluntary associations of individuals, interchangeable in principle with any other set of preferences. Third, the person deprived of these institutions — the unencumbered self come to life, the Sheila without even the weakened inherited practice — is not a more autonomous person; he is exactly the person most susceptible to mass ideology, because the hunger his dissolved ties have left behind is a hunger that no merely personal ethics can satisfy.

This third point is the one that matters for our book. It is the bridge to Volume II. Arendt and Hoffer, writing in 1951, had noticed that mass movements recruit from among the atomized — from people stripped of family, class, craft, neighborhood, faith. They left the question of what had done the stripping largely unanswered. The communitarians, three decades later and working on a different problem, produced the answer. The stripping is not something that happens to a liberal society from outside. It is something the liberal society does to itself, patiently and over generations, by treating the institutions that form its citizens as if those institutions were preferences and not preconditions. This is not a moral failure. The liberals are not the villains of the story. It is a structural feature of the philosophical self-understanding the liberal tradition has bequeathed. If you tell a society for long enough that the individual is prior to his attachments, eventually the attachments will fray, because a society's self-understanding is not a harmless set of private beliefs; it is the operating system of its institutions. And when the attachments have frayed past a certain point, the lonely and unencumbered self produced by the fraying becomes available to any movement willing to offer him, however falsely, a world.

Put this way, the communitarian argument is not a nostalgic plea for a vanished past. It is a causal hypothesis about modern politics. It says that the characteristic pathologies of late liberal societies — the loneliness, the rage, the susceptibility to conspiracy, the cult-like partisanship, the appetite for strongmen — are not deviations from liberal modernity but fruits of it, produced by a philosophy that cannot see or say what it is doing to the institutions that used to form its citizens. This is a claim our book largely accepts, and we will defend that acceptance in the chapters to come.

***

Before we name the limits of the communitarian position, we want to pause and say clearly that these are our intellectual teachers. The book you are reading could not have been written without them. The single most important conceptual move in our whole argument — that atomization and mass movement are two faces of a single phenomenon, and that the lonely self is the raw material of the mob — is a move Arendt and Hoffer set up and the communitarians supplied the explanatory vocabulary for. MacIntyre taught us to see that a moral vocabulary can be a ruin walked through by people who do not realize it is a ruin. Sandel taught us to see that the self doing the choosing in liberal theory is not a real self at all, and that the philosophical incoherence is not a harmless stipulation. Taylor taught us that the self we call modern is a historical achievement that can be understood and therefore, in principle, criticized. Walzer taught us that the view from nowhere is a metaphor for a view from somewhere that has forgotten where it is. Bellah and his collaborators taught us to listen for the gap between a person's language and his life, and to recognize the gap as a cultural fact and not a personal failing. Etzioni taught us that a diagnosis without a livable practice is only half a thing. We are, in an honest sense, students of this tradition, and this chapter is our attempt to repay some of what we owe it.

And yet we think the tradition has limits, and we owe our readers, and our teachers, an honest statement of what those limits are.

The first limit is the one that has always worried liberal critics of communitarianism, and it is not a worry that can be waved away by pointing to the communitarians' own moderation. The worry is this: once you have said that the self is constituted by its community, you have handed an enormous amount of moral authority to whoever happens to speak for the community. And communities do not always speak in the voices we might wish. The Jim Crow South was a thick community. So were the Soviet collectives, the German towns of the 1930s, the religious congregations that covered for abusive clergy, the tight-knit neighborhoods that ran out families of the wrong color or faith. A political philosophy that loses the capacity to criticize communities from outside has no resources to say what was wrong in any of these cases. It must, in the end, appeal to something — a principle, a right, a standard — that the community itself did not generate, and to that extent it owes a debt to the liberalism it set out to criticize. The best of the communitarians know this. Walzer is explicit about it; Sandel has become more explicit about it in recent decades; Taylor has always been; even MacIntyre, in his later work, has taken pains to distinguish genuine traditions of rational inquiry from mere identity-forming groupings. But the *tendency* of the communitarian argument, if pushed one step too far, is to dissolve the standpoint from which communities themselves can be judged, and that tendency has to be resisted. It has to be resisted because the twentieth century resisted it, at enormous cost, and the memory of what thick communities have done to their dissenters, their heretics, their women, their minorities, and their strangers is not the fabrication of hysterical liberals. It is the history.

The second limit is closely related and more prosaic. The communitarians, for all their diagnostic brilliance, have produced remarkably little in the way of policy. Read the five writers we have surveyed and you will find almost no programmatic detail about what a communitarian government would actually do. Etzioni is the exception, which is precisely why his movement stalled where it did: the moment responsive communitarianism proposed concrete policies, it split along the same lines as every other political movement, and its cross-ideological coalition evaporated. The philosophers stayed at the level of sensibility because sensibilities are what the philosophers could defend. Sensibilities are not nothing; a culture's sensibility is the soil in which its policies grow, and a shift in communitarian sensibility may be more consequential in the long run than any particular policy win. But a reader of this chapter who asks what the communitarian response to, say, family-formation collapse or educational credentialism or pharmaceutical monopoly would actually be will find, in most of the texts, a gesture rather than a program. This is a real cost, and we will try in later volumes, modestly, to do better.

The third limit is the deepest. The communitarian argument, stated carefully, is that communities constitute selves. But not every community is a good community, and the argument, as stated, gives us no clean way to tell the difference. Some communities form citizens; others form cult members. Some traditions cultivate judgment; others extinguish it. Some associations rebuild the individual; others consume him. The communitarians know this and usually gesture in the direction of the distinction — MacIntyre by distinguishing genuine "practices" from bureaucratic or exploitative mimicries of them, Taylor by distinguishing horizons of significance worth inhabiting from those that are not, Walzer by taking the internal meanings of goods as critical standards rather than brute facts — but the distinction remains undertheorized in the tradition as a whole. What the communitarians have given us is a brilliant negative argument: the unencumbered self is a fiction, and the fiction is poisonous. What they have not given us is a robust positive criterion for which communities deserve our loyalty and which do not. A book that is going to distinguish the citizen's association from the radical cult — which is, ultimately, what our book is trying to do — needs more than the communitarian tradition alone provides. It needs, we will argue, the republican vocabulary of civic virtue and non-domination and law, and the liberal vocabulary of rights and dissent and open inquiry, to supplement the communitarian insight about the self. That is the argument the rest of Volume I is going to make.

***

So we accept most of the communitarian diagnosis. We think Sandel is right that the unencumbered self is philosophically incoherent. We think Taylor is right that the modern self is a historical achievement with built-in pathologies as well as goods. We think MacIntyre is right that the modern moral vocabulary is a ruin, and that the mass movement exploits the ruin by offering a counterfeit tradition. We think Walzer is right that the view from nowhere is the nowhere-view of a somewhere that has forgotten itself. We think Bellah is right that the gap between American language and American life is an open wound the culture has not yet learned to dress. These are not concessions; they are endorsements. They shape everything that follows in this book.

But we do not think the diagnosis licenses the next step that some readers of these writers, and some intellectual descendants of this tradition, have been tempted to take. The next step is to say: if liberalism is built on an incoherent self, and if the institutions it produces hollow out the citizens it depends on, then the solution is to abandon liberalism and return to a world of thick, authoritative communities with the power to form their members unimpeded. That step — which is the step the next chapter's figures have taken, each in his own way — we refuse. We refuse it for the reasons the communitarians themselves, at their best, know. We refuse it because the thick communities of history were thick because they were narrow, and the narrowness was paid for by people whose names we now know and whose exclusion we can no longer pretend was a reasonable price. We refuse it because the modern individual, for all his pathologies, is a moral achievement our ancestors would have called a miracle, and no serious philosophy of the citizen can ask him to give up the autonomy that makes his consent to any community meaningful in the first place. We refuse it because we have read the century the communitarians wrote against, and we have read the histories the post-liberals appear willing to elide, and we know what happens when a sophisticated intellectual tradition decides that the modern respect for the individual was a mistake.

The task, as we understand it, is harder than either camp has made it. The task is not to return to a world of thick communities without individual rights, and it is not to persist in a world of individual rights without any communities thick enough to form the person who holds them. The task is to hold both — to build a public philosophy and a set of institutional practices in which the individual is respected as an end in himself *and* formed by the associations in which his character actually takes shape. That is the synthesis the final chapter of this volume is going to argue for. It is what Richard Dagger, working quietly for decades alongside the more famous figures of this chapter, called *republican liberalism*, and what Sandel himself, late in his career, has increasingly defended under a different name. It is what, we think, the American founding at its best understood, and what the twentieth century, at its worst, forgot.

For now, though, the argument presses on. Having let the communitarians make their case, we have to reckon with the figures who have taken a further step — the post-liberals, who say the problem is not that liberal philosophy has a blind spot but that liberalism is itself the disease. That is the next chapter, and it is the sharpest test our synthesis will face.
