# Chapter 7
## Republican Liberalism: A Usable Answer

We began this volume with the oldest question in political philosophy, the question that every tradition we have walked through is an attempt to answer. What does a human being owe the political community, and what does the community owe the human being? We noted then that the answer was not obvious, had never been obvious, and that the Western tradition had four serious, incompatible candidates on offer. We have now taken each of those candidates in turn. We have listened to the civic republicans from Aristotle to Pettit; we have listened to the classical liberals from Locke to Fukuyama; we have listened to the communitarians from Sandel to Taylor; we have listened to the post-liberals from Deneen to Vermeule. We have watched the Federalist and the Anti-Federalist make the same argument in American accents, Tocqueville mark what was extraordinary about the country that emerged, and Lincoln and Dewey try, in their different centuries, to save it.

We have put off saying what we think. It is time.

Our thesis is one sentence long. *The citizen is an individual who belongs.*

We want to be understood precisely. This is not a compromise between liberalism and republicanism. It is not a both-sides gesture of the kind that editorial writers perform when they do not want to be accused of a view. It is not a middle muddle, arrived at by splitting the difference between traditions and hoping no one notices the incoherence. It is the thesis that the deepest versions of the two traditions, read honestly, converge. Neither is complete without the other. Classical liberalism, pursued to its conclusion, requires the civic substrate it has been silently consuming; civic republicanism, pursued to its conclusion, requires the rights-bearing individual whose conscience it has sometimes been tempted to override. A citizen who is only an individual is a consumer with a ballot. A citizen who only belongs is a member of a tribe. The person we have in mind, the person the preceding chapters have been circling, is both at once.

We will make the argument by laying out four commitments, each of which the remaining two volumes will defend in practice. We want the reader to have them in view before we go any further.

***

The first commitment is that the person is prior to the state, but not prior to the community.

Locke was right about the first half. The state is a trust, held from free persons, who retain their rights whether or not the state chooses to recognize them. This was a moral discovery of the first rank, and every decent political order since the seventeenth century rests on it. A polity that forgets it ends at the re-education camp. When we say, as we do throughout this book, that we are not writing against the liberal revolution but writing to complete it, this is one of the things we mean. A person does not owe her existence to the polity. The polity does not own her. If it tries to, she is within her rights to resist it, and her resistance is not sedition but fidelity to a deeper law.

But Locke was wrong about the second half. He imagined the free persons as pre-social, as choosers surveying a landscape of possible associations from a state of nature and rationally selecting among them. The philosophical construction is useful as a thought experiment and disastrous as an anthropology. Real persons are never pre-social. They are born into families they did not choose, languages they did not invent, histories they did not author, and moral vocabularies that were given to them before they were old enough to refuse the gift. They become persons through these memberships. They learn to reason by being taught to reason; they learn to love by being loved; they learn what counts as a life worth living by watching people who are already living, and failing to live, such a life. The communitarian critics were right about this, and the classical liberals have still not fully absorbed it. The self that is capable of choosing is not found; it is grown.

What does this mean politically? It means that the state may not treat persons as its property, but neither may it treat them as if their capacities for agency, reason, and choice came from nowhere. The person whose rights the state is bound to respect is a person who has been formed — by family, by neighborhood, by school, by congregation, by workplace, by voluntary association, by shared cultural inheritance — and who will continue to require formation if she is to retain the capacities that make her rights meaningful. A polity that protects a person's right to think but is indifferent to whether she has the conditions to learn how to think is a polity that will eventually be protecting a right that no one can any longer exercise. Rights without the formative substrate of rights are letters of credit drawn on a bank that has closed.

The citizen, then, is someone whose personhood is prior to the state in the moral sense that classical liberals have always insisted on, and who is at the same time a creature of a community in the sociological sense that communitarians have always insisted on. The two are not in contradiction. They are two angles on the same fact. The human being is a being who must be formed in order to become free — and, once free, cannot be reduced to the forming conditions without being degraded. This is not a paradox. It is the ordinary structure of any formed life. A pianist is formed by her teachers and is not, for that reason, the property of her teachers. A scholar is formed by her tradition and is not, for that reason, barred from correcting it. A citizen is formed by her polity and is not, for that reason, available to be conscripted into its designs.

***

The second commitment concerns liberty itself. The classical liberal tradition, at its sharpest in Berlin, made a distinction between negative and positive liberty — between freedom *from* interference and freedom *for* some substantive purpose — and argued, with the twentieth century's cautionary tales in view, that the first was the only version a decent politics could aim at. The republican tradition, at its sharpest in Pettit, has since argued that this dichotomy misses the phenomenon that republicans have always cared about most. There is a third thing: liberty as *non-domination*. To be free, on this account, is not merely to be uninterfered with. It is to be not subject to the arbitrary will of another.

The example Pettit likes to use is the kind master. A slave whose master has decided, for now, not to beat her is not interfered with. If interference is all we care about, she is free. But she is not free, and we all know she is not free, because she lives at her master's pleasure. The master could change his mind tomorrow. The very structure of her life is that her well-being depends on someone else's discretion. The republican claim is that this structure is itself the unfreedom, independently of whether the master happens to be exercising it harshly in any given week. The liberty republicans care about is not the absence of actual interference. It is the absence of the *standing capacity* of another to interfere at will.

Once you have the concept you see it everywhere. The worker whose employer will not fire her this month but could, for any reason or none, fire her next month, is not living at her own pleasure; she is living at his. The tenant whose landlord has raised the rent by ten percent and could raise it by a hundred is not negotiating, she is being permitted. The citizen whose rights exist because the regime currently finds it convenient to let them exist is not a rights-holder but a beneficiary. Each of these persons, in the strict Berlinian sense, may be uninterfered with at the moment. Each is also, in the older sense that republicans have preserved, unfree.

We take both halves of this argument seriously, and we build on both. The classical liberal guarantees — freedom of speech, freedom of conscience, property, due process, equal standing before the law — are protections against interference, and they are indispensable. Any regime that hopes to produce citizens has to secure them. But these guarantees do not, by themselves, protect against domination. A society in which the law on the books respects every right but in which an employer can fire you for your politics, a platform can deplatform you for its mood, a prosecutor can ruin you with the process itself, an administrative agency can extract your compliance through the cost of noncompliance — such a society has not achieved freedom simply because, on any given day, most of these powers are held in reserve. The republican supplement is precisely the set of institutions and habits that prevent the arbitrary hand from falling in the first place: the dispersion of power across branches and levels of government, the independence of associations from both state and market, the practices of deliberative self-government that give ordinary persons a voice in the conditions that bind them, the culture of civic equality that makes it unthinkable to treat a fellow citizen as a subject.

A usable account of freedom, we are saying, needs both. Negative liberty without the republican supplement becomes the freedom of the formally uninterfered-with to be informally dominated. Republican liberty without the classical liberal guarantees becomes the freedom of the self-governing majority to override the conscience of the minority in the name of virtue. Either, alone, is a half-answer. Together they are close to a whole one. The citizen we are describing is a person whose rights protect her from interference and whose polity, through its institutions and its culture, protects her from domination. Both jobs have to be done. A politics that specializes in one and neglects the other will fail at both eventually, because the neglected half will be invaded by the very forces the attended half was trying to hold back.

***

The third commitment is where we have to say the hardest thing. It is the book's answer to the quarrel between Rawls and Sandel, which is to say to the central philosophical argument of the last half-century of Anglophone political theory.

The polity should be neutral about ways of life, but it should *not* be neutral about the conditions that make persons capable of choosing a way of life.

Let us unfold this carefully, because it is easy to hear the first clause and assume the second and easy to hear the second and lose the first. The liberal tradition is right that the state must not impose a particular conception of the good on its citizens. The state that decides for its citizens which religion to hold, which kind of family to form, which aesthetic and moral ideals to pursue — this state is a tyranny, regardless of whether the conception of the good it is imposing happens to be correct. The dignity of a person requires the latitude to work out for herself, in the company of those she loves and trusts, the question of how to live. The state should treat her as a being entitled to pursue her own answer to that question. Classical liberalism is not wrong about this. It is the deepest moral insight the modern political tradition has produced.

But the polity — and we mean here not only the state, but the whole texture of public life, the schools and workplaces and associations and cultural institutions that together compose a people's common world — cannot be indifferent to whether its members are *capable* of such pursuit. The formation of persons capable of free and reasoned choice is not a private hobby. It is a common good, and the communities and institutions that accomplish it are performing a public function regardless of whether they are explicitly public bodies. A child who has not been taught to read cannot choose her own way of life in any meaningful sense, because she cannot reach the materials out of which a way of life is built. An adult who has never watched anyone deliberate with her about something that mattered cannot deliberate well herself, because deliberation is a practice learned by apprenticeship. A person who has never belonged to an association in which she was accountable to others and in which others were accountable to her has not acquired the disposition that membership in any voluntary community requires.

We are saying, in the old idiom, that liberty has conditions. We are saying, in a more contemporary idiom, that *capability* is prior to *choice* in the order of political concern. We are saying, in the idiom of this book, that a polity has to attend to the formation of its members, not because it has decided on their behalf what kind of life they should live, but because it has not, and because only a being so formed is in a position to answer the question for herself.

The metaphor that comes naturally here is the farmer's. A farmer who refuses to cultivate his fields on the ground that he does not want to impose wheat on his soil has not avoided imposition; he has merely imposed neglect, and his fields will grow weeds and nothing else. A polity that refuses to attend to the formation of citizens on the ground that it does not want to impose a conception of the good has made the same mistake. The fields do not stop growing. The question is only what they grow. A polity that lets its schools decay, its associations wither, its public culture be colonized by the shallowest forms of commerce, its deliberative institutions turn into theater — such a polity has not preserved neutrality. It has abdicated. And what grows in the abandoned ground is not the free and reasoned pursuit of a chosen life. It is the loneliness and susceptibility Arendt and Hoffer described, which is the raw material of the tribe.

So we commit to a view that the Rawlsian will find too thick and the Sandelian will find too thin. The state, on most questions of how to live, should be neutral. It should not tell its citizens which god to worship or which art to love or which vocation to choose. But the polity as a whole, and the state within its proper sphere, should be deeply engaged in the question of whether citizens have what they need to choose well. Education, a public culture of reasoned argument, the preservation of associational life, the access to meaningful work, the preservation of spaces in which ordinary persons can practice self-government at the scales at which they live — these are the material of freedom, not optional decorations added after the freedom has been secured. Classical liberalism has always depended on these goods. It has usually been better at consuming them than at replenishing them. We are saying: name the dependency. Fund the dependency. Treat the dependency as what it is, which is a public matter in the strict sense.

***

The fourth commitment is the one we have been hinting at for six chapters and it is the one most often misheard. We mean to say something specific about virtue.

Virtue has come down to us, in American public life, as a deeply contaminated word. It connotes moralism, censoriousness, sexual hypocrisy, and the particular voice of the person who tells other people how to live. This is not what the word meant in the republican tradition, and it is not what we mean by it here. Virtue, in the older sense the tradition preserves, is the *disposition to act well in the role one is occupying*. It is not a set of private feelings about the good. It is not a bedroom inspectorate. It is not a uniform to be enforced on the neighbors. It is a trained capacity for doing a particular kind of thing well, where the kind of thing is given by the role.

A doctor has virtues of a doctor. They are not the same as the virtues of a soldier, or a parent, or a juror, or a legislator. A juror who cannot subordinate her private sympathies to the requirements of the role is not exhibiting authenticity; she is failing to be a juror. A legislator who treats her seat as a platform for personal brand-building rather than as a constraint on how she may act is not expressing her true self; she is cheating on the institution she swore to serve. A doctor who prescribes what the patient asks for rather than what the patient needs is not respecting autonomy; she is abandoning her craft. In each case there is a role with internal standards, a practice that has been developed over generations of people who have done the work well and poorly and learned from both, and the virtue is the acquired disposition to meet those standards.

This is the tradition Alasdair MacIntyre tried to recover when he wrote *After Virtue*, and it is the tradition Yuval Levin is writing in when he asks, of every institution, whether it is still a *mold* that forms the character of those who pass through it or whether it has become a *platform* on which they perform. We will argue at length in Volume III that institutional renewal, properly understood, is virtue ethics extended to modern life. We will argue that the doctor who has let the practice of medicine form her, the teacher who has let the practice of teaching form him, the reporter who has let the ethics of reporting form her, the legislator who has let the constraints of office form her — each of these is doing something more important than it looks. Each is keeping alive a practice whose death would mean the loss of a particular kind of good thing that a society cannot produce by any other means.

None of this is moralism. No one in the tradition we are drawing on is proposing that the state jail bad doctors, shun rude legislators, or publish a list of citizens who have fallen short. The practice of virtue is internal to the role and is maintained by the community of other practitioners and by the self-respect of the person occupying the role. That is the whole point. What the republican tradition adds to the picture is that one of the roles everyone occupies — whether they have noticed or not — is the role of *citizen*, and that this role, like any other, has standards internal to it, which a person either meets or does not. You cannot be a good citizen merely by feeling that you are one. You can be a good citizen only by doing the things the role requires: attending to public matters, showing up, reasoning honestly with people who disagree with you, keeping your promises to institutions that are not under your control, recognizing that others in the polity are your equals whether you like them or not. These are not private virtues. They are the work of the role. A citizenry that has lost them has not lost its moral fiber in some metaphorical sense; it has lost a specific practical capacity on which the polity depends.

***

With the four commitments in view, let us say directly where this puts us in the arguments of the preceding chapters.

To the post-liberals we say the following. Yes, modern liberal societies have hollowed out the intermediate institutions — the families, congregations, neighborhoods, unions, lodges, clubs, local governments, and voluntary associations that used to stand between the individual and the nation-state. Yes, this hollowing has produced a characteristic modern pathology of lonely, susceptible persons and mass movements that feed on them. Yes, the bracketing of the good, pressed far enough, has cost us a shared moral vocabulary without which citizens cannot even tell each other what they want. On all of this, you are correct, and the diagnosis is worth taking seriously.

But the prescription is the catastrophe. The response to hollowed intermediate institutions is not to replace the liberal rights with a substantive regime that will fill the hollow with the preferred content of whoever has won the argument. It is to recover the civic-republican supplement that the American order at its best had, has partly lost, and can still, with work, rebuild. Rights without virtue is the procedural republic Sandel diagnosed: a machine for managing conflicts among strangers with no common life. Virtue without rights, however, is the illiberal republic, and history has been unkind to it in every century in which it has been tried. It ends, when it ends badly, in the persecution of the person whose conscience the majority has decided not to respect. It ends, when it ends ordinarily, in the slow suffocation of the minority, the dissenter, the oddball, and the reformer — the very figures without which a polity goes stupid and then corrupt. We do not want this. No one should want it. A usable answer preserves the rights *and* rebuilds the virtues. Both are required; neither is optional.

To the communitarians we say the following. Yes, the self is encumbered. Yes, the liberal picture of the chooser behind the veil is a philosophical abstraction that has never corresponded to any actually existing person. Yes, persons who are encouraged to think of themselves as uniquely self-authored become persons who find themselves unable to explain their lives to themselves or anyone else. You are right about this, and the post-Rawlsian generation in liberal theory is right to have taken the criticism on board.

But the encumbered self has also, historically, been a woman barred from the career her mind equipped her for, a gay man constrained to simulate a life he did not have, a black citizen in Jim Crow who was told by every institution around him that his place was beneath. The encumbrances from which liberal rights have rescued such persons were not incidental features of their communities; in many cases they were the defining features. To celebrate encumbrance as such, to treat the thickness of inherited community as an unqualified good, is to abandon the persons for whom thickness has been a cell. The answer cannot be to choose between encumbered community and individual rights. It has to be to insist on thick community *and* on the individual's rights to exit, criticize, and reform the community. The greatest moments of American civic-republican life, the ones we most want to recover, were not communities of conformity. They were the abolitionist meetings and the labor halls and the civil rights churches and the suffragist parlors — communities of *dissent* against the wider society, in which the solidarity of the members was precisely what gave each of them the standing to speak back to the country. That is the model. The thick community of equals, bound together by a common project and by mutual respect, in which the individual is not diminished by membership but enlarged, and in which membership itself is continuously open to argument. Anything less is either loneliness or conformity, and we do not want either.

To the classical liberals, including the ones we most admire, we say the following. Yes, the individual is an end in herself. Yes, the political community exists for its members and not the other way around. Yes, to treat any person merely as a means is the cardinal political wrong. We take none of this back. It is the moral floor of our position, and the place where the argument begins.

But the individual is not a brute fact. She is formed. The classical liberal tradition has always depended on civic resources it did not acknowledge it was consuming. Adam Smith knew this; he wrote *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* before he wrote *The Wealth of Nations* because he understood that a commercial society presupposes a moral substrate it does not itself produce. Tocqueville knew this; his description of American associations is the description of a country whose liberty is made possible by non-liberal forms of solidarity. Dewey knew this; his insistence on democracy as a way of life was an insistence that the formal institutions of liberal democracy are the tip of an iceberg whose submerged mass is civic practice. Jonathan Rauch has more recently called the submerged mass the *constitution of knowledge* — the set of norms, institutions, and practices by which a free society establishes what is true and what is false — and has noted that classical liberalism has been spending this constitution without noticing the draw-down. In each case the point is the same. Freedom is not self-sustaining. It has an ecology. The ecology has to be tended, or the freedom dies.

We are asking classical liberals to name this dependency. Once you have named it, you have to fund it. Not necessarily with money; often with attention, with participation, with the forbearance of treating civic life as a hobby. But to fund it somehow. A tradition that goes on celebrating its results while refusing to resupply its conditions is a tradition that is eating its own seed corn and will be surprised by the famine it is producing.

***

At this point we owe the reader a more precise account of the philosophical ground we are standing on. The synthesis we are sketching is not ours alone, and it is not new. The most worked-out academic version of it appears in Richard Dagger's *Civic Virtues*, published in 1997, and we take that book as the most important direct antecedent of the argument we are making.

Dagger's project, in brief, was to show that the standard opposition between liberalism and republicanism is misleading. Liberalism, Dagger argued, is committed to autonomy — to the capacity of persons to govern themselves, choose their ends, live their own lives. Republicanism is committed to civic virtue — to the disposition of citizens to act for the common good, to participate in self-government, to subordinate private interest to public concern where the role requires it. These are sometimes posed as rivals. Dagger's point was that, read carefully, they are not. Autonomy, properly understood, is not merely the absence of external constraint; it is a *capability*, and capabilities are developed. The conditions under which persons become autonomous include education, participation in shared decision-making, the experience of being treated as an equal by equals, the exercise of responsibility in roles that matter. These are civic conditions. They are produced and maintained by the practices republicans care about. The liberal ideal, pursued to its depth, requires the republican practice to realize it.

Conversely, civic virtue, properly understood, is not the dissolution of the self into the polis. It is the disposition of a free person to act, voluntarily and with reflection, for the common things. The civic virtues Dagger catalogues — civility, open-mindedness, honesty, a willingness to deliberate, a sense of fairness, a capacity to abide by procedures you did not choose — are virtues of *autonomous* persons. They presuppose the liberal self, just as the liberal self presupposes the civic conditions. The republican ideal, pursued to its depth, requires the liberal framework to make it compatible with dignity.

Dagger's term for the synthesis is *republican liberalism*, and we have borrowed it because it says what we want to say. A politics organized around the cultivation of the civic virtues and the protection of individual rights; a conception of liberty that includes both non-interference and non-domination; a conception of the citizen as someone who is formed by the polity and whose rights constrain what the polity may do to her.

Where we extend Dagger rather than simply adopting him is in what we do with the view next. Dagger's project is a philosophical reconciliation, aimed primarily at the academic literature in which the liberal-communitarian debate had been conducted for a generation. Our project is programmatic. We take the reconciliation as the premise, not the conclusion, and we devote the next two volumes to asking what it implies for understanding the particular pathologies of American life in the 2020s and for the practical work of repair. The diagnoses of Volume II and the practices of Volume III are what the philosophical position of Volume I is *for*. A reconciliation that stayed in the library would be a reconciliation that had not earned its name. We are trying to take it into the street.

***

We anticipate an objection, and we should answer it before we close. The objection is that our position is not a distinctive one at all. It is, the objector will say, merely liberalism plus a gesture toward civic-ness, the kind of thing a columnist gestures at when she wants to sound balanced without actually committing to anything. To this objection we reply that the position we have sketched has at least four distinct commitments, each of which would be denied by one or more of the traditions it is adjacent to, and each of which commits us to stances most of those traditions are reluctant to take.

We reject the bracketing of the good, but we do not reject it in the post-liberal direction. Certain goods — the goods of civic life itself, the conditions of personhood, the practices of self-government, the maintenance of deliberative institutions — must be publicly pursued, and a polity that pretends to neutrality about them is a polity that has already chosen. But most goods — the goods of religion, of culture, of intimate life, of personal pursuit — remain properly private, and a polity that colonizes them in the name of civic formation has become the thing the liberals rightly feared. The line is not always easy to draw, and we will spend parts of Volume III trying to draw it in specific cases. But the existence of a line is itself a distinctive commitment, and not the one the pure liberal or the pure post-liberal would endorse.

We reject the liberal presumption that rights alone will produce the citizens who maintain the rights. The rights are necessary. They are not sufficient. The production of citizens is a separate and larger task, partly public and partly private, partly political and partly cultural, and it requires its own attention and its own resources. A political order that treats the production of citizens as a byproduct of correctly specified procedures will be a political order that runs down its civic capital until none is left.

We reject the communitarian presumption that any thick community is better than a thin liberal one. Some thick communities are tyrannies. Some are cults. Some are, in the specific sense Arendt and Hoffer identified, mass movements. The question to ask of a thick community is not only whether it provides belonging but whether the belonging it provides is compatible with the dignity of its members. A community that requires the surrender of conscience, the suppression of dissent, the humiliation of the deviant, is not a remedy for atomization; it is its mirror image, the other face of the same pathology. We want community. We are not indifferent to what kind.

We reject the post-liberal presumption that liberalism is the disease rather than a stage in its own partial solution. We have tried throughout this book to take the post-liberal diagnosis seriously and to concede where it is right. But the prescription rests on a misreading of what liberalism has achieved. The moral recognition of the individual is not a mistake to be corrected. It is a permanent advance, one of the few things the modern West has gotten unambiguously right, and any politics that proposes to throw it over in the name of restored solidarity will produce neither solidarity nor a tolerable politics. The task is not to transcend liberalism. It is to complete it.

Together, these four rejections describe a distinct position. We are not a species of post-liberalism, and we are not a species of the managerial liberal consensus that post-liberalism is reacting against. We are not a species of communitarianism, and we are not a species of the libertarianism that communitarianism was right to criticize. What we are, we have said, is republican liberalism — the older American idea, recovered and made useable for a moment that has forgotten it.

***

Some of this will strike readers as hard, and where it is hard we mean to say so. We are asking the classical liberal to accept that the freedom he cherishes has conditions he has not been paying enough attention to. We are asking the republican to accept that the virtues she cherishes cannot be cultivated in a political order that permits the majority to override conscience. We are asking the communitarian to accept that not every thick community is better than a thin one, and that the rights to exit and to dissent are not concessions to liberalism but constitutive of any community worth belonging to. We are asking the post-liberal to accept that his diagnosis, which we have honored, points to a different prescription than the one he has reached for. Each of these asks is real. Each will feel, to the reader whose camp we are pressing on, like a demand to give up something dear.

We can only say, in response, that we do not think anything is actually being given up. We think the versions of each tradition we are urging are the traditions' own best versions, the ones their deepest voices arrive at when they have thought the matter through. We think classical liberalism is better off acknowledging its civic preconditions than pretending it does not have them. We think civic republicanism is better off acknowledging the rights-bearing individual than pretending she is a regrettable modern invention. We think communitarianism is better off arguing for the specific thick communities that are worth defending than for thickness as such. We think post-liberalism is better off as a supplement to liberalism than as a replacement for it.

And we think, more broadly, that the argument of Volume I has now done what it needed to do. The citizen is an individual who belongs. The person is prior to the state but not prior to the community. Liberty is both non-interference and non-domination. The polity is neutral about ways of life but not about the conditions of choice. Virtue is the disposition to act well in the role one is occupying, and citizenship is one such role, and a demanding one. These are the commitments we will carry forward.

Volume II will ask what has actually happened to the citizen in our time. It will work through the six partial diagnoses — the return of mass-movement psychology, the collapse of social capital, the decay of institutions, the polarization findings, the platform public sphere, the tyranny of merit — and argue that they are not rival explanations but aspects of a single phenomenon, the phenomenon Arendt and Hoffer named in 1951. The argument of Volume II will make sense only to readers who have absorbed the argument of Volume I, because a diagnosis is intelligible only against a picture of health.

Volume III will ask what can be done, at the scales at which any of us actually live. It will draw on the empirical literature on depolarization, on the history of successful civic revivals, and on the practices — personal, institutional, and political — that have been shown to work. It will be a practical book, organized around things that can be begun this week at no cost. But its practicality will be intelligible only against the philosophical picture this volume has tried to lay out, because the question of what to do depends on the question of what we are trying to make.

What we are trying to make is the citizen. Not the voter, not the consumer, not the partisan, not the follower, not the content producer or the content consumer. The citizen: the person who belongs to a polity of equals, who bears its burdens and partakes of its goods, who can address her fellows in the second person plural and be addressed back, who owes the polity and is owed by it, whose individuality is not compromised by her membership and whose membership is not cheapened by her individuality. This is an old figure, and we are aware that much of the reading public has been taught to regard her as a ghost. We do not think she is a ghost. We think she has been displaced, distracted, and discouraged, and that her recovery is not a matter of nostalgia but of the practical conditions of any tolerable common life. We have tried in this chapter to say who she is. In the volumes that follow, we try to say where she went, and what it will take to bring her back.
