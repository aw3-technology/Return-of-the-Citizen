# Chapter 3
## The Liberal Revolution

The tradition we walked through in the preceding chapter is the oldest continuous strand of political thinking the West has produced. It begins with a polis that precedes the person, an Athens and a Rome in which the citizen is a role completed by participation rather than a right exercised against intrusion, and it carries that assumption, through every mutation of language and circumstance, from Aristotle to Arendt. The tradition we turn to now is, by comparison, startlingly new. It is no older than the seventeenth century. The oldest of its canonical texts was published within living memory of Shakespeare. And yet this tradition — the liberal revolution, the revolution of rights — has done something the republican tradition did not do and perhaps could not have done. It has reversed the ancient presumption. It has insisted that the person precedes the polity, that political authority is answerable to each individual it binds, and that the purpose of a government is not to complete us but to leave us the space in which to complete ourselves.

That reversal is the moral center of modern political life. It is what stands behind the phrases that even people who have never read a page of Locke or Kant or Mill can recite from memory: that governments are instituted among men, that they derive their just powers from the consent of the governed, that every person is endowed with inalienable rights, that no one may be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law. These phrases are so deeply lodged in the American vocabulary that it is easy to miss how strange and specific they are. No ancient Greek wrote them. No Roman would have signed them. No medieval chancery would have drafted them. They are the signature of a particular philosophical revolution whose premises we have so thoroughly internalized that we mistake them for common sense, and it is worth the effort of de-familiarization to see what they actually claim.

The central liberal move is stated in a sentence. The individual is prior to the political community. Rights are not granted by the state; they are held against it. The purpose of political order is to secure those rights so that persons may pursue their own conception of the good. Every other commitment of the tradition — consent, constitutionalism, toleration, the rule of law, due process, the division of powers, the protection of conscience, the protection of property, the protection of speech — follows from that starting point. One begins with the person, not with the regime. One asks what the regime must do, and what it must not do, to be compatible with the dignity of the person. Political authority requires justification to each individual it binds, and the individual who cannot be given such a justification is being wronged, not governed.

This is what we mean, in the pages that follow, by the liberal revolution. It is the tradition that asks, for the first time in the history of political thought, the question that now seems to us so obvious as to hardly need asking: what right does any political power have to bind any particular person? It is also the tradition — and this is the deeper point — that supplies an answer under which most of the readers of this book have lived every day of their lives without having to think about it. The liberal revolution is not an ideology we chose. It is the atmosphere we breathe.

***

Its first serious breath was Locke's.

John Locke, writing the *Second Treatise of Government* in the shadow of the English Civil War and publishing it in 1689 as the Glorious Revolution was being justified after the fact, did what no political theorist before him had quite done. He began his argument not with the polity, and not with God's ordination of kings, but with an imagined condition in which there was no political order at all — the state of nature — and asked what rights a human being would possess in such a condition. His answer was the sentence the American revolutionaries would paraphrase a century later: every person has a natural right to life, liberty, and property. These rights are not conferred by governments, because they exist prior to the existence of any government. They are, in Locke's idiom, the endowment of a creator, but the political implication is one an atheist could sign: the individual is the bearer of rights before any king is the bearer of a crown.

Government, on Locke's view, is therefore an instrument. It exists to secure the rights its subjects already possess, because the inconveniences of the state of nature — the partiality of private judgment, the inadequacy of private force — make a common public power necessary for the protection of rights that existed before the common power did. But this instrumental framing has a radical consequence. If government exists to secure rights, then a government that fails to secure them, or, worse, one that violates them, has forfeited its warrant. It becomes what Locke calls a tyranny, and a tyranny may be resisted. The right of revolution — the right of a people to throw off a government that has turned against the purposes for which it was instituted — is written directly into the Lockean argument. It is the sentence Jefferson lifted nearly word-for-word. It is the sentence that made every subsequent revolt against arbitrary power in the Atlantic world legible as a Lockean claim.

The Locke who matters to us is not the Locke of the seventeenth-century disputes about consent and tacit consent, property and the mixing of labor, the Hobbes-Locke-Rousseau triangle that undergraduates are asked to draw on examinations. The Locke who matters is the one who performed the conceptual inversion: the one for whom political authority stands accused until it has given an account of itself, and for whom the accuser is the ordinary person whose life and liberty and property are at stake. That inversion is what the American framers inherited. It is what the Declaration performs in paragraph after paragraph — an indictment of a specific king measured against a standard older than he is. And it is what every bill of rights, every constitution, every human rights declaration the liberal West has produced since 1689 has quietly repeated. The individual has rights. The state must respect them. The state that does not respect them has failed at the only job for which a state exists.

***

Adam Smith is the figure the tradition has, almost without noticing, mutilated. He is remembered as the prophet of the invisible hand, the apostle of self-interest, the economist who proved that greed is good. He wrote no such thing. The flattening of Smith is what happens when a thinker becomes more useful as a slogan than as an author, and we mean, in this chapter, to resist the flattening, because the real Smith is one of the indispensable figures in the liberal tradition and one whose recovery is essential to any serious account of it.

Smith wrote two books. The one everyone has heard of is *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*, published in 1776, the year of the American Declaration. The one hardly anyone outside the academy has read is *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, published seventeen years earlier, which Smith revised and expanded throughout his life and which he considered his chief work. The two books are halves of a single argument, and the argument is nothing like the caricature. Commercial society, Smith said, is possible only because human beings are moral animals before they are economic ones. The market functions because the people in it possess certain dispositions — sympathy, prudence, justice — that are formed in the ordinary moral life of families, neighborhoods, and trades and that cannot be generated by the market itself. A society of pure self-interest would not produce prosperity. It would produce the kind of systematic cheating and violence that makes prosperity impossible. The butcher and the baker whose self-interest Smith famously invokes are not amoral optimizers. They are tradesmen embedded in a dense local fabric of trust, reputation, and conscience, without which their self-interest would have nothing to work on.

The Smith who matters to this book is the Smith for whom freedom and decency are linked at their roots. Commercial society is to be defended, in his argument, because it enlarges the sphere within which ordinary people can pursue ordinary lives without being at the mercy of arbitrary lords, but it is to be defended only so long as the moral culture that makes it possible is maintained. When Smith warned, in both books, that the division of labor could degrade the workers who staffed it — reducing them to routines so narrow that the characteristic virtues of a free citizen could no longer develop — he was stating, in the eighteenth century, the problem the twentieth century would rediscover in the language of alienation. The flattening that turned Smith into a cheerleader for laissez-faire was performed against the grain of his own text. The real Smith is a moralist who thought commerce was compatible with human dignity under specific conditions, not a mystic who thought commerce generated dignity as a byproduct.

We will have reason, in later chapters, to return to the consequences of the flattening. The post-liberal critics of liberalism, when they indict the commercial reduction of freedom, are often indicting a Smith that Smith would not have recognized. It is important, at this stage, to name the thinker who is actually there in the tradition. He is one of the most morally serious figures liberalism ever produced, and treating him as a caricature disarms one of the tradition's best defenders.

***

If Locke supplied the political move and Smith supplied the economic one, Kant supplied the philosophical depth the tradition required to survive its own critics. Immanuel Kant, writing in Königsberg in the last third of the eighteenth century, asked what it would mean to treat every person as the bearer of a dignity that could not be traded away, and he gave an answer so austere and so demanding that every subsequent argument about human rights has lived in its shadow.

The formulation that matters for our purposes is what Kant called the second formulation of the categorical imperative: "So act that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end and never merely as a means." The sentence is easy to misread as a prohibition on using other people, which is not what Kant meant. We use other people constantly, in every transaction and every cooperation, and the sentence does not forbid this. What it forbids is treating them *merely* as means — treating a person as though they were a thing, as though their status as an end in themselves, as a being with their own purposes, their own reason, their own capacity to legislate the moral law, could be set aside when it was convenient to set it aside. The sentence is the philosophical form of the ancient prohibition on slavery, torture, and conscription-into-the-purposes-of-another, and it is the ground on which Kant builds his account of human dignity.

That account is what distinguishes the liberal tradition from every utilitarian, perfectionist, or authoritarian rival. A utilitarian could, in principle, justify the destruction of an innocent person if the aggregate good were sufficiently great. A perfectionist could justify coercion on the ground that a person was being made better by it against her will. An authoritarian could justify the subordination of the person to the regime on the ground that the regime represented a higher reality to which she was obligated. Kant closes all three doors. The person is an end in herself. No calculation of aggregate welfare, no judgment about her good, no appeal to a collective above her, can justify treating her as a mere instrument. Her autonomy — her capacity to determine her own life in accordance with reasons she can recognize as her own — is the thing political order is there to protect, and anything that treats her as though she did not possess that capacity is a wrong that cannot be defeated by the benefits that might follow from committing it.

This is the philosophical bedrock of modern rights. It is why we say, and why we believe, that torture is impermissible even when it would produce useful information. It is why we say, and why we believe, that a person's religion, her speech, her conscience, her body, are not available to the state even when the state has reasons it considers sufficient. It is why the standard of political justification for the liberal tradition is not the aggregate welfare of the population but the acceptability of the arrangement to each person it binds. Kant wrote in the prose of the late Enlightenment and in the vocabulary of a particular German philosophical tradition, and his arguments are not easy. But the thing he saw — that the dignity of the person is the non-negotiable starting point of any legitimate political order — is one of the few philosophical claims that has survived every serious attempt to dislodge it, and it is the deepest source the liberal tradition has.

***

The figure on whom this book most directly leans is John Stuart Mill. There are reasons. Mill is, of the canonical liberals, the one who wrote most directly about the problem we are trying to write about — the problem of a free person in a society that is increasingly dominated by mass opinion and mass sentiment — and he saw it coming with a clarity that we have, over the intervening century and a half, mostly forgotten.

*On Liberty*, published in 1859, is a short book whose central claim is stated in its opening chapter and defended in those that follow. The claim is what later readers have called the harm principle. "The only purpose for which power can be rightfully exercised over any member of a civilized community, against his will, is to prevent harm to others. His own good, either physical or moral, is not a sufficient warrant." The sentence is easy to read quickly; it rewards slow reading. Mill is not saying that the individual's good does not matter. He is saying that the individual is the judge of his own good, and that the social and political apparatus has no standing to override his judgment in the name of his own improvement. The paternalism of the state, the paternalism of the majority, the paternalism of respectable opinion — each is an intrusion on the sphere within which the person has the authority to live his own life. The state may act to prevent harm. It may not act, in Mill's careful formulation, merely to produce what someone else takes to be the person's good.

The second argument of *On Liberty*, and for our purposes the more important one, is the argument for liberty of thought. Mill believed that truth is not a possession but a practice, and that the practice requires a specific social condition to function. It requires what he called, in a phrase that should be carved above every newsroom and every university, the "collision of adverse opinions." A belief that has never been tested against the strongest case for the opposite position is not, on Mill's view, a belief at all; it is a prejudice. The person who holds it cannot give a reason for it that is stronger than the authority of those around him who also hold it. The society that has suppressed dissent, or that has so shaped opinion that dissent is no longer imagined, has not achieved truth. It has achieved a uniformity that is indistinguishable, from the inside, from truth, and that will be indefensible the moment it is seriously challenged.

The harm principle protects the person from coercion. The collision of adverse opinions protects the culture from decay. Mill saw that they were two halves of the same argument, because a society that permits coercion in the name of the good will also find it convenient to suppress the opinions that question what the good is, and a society that suppresses those opinions will soon find that it has no principled resistance to coercion. Freedom of conduct and freedom of thought are not separate items on a list of civil liberties. They are the conditions under which a citizen remains capable of the reasoning that makes her a citizen at all. This is the point in Mill we most need now, and it is the point we will return to in Volume III, because a public sphere that has lost the capacity to host a collision of adverse opinions is a public sphere that has lost the capacity to produce citizens.

Mill is also the liberal most alert to the danger that freedom would be consumed by the very mass society it had helped to produce. He saw, in the commercial republics of the mid-nineteenth century, the beginnings of what Tocqueville had described in America — a society in which formal liberty coexisted with a crushing uniformity of opinion, a society in which no one was forbidden to think differently but no one, in fact, did. He called this the despotism of custom. It is what we would now call the algorithmic monoculture, or the partisan feed, or the social-media mob. The ancestry is the same. Mill understood that liberty is not merely a legal status; it is a practice that requires particular conditions to survive, and that those conditions can be eroded by social pressures as effectively as by state coercion. A book about the return of the citizen leans on Mill because Mill is the one who saw that the citizen could be destroyed by the very freedom in whose name the destruction would be carried out.

***

A century after Mill, Isaiah Berlin delivered the lecture that has organized every subsequent argument about freedom. "Two Concepts of Liberty," given as his inaugural address at Oxford in 1958, drew a distinction so lucid that its terms have passed into ordinary political speech. There are two ideas of liberty, Berlin said, and they are not the same. Negative liberty is freedom *from* — freedom from interference, freedom from coercion, freedom from arbitrary authority. It is the sphere within which a person may act without being stopped. Positive liberty is freedom *to* — freedom to realize one's true self, freedom to achieve one's higher purposes, freedom to become the person one was meant to be. Both ideas, Berlin allowed, have respectable histories. Both capture something real in our sense of what it is to be free.

But positive liberty, Berlin warned, contains within it a specific and terrible slide. If freedom is the achievement of one's true self, then someone else may come to believe that they know better than you what your true self is. If freedom is the realization of one's higher purposes, then someone else may claim to see those purposes more clearly than you do. And if they can make that claim stick, they can describe their coercion of you as the liberation of your true self from your false self. The prisoner is really the free man. The dissident is really in the grip of false consciousness. The resistor is being forced to be free. Every twentieth-century tyranny that marched under the banner of liberation performed exactly this move, and Berlin was writing in the shadow of a century in which both the Bolshevik and the fascist had claimed, with equal sincerity, that their coercion was their victim's deepest freedom.

Berlin did not say that positive liberty was always wrong, and he did not say that negative liberty was always sufficient. He said that the slide from positive liberty to the justification of coercion for the subject's own good was a real danger that real people had actually taken, and that a tradition that cared about the freedom of actual human beings needed to keep negative liberty front and center precisely because it was the form of liberty that could not be turned against the person in whose name it was being invoked. A negative liberty has an obvious test. The person who has it is not being interfered with. A positive liberty has no such test, because its meaning depends on a theory of the true self that can be supplied by any authority willing to insist on its theory strongly enough. This is why the liberal tradition, when pressed, has returned and returns to negative liberty as the core. Not because it thinks the positive conception is wrong, but because it knows what the positive conception can be made to justify when the wrong people get hold of it.

***

The most ambitious restatement of the liberal tradition in the twentieth century was an attempt, published in 1971, to put liberal political philosophy on a rigorous footing after the catastrophes that had consumed the first half of the century. John Rawls's *A Theory of Justice* is one of the few academic books of the last hundred years that genuinely changed the shape of its field, and it did so by asking a simple question and pursuing it with a relentlessness that most political philosophers before and since have not matched. What principles of justice would free and rational people, concerned to advance their own interests, agree to if they had to choose from behind a veil of ignorance — not knowing which position in the resulting society they would occupy, not knowing their class, race, talents, conception of the good, or moment in history?

The thought experiment, which Rawls called the original position, is the most famous piece of philosophical machinery the tradition has produced in the last century, and it has been endlessly attacked and endlessly extended. Its purpose, which critics sometimes miss, is not to tell us what a just society looks like. Its purpose is to generate a standard of political justification that does not depend on the particular advantages of the people doing the justifying. The veil of ignorance is a way of asking whether a principle of political order could be defended to everyone it binds, not merely to those whom it benefits, and Rawls believed — on the strength of a long and intricate argument we cannot do justice to here — that the principles free people would choose from behind the veil would be something like the following. First, each person is to have an equal right to the most extensive basic liberties compatible with similar liberties for others. Second, social and economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are both to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged and attached to positions open to all under conditions of fair equality of opportunity.

It is worth saying, at the risk of seeming partisan in a chapter that is trying to be fair, that the Rawlsian achievement is extraordinary. Before Rawls, serious liberal political theory had been under philosophical siege for decades, cornered by utilitarianism on one side and various forms of skepticism on the other, and it had lost the capacity to articulate its own premises with confidence. Rawls reconstructed the tradition on a basis that drew on Kant without being dependent on Kant's metaphysics, that treated the equal standing of each person as the foundational commitment of political morality, and that generated substantive principles of justice from that commitment without collapsing into either procedural thinness or perfectionist paternalism. The sentence that summarizes the whole project — "the right is prior to the good" — is what Rawls meant by insisting that principles of justice must be acceptable to persons whose conceptions of the good life differ, and therefore must be defensible independently of any particular conception of what that life should contain.

We will say, in the section that follows, where we think the Rawlsian program ran into trouble. We want to be clear here about what it accomplished. The twentieth century had produced, in the name of the good, in the name of the true self, in the name of the historical destiny of a people or a class, some of the most comprehensive evils human beings have ever committed. A political philosophy adequate to the moral lessons of that century had to find a way to insist that political authority must be justified to each person it binds in terms that do not require her to accept, as a precondition, some particular account of what her life is for. That is the work Rawls did. It is the reason his book became the reference point against which every subsequent political philosophy has had to position itself. It is also the reason his book is a moral document as much as a philosophical one — an attempt, in the shadow of the camps and the gulags, to describe a politics that treats each person as what Kant said she was.

***

The sober contemporary defender of this tradition is Francis Fukuyama, whose 2022 book *Liberalism and Its Discontents* is the best statement the liberal tradition currently has of why its critics on both flanks are serious and why its critics on both flanks are nonetheless wrong about the cure. Fukuyama is not a polemicist. He has read Deneen, he has read the identity-politics left, and he acknowledges without flinching what both camps see: that liberalism has been flattened in practice into a thin proceduralism that has starved its adherents of the shared goods on which a political community depends, and that the resulting vacuum has been filled by illiberal movements that offer what liberalism has neglected to offer. His response is not to capitulate to either side but to argue that the pathologies both diagnose are distortions of liberalism rather than liberalism's logical outcome, and that the repair is a recovery of what liberalism was rather than a replacement of liberalism with something else.

We will take up, in the chapters that follow, whether Fukuyama's optimism is warranted. It is enough to say here that his book is the most credible contemporary version of the tradition's self-defense, and that any honest engagement with the critiques of liberalism — the communitarian critique in Chapter 4, the post-liberal critique in Chapter 5 — needs to have read Fukuyama before it concludes that the tradition has nothing left to say.

***

We have walked through this tradition at some length because we believe — and the book's title notwithstanding — that the liberal revolution was moral progress, not merely a philosophical preference. This is the claim we want, at the center of the chapter, to state without hedging.

The recognition that every person is an end in herself, that no one is merely a subject, that political authority requires justification to each person it binds, is one of the durable goods of the modern age. It is not a matter of taste. It is not one conception of politics among the several on offer. It is an achievement, hard-won over centuries, against which the political alternatives are to be measured. The horrors that liberal rights protect against — arbitrary arrest, confiscation of property, torture, religious persecution, caste hierarchy, the reduction of persons to the instruments of a ruler's purposes — are real. They are not abstractions. For most of human history they were the ordinary content of political life, and in most of the world for most of the twentieth century they were the ordinary content of political life, and in much of the world today they remain the ordinary content of political life. The reason most readers of this book have not personally experienced them is not that human nature has improved. It is that the political and legal architecture of the liberal revolution has held, in our particular corner of the world, for a particular historical moment, against pressures that in other times and other places have swept such protections away.

The writ of habeas corpus. The right against self-incrimination. The prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment. The protection of religious conscience. The protection of speech. The protection of property from seizure without compensation. The requirement that a person be charged with an offense known to the law before being deprived of her liberty. The principle that the law applies equally to the person who wrote it and to the person charged under it. These are not procedural niceties. They are the mechanisms by which the claim that every person is an end in herself is made to bite in the institutions that would otherwise treat her as a means. They are written in the Bill of Rights, in the European Convention on Human Rights, in the Universal Declaration of Human Rights adopted by the General Assembly in 1948 — a document whose drafting, it is worth remembering, was the direct moral response of the international community to what had been done to human beings in the preceding decade. They are the inheritance the liberal revolution bequeathed to the modern world, and the inheritance is not negotiable.

The steelman of liberalism is not a desiccated procedural republic. The steelman is a polity in which each person is permitted, and supported in, the pursuit of her own best understanding of a life, and in which the state does not pretend to know better than she does what her life is for. That polity is a generous vision. It is the vision under which a religious minority, a sexual minority, a philosophical eccentric, an artist whose art no one else yet understands, a person whose conscience tells her to refuse what the majority demands — each of these, in a liberal order, has a standing that no prior political tradition ever gave them. The person who does not want to fit is protected. The life that the majority does not recognize is permitted. The authority that would coerce conformity, in the name of the nation, the faith, the race, the revolution, the market, or the cosmic plan, is held to account by a standard higher than its own ambition. The generosity of that vision — the willingness to extend to each person the space within which her own life is hers — is what we mean when we say the liberal revolution was moral progress. And we are on liberalism's side.

***

We are also, and for the same reasons, obligated to name the internal strains the tradition has developed, because the critiques that will occupy the next two chapters are serious and the liberal who pretends otherwise is indulging in complacency. The strains are not destroyers of the tradition. They are the places where it has become clear that the tradition, left to itself, does not have the resources to solve the problems it generates, and that this is one of the reasons the citizen has become harder and harder to find.

The first strain is the bracketing of the good. Rawls said that the right is prior to the good, and the formulation was a principled attempt to justify political order to persons who disagreed about what the good life contained. It has proved, in practice, harder to sustain than the argument supposed. A society organized around the bracket cannot, by its own stated commitments, articulate the shared goods that hold it together, because to articulate them would be to violate the bracket. A politics of this kind finds itself, at moments of crisis, unable to say what it is for, because its official philosophy has forbidden it to say what it is for, and the citizens raised in it find themselves incapable of describing the public life they share, because the language in which it might be described has been ruled out of public order. The critics will say that a political community cannot survive on procedural commitments alone, that it needs a thicker moral vocabulary, and that the liberal insistence on the bracket leaves its adherents without the resources to resist the tribes that offer such a vocabulary by offering a worse one. They are not entirely wrong.

The second strain is what Michael Sandel, in a book we will take up in the next chapter, called the unencumbered self — the picture of the person implicit in the Rawlsian original position, the self that exists prior to any of its ends and chooses them freely from a position of reflection uncontaminated by community, tradition, or constitutive attachment. Sandel's charge, which we will develop in the chapter to come, is that this self is a philosophical fiction and that the fiction has sociological consequences. The actual selves that liberalism purports to protect are not unencumbered. They are formed, all the way down, by the families, neighborhoods, congregations, languages, and traditions into which they were born, and a political philosophy that pretends otherwise — that treats those formative attachments as so many preferences to be adjusted by the chooser at her convenience — does not protect the self. It corrodes the self, by teaching the self to regard its own formative ties as external to who she really is, and ends up producing exactly the kind of deracinated individual who is raw material for the mass movements the tradition is supposed to protect her against. The critique is older than Sandel and deeper than any one book, and its deepest version is that a liberalism that takes the unencumbered self as its anthropology has hollowed out the persons whose freedom it was supposed to secure.

The third strain is what we will call the commercial drift. Locke listed three natural rights — life, liberty, property — and the inclusion of property was part of the tradition from the beginning. Over three centuries the weight placed on that third right has grown, and with it a tendency to reduce freedom to market choice and the citizen to the consumer. This tendency is not intrinsic to the tradition. Smith, as we saw, would not have recognized it. Kant would have been appalled by it. Mill warned against it. Rawls constructed an entire theory designed to insist that the basic liberties were prior to economic distribution and that the worst-off had a claim on the society that mere market outcomes did not capture. And yet the drift has happened. Contemporary liberal societies routinely present themselves to their members as marketplaces of lifestyle options, and the language of freedom has, in much public discourse, become indistinguishable from the language of consumer choice. The post-liberal critics see in this drift the essence of liberalism, and they are wrong to see it as the essence. But they are right that it is a real tendency within the tradition, and that the tradition has not always had the philosophical resources to resist it. The commercial drift is not what the best liberals wanted. It is something the best liberals should have been more alarmed by.

***

These strains do not falsify the liberal revolution. They locate it. They tell us that the revolution succeeded, brilliantly, at the work of the last three centuries — the limitation of arbitrary power, the protection of conscience, the legal recognition of each person's standing — and that it has run into difficulties at the work the coming century requires, which is the cultivation of the shared life in which the individual protected by those rights can live something more than a protected loneliness. The tradition the liberals supplanted, the republican tradition we walked through in Chapter 2, had no worked-out theory of individual rights and suffered for the lack of it. The tradition the liberals built supplied the theory, and bequeathed to us the institutions, and enabled the freedoms under which most of us live. We are not prepared, in the pages that follow, to trade those freedoms for anything. The point is that the liberal revolution did not replace the republican tradition. It supplemented it. The question the rest of this volume pursues is whether the two can be held together — whether the moral achievement of the liberals and the civic achievement of the republicans can be combined into a single adequate answer to the question of what the citizen is and how the citizen is to be.

The communitarians we take up in the next chapter will argue that liberals have not solved this problem and, on their own resources, cannot solve it. The post-liberals we take up in the chapter after that will argue that the problem is unsolvable on liberal premises at all, and that liberalism must therefore be replaced. We think both of those judgments are too quick, and we will say so in Chapter 7, where we describe the synthesis that animates this book. But we say here, at the end of the liberal chapter, that we owe liberalism more than a defensive nod. We owe it the recognition that the citizen as a figure of the modern age is not thinkable without it. The person who bears rights against the state, who is addressed by political power as someone to whom a justification is owed, who cannot be tortured or conscripted or silenced or stripped of her property at the pleasure of a ruler — that person is the liberal achievement. The republican tradition gives us the citizen as a member. The liberal tradition gives us the citizen as a bearer of dignity that no membership can override. Any account of the citizen adequate to modern life has to hold both, and the work of this book, from here to the end of the first volume, is to show how it can be done.
