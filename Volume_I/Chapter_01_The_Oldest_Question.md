# Chapter 1
## The Oldest Question

Somewhere in the world tonight a woman will close her laptop, push back from a kitchen table, and try to explain to herself what she owes the country she happens to live in. She has had a hard day. Her job is not quite what she was promised it would be. Her rent has gone up again. Her parents, a thousand miles away, are getting older in a place she is not sure she will move back to. On the screen in front of her, before she closed it, two strangers were screaming at each other about a politician neither of them has met. She closes the laptop not because she has reached a conclusion but because she has reached a limit. And in the silence of the kitchen, for perhaps the first time that week, an old and embarrassing question rises unbidden: *what does any of this have to do with me?*

It is an embarrassing question because we have been trained, in the moral language of our time, to have instant answers to it. She should vote. She should be informed. She should follow the news. She should buy from ethical companies and avoid unethical ones. She should be "civically engaged," a phrase that has come, in our generation, to mean almost nothing at all. But none of these answers touches what she is actually asking. She is not asking what a responsible consumer does. She is asking what she *is* — whether the nation of three hundred and thirty million strangers whose laws she lives under has any real claim on her, and whether, conversely, she has any real claim on it. She is asking the oldest question in political philosophy, and she is asking it alone, at a kitchen table, which is roughly where most Americans now encounter it.

The question is this. What does a human being owe the political community, and what does the community owe the human being in return?

It is the oldest question because every serious political thinker in the Western tradition, from Plato to the present, has had to answer it before getting anywhere else. It is the hardest question because a political order cannot exist without an answer, and yet no answer is obviously right. And it is the question our age most needs to ask again — not in the vague and moralizing register in which it is usually mumbled, but clearly, in the manner of people who understand that everything else depends on what they say next.

This book begins from the premise that the question has not been settled. It has been answered, and answered seriously, and answered incompatibly, at least four times in the history of our civilization. Each of those answers has sophisticated defenders still writing today. Each of them, taken alone, produces a recognizable political style and a recognizable set of pathologies. And the public life of the early twenty-first century has been warped, in part, by our reluctance to admit that the question is still open — by a series of attempts to evade it rather than confront it. The citizen has gone missing, we argued in the introduction, because we no longer know what a citizen is. That is not a rhetorical claim. It is a philosophical one, and we mean to defend it at length.

But first: a word about why the question is hard, and why the four answers that have been given to it are worth lingering over before any of them is chosen.

***

There is a temptation, in any book of this kind, to skip to the part where the author tells you what he thinks. That is the temptation of the pundit, who has strong opinions and a column to fill, and it is the reason most books about politics decay within ten years of publication. We are going to resist it. A question that has been live for twenty-five centuries does not deserve to be settled in a paragraph, and a reader who has come this far deserves to see the question worked before she sees the answer. More practically, we have found that every contemporary political argument we have encountered — our own included, before we started writing — turns out to rest on an unexamined answer to the oldest question. The culture-war argument between a conservative and a progressive on cable news is very rarely an argument about the policy on the table. It is almost always an argument, two or three layers down, about what a human being is in public and what a political community is for. Neither debater typically knows that that is what they are arguing about, which is why the arguments never end.

So let us try to say, as plainly as possible, what the four serious answers have been.

The first answer is that the human being is completed by the political community. On this view, associated with Aristotle in the *Politics* and carried forward through Cicero and Machiavelli into the contemporary work of Hannah Arendt and Philip Pettit, the polis is not an add-on to a pre-existing individual. It is the environment in which the capacities that make us human — reason, speech, judgment, friendship, justice — are developed and exercised. A person outside the polis, Aristotle wrote, is either a beast or a god. He meant it literally. The polity is not a service provider; it is the place where a creature of our kind becomes what it is. The citizen, accordingly, is not a holder of rights but a participant in ruling and being ruled in turn. Freedom, on this picture, is not the absence of interference. It is the condition of being a partner in a common life under laws one has some hand in making.

The second answer is that the human being is prior to the political community, and that the community exists to secure the rights the individual already holds by nature. On this view, associated with John Locke in the *Second Treatise* and carried forward through Kant and Mill into the contemporary work of John Rawls and Francis Fukuyama, the person comes first, and political authority is legitimate only to the extent that it can be derived from the consent of persons. Rights are held *against* the state. The state's central business is not to form citizens but to leave them alone — or, more precisely, to secure the conditions under which each can pursue his or her own conception of the good without being dominated by the conceptions of others. The citizen, accordingly, is a rights-bearer, a consenter, a subject of equal concern and respect. Freedom is non-interference, or something close to it. The political community is, in the severe phrase of the tradition, a framework rather than a fate.

The third answer is that neither of the first two is quite right, because each misdescribes the kind of being the human being actually is. On this view, associated in its modern form with Alasdair MacIntyre, Michael Sandel, Charles Taylor, and Michael Walzer, the real self is not the reasoning agent of liberal theory nor the public-minded citizen of civic republican theory but something more deeply constituted: a creature made by traditions, languages, and relationships it did not choose, pursuing goods it inherits rather than goods it invents. A political order that pretends otherwise — that tries to be neutral among ways of life, that treats the self as the unencumbered chooser of its own values — produces, in practice, atomized individuals who no longer know who they are and who therefore become available for capture by any movement that will tell them. The citizen, on this account, is a particular human being embedded in particular communities with particular commitments, and the political task is not to abstract from those particularities but to honor them. This is the answer of the communitarians.

The fourth answer is the most recent and the most disquieting. Associated with Patrick Deneen, Adrian Vermeule, Sohrab Ahmari, and Yoram Hazony, and foreshadowed by Christopher Lasch, it holds that the liberal project has not gone astray but has *succeeded* — that what it set out to produce, it has produced, and that what it has produced is the wreckage around us. The unencumbered self, the global market, the administrative state, the hollowing of family and congregation and neighborhood, the loneliness and despair that now register in the suicide statistics of every Western country — these are not failures of liberal modernity but its fruits. The answer follows: liberalism cannot be reformed from within, because its internal logic is the problem. It must be replaced by something older and thicker, some recovery of a substantive common good the liberal order was designed to evade. The citizen, on this picture, is the bearer of a tradition that the current regime is actively dissolving, and whose recovery will require something more than a seminar.

We will spend a chapter on each of these traditions in what follows, and a chapter on the particularly American quarrel in which all four have been refracted for two centuries. We will not name a winner prematurely. What we want the reader to see, at the very start, is that each of these answers has been given by serious people, defended by serious argument, and confirmed in practice by serious political cultures. None is a straw. Each captures something the others struggle to capture. And the moment at which we start choosing among them — which we will eventually have to do — is a moment of real philosophical commitment, not the moment at which we finally stop asking the question.

***

Why does it matter that we get the question right before we get too fond of any answer?

Because most contemporary political argument is defective at exactly this point, and the defect is not a matter of taste. When a modern American politician says "the American people deserve better," he is not making an argument; he is assuming an answer to the oldest question. He is assuming that "the American people" exists as a singular moral subject, that it has interests distinguishable from the aggregated preferences of its members, that it is owed something by someone, and that the someone is generally not himself. When a modern American commentator says that voters are choosing "against their own interests," she is assuming an answer too — one in which the interests of an individual are detachable from his membership in a place, a class, a faith, a story, and in which her own view of those interests is the view from nowhere. When a modern populist says that the elites have "betrayed the real country," he is assuming yet another answer — that there is a real country beneath the legal one, that it has a moral standing the legal one lacks, and that he speaks for it. None of these speakers has done the philosophical work to earn his assumption. Each is shouting into a public square in which the foundational question has been bracketed, and the bracketing is mistaken for neutrality.

The effect, across a generation, has been to reduce the citizen to a series of pictures, each of them partial and each of them distorting. The citizen has become a consumer, whose sovereignty consists in choosing among goods others have produced and whose political life is exhausted by the act of preferring this product to that one — and, by analogy, this candidate to that one. The citizen has become a voter, a unit measured every few years and otherwise left alone, addressed primarily through algorithmic advertising and dismissed between election cycles. The citizen has become a taxpayer, a source of revenue whose moral claim on the system is proportional to the size of his contribution, which is the picture the libertarian right and the technocratic center share without wanting to admit it. The citizen has become a victim, a recipient of harms whose political identity is organized around the wrong done to him and whose legitimate claims are indexed to that wrong, which is the picture much of the contemporary left has inherited without examining. Each of these pictures is an answer to the oldest question — a very thin answer, an answer small enough to fit inside a slogan, but an answer — and each of them, put into political practice, produces a characteristic deformation of public life.

The consumer-citizen cannot build anything, because he cannot conceive of a good that is not private. The voter-citizen cannot sustain anything, because his participation is too thin and too episodic to form habits. The taxpayer-citizen cannot love anything, because his relation to the polity is a cost-benefit calculation that returns, as cost-benefit calculations do, a negative answer in most of the cases that matter. The victim-citizen cannot act in concert with anyone who is not himself a victim in the same register, which is to say, cannot act politically at all. What these deformed pictures have in common is that they all locate the citizen outside the political community, looking in — as customer, as spectator, as contributor, as complainant — rather than inside it as a member. And they do so, we think, because the deeper question of what it means to be inside has been abandoned. You cannot recover a practice you have ceased to describe.

This is why we insist on starting here. The argument of this book is that every serious contemporary diagnosis of our public life — the loneliness, the polarization, the institutional rot, the platform pathologies, the rise of mass movements on left and right — is downstream of a forgotten philosophical question. Get the question back, and most of the contemporary confusions begin to sort themselves out. Leave the question buried, and you are condemned to treat symptoms whose root you have agreed not to see.

***

Consider the word itself. *Citizen* is not a neutral English term, though it functions as one in ordinary speech. It is the Anglicization of the French *citoyen*, which is the Frenchification of the Latin *civis*, which is the Latinization of a concept the Romans inherited and transformed from the Greek *polites*. At every stage the word has carried two meanings in tension — membership in a political community and participation in its common life — and the tension has been productive rather than accidental. If the etymology is worth pausing over, it is because the English version of the word has lost the tension almost entirely. "Citizen" in contemporary American English means something close to *person with papers*. In its oldest and most demanding sense, it meant something much nearer to *partner in a common thing*.

The Greek *polites* is built on the root *polis*, the self-governing city. A *polites* was not merely someone who lived inside the walls; metics and slaves lived inside the walls, and they were not *politai*. A *polites* was a full partner in the ruling of the city — someone who served on juries, voted in the assembly, held offices by rotation, fought in the phalanx, and inherited the founding stories of the place as his own. Aristotle, in Book III of the *Politics*, defines the citizen precisely as one who "shares in ruling and being ruled" — not a subject of rule, not merely a rights-bearer, but a participant in the reciprocal authority of a free community. The definition is relentlessly participatory. A citizen who does nothing is a citizen in name only; a citizen who is ruled without ever ruling is not yet a citizen at all. And the community itself, on Aristotle's account, is not a mere alliance of individuals for mutual advantage. It is a *koinonia*, a communion, organized around a shared conception of the human good and sustained by the friendship of its members. A *polites* is not a customer of his polis. He is, in a deep sense, part of what the polis is.

The Latin *civis* carries the same structure but inflects it differently. The Romans were not Athenians. Their city was not a face-to-face democracy; their political imagination was shaped by the long history of the republic, by law, by contract, by the structural checks that ran between Senate and people, consul and tribune, patrician and plebeian. Cicero, in *De Re Publica*, gave the tradition its most enduring formulation. The *res publica*, he wrote, is not any gathering of human beings any way associated, but an *iuris consensu et utilitatis communione sociatus* — an assemblage united by a common agreement about law and by a common sharing in advantage. The phrase is untranslatable in its density, but its two pillars are worth holding. First, the republic is a *res* — a *thing*, a public thing, something that has being apart from any one of its members. Second, it is the property of a people, the *populus*, defined not as an aggregate but as a moral community constituted by its agreement about justice. A *civis* is not just a resident of Rome. He is a co-owner of the *res publica*, with obligations to it and rights within it, and his political life is the exercise of that co-ownership. When the *res publica* falls into the hands of a single man, as Cicero knew it was falling in his lifetime, it ceases to be a *res publica* at all — no matter what anyone continues to call it. The vocabulary already includes a warning against its own degradation.

By the time the word reaches the French Revolution, it has acquired a still more charged edge. The *citoyen* of 1789 is not an inheritance from the past but a claim against it. To be addressed as *citoyen* rather than *monsieur* was to be addressed as a moral equal, a partner in the new order, a bearer of rights that could not be alienated by throne or altar. The word carried the democratic revolution inside it. And yet the revolutionaries who used it drew, often quite explicitly, on the Roman and Greek vocabulary they had read in their *lycées*: the *citoyen* was the *civis*, the *civis* was the *polites*, and all three were members of a self-governing people who had taken their freedom seriously enough to fight for it. The word, across the whole arc of its history, refuses the modern contrast between individual and community. To be a citizen is to be a particular kind of individual — one whose personhood is partly constituted by belonging to a free commonwealth, and whose freedom is partly the freedom to help shape what that commonwealth will become.

This is not an antiquarian point. The reason the word matters is that in carrying forward its etymology we are carrying forward the weight of the oldest answer — the civic-republican answer — woven into the term itself. When a modern American hears the word *citizen*, even if he does not know any of this, some trace of it reaches him. He feels, obscurely, that a citizen is not merely a resident and not merely a voter, that the word is asking more of him than either of those pallid terms would. He is right. The word is asking more of him. It has been asking more of him for twenty-five hundred years.

The modern difficulty is that the rest of our political vocabulary has been emptied of the corresponding demand. We speak of *users*, *consumers*, *stakeholders*, *constituents*, *followers*, *subscribers* — a whole lexicon in which the political subject is constructed as recipient rather than partner. The citizen survives in this company as a ceremonial relic, invoked on Independence Day and forgotten the rest of the year. And the invocation does not save him, because a word that is not practiced does not carry the concept it names. The Roman senators continued to convene after Augustus had drained the republic of its substance. They continued to call themselves senators. It did not help them, and it did not help the republic. The office outlived the thing.

***

Here the central tension of this book begins to show itself. The case we have just made — that citizenship is a participatory, public, communal thing, with its roots in Aristotle and Cicero rather than in Locke and Kant — is a case that leans hard in one direction. And the direction is only half the truth we want to tell. A tradition that reveres the polis and says nothing about the individual is a tradition that has forgotten what the modern moral revolution has taught us: that every human being, before he is a citizen, is a *person*, an end in himself, whose dignity does not wait upon his political contributions and cannot be revoked by his community. The Greek polis was, among other things, a society of slaveholders. The Roman *res publica* was a society in which the humanity of women, of non-citizens, of provincials, was systematically undercounted. The genius of the modern age — the genius of Locke and Kant and Mill and the long liberal tradition that has flowed from them — has been the insistence that the person cannot be reduced to his civic role. There is a Kantian dignity in every human being that we are not free to disregard in the name of a common good. There is a Millian liberty of conscience and of expression that no majority has the standing to extinguish. There is a Rawlsian conception of equal moral status that has become, for any decent modern polity, a floor beneath which it cannot sink.

To write as though we could simply revive the classical conception of citizenship without carrying those modern achievements forward would be to write nostalgia rather than argument. It would be, in fact, to misread the classical tradition itself — because the classical conception was always tangled up with the inequalities of its time, and what we admire in it is precisely what survives the stripping away of those inequalities. What we need is not a return to the polis of free adult male property-owners but an extension of what the polis *meant* to everyone. The ancient insight that the human being is completed through participation in a common life has to be married to the modern insight that the human being has a standing prior to any common life. Neither insight, alone, is adequate. Each, alone, produces the pathologies that the other tradition rightly fears.

We approach this marriage, the two of us who are writing, from different sides. One of us instinctively begins with the individual — with the reasoning person who must not be absorbed into any mass, whose conscience must not be colonized, whose freedom to think and to disagree is the condition of every genuine political act. The other instinctively begins with the polity — with the common life in which character is formed, without which the reasoning individual is a fiction and the disagreements are tribal noise. We have spent several years arguing with each other about which beginning is the right one. We have come to the conclusion, and we will argue it through this volume, that the apparent disagreement is the book: the two halves are not rivals but necessary complements, and the tradition we will end up defending — what Richard Dagger has named *republican liberalism*, and what we will extend — is the one that holds them together.

The name is unlovely but precise. *Republican* points to the older tradition of participation, civic virtue, and liberty as non-domination — the tradition in which the citizen is a partner in a common thing. *Liberal* points to the modern tradition of individual rights, freedom of conscience, and equal moral standing — the tradition in which the citizen is an individual whose dignity is not at the disposal of the community. The two words, held together, describe a human being who is at once a member and a self, a partner and an agent, a participant and a rights-bearer. A citizen, on this picture, is an individual *who belongs*. He belongs without ceasing to be an individual. He is an individual who does not confuse his freedom with his isolation.

Stating the thesis this bluntly is already to preview what the rest of Volume I will have to do. We have to show why neither of the component traditions, on its own, can carry the weight. We have to show why the other two serious answers — the communitarian and the post-liberal — are indispensable critics of the liberal and the republican strands, and why, for all their power, they cannot be the final word either. We have to show that the marriage is philosophically possible and not just rhetorically convenient. We have to show, finally, that this is the answer most closely matched to the American tradition itself — a tradition whose distinguishing feature, from the Federalist and Anti-Federalist debates forward, has been the refusal to choose cleanly between the individual and the common thing. That is the work of the six chapters that follow.

***

One more commitment is worth declaring, because it will determine everything that follows. We do not intend to begin from policy, and we do not intend to begin from party. We do not intend, in this volume, to recommend a reform of campaign finance or a change to the primary system or a new media regulation. Those arguments have their place — we will make some of them in Volume III — but they do not belong at the beginning of a book like this one, and the reason is not a matter of prudence but of method. A political program that is not grounded in a clear conception of the citizen will, over time, produce outcomes the program's authors did not intend. The history of the twentieth century is in part a history of political programs whose authors had no considered answer to the oldest question, and who were therefore unable to recognize the humanity they were grinding under when their programs met reality. A movement that knows what it wants to do but cannot say what a person is in public life will eventually find itself doing things to persons that it cannot justify to anyone, including itself.

So we begin, instead, with first principles. We begin by asking what a citizen *is*. We will not, in doing this, pretend to stand outside our own moral commitments; we are Americans and children of the Western tradition, writing for a readership that is largely the same, and the whole project is shot through with inheritances we could not disown if we wanted to. But we can at least try to make the commitments visible rather than smuggling them in under the cover of policy proposals. If we are right that the citizen has gone missing, then the first task is to describe the figure whose absence we are naming. Everything else — the diagnosis in Volume II, the program in Volume III — follows from that description.

The chapters that come next will take the four traditions in turn. Chapter 2 will enter the republican inheritance, from Aristotle's *Politics* through Cicero's *De Re Publica* and Machiavelli's *Discourses* to Arendt's space of appearance and Pettit's non-domination — the long argument that the citizen is completed by public life and that liberty consists in not being at the mercy of another's will. Chapter 3 will present the liberal revolution, from Locke and Kant through Mill and Berlin to Rawls and Fukuyama — the argument that the person precedes the polity, that rights are held against the state, and that the bracketing of substantive conceptions of the good is not an evasion but a moral achievement. Chapter 4 will present the communitarian reply, from Sandel's critique of Rawlsian neutrality through MacIntyre's *After Virtue* and Taylor's *Sources of the Self* — the argument that the unencumbered self of liberal theory is a philosophical fiction that, when believed, produces sociological catastrophe. Chapter 5 will take up the post-liberal turn, from Deneen through Vermeule and Ahmari and Hazony, and will engage its claim that the liberal order has succeeded in exactly the ways that matter most, and that its success is the problem. Chapter 6 will argue that the American founding debate, between the Federalists and Anti-Federalists, is the first great local version of the quarrel among all four, and that Tocqueville, Lincoln, and Dewey are the American voices in which its deepest resolutions have been glimpsed. And Chapter 7 will offer the synthesis we have named — *republican liberalism* — in the form we intend to carry into the rest of the book.

That is the shape of Volume I. It is meant to be the foundation on which the diagnosis of Volume II and the program of Volume III can stand, and it is meant to be more than preparation. A philosophical foundation that answers its question is already, in some sense, a victory. The question, to restate it one last time in the simplest form we can find, is this: *what does it mean to be a person in public with other people?* Not what does it mean to be entertained by them, or to trade with them, or to defeat them, or to blame them, but to be *with* them, in a common thing, under laws we are both bound by, toward ends we share because we have agreed to share them. The woman at the kitchen table knows, in her bones, that this is the question her evening has forced upon her. She has been told for most of her adult life that she does not need to answer it — that all she needs to do is consume, vote, pay, complain, and hope. She is beginning to suspect, in the silence after the closed laptop, that the telling was a lie. She is right. She is going to need a better answer, and so, all of us, are we.

Let us see whether one can be found.
