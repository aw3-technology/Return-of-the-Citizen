# Chapter 10
## Bowling Alone, Still

Before we do anything else in this chapter, we want to say plainly what the evidence shows. Robert Putnam's argument that the civic and associational life of the United States underwent a sustained decline in the second half of the twentieth century — first published as an essay in the *Journal of Democracy* in 1995, expanded into the book *Bowling Alone* in 2000, and revised with a new introduction and updated data in 2020 — is the most empirically grounded civic-decline thesis in print. It has been caricatured. It has been disputed. It has occasionally been reported, especially on the internet, as disproven. It has not been disproven. Its core findings have held up across three decades of replication, extension, and reanalysis. The decline Putnam identified is real, it has been measured in many ways by many hands, and on the measures that matter most it is worse now than it was when he first put his numbers on paper. That is where a serious treatment of the social-capital argument has to begin: not with the controversy, which is mostly a matter of misreading, but with the findings.

What the findings say is that somewhere between roughly 1960 and roughly 1995, Americans stopped doing a great many of the things that had previously made up the ordinary texture of their shared life. They attended fewer public meetings. They signed fewer petitions. They belonged to fewer organizations that actually met, as distinct from mail-in check-writing memberships in national advocacy groups. They knew their neighbors less well. They met their friends less often in person. They had people over for dinner less often. They participated less in the specific voluntary associations that had been the weight-bearing beams of American civic life — the Parent Teacher Association, the Elks, the Rotary, the Knights of Columbus, the B'nai B'rith, the women's clubs, the union local, the bowling league, the Masons, the volunteer fire department. And they reported having fewer close friends and fewer people they could confide in about something that mattered.

This is not a list of nostalgic grievances. It is a list of survey findings, repeated, cross-tabulated, checked against competing datasets, age-adjusted, and replicated by scholars who had no stake in Putnam's thesis. The cumulative force of the list is the book's argument. The decline is not uniform and it is not universal; some forms of community involvement went up during the period Putnam studied — parents coach their children's soccer more than they did in 1960, for example, and some online forms of engagement have grown since — but the overall trend across the range of measures he examined is robustly downward and has held up whether one looks across cohorts, across age groups, or across periods. The updated 2020 edition shows the curve continuing in the same direction through roughly 2015, with certain specific measures, especially those bearing on loneliness and on the availability of close confidants, now visibly worse than they were in 2000.

It is worth pausing on what gives this literature its unusual weight. *Bowling Alone* is not the report of a single survey or the argument of a single dataset. It is the synthesis of approximately half a million interviews, drawn from the DDB Needham Life Style surveys, the General Social Survey, the Roper Social and Political Trends archive, the National Election Studies, Gallup, the Yankelovich Monitor, and time-diary studies in which Americans recorded, sometimes to the quarter-hour, how they actually spent their days. The argument that emerges is a convergence. When several independent instruments — asking different questions, to different samples, at different times, by different researchers — all point in the same direction, the direction has a different evidentiary status than any single arrow would have. That is why the book does what it does. It is not a theory in search of data. It is a pattern that became visible once somebody put enough of the data in one place to see it.

***

The finding inside this finding, and the one that matters most for the argument of this book, is Putnam's distinction between bonding and bridging social capital. The distinction is not original to him — sociologists had been circling the idea for years, and Mark Granovetter's famous 1973 paper on "the strength of weak ties" is an ancestor — but Putnam gave it the form in which it has entered general usage, and he put it to work in a way that illuminates the present crisis more sharply than any other single conceptual move in the literature.

Bonding social capital is the kind of tie that binds people who are similar to one another: extended family, ethnic church, fraternity or sorority, the group of friends from the old neighborhood. Bonding ties are inward-facing. They are essential — they carry people through unemployment, illness, grief, the birth of children, the death of parents — and no serious observer wants to see them weaken. But they have their own characteristic pathology, which is that they reinforce the tribe as a tribe. A community composed only of bonding capital is a community prepared to stand with its own against others.

Bridging social capital is the kind of tie that connects people who are *not* similar: the co-worker with different politics whom you nevertheless see every week, the neighbor at a different income level with whom you share a street, the cross-denominational service project, the mixed-race bowling team, the school board meeting where the lawyer and the welder end up on the same committee. Bridging ties are outward-facing. They do the work that a diverse democracy needs done, because they produce the cross-cutting obligations that moderate factional identity. The man who goes to the Rotary every Thursday with people who voted differently than he did cannot comfortably believe that the people who voted differently are monsters, because he has been eating pancakes with them for fifteen years and he knows perfectly well that they are not. The woman who volunteers at the food pantry alongside the evangelical who disagrees with her on almost every political question nonetheless has to concede that the evangelical is there, bagging groceries, at six in the morning. Bridging ties put faces on abstractions. They put manners on disagreements. They make pluralism survivable.

Putnam's evidence is that both bonding and bridging capital have been eroding in the United States, but that bridging capital has been eroding faster. That finding is, for our purposes, the one to sit with. The specific forms of association that are disappearing most rapidly are precisely the ones that used to throw different kinds of Americans into the same room on a recurring basis — the union local that mixed the plant floor with the front office, the fraternal order that mixed the merchant with the mechanic, the mainline church that mixed the schoolteacher with the small-business owner, the PTA that mixed the stay-at-home mother with the working mother and the father whose shift started at five. As those ties erode, the only ties left, for many people, are ties to people more or less exactly like themselves. And Madison's mechanism from *Federalist 10* — the mechanism by which a large commercial republic, rich in cross-cutting memberships, was supposed to check the tyranny of any single faction — begins quietly, structurally, to fail. If a citizen belongs to no association that cuts across the lines of political identity, then political identity stops being one loyalty among many and starts being the loyalty that organizes all the others. This is the pathway by which affective polarization, which we will come to in Chapter 12, is not merely a bad mood; it is the predictable output of a social structure in which bridging ties have grown thin.

***

Putnam's account of *why* the decline happened is, in its way, as striking as the account of *that* it happened. Sitting with his data, he arrives at a causal decomposition that has become famous among people who read this literature: about ten percent of the decline, he estimates, is attributable to pressures of time and money, especially on two-earner households whose schedules do not permit the weeknight meeting or the Saturday potluck; about ten percent to suburbanization and automobile commuting, which lengthen the daily return home and pull social life indoors; about twenty-five percent to the rise of electronic entertainment, primarily television, which by the late 1990s was consuming on the order of four hours of the average American's day; and — the largest share, and the most uncomfortable — about fifty percent to generational change.

We want to be honest that these numbers are Putnam's best estimate, derived from regression decompositions that he explains at length in an appendix of the book and that any reader is welcome to go poke at. Nobody, including Putnam, believes the estimates are precise to the percentage point. What the decomposition captures is the ordering: the generational factor is much the largest, television is second, suburbanization and household time pressures are real but relatively modest. Any debate about the details does not disturb the shape.

The generational finding is worth dwelling on because it is the one most likely to be misread. Putnam's claim is not that older Americans are morally superior. His claim is statistical. A particular cohort — roughly, Americans born between 1910 and 1940, the cohort that came of age during the Depression, the New Deal, and the Second World War — was unusually civically engaged across the entire arc of its life. Its members joined more organizations, attended more meetings, knew more neighbors, voted at higher rates, and remained engaged into old age at rates we have not seen since. Putnam calls them the "long civic generation." They grew up in a moment that demanded shared effort and delivered, in return, a shared public world; they carried the habits of that moment with them through the rest of their lives. The cohorts that followed — Boomers, Generation X, Millennials — have each been less civically engaged than the cohort before, and the decline has not been recaptured by any later mobilization.

What this means, and this is the uncomfortable part, is that a great deal of the civic decline we observe is not a change in behavior among the same Americans. It is a change in *which* Americans are alive. The long civic generation died or aged out. It was replaced, steadily, by cohorts that had not been formed in its particular school. Nobody in the post-1940 cohorts lived through the precise conjunction of events that had produced those habits, and the habits themselves — the Thursday-night meeting, the union card, the church committee, the Rotary pin — did not transmit the way the generation that formed them had expected they would.

The generational finding has an implication that Putnam does not quite say outright but that we will say here: changing the civic trajectory of the country is going to be much harder than a policy intervention can accomplish, because it requires changing the dispositions of people who are already adults. The dispositions were laid down early, by what did and did not happen in the first twenty years of a person's life. You do not reverse that by writing a memo, or by passing a law, or by launching a campaign. You reverse it, if you reverse it, by changing the texture of ordinary experience for people who are young now, so that the cohort rising behind us has something to grow into that previous cohorts did not. This is a slow proposition. It is also, we will argue in Volume III, the only proposition available.

It is worth adding that the generational picture is a picture of something concrete, not of something mystical. The long civic generation was not formed by some vanished American essence; it was formed by particular experiences in particular places. Its members had, as children and young adults, actually sat in meetings where decisions were made. They had actually been told, at twelve or fourteen, to run an errand for the Lions Club. They had actually stood next to adults who were not their parents, doing work with those adults in a shared cause. They had, most of them, learned to drive a car on a country road where the nearest helpful stranger was a neighbor; they had, most of them, walked into a church or a synagogue on a schedule not of their choosing; they had been drafted, or watched their brothers and cousins be drafted, into a national undertaking that was not theoretical. The civic habit is not, at bottom, an attitude. It is a muscle that has to be built by use, early and repeatedly, in the company of people already using it. The post-war cohorts did not, by and large, have those experiences in anything like the same intensity. The cohorts after them had less of them still. The decline is not a moral failure of the young; it is the predictable trajectory of a society that has, with the best of intentions and for a long list of understandable reasons, organized itself so that those experiences are rarer for every successive generation of children.

***

The 2020 revision of *Bowling Alone* is where the story gets darker, and where the readers who had hoped that the trends of the 1990s were a temporary blip find themselves confronted with the fact that they were not. The decline did not stop in 2000. It continued. On almost every measure Putnam foregrounded in the original book, the data through the mid-2010s show the line moving in the same direction it was already moving, sometimes at the same slope and sometimes at a slightly steepened one. Organizational membership continued to fall. Informal socializing continued to fall. The number of Americans who report that they have no close friend in whom they can confide about important matters continued to rise, and by some measures the rise accelerated after 2000.

The measure that has drawn the most attention, because it is the most human, is the one about confidants. When the General Social Survey asks Americans how many people they can discuss important matters with, the modal answer in 1985 was three. By the mid-2000s the modal answer, in at least one widely reported analysis, was zero — that is, a plurality of Americans reported having no one with whom they could discuss important matters. That specific finding has been debated and, in some reanalyses, softened; the trend line has been shown to depend on survey-method choices that matter. We do not want to overstate the precision of the number. But even the conservative reading of the literature is bleak. Across a range of instruments, Americans report fewer close relationships, less trust in their neighbors, and more loneliness than they did a generation ago, and the Surgeon General's 2023 advisory naming loneliness an epidemic is not a rhetorical flourish but an acknowledgment of what the data have been showing for some time.

The 2020 revision also addresses, squarely, the hope that had animated a lot of commentary in the early 2000s — the hope that online association would substitute for the decline of in-person association, and that the membership of Facebook groups, Reddit communities, Discord servers, and Twitter followings would come to count, in the civic ledger, as the functional equivalent of what was lost when the bowling league folded. Putnam's assessment, based on the evidence he can assemble, is that the substitution has not happened in the measures that matter most. Online forms of association have provided certain real goods — for the geographically isolated, for people with rare interests or rare identities, for diasporic communities — that would not otherwise be available, and we do not want to pretend otherwise. But the specific function that the in-person association performed — reliable, durable, face-to-face, locally situated, cross-cutting, repeated over years — has not been replaced by anything the internet has yet produced. The kinds of ties that are thickest online are precisely the kinds that are weakest on the civic dimension: ties within identity groups rather than across them, episodic rather than durable, performative rather than obligating.

***

A book that has been the subject of as much argument as Putnam's has generated its share of objections, and a responsible treatment of the argument has to take them seriously. The three we hear most often go roughly as follows.

The first objection is that Putnam was measuring the wrong things — that the decline of the Elks and the Kiwanis is simply the replacement of one set of associations by another, that the American genius for voluntary association has reinvented itself in online form, and that the real story of the last fifty years is a transformation, not a decline. This objection has a kernel of truth and a larger hollow core. The kernel is that certain forms of weak-tie engagement — signing an online petition, donating to a national advocacy group, joining a hashtag, participating in a subreddit — have grown. The hollow core is that those forms do not produce the civic goods that were produced by the forms they are supposed to have replaced. A follower count is not a neighbor. A Slack channel is not a union hall. A subreddit is not a church. The evidence that the thicker forms have shrunk is not disturbed by the evidence that the thinner forms have grown; the two findings are compatible, and Putnam has been careful about acknowledging both. If anything, the rise of the thinner forms is part of the problem, because it gives people a socially acceptable way to feel that they are participating while sparing them the inconvenience of actually being present to other people.

The second objection is more philosophical. It holds that civic life was overrated to begin with — that the Elks and the Rotary were instruments of conformity, that the PTA was a vehicle of white middle-class gatekeeping, that the church potluck was a scene of quiet patriarchy, and that if those things have declined, good riddance. We want to grant the portion of this criticism that is true. The mid-twentieth-century associational world was in many respects unjust. It excluded women from offices of real authority; it was in many places racially segregated; it enforced conformities that deserved to be broken. That is part of why its decline was for a long time greeted, in educated circles, with more relief than alarm. But the argument is not an argument against Putnam's findings. It is a refusal of the question he is asking. The question is whether the associational life that was lost performed civic functions whose loss has cost us something, and the evidence that it did is overwhelming: civic participation correlates, across a very large literature, with better health, longer life, better educational outcomes for children, higher institutional trust, lower rates of political extremism, and higher subjective well-being. The old associational world had injustices inside it that had to be corrected. What did not have to follow, and what in fact followed anyway, was the loss of the associational world itself. That is a loss that is not redeemed by the correction of the injustice, and anyone tempted to shrug at it has not reckoned seriously with what the loss has done.

The third objection is that the decline is not as bad as Putnam says. This is the objection that the 2020 update most directly answers. On the measures Putnam foregrounded — organizational membership, informal socializing, institutional trust, the count of close confidants — the picture has, if anything, darkened since 2000. The scholarly community that has worked most carefully with these datasets, including researchers who began skeptical of Putnam, has largely converged on the view that the core findings are robust. What is debated are the magnitudes, the exact timing of the turning points, and the relative weight of the various causal factors. What is not seriously debated, in the literature we have read, is the direction.

We should add one further caveat, which Putnam himself has developed in later work and which matters enormously for the argument of this book. The decline has not been evenly distributed across American society. In *Our Kids*, published in 2015, Putnam documented, with the same combination of statistical patience and ethnographic portraiture that characterizes *Bowling Alone*, that the collapse of civic and associational life has been far more severe at the bottom of the American class structure than at the top. Professional-class Americans have kept, if in diminished form, a great deal of the associational infrastructure they inherited: their children still do the extracurriculars, their churches and synagogues still function, their neighborhoods still have functioning PTAs and block parties, their marriages are more stable, their friendships denser. Working-class Americans, especially those without a four-year college degree, have experienced a much more severe stripping-out of every one of those resources. The marriage rate has fallen more sharply; the congregational attachment has collapsed more completely; the extracurriculars have become unaffordable; the unions that once provided a robust civic infrastructure of their own have largely disappeared. To speak of a general "American" decline, as if the line were the same in Darien and in Dayton, is to miss what *Our Kids* documents so painfully: that the people who had the least civic slack to lose are the people who have lost the most. Any honest reading of the social-capital literature has to acknowledge that the decline is a class story as much as it is a national one, and that any program of renewal that does not reach the communities that have lost the most will be a program of renewal for people who already have enough.

The contrast Putnam draws in *Our Kids* between the Port Clinton, Ohio, of his own 1950s childhood and the Port Clinton of the 2010s is worth holding in mind as an image. The high school he graduated from had, in his telling, been a genuine cross-class institution — the children of the factory owner and the children of the factory worker sat next to each other in the same classrooms, played on the same teams, attended the same parties, and ended up at roughly comparable adult destinations in roughly comparable proportions. Two generations later, the same town had been split by the decline of its manufacturing base into two more or less separate worlds, one of which still had the old social infrastructure more or less intact and one of which had effectively lost it. The children of the first world still had coaches and mentors and youth-group leaders and college-educated parents and intact marriages; the children of the second world often had none of those things. Both groups of children lived within the same town limits and attended, at least nominally, the same public school. Their civic inheritance was not the same. The political implication is stark. When we speak, in the chapters ahead, about the rise of populist movements and the appeal of tribal politics, we should not be surprised that the movements and the tribalism have found their most fervent constituencies in communities where the associational infrastructure has most completely collapsed. Hoffer and Arendt would not have been surprised. The lonely communities are the ones available for capture.

***

We can now say, in the larger frame of this book, what Putnam's evidence is doing. The previous chapter made the philosophical case that mass movements recruit from the atomized — that the lonely, world-stripped individual, in Arendt's phrase, is the common ground for terror, and that Hoffer's true believer is fleeing a self that has nothing to hold onto. That case was made in the language of political philosophy and the psychology of mass formation, and it was made, chiefly, by two thinkers working in the aftermath of European catastrophe and writing in the shadow of Hitler and Stalin.

Putnam's evidence is what that case looks like when it is made in the language of survey instruments and longitudinal data about the United States at the end of the twentieth century. Americans have been losing, measurably, for decades, the associational life that was once the school of citizenship. They have been losing the specific kind of associational life — bridging, cross-cutting, durable, local, face-to-face — that thickens a pluralist democracy and makes factional identity bearable. They have been losing the confidants, the neighbors, the weeknight meetings, the weekend potlucks, the committees, the leagues, the halls. And the rise of mass formations in our politics, the rise of the tribal loyalties in which a great many Americans now experience their political identity, is not a coincidence with this loss. It is, at least in large part, its consequence. You cannot strip out the associational life that forms the citizen and expect the citizen to keep showing up. What shows up instead is the audience for the movement.

This is where we want to be careful about the scale of our claim. We are not saying that Putnam's findings explain everything, and neither is Putnam. Affective polarization has drivers of its own. The collapse of institutional trust has drivers of its own. The transformation of the public sphere by platform capitalism has drivers of its own. The hollowing-out of work and merit has drivers of its own. Each of the chapters that follow will take up one of those partial explanations in turn. What the social-capital literature provides is not the complete theory of how we got here. It is the empirical connective tissue that runs under the other explanations. It is the evidence that underneath the polarization, underneath the platform pathologies, underneath the distrust, underneath the populist surges, the associational ground has been thinning for sixty years, and that thinning is part of what makes the rest of it possible. A society with robust bridging ties can absorb a great deal of polarizing pressure without breaking. A society in which the bridging ties have frayed is a society in which the same pressure cracks things.

***

There is a way of telling this story that leaves the reader with nothing but despair, and we want to close the chapter by refusing that way. Putnam's 2020 follow-up volume, *The Upswing*, written with Shaylyn Romney Garrett, makes an argument we consider essential to the forward-looking half of this book and to which Volume III will return in detail. The argument is that the United States has been through something like this before.

In the decades before the turn of the twentieth century — the period we call, after Mark Twain, the Gilded Age — the country displayed a roster of pathologies that will be uncannily familiar to a contemporary reader. Inequality was high and rising. Political corruption was open and brazen. Trust in institutions was at a low ebb. Immigration and urbanization were pulling the country into new and unsettling configurations. The party system was rigid, polarized, and unresponsive to actual problems. Mass movements of various flavors — populist, nativist, utopian — surged across the landscape, each promising a total break with a present that was felt to be unendurable. Civic life was thin in the places that had been thickened by migration and commerce; the associational infrastructure that would later undergird the mid-twentieth-century boom had not yet been built.

What followed, Putnam and Garrett document, was a long, slow, bottom-up civic revival. It began not in Washington but in settlement houses and women's clubs and Chautauqua tents, in the founding of the Rotary and the Boy Scouts and the Kiwanis, in the professionalization of medicine and law and journalism, in the building of the modern university and the modern public library and the modern city park. It was followed, and reinforced, by the political reforms we associate with the Progressive Era: civil service, direct election of senators, the secret ballot, the regulatory state. It took decades. It was uneven and incomplete. It carried forward, because it had to, many of the injustices of the society that produced it. But it happened. The country pulled itself, over forty years, out of a trough that had looked from the inside like a terminal decline, and it did so through a million small civic acts that were, most of them, not undertaken as grand political projects but as particular responses to particular local conditions.

*The Upswing* is the single most hopeful book on our desk as we write this, and we will return to it at length in Volume III. It is hopeful not because it promises that everything will be fine — Putnam and Garrett do not make that promise, and we will not either — but because it demonstrates that the shape of the curve we are currently on is not a new shape in American history, and that the last time the country was on a similar curve, it climbed out. The climb was not accomplished by any one generation, any one movement, any one piece of legislation, or any one charismatic leader. It was accomplished by an accumulation of people, over a very long time, deciding that the civic world they had been handed was not good enough and choosing to build a better one, one association at a time.

***

We want to close with an honest accounting of where the Putnam tradition does and does not get us, because readers who carry the argument of this chapter into the rest of the book should know its limits as well as its strengths.

The first limit is that Putnam's own prescriptions, in his own writing, tend to be modest, and they have been criticized, not unfairly, as insufficient to the scale of the problem he documents. The closing chapters of *Bowling Alone* offer a list of suggestions — civic education, youth engagement, workplace reform, religious revival, arts and culture, better urban design — that are unobjectionable and, in our view, largely correct, but that do not amount to a program capable of reversing a sixty-year generational trend. Putnam is a careful social scientist, and the carefulness that makes his diagnosis trustworthy makes his prescriptions cautious. We will need more than caution in Volume III.

The second limit is that social-capital language, in less careful hands than Putnam's, can become anodyne. It can be mobilized to dress up nostalgia as policy — to advocate for Things As They Were as though the mid-century associational world were the only possible civic world, and as though its injustices were a price worth quietly paying for its benefits. That is not Putnam's position, but it is a position that is sometimes taken in his name. Any serious program of civic renewal has to reckon with the fact that we cannot and should not reconstruct the particular associational world of 1955. The world we build has to be a world compatible with the freedoms and equalities we have gained in the decades since. The tradition represented by Putnam does not supply, on its own, the philosophical account of what should and should not be carried forward.

The third and deepest limit is that the social-capital tradition is empirical and pragmatic, and it has no built-in philosophical account of why civic life matters beyond its instrumental benefits. *Bowling Alone* tells us, in great and convincing detail, that civic participation correlates with longer lives, better-educated children, higher institutional trust, and lower political extremism. These are real goods and they are worth naming. But they do not, by themselves, tell us why a person should care about public life if she happens to be lucky enough to be healthy, well-educated, trusting, and moderate without it. They do not tell us why participation in a common world is constitutive of a full human life rather than merely conducive to a long one. For that argument, we have to go back to the thinkers we engaged in the previous chapter and in the volume before it — to Arendt's claim that the space of appearance is where a person becomes real to others and thus to herself, to Hoffer's sobering insight that the lonely self, left to itself, will not remain a self for long, and to the older republican tradition in which the citizen is not a person who has been persuaded to do civic things but a person whose personhood is finished by doing them.

The Putnam evidence is, in other words, the empirical floor of the diagnosis this book offers. It is not the whole of the diagnosis, and it is not the philosophical account that makes the diagnosis matter. It is the floor. Without the floor, nothing else stands. With the floor, we can begin to build what comes next.

Americans are, on the evidence of the best longitudinal research we have, measurably more isolated, measurably less associationally engaged, and measurably more politically tribal than they were sixty years ago. The trend has continued through the most recent data we have. The bridging ties that once moderated factional identity have frayed more than the bonding ties that intensify it, with predictable consequences for our politics. The decline has been much more severe at the bottom of our class structure than at the top, with predictable consequences for whose grievances dominate our public life. And nothing about this state of affairs is a metaphor. It is a set of measured facts about the actual lives of actual people, and any response to the present crisis that does not begin with those facts is a response to something other than what is happening.

We are, as the title of this chapter suggests, still bowling alone. The bowling is worse than it was when Putnam first named it. And the question that Volume III will have to answer — the question that any honest reader of this chapter has a right to press on us — is what, concretely, is to be done. We will take up that question in its own time. For now, we stay with the picture the data draw. It is a picture of a country that has been losing, quietly, for a very long time, the ordinary social infrastructure that made citizenship possible. That infrastructure is not gone. It is thinned. It can be thickened again. The Americans who thickened it the last time did not know, at the time they began, that they were building what would become the civic world of the twentieth century. They were just doing the next thing in front of them. That is probably how it will have to begin again.
