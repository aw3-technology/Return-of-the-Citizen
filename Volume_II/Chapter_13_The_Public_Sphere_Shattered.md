# Chapter 13
## The Public Sphere Shattered

In 1962 a young German sociologist, lately returned from the rubble of his country's catastrophe and looking for the intellectual conditions that had made its democracy so brittle, published a book that would quietly rearrange the way everyone thereafter thought about democratic life. Jürgen Habermas's *The Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* is not an easy book, and it is not, in the strict sense, a book about politics at all. It is a book about a communicative space — a *Öffentlichkeit*, literally a "publicness" — that had emerged in the coffeehouses of eighteenth-century London, the salons of Paris, the reading societies and moral weeklies and early newspapers of Hamburg and Philadelphia, in which private persons assembled as a public and reasoned together about the matters the state was doing in their name. This space, Habermas argued, was the hidden precondition of liberal democracy. Formal voting rights, constitutional limits on power, free elections — none of these would have produced what we now call democratic self-government without the prior existence of a place where citizens could form, together, an opinion that deserved to be called public rather than merely aggregated.

We want to be careful about what Habermas did and did not claim, because his argument has been caricatured almost as often as it has been quoted. He did not claim that the eighteenth-century public sphere was ideal. He knew perfectly well that it was narrow. It was overwhelmingly male; it was overwhelmingly bourgeois; it excluded the laboring poor, the enslaved, and the colonized populations whose labor underwrote the commerce that paid for the coffee. It reproduced class hierarchies even as it made arguments against absolute monarchy. What Habermas claimed, more modestly and more powerfully, was that within its narrow compass this public sphere was genuinely deliberative — that when the men gathered in Lloyd's or at Madame Geoffrin's salon or in the pages of the *Tatler* argued about policy or philosophy or religion, they argued under a set of implicit rules that made the arguing worth something. Status was temporarily suspended in favor of the better argument. Reasons counted. Positions could be changed by evidence. The subject of discussion was, in principle, anything the state was doing, and nothing was in principle off the table because of who was asking.

The subsequent history of democracy, on Habermas's account, was the extension of this form beyond the class that first occupied it. The cause of democratic inclusion — the enfranchisement of the unpropertied, of women, of racial minorities, of the colonized — was the insistence that the deliberative form which had been confined to a particular class of men was the birthright of every adult. The corrective to the narrowness was not to abandon the form but to demand admission to it. The abolitionist press, the women's suffrage movement, the civil rights movement — all of them worked, in one of their registers, by appealing to the norms of the public sphere and demanding that those norms be applied without exception. They argued, in effect: *your own rules commit you to hearing us.* And because the rules were real, the rules could be invoked.

Democratic expansion, in other words, depended on the public sphere's form — not its original membership. The rules of mutual address, of reason-giving, of treating the other as someone who might convince you, were the tracks on which inclusion ran. Without them, inclusion is merely the arrival at a table where no one is talking to anyone.

This is the concept we now find in trouble.

***

Sixty years after the book, in 2022, Habermas returned to the subject. The essay — "A New Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere," published in *Theory, Culture & Society* — is the work of an old man who has lived through the rise and partial collapse of the postwar democratic settlement he helped theorize, and it is written with a sobriety that borders at moments on grief. His argument, stripped of its philosophical apparatus, is that the digitalization of public communication is not merely shifting the public sphere to new venues. It is fragmenting that sphere past the point where it can perform its democratic function.

The older public sphere, even in its imperfect mid-twentieth-century mass-media form — a few networks, a handful of newspapers of record, a shared evening news — at least produced an approximation of a common object of attention. When Walter Cronkite or Edward R. Murrow spoke about the war in Vietnam, disagreement took place on the basis of a rough shared stipulation of what had happened that day. The disagreement could be fierce; what the disagreement could not easily be was about whether the events themselves were real. Habermas's 2022 argument is that the digital public sphere has dissolved even that thin shared basis. We no longer disagree within a common frame. We disagree about the frame. And because deliberation requires, at a minimum, that the process of forming opinions be *shared* — that we be reasoning about the same object, with some minimal agreement about what counts as evidence — the disappearance of the frame is not a small thing. It means that democratic procedure can continue to be followed — elections held, laws passed, courts convened — while the substance that once made those procedures meaningful has quietly drained away. Democracy becomes procedurally intact but substantively hollow.

This is the large and careful diagnosis. It is also, we should note, one that some of the digital optimists have legitimately pushed back on. The new media environment is not merely corrosive; it has given voices to people who were excluded from the mid-century gatekept media almost as categorically as the eighteenth century excluded the poor. It is where dissident Iranian feminists and Hong Kong protesters and North African revolutionaries found each other. It is where the isolated gay kid in a hostile small town discovers he is not alone in the world. These are real democratic goods and we are not going to pretend otherwise. The question Habermas's essay forces is whether the architecture that makes those goods possible also, by the same mechanisms, makes something else — the shared frame within which democratic deliberation can take place at scale — structurally more difficult to sustain. That is the question a serious account has to engage.

***

The popular version of the argument has been made most insistently in recent years by the social psychologist Jonathan Haidt, and because his version is the one most readers will have encountered, we want to walk through it carefully and then set it alongside the more cautious empirical literature that has grown up around it.

Haidt's thesis, developed across a series of *Atlantic* essays in 2022 and 2023 and consolidated in his 2024 book *The Anxious Generation*, has two linked components. The first is about adolescent mental health: somewhere around 2012 and 2013, on measure after measure — depression, anxiety, self-harm, suicide, loneliness — something went badly wrong for American and more broadly Western adolescents, and the curves do not bend back. Girls are hit harder than boys. The cohort effect is striking enough that it is visible to anyone who has spent time on a college campus or in a high school over the past decade. The second component is about politics: the period after 2012 is also the period in which American political polarization — specifically affective polarization, the measure of how much partisans dislike and distrust each other — accelerates to levels the country has not seen since before the Second World War. Haidt's explanation for both phenomena is, in his telling, the same phenomenon: the transition, around 2010 to 2012, to a universally smartphone-based social-media environment whose algorithms are optimized for engagement, and whose engagement maxima happen to be located in emotionally activating, out-group-hostile content.

The evidence Haidt adduces is not trivial. The time-series data on adolescent depression and self-harm is, to put it bluntly, striking. The line goes flat through the 2000s and bends sharply upward around 2012 in most of the Anglosphere and much of northern Europe. The curves for screen time, social-media adoption, and smartphone ownership track almost exactly. Haidt's argument is that the simultaneity is too clean to be coincidence and that the causal story he proposes — a fundamental rewiring of adolescent social life around platforms engineered for compulsive use and optimized for outrage — has more predictive force than the competing explanations on offer. The 2008 financial crisis is too early. The opioid epidemic affected a different demographic. School-shooting fear and climate anxiety are present but fail to explain why the timing is so specific and so global.

We find this argument compelling in its broad outlines, and we are not among the critics who dismiss it as a moral panic. But we are also obligated to note that rigorous scholars disagree, and a book that pretends the disagreement is not there has already failed its own epistemic standards.

The most serious empirical skepticism comes from Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski at Oxford, whose work on adolescent well-being and screen time has consistently found that social-media use accounts for a very small fraction of the variance in adolescent mental-health outcomes — smaller than the variance explained by regular eating of breakfast, on one of their more cited calculations. Their point is not that screens are harmless. It is that the causal story Haidt tells requires effect sizes that are not consistently visible in the individual-level data, and that the ecological correlation between national smartphone adoption curves and national depression curves, while striking, does not settle the matter. Their critique has been influential enough that the most careful reviewers of the literature now describe the evidence as *suggestive but contested* rather than *established*. The most honest reading of this debate, so far as we can tell, is something like this: something real happened to adolescent well-being in the early 2010s; the phone-and-social-media hypothesis has more predictive support than the available alternatives; the effect is probably smaller than the strongest Haidt claims suggest and larger than the strongest skeptics concede. We should say what we know, admit what we don't, and stop pretending the disagreement has been resolved in favor of whichever camp we are already in.

***

On the political side, the most important countervailing voice — and the one we will spend some time with, because it has shaped our thinking more than any other single source — is the Duke sociologist Chris Bail, whose 2021 book *Breaking the Social Media Prism* is, to our minds, the most empirically careful book on the platform-and-polarization question anyone has written.

Bail's central finding is that the popular explanation of social-media polarization — *echo chambers* — is largely wrong, and wrong in an interesting way. The echo-chamber story holds that the platforms sort users into ideologically homogeneous pockets, exposure to disagreeing views is therefore reduced, and polarization increases because people never see the other side. If this were true, the obvious fix would be to expose people to more of the other side. Bail and his collaborators ran the experiment. They paid Republicans and Democrats to follow a Twitter bot that showed them content from the other side's political opinion leaders, and measured attitudes before and after. The result was not that polarization decreased. Polarization *increased*. Republicans exposed to Democratic content became more conservative; Democrats exposed to Republican content became more liberal, though the effect was smaller on that side.

The finding rhymes uncomfortably with decades of social-psychology research on what happens when identity-salient groups are exposed to opposing positions without structure, without shared task, without equal-status contact. The raw exposure is not the repair. In some conditions the raw exposure is itself the injury. A great deal of well-intentioned prescription about our current predicament — "pop your filter bubble," "read the other side's paper," "follow people you disagree with" — turns out to rest on a mechanism that does not behave the way our folk model says it does. Bail's work does not mean the prescription is always wrong. It means the prescription, administered without the structural conditions that make cross-partisan exposure generative rather than corrosive, often produces the opposite of what it promises.

What is actually happening on the platforms, on Bail's account, is something he calls the *prism effect*. Social media is a medium in which the most extreme voices on each side are disproportionately visible, because extremity is rewarded by the attention economy; the moderate voices on each side are disproportionately silent, because moderation does not move the engagement needle; and the users on each side, scrolling through feeds populated by these amplified extremes, form a picture of the other side that corresponds to almost nobody on the other side. The out-partisan one hates on the feed is a caricature. The in-partisan one sees modeled on the feed is also a caricature — an extremist who makes one's own moderate co-partisans invisible, and who thereby generates a silent secondary pressure toward extremity, or toward silence, for everyone watching.

This is the word *prism* is doing. A prism does not block the light on the other side; it distorts it. You see the other side, but you see it refracted into a shape that does not correspond to the object. And because the refraction happens on your own side as well, the image of oneself one forms on the platform is also distorted — more loyal to the tribe, more contemptuous of the opposition, more certain, more performative than one is in one's actual life off the platform. Bail's interviews with heavy platform users include a striking recurring theme: many of them describe themselves as having two political selves, an online one and an offline one, and the online one is more extreme, more angry, more scornful, than they themselves believe themselves to really be.

The mechanism that drives all of this, on Bail's account, is not information-seeking. It is status-seeking and identity performance. People post to signal group membership, not to share knowledge. The algorithms reward what gets reactions. The reactions are driven, reliably, by moral-emotional content, and especially by out-group anger. The engine is not a knowledge engine. It is a status engine — and status, in a mediated environment at global scale, turns out to select against the habits of mind a public sphere requires.

***

A more recent strand of empirical work has taken Bail's argument into the guts of the platforms themselves, where the algorithms actually live. The computer scientist Smitha Milli and her collaborators have run experiments in which users on X are randomized between the algorithmically curated timeline and a chronological baseline, and the algorithmic timeline's content is then analyzed for emotional and out-group-hostile valence. The findings, replicated now across several platforms: the algorithmic timeline contains systematically more emotionally activating content, and systematically more out-group-hostile content, than the chronological feed. Exposure to the algorithmically curated content produces measurable increases in affective polarization. And — this is the finding that should stop the reader — users report that they would *prefer* the less algorithmic timeline. The optimization is not doing what the users themselves, on reflection, want it to do.

This matters, because it moves the argument off the individual level and onto the structural level. For a long time, the debate about social media and democracy had the following shape: the pessimists blamed the platforms, the optimists blamed the users, and the argument stalled at the question of whose fault it was. The Milli-style findings suggest the question is not best posed that way. Platforms are not neutral pipes through which user preferences flow. They are optimization engines aimed at engagement. Engagement is not the same as a functioning public sphere. In most cases engagement selects *against* the conditions a functioning public sphere requires — deliberation, context, slowness, patience, the willingness to let an argument unfold over more than the seven seconds required for a thumbstroke. The problem is not that users have bad taste. The problem is that the architecture systematically amplifies the content that produces the worst democratic externalities, *even when the users themselves do not want that content to be amplified*.

This is the frame in which the platform question becomes a structural question rather than a moral one. It is not that users should have more discipline, though they should. It is not that platforms should have better ethics, though they should. It is that a communicative environment optimized for engagement will, absent countervailing structure, produce a public sphere that selects against deliberation. And a public sphere that selects against deliberation will not deliberate, no matter how many individual users maintain the private habits that a deliberative sphere would require.

***

We want to pause here to name something that strikes us, in both the empirical and the popular literature, as rare and valuable. Haidt and Bail disagree substantively. Haidt believes the platform effect is larger than Bail does; Bail believes Haidt's picture overstates the echo-chamber contribution and underweights the prism mechanism; they come out in different places on several of the key empirical questions. And yet, for the past several years, they have maintained a public, open, jointly curated Google Doc — "Social Media and Political Dysfunction: A Collaborative Review" — in which they catalogue the studies on the question they are arguing about, annotate them, disagree about what they show, and keep the document open to anyone who wants to see the current state of the argument. This, we think, is close to what a functioning public sphere ought to look like when two serious people disagree about a contested empirical question. They are not trying to destroy each other. They are not producing competing monographs that pretend the other's evidence does not exist. They are maintaining a shared reading list. They are making their disagreement visible and productive rather than invisible and corrosive. It is not a perfect model — it is two professors with secure academic posts and the resources to keep a document alive — but it is a model, and the rarity of the model is itself a piece of evidence about the ambient state of the public sphere.

***

Where, having walked through this literature, do we come out?

We think the public sphere has in fact been transformed in a way that degrades democratic deliberation, and we think the transformation is not primarily a matter of individual bad habits, though individual bad habits matter. It is a matter of the architecture of the mediated environment in which most political speech now happens. Four features of that architecture, in our view, are doing most of the damage, and we want to name them as clearly as we can.

The first is the algorithmic amplification of emotionally activating, out-group-hostile content. This is the Milli finding, replicated enough now that we should treat it as established: the ranking algorithms on the major platforms systematically elevate content that triggers moral-emotional reactions, and especially content that triggers anger at the other side. This is not a judgment about any particular platform's intentions. It is a structural consequence of optimizing for engagement in a two-camp political environment. The engagement gradient runs through out-group anger, and so the algorithm, following the gradient, ends up selecting for out-group anger.

The second is the reduction of moderate, slow, context-rich communication. The form of the platforms — short, decontextualized, punchy, reaction-inviting — is not well suited to the kind of speech a deliberative public sphere needs. This is not to romanticize the eighteenth-century salon or the mid-century newspaper of record, both of which had severe pathologies of their own. It is to note that the formal properties of the medium shape the content that flourishes in it. Habermas's argument, all the way back in 1962, was that the coffeehouse afforded a kind of exchange — in-person, extended, topic-bounded, with a stipulated suspension of status — that the print medium of the day could supplement and extend. The platform medium affords something different: rapid, context-stripped, status-saturated, reaction-driven. What flourishes in it is not what flourished in the earlier form.

The third is the collapse of the gatekeepers who, for roughly a century, enforced minimum standards of accuracy and relevance in the main channels of public speech. This one we want to be careful with. The mid-century gatekeepers were not heroes. They were largely white, largely male, largely drawn from a narrow professional class, and they used their gatekeeping power to marginalize voices that deserved to be heard. The platform revolution's democratizing effect — the fact that the previously silenced can now speak — is a real gain, and we are not nostalgic for the world that excluded them. But the disappearance of gatekeeping was not a pure liberation. The gatekeepers were also, at their best, enforcing norms of verification, of context, of relevance, of a distinction between what could be said responsibly in public and what could not. Those norms were, at a minimum, one of the mechanisms by which the mid-century public sphere produced something resembling a shared picture of the world. Their collapse has not been replaced by anything playing the same role. What we have now is less exclusion and more noise, and it is not obvious that the tradeoff, in its current form, favors democratic deliberation over the alternative.

The fourth is the emergence of what we might call a *status economy of public opinion*: a system in which performing opinion in public generates reward — attention, followers, influence, money — independent of any real-world consequences for the speaker. In older communicative environments, publicly staking a position had costs that tracked, at least loosely, the truth of the position. A reporter who filed a false story, a scholar who published a fabricated study, a pundit who made a confident prediction that failed — all faced some version of reputational consequence within a professional community that knew the field and cared about the record. The platform environment has, to a significant degree, decoupled the reward for stating an opinion from the truth of the opinion. What is rewarded is the *performance* of the opinion — its vividness, its loyalty-signal to the right audience, its out-group-hostility — and these properties are not well correlated, and are often anti-correlated, with the opinion's accuracy. In such a system the cost of being wrong about a fact is low; the cost of being off-tribe is high. The incentives run directly against the epistemic virtues a public sphere is supposed to cultivate.

The hardest honest claim we can make here — the one we keep coming back to, because it refuses to be explained away — is this: the prism Bail describes is not neutral. A medium that distorts the political landscape by amplifying extremes and silencing moderates, that decouples reward from truth, that optimizes for engagement at the expense of deliberation, is not merely a prism. It is a prism that *selects against deliberation*. A public sphere that selects against deliberation will not deliberate. You can tell the people in such a sphere to deliberate all you like. The architecture is stronger than the advice.

***

There is a complementary argument to Habermas's and Bail's that we think deserves its place in any serious account of what has happened to the public sphere, and we want to say something about it before we turn back to the book's central thesis. Jonathan Rauch's 2021 book *The Constitution of Knowledge* is, on its surface, a book about the epistemic side of the current crisis: the collapse of the institutions and norms by which a society produces warranted public belief. Peer review in the sciences. Journalistic verification and editorial correction. Professional standards in the law, in medicine, in the civil service. The accumulated practices by which a society distinguishes, imperfectly but usefully, between what is roughly known and what is merely asserted.

Rauch's claim is that these institutions, taken together, constitute something — he calls it the *constitution of knowledge* — that works in a way analogous to how the political constitution works. It distributes authority; it requires checks; it forces claims to pass through multiple points of possible disconfirmation before they can be treated as knowledge; it produces, at the far end of the process, something that is not Truth with a capital T but is far better than any single person's assertion. And his argument is that this constitution of knowledge has been under sustained assault from two directions in our moment — a trollish populist right that simply denies the authority of expertise, and an identity-saturated progressive left that denies the possibility of expertise unmarked by the identity of the expert — and that the damage from the two assaults compounds. The institutions that were supposed to produce warranted public belief are no longer doing so with anything like their former reliability. What replaces them is not a better system. It is a worse one, in which public belief is produced by the same status-performance mechanisms we have just been describing.

We read Rauch as complementary to Habermas and to Bail rather than as arguing something different. Habermas says the public sphere is where opinions are formed through shared reasoning. Bail says the current public sphere distorts the formation. Rauch says the public sphere is not only a place for forming opinions — it is one of the institutions whose properly functioning form actually produces knowledge, and when it breaks, the knowledge-producing function breaks with it. Taken together, the three arguments say something like: the space in which we are supposed to form opinions and the space in which the society is supposed to produce warranted belief are the same space, and when that space is restructured by an architecture that selects against deliberation and against verification, the society loses both the deliberative capacity and the epistemic capacity at once. It is not a crisis of minds. It is a crisis of institutional conditions for the minds.

***

We want to tie this chapter back to the book's central thesis, because the public-sphere argument is not a separate argument. It is continuous with, and makes worse, the phenomena we have been tracking since Chapter 9.

Recall where Arendt and Hoffer left us. The raw material of mass formation, they argued, is the lonely individual — the person who belongs to nothing and is thus available for capture by anything. Lonely people, they showed, do not become the cadres of mass movements because they have thought their way there. They become the cadres because the movement offers them a world to belong to, a cause to lose themselves in, a devil to hate. The movement's appeal is not cognitive. It is existential. And the pre-condition of the appeal is the prior stripping-away of the texture of ordinary life in which a person could, without a movement, be someone.

A public sphere of the kind we have just described *accelerates the formation*. Consider what the prism does, specifically, to a lonely person scrolling a feed. The feed makes the enemy vivid. The out-group, rendered in caricature by the algorithmic selection for its most extreme voices, is present at all hours, available for contempt, saturating the inner life of the viewer with the feeling of being under siege by a hostile force that wants to destroy her and everything she cares about. The feed also makes the ordinary fellow citizen abstract. The moderate neighbor, the co-worker who votes the other way but is willing to talk, the unglamorous town-meeting participant who does not care much about politics but shows up to zoning hearings — these people do not appear on the feed at all, because their speech does not trigger engagement. The public the lonely scroller inhabits, mediated through the prism, is one in which the only visible others are enemies and allies, both extreme, and the vast middle that actually constitutes the country is edited out.

This is the perfect preparation for mass formation. Arendt's lonely individual had at least the advantage of being lonely *without* a vivid picture of the enemy. The mass movements of her century had to construct that picture themselves, through propaganda organs and party newspapers and rallies. Ours is a public sphere in which the equivalent picture is constructed continuously, by no one in particular, as a byproduct of the optimization of attention. The enemy is supplied by the architecture. The movement that organizes the enemy-hatred has only to harvest it.

The argument cuts the other way on Putnam as well. If the decline of bridging social capital — the loss of the ties across lines of difference that Chapter 10 identified as the most corrosive feature of the civic collapse — was always going to be hard to reverse, the shattering of the public sphere has made it harder still. Bridging ties, historically, were formed partly in the local institutions that thinned in the second half of the twentieth century, and were partly sustained by the shared media ecology that at least gave cross-partisan Americans a rough common frame to disagree within. Both of those conditions have eroded. The local institutions have thinned. The media-ecological ground has moved. The people who might form a bridging tie are no longer inhabiting the same perceptual world to begin with, and the attempt to build a bridge across a perceptual chasm is much harder than the attempt to build one across a partisan one.

And the institutional-platform pathology we took up in Chapter 11 is, we now think, partly downstream of the public-sphere pathology. The incentives of the platform public sphere — the reward for public performance of opinion, the punishment for private craft, the substitution of brand for formation — radiate outward into the institutions themselves. A politician who inhabits a platform public sphere will become a platform politician, because the platform public sphere is the environment her reputation lives in. A journalist, a scholar, a clergyman, a doctor facing the same incentive gradient will face the same temptation. Levin's argument that institutions have become platforms is correct, but it is incomplete without the observation that the ambient medium in which all of them now operate is itself a platform. The institutions are downstream of the public sphere as much as the public sphere is downstream of the institutions. The two pathologies reinforce each other.

***

If the diagnosis is structural, what is the repair? We will spend the whole of Volume III on this question and will not try to anticipate its answers here. But we want to leave the reader with a sense of where we think the repair can plausibly happen, because a diagnosis that does not gesture toward repair is a diagnosis that breeds the fatalism it claims only to describe.

Almost none of what can be done about the public sphere happens at the scale of "the public sphere." The scale is too large; the architecture is too entrenched; the centralized reform that would be required to change the architecture is not politically available on any timeline that matters. What can be done happens at smaller scales, and the smaller the scale, the more available the action.

One scale below the national public sphere is the deliberative institution — the citizens' assembly, the deliberative poll, the structured small-group conversation across difference. The empirical literature here is encouraging, and we will spend a chapter on it. The finding, in outline, is that ordinary citizens, given time, information, and good facilitation, deliberate seriously and converge on considered positions even across severe partisan divides. The precondition is not better citizens. It is better conditions — small, bounded, face-to-face, topic-specific, with ground rules. These are the communicative conditions Habermas theorized in 1962. They are possible to reconstitute at small scales even when they are not possible to sustain at scale. They will not replace the national public sphere; they will, in some limited way, serve as its rehearsal spaces, and as existence proofs that the thing the national public sphere no longer easily produces is still producible by human beings when the conditions are right.

A scale below that is the individual practice, and here we want to say the thing that we keep wanting to say and keep suspecting we are not yet saying clearly enough. The most available action anyone in our moment has, in response to the shattered public sphere, is the refusal to participate in its worst incentives. This is not a counsel of retreat. The monastic retreat from the public is exactly the failure mode we warned against in the introduction, and we are not now reversing that. It is a counsel of discipline — the discipline of not posting the outraged take, of not taking the bait, of not performing the in-group signal, of reading the article before quote-tweeting the headline, of noticing when one is being operated on by the engagement engine and declining to be operated on. In Chapter 17 we will call this critical thinking as civic discipline and will develop it more systematically. In Chapter 20 we will return to it as a personal practice. For now we want only to name that it exists, that it is available, and that in an architecture that selects against deliberation the refusal to perform the selected-for behaviors is itself a small contribution to the sum of deliberative capacity in the society. Most of the meaningful moral action in a rigged environment is at the level of the individual choice to be rigged or not.

***

We want to close this chapter where we began it, with Habermas in 1962, and with a small observation that we have been circling for the entire chapter.

The eighteenth-century public sphere was not a natural phenomenon. It did not arise because human beings are naturally deliberative, or because the men of the Enlightenment had better habits than we do. It arose because a specific set of institutional conditions — print technology at a certain density, a commercial society at a certain maturity, a set of legal protections on speech and assembly at a certain depth, and a set of shared social norms about how men assembled in public were supposed to address one another — conspired to produce a medium in which a certain kind of communication was possible. The medium did not make the men deliberative. It made deliberation available to them, given a certain amount of effort on their part. When the conditions changed, the medium changed, and the deliberation that had been available through it either found new conditions or disappeared.

We are in one of the moments when the conditions have changed. The new medium is optimized for something that is not deliberation, and it is optimized for that something by mechanisms that operate below the level of the individual user's conscious preference. Whether the democratic function the old public sphere performed can be reconstituted under the new conditions is the open question of our moment, and we do not know the answer. What we do know is that the reconstitution will not happen by accident, and it will not happen by exhortation. It will happen, if it happens, by the deliberate construction of smaller-scale spaces in which the deliberative conditions can be reassembled; by the personal disciplines that make a person usable as a citizen in such spaces; and by whatever institutional and technological reforms can, over time, make the larger-scale medium less hostile to the thing we are trying to preserve.

It is fair to ask whether this is enough. A prism at global scale is not going to be abolished by a few citizens' assemblies in Ireland and a few thousand individual refusals to post the outraged take. We know this. We are not telling the reader that the answer to a structural problem is a sufficient dose of individual virtue, because we believe that answer is false and we have said so in every chapter of this book. What we are telling the reader is that structural problems, when they cannot be addressed at their own scale, have to be addressed at the scales where action is actually possible, and then those actions have to be aggregated, slowly, into the pressure that eventually moves the structure. This is how almost every durable democratic reform in the history of the countries we inhabit has actually happened. It is the only pattern with any track record. We are not arguing that it is easy. We are arguing that it is the thing to do.

The public sphere has been shattered. It has not been abolished. What a public sphere is — a space in which private persons assemble as a public and reason together about the matters the state is doing in their name — is a thing human beings are capable of making, when they set their minds to making it. They have made it before, under worse conditions than ours in many respects. They can make it again. The question is whether we will.

We think we can. The rest of this volume, and the volume that follows, is our attempt to say how.
