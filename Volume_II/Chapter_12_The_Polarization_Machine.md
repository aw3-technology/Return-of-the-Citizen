# Chapter 12
## The Polarization Machine

There is a sentence one hears now, at dinner tables, in classrooms, in the kind of strained conversation that used to be an ordinary exchange between neighbors, that would have struck an American of the mid-twentieth century as the symptom of a disease: *they are not just wrong; they are evil*. Sometimes it is said out loud. More often it is implied — carried in the tone with which the other party is named, in the grimace that accompanies the mention of the other candidate, in the pre-emptive dismissal of whatever argument the other side was about to make. It is the background music of our public life. It is also, by every measure the empirical literature can now supply, new. Americans disagreed with one another plenty in 1960, in 1976, in 1992. They did not, by and large, believe their fellow citizens in the opposing party were their enemies. They do now. And the question this chapter tries to answer is what exactly has happened, and how much of it is what it appears to be.

The first thing to say is that *polarization* is not one phenomenon but several, and the confusion among them is part of what makes the public conversation about polarization so unhelpful. It is worth slowing down long enough to pull them apart. There is what political scientists call *policy polarization* — the question of whether Americans, as individuals, now hold policy views further from the center than they used to. There is *ideological polarization* — the question of whether the two parties, as coalitions, have become more ideologically sorted, more internally consistent, more unlike each other in their platforms and their voting patterns. And there is *affective polarization* — the question of how Americans feel about members of the other party, and about the other party as such. These are three different questions. The answers are not the same.

On the first, the news is mild. Despite the noise, Americans do not, on average, hold policy views that are radically more extreme than the policy views of their grandparents. The distribution of opinion on most concrete policy questions — taxes, the minimum wage, abortion access, immigration levels, the size of the military — has shifted and widened, but not dramatically, and not uniformly in a single direction. There are real movements at the edges, and the edges matter; but the median American voter, asked what he actually wants on most issues, gives answers that would have been recognizable as mainstream a generation ago. Policy polarization among ordinary citizens is real but modest.

On the second, the news is significant. The parties themselves have become much more ideologically sorted than they were in the decades after the Second World War. This is the familiar story of the end of the Southern Democrats and the extinction of the Rockefeller Republicans — the story, told by political scientists over the past twenty years, of how American politics moved from a system in which each party contained multitudes to a system in which each party became a more coherent ideological vehicle. Liberals are now in one party; conservatives are now in the other. There is almost no overlap in congressional voting. The coalitions have become cleaner, the choices sharper, the parties more programmatic. By most definitions, this is a good thing for the voter, who can at last know what he is choosing. It is also, as we will see, a disaster for civic life, because it removes the cross-cutting pressures that used to force partisans of different stripes to treat one another as human.

But it is the third — affective polarization — that is the master finding of the last fifteen years of political science, and it is the one this chapter is centrally about. Affective polarization is the name Shanto Iyengar and his colleagues at Stanford gave, beginning in the early 2010s, to a phenomenon that had been slowly accumulating in the data and that they were the first to state clearly. In a 2012 paper titled "Affect, Not Ideology: A Social Identity Perspective on Polarization," Iyengar and his co-authors showed that the feelings ordinary Americans expressed about members of the other party had become, over roughly four decades of survey data, remarkably more hostile — more hostile than their actual policy disagreements could begin to explain. Americans had not, on the whole, moved to the poles. What they had done was come to despise each other. The distance between the feelings the average Democrat had about Republicans, and vice versa, had widened into something resembling an ethnic or religious animus.

This sounds like the kind of finding one is tempted to discount because it seems too on-the-nose, a social-science confirmation of what one already believes from reading the news. But the data are stubborn and the methodology is sound. The American National Election Studies — the long-running survey that is as close as we have to a continuous longitudinal record of American political feeling — have asked respondents, since the 1970s, to rate the two parties on a "feeling thermometer" that runs from zero (cold, hostile) to one hundred (warm, favorable). For most of the twentieth century, partisans rated their own party warmly and rated the other party coolly but not icily. Members of the opposing party were, roughly, the temperature of a business acquaintance one had reasons not to trust. Over the past forty years, the warmth toward one's own party has stayed roughly constant while the coldness toward the other party has deepened into something unprecedented. The gap is now the defining fact of the measurement. Americans do not feel more strongly about their own side than they used to. They feel much worse about the other side.

Iyengar's contribution was to reach for the right analytic framework to describe what was happening. He borrowed from the social-identity tradition in psychology — the tradition of Henri Tajfel and his successors, whose minimal-group experiments in the 1970s had shown how quickly and thoroughly human beings could be induced to form in-group loyalties and out-group hostilities on the flimsiest of pretexts. Partisan identity, Iyengar proposed, was no longer operating in the American mind as a mere political preference. It was operating as a *social identity* in the technical sense — a group membership around which in-group favoritism and out-group derogation organized themselves, with all the attendant cognitive and emotional consequences. The Democrat was no longer merely the person who voted for Democrats. He was a kind of person, carrying a package of presumed traits, warranting a package of presumed feelings. The Republican was not merely the person who voted for Republicans. He was, for the Democrat across the street, something like a member of a rival tribe.

If this were the end of the story, it would already be grim enough. But Lilliana Mason, in her 2018 book *Uncivil Agreement*, added a layer that makes it grimmer. Her central observation is that American partisan identity has become *stacked* with every other identity that used to cross-cut it. Once upon a time — not so long ago — partisan identity in the United States ran across lines of race, religion, region, education, and cultural style. There were white Protestant Democrats in Louisiana and Jewish Republicans in New York and union-loyal Democrats in Detroit whose sons voted Republican when they moved to the suburbs. A person's political side was one of many sides he inhabited, and it was not reliably predictable from his other memberships. That is no longer the case. The two parties have become, in Mason's term, *mega-identities*. Race, religion, region, cultural sensibility, even consumption patterns and musical taste now pattern with party at levels unseen in the postwar period. When a person's political opponent is also, with statistical reliability, a person of the other race, the other religion, the other region, the other cultural tribe, then political conflict ceases to be a disagreement about policy. It becomes a conflict in which the opposing party is the embodiment of everything-the-other-people-are against everything-you-are. Mason's work tracks, with unusual care, how this identity-stacking intensifies emotional response. The more identities a person layers onto his partisanship, the more hostile his feelings toward the other side tend to be — not because of anything the other side has done, but because there are more pieces of the self at stake in every political contest.

This is the modern condition of which the feeling thermometer is registering the temperature. The Democrat or Republican of 1960 inhabited a polity in which his opponents were also, with some regularity, his co-religionists, his neighbors, his fellow union members, his fellow fraternal-order members, his fellow bowlers. The intersections moderated the partisanship. The partisan could not entirely loathe the opposing party without loathing substantial pieces of his own life. The Democrat or Republican of the present inhabits a polity in which most of those intersections have thinned or vanished. His partisanship no longer contends, inside his own social life, with other loyalties that would pull against it. It is free to swell into the whole of his political soul. When it does, it acquires the tonal qualities of religious enmity — because, in the absence of countervailing identities, it has moved into the spaces religious enmity used to occupy.

***

The feeling thermometer is one measure. It is not the most alarming one. Over the last decade, Nathan Kalmoe and Lilliana Mason, working together, have documented something considerably more disturbing: the willingness of ordinary American partisans, when asked in surveys, to endorse hypothetical acts of violence against the other party. The numbers are not uniformly terrifying — most Americans do not, when asked, embrace political violence, and the survey methodology has real limits that honest readers of this research should register — but the numbers have risen, the rise correlates with affective polarization, and the floor of partisan hostility has moved in a direction that no democracy wants its floor to move. Kalmoe and Mason also document the rise of *dehumanizing* language in partisan discourse: the ascription of subhuman qualities to members of the other party, the framing of the opposing party as not merely wrong but morally or cognitively defective, the quiet spread of the vocabulary of vermin, disease, and infestation. This is not the language of ordinary political disagreement. It is the language that scholars of genocide use as a warning signal. That it is now audible in the mainstream of American partisan life is a fact to which serious observers must pay attention.

Rachel Kleinfeld, at the Carnegie Endowment, has produced the most useful policy-oriented synthesis of this material, drawing together the affective polarization research, the dehumanization findings, and a comparative literature on polarized democracies around the world. Her central observation is one that American readers, accustomed to the exceptionalist assumption that the United States is immune to the failure modes of democracies elsewhere, should sit with for a while. The United States now resembles, on a range of measurable indicators, other countries that have experienced severe democratic stress in the past generation — Venezuela before Chávez, Turkey before Erdoğan's consolidation, Hungary before Orbán's restructuring, the Philippines before and during Duterte. Not in its wealth, not in its institutions, not in the depth of its civic traditions; all of those remain stronger in the United States than in the comparison cases. But in the feeling-temperature of its partisan politics, in the degree to which partisans of each side now regard the other as an existential threat, in the willingness to excuse norm-breaking by one's own side as a necessary response to the norm-breaking of the other, the United States now sits in a cluster of countries whose subsequent trajectories are not reassuring. The path from our condition to democratic backsliding is well-documented in the comparative literature; it is not a speculative projection. Countries in this condition do not always continue to decline. But the conditions under which they recover are specific, and they require work. They do not recover by accident.

We do not introduce Kleinfeld's analysis to frighten the reader. The book is not written to frighten. We introduce it because one of the routine moves in American discussion of polarization is to shrug it off as so much elite hyperventilation — as an urban, educated, cable-news anxiety that does not touch most Americans' actual lives. There is something to this shrug, and we will come back to it. But the data on partisan hostility, on dehumanization, and on the tolerance of political violence are not confined to the elite. They extend through the population, and the rising floor they document is a fact about the country itself. Polarization in the United States is not a media invention. It is a real feature of the political culture, and it is operating, at this moment, in territory that comparative political science has learned to recognize.

***

The most important empirical finding of the last decade on American polarization — the one that will do the most structural work in this book — is the *perception gap*. The phrase is most associated with the organization More in Common, which published a series of reports in 2019, 2020, and 2021 drawing together survey data on what Americans think members of the other party believe. The findings, which have been replicated enough times now to count as established, are as follows.

Americans systematically overestimate how extreme the views of the other party are. Republicans think Democrats are, on average, considerably more radical than Democrats actually are. Democrats think Republicans are, on average, considerably more radical than Republicans actually are. The gap is not small. It is typically in the range of twenty to thirty percentage points, across a wide array of policy questions. Ask a Republican what share of Democrats holds a given extreme position, and he will name a figure far larger than the share that actually does. Ask a Democrat what share of Republicans holds a given extreme position, and he will do the same. Both sides are systematically wrong in the same direction, and wrong by roughly the same magnitudes. Both sides picture their opponents as a caricature of themselves, and both sides treat their opponents as if the caricature were accurate.

This, by itself, is a finding that ought to reshape how the country thinks about its own politics. The feeling thermometer registers the heat. The perception gap identifies one of the reasons the heat keeps climbing: each side is reacting, not to the actual other side, but to a fictional version of the other side that is much more extreme, much more hostile, much more threatening than the real one. A politics of images cannot be moderated by policy accommodations that were never the source of the heat. One cannot negotiate one's way out of a dispute whose shape both parties have misapprehended.

But the deeper finding in the More in Common work is the one that this book will lean on most heavily, because it cuts against the intuition that seems, at first, obvious. It would stand to reason that the more one pays attention to politics, the more news one consumes, the more political social-media content one ingests, the better-informed one would be about the other side. That is not what the data show. The data show the opposite. The most politically engaged Americans have, on average, the *most inaccurate* perceptions of the other party. The more news one consumes, the worse one's perception gap tends to be. Heavy consumers of cable news, heavy consumers of partisan social media, heavy consumers of political podcasts — the people who would, if asked, describe themselves as well-informed — are the population with the largest distortions of the other side. Their fellow citizens who pay less attention have, paradoxically, a more accurate picture of the country.

This is counterintuitive only if we misunderstand what most consumption of political media is for. It is not, for the most part, an act of learning about one's country. It is, as Eitan Hersh has argued with unusual directness in his 2020 book *Politics Is for Power*, something closer to what Hersh calls *political hobbyism* — the consumption of political content as an identity-confirmation and identity-maintenance activity. The hobbyist does not read the news in order to act in the world. He reads the news in order to feel a certain way about his side and about the other side, in order to have the day's grievance to chew on, in order to participate emotionally in a running national drama whose shape is stylized and familiar. Hersh's intervention is to note, bracingly, that many Americans who believe themselves to be deeply engaged in politics are not *doing* politics in any meaningful sense. They are not volunteering for campaigns. They are not organizing their neighbors. They are not showing up at school board meetings. They are not running for local office. They are not writing letters, joining parties in any real local-participation sense, or doing the dull work that actually moves democratic outcomes. They are consuming. And the media they consume, designed to hold their attention by engaging the emotional centers most responsive to tribal alarm, delivers a picture of the opposing side optimized for outrage rather than accuracy. The more they consume, the more distorted their picture of the other side becomes. The more distorted their picture, the more alarmed they are. The more alarmed they are, the more they consume. The feedback loop is the product.

And social media makes the loop tighter. Frequent users of political social media have, in the More in Common data, larger perception gaps than non-users. This is not because social-media users are getting more information; it is because the information they are getting has been selected, by the attention economy operating on the platform, from the tail of the distribution where the most extreme voices and the most inflammatory framings are to be found. We will return to the platform pathology in the next chapter. What matters here is the finding as finding. The people whose mental image of the other party is most distorted are not the disengaged; they are the engaged, and the means by which they stay engaged is also the means by which their image of the other party becomes less and less accurate.

One further detail from the perception-gap research is particularly worth attending to. Higher education, on its own, does not shrink the perception gap. Often it enlarges it, especially on the left. The well-educated Democrat, who has read more, thought more, and considered himself better positioned to understand the state of the country, turns out, on average, to have a *larger* misperception of what Republicans actually believe than the less-educated Democrat does. The same pattern, to a lesser extent, appears on the right: higher education does not consistently produce more accurate perceptions of the other side. This is a humbling finding for the educated reader — which is to say, for most readers of a book like this one. The credential does not confer understanding of one's fellow citizens. It often confers, instead, membership in a more homogeneous social environment in which the cartoons of the other side circulate with less resistance. The person who has been to graduate school has probably spent more of his adult life surrounded by people who share his political priors than the person who has not, and the cartoons in his head about the people who do not share those priors are, on average, worse.

We dwell on this because it is the empirical hinge of the whole chapter and of much of the book. A politics of affective polarization is being conducted, on both sides, largely against opponents who do not exist. The heat and the image are feeding each other. The consumers of the heat — the engaged, the educated, the well-informed as they would describe themselves — are the least accurate observers of the country whose politics they believe themselves to be following most closely. The citizenry is hostile to an image of itself it has itself constructed. This is not a figure of speech. It is a research finding, and it has survived enough replication that one can build on it without apology.

***

There is another structural fact about American polarization that has come into focus in the last few years, and that requires its own treatment. In 2024 Matt Grossmann and David Hopkins published *Polarized by Degrees*, the most systematic treatment yet of what has come to be called the *diploma divide*. Their central claim, backed by a substantial empirical architecture, is that the most important demographic realignment in American politics over the past generation is the re-sorting of the two parties along educational lines. College-educated Americans have, over the past two decades, moved decisively toward the Democratic coalition. Non-college Americans have moved, at a somewhat slower but still decisive pace, toward the Republican coalition. The sort has been going on across every major Western democracy; in the United States, it is now the single strongest demographic predictor of partisan affiliation, outpacing income, urbanicity, and most of the demographic variables that used to dominate the literature.

The consequence is not merely that the two parties now draw from different educational populations. It is that the two parties now live in substantially different *information ecosystems*, hold different *cognitive styles*, respond to different forms of argument, and trust different kinds of authority. Grossmann and Hopkins call this an *asymmetric polarization* because it is not a matter of the parties moving equally far from a shared center. It is a matter of the parties diverging along a dimension along which they differ in kind. The educated party is more reliant on institutional authority, more responsive to expert consensus, more prone to reason from official statistics, more trusting of the professional press. The less-educated party is more reliant on personal networks and trusted voices, more skeptical of institutional authority, more responsive to populist cues that frame institutions themselves as captured, more trusting of the emergent media that has grown up outside the professional press.

It is essential, in approaching this finding, not to pretend it describes a virtuous party and a vicious one. Both susceptibilities are susceptibilities. The educated party's trust in institutional authority becomes a vulnerability exactly when the institutions it trusts are themselves captured by the cultural assumptions of the educated party; the feedback loop looks, from outside, like a reasoning process but is in fact a homogeneous echo whose participants mistake their consensus for truth. The less-educated party's skepticism of institutional authority becomes a vulnerability exactly when that skepticism is harvested by figures whose relationship to truth is instrumental; the dismissal of institutional checks allows lies to propagate that an earlier political culture would have laughed off the stage. Each party is susceptible, along the grain of its cognitive style, to a characteristic failure mode. Each party is now living out that failure mode in full view of the other. Each party is horrified by the other's failure mode and blind to its own, and each mistakes the other's failure mode for confirmation that the other party is not merely wrong but unfit for self-government. That the two parties are now *epistemically* as well as *politically* different is one of the quieter and more dangerous features of the American condition.

It is not hard to see why the diploma divide amplifies everything else the chapter has so far described. A polity sorted along an educational line that also tracks the informational, cultural, and cognitive lines of modern life has lost, with that sort, most of the remaining cross-cutting ties that used to moderate partisanship in the postwar period. The Republican factory worker and the Democratic banker who used to share a civic club, a church, or a union local are not, with statistical frequency, sharing those spaces now. They have been sorted into different places, where different voices reach them, and where the voices tell them stories about one another that they have no counter-evidence to resist.

***

Why is this happening? Like every question of this kind, it does not admit of one answer, and the answers it does admit of are braided together in ways that resist tidy separation. But the candidate explanations, each with real support in the literature, can at least be named.

The first is the end of cross-pressured identities — the decades-long decoupling of partisanship from the other memberships that used to pull against it. The Southern Democrat, the northern ethnic Catholic Democrat, the Rockefeller Republican, the libertarian-leaning Western Republican: each of these was a figure whose partisan loyalty was in tension with some other loyalty, and the tension acted as a kind of internal brake on partisan fervor. As the parties have sorted, the brakes have come off. This is the Mason point at a different level of analysis: it is not only that individual voters have stacked their identities; it is that the coalitions themselves have lost the internal heterogeneity that used to ensure that no partisan could indulge total hostility to the other side without alienating some of his own.

The second is the *nationalization* of American political life. A generation ago, a great deal of American politics — in school boards, in state legislatures, in the daily operations of city government — was local in content and local in interpretation. The politics of one's town was reported by a local paper, decided on local terms, and understood through local frames of reference. That world is gone. Most local papers are gone, and the ones that survive are thinner than they were. The interpretive frame for local events is now almost entirely imported from the national conversation. A dispute about a school curriculum in a town of twelve thousand is now read, by its participants and by the commentariat, as a local engagement in a nationwide culture war. When everything local is interpreted nationally, local politics loses its moderating effect — the neighbor who voted the other way is no longer a neighbor one knows about the school board; he is a foot soldier in the other national army.

The third is the media transformation. A public sphere organized around a small number of broadcast institutions, each of which was obliged by the economics of mass audiences to avoid alienating large fractions of the country, produced a political media that was, by today's standards, tepid, institutionally cautious, and editorially bounded. The replacement of that media environment with a niche-driven, personality-driven, engagement-optimized environment has had direct consequences for the emotional temperature of politics. The old media's incentives pushed toward the center; the new media's incentives push toward whatever keeps the viewer, reader, or scroller emotionally engaged, which turns out, by the psychology of engagement, to be outrage. The next chapter takes this up in detail. What matters here is that the shift is not neutral with respect to polarization; it has been an active driver.

The fourth is the platform public sphere itself — the social-media and algorithmic environment in which a rising share of political perception is formed. Again, we defer the full treatment to the next chapter. The finding that matters here is that exposure to algorithm-selected political content increases affective polarization, and that the structure of the platforms tends to amplify out-group hostility because out-group hostility is a reliable producer of the engagement metrics the platforms are tuned to maximize. The platforms did not create polarization. They did, over the past fifteen years, give it a bigger megaphone, a faster feedback loop, and a more efficient mechanism for the distribution of distorted images of the other side.

The fifth is the status story, which we take up at length in Chapter 14. The decline of the communities and vocations that sustained a certain kind of American life over the past half-century has produced, on the one side, a population whose felt status loss is acute and whose politics is increasingly organized around the recovery of respect; and, on the other side, a credentialed class whose felt status ascendancy coincides, uncomfortably, with the declining fortunes of the communities from which many of its members came. The collision of these two status stories — the declining community's anger at being told its loss is deserved, and the credentialed class's incomprehension at the hostility its ascendancy has provoked — is a fuel source for affective polarization that policy arguments do not reach, because the underlying dispute is about who is owed respect in the order as a whole.

The sixth, and in some ways the most important to name explicitly, is the behavior of elites. Affective polarization among ordinary citizens does not generally run ahead of elite polarization. It tracks elite polarization, with a lag. When party leaders, major officeholders, prominent commentators, and the central figures of each coalition behave as if the other side is an existential threat, ordinary partisans follow. When elite rhetoric treats the opposing party as illegitimate, ordinary partisans absorb the framing, and the feeling thermometer obligingly registers it. There is no depoliticization of affect possible in an environment where the visible leaders of one's coalition are, week in and week out, modeling contempt for the other side. A country whose elites have decided to conduct politics as war will produce a citizenry whose political feelings are, eventually, warlike. Much of the recovery we will eventually argue for begins, or has to begin, with a change in elite behavior — not because elites are the problem, but because elites are the weather, and the weather is teaching the country what kind of feelings are appropriate to politics. The feelings we are being taught are not compatible with self-government for long.

***

There is a further connection to draw, and it is the one around which this book's contribution to the polarization literature mainly organizes. The polarization research, like most research, tends to treat affective polarization as a more-or-less freestanding phenomenon with its own causes — elite cues, media environments, platform dynamics, status shifts. Those are real. But the argument of this book is that affective polarization is also, and deeply, a *symptom* of the atomization and social-capital collapse traced in Chapter 10. The two diseases are not separate. They interlock.

Consider the profile of the person who is, empirically, most moderate in his affective stance toward the other party. He is not necessarily politically moderate in his views. He is someone who belongs to a strong local community, who has real friendships across lines of difference — the coworker, the fellow board member, the neighbor, the old schoolmate — whose weekly life includes some form of civic or associational work that matters to him regardless of which party controls Washington, whose sources of meaning are not exhausted by his politics. Such a person is, on the whole, less hostile to the other party than his more isolated counterpart. His cross-cutting ties do what cross-cutting ties have always done: they make the other party, in his imagination, harder to caricature, because he already knows specific people who belong to it and whose humanity he cannot deny without denying something of his own life.

Consider, by contrast, the profile of the person who is, empirically, most hostile. He is not necessarily more extreme in his actual policy views. He is someone whose social life has contracted, in the way documented at length by Putnam, around a narrower and more homogeneous circle. His closest encounters with the other party are mediated — by cable, by podcast, by the algorithmic feed — and those mediated encounters are, as we have seen, selected for the most inflammatory exemplars of the other side. He belongs to fewer associations. He has fewer cross-cutting friendships. His political identity has moved, by default, into spaces that other identities used to share. He is the ideal consumer of political hobbyism, because his life has been cleared of the other things politics used to compete with.

This is why the two halves of this book's diagnosis cannot be separated. The lonely person is the affectively polarized person. The polarized polity and the atomized polity are not two different polities. They are the same polity seen from two different angles. Any serious account of American polarization that treats it as a problem of ideas, or a problem of platforms, or a problem of elites, without also treating it as a problem of the social infrastructure of ordinary life, is going to produce interventions that cannot work. Any serious account of American social-capital collapse that does not notice that the vacated social spaces have been filled by a particular kind of partisan attachment is going to produce an inadequate picture of what it is that has been lost. The phenomena are one phenomenon.

That is the theoretical claim this book has been building toward since the Introduction. Hoffer and Arendt saw it first, in a different vocabulary: the atomized person is the person available for the mass movement. We are seeing, in contemporary political science, the same dynamic in more measurable form. The person whose local community has thinned to nothing is the person whose partisan identity has swollen to everything. The person whose partisan identity has swollen to everything is, by predictable psychological mechanics, the person for whom the other party has become the enemy. The perception gap is not an independent variable. It is what happens to the mental image of the other side when there is no ballast of real, specific relationships to check it against.

***

The good news in the empirical literature — and it really is good news — is that the perception gap is correctable, and that correcting it reliably reduces affective polarization. This is the finding on which much of Volume III's practical program will rest, and we flag it here because it is the hopeful counterpart to the grim material of this chapter. A sustained line of experimental research, much of it now gathered under the umbrella of Robb Willer's Strengthening Democracy Challenge at Stanford and in the related megastudies, has shown that interventions which show partisans what the other side actually believes — as opposed to what they think the other side believes — produce statistically significant, durable reductions in affective polarization. The mechanism is straightforward. Correct the caricature, and the emotions scaled to the caricature decline. Show a Republican what his Democratic neighbor actually thinks about a given issue, not the cable-news stereotype of what a Democrat is supposed to think, and his feelings about Democrats soften, measurably and for some period after the intervention. Show a Democrat the same about Republicans, and the same effect follows.

An adjacent and crucial finding needs to be stated here with care. Unstructured exposure to the other side does *not* reliably reduce affective polarization, and in some experimental conditions actively worsens it. This is the finding Chris Bail has made central in his work on the platform environment and will receive its full treatment in the next chapter. Simply putting partisans in the path of opposing voices — on Twitter, on television, on a social-media feed — can, by exposing each side to the most obnoxious examples of the other, deepen hostility rather than reduce it. The interventions that work are not naive exposure. They are *structured*, *evidence-based*, *correction-oriented* engagements in which the participant is presented with the real distribution of views on the other side, or with real individuals speaking in good faith about their actual beliefs, under conditions that allow the correction to register. The difference between what works and what does not is the difference between contact and caricature. Real contact — structured, careful, honest — reduces polarization. Caricature, even caricature delivered by people of the other side, amplifies it.

This is a serious operational finding, and it is one of the reasons Volume III is not a book of platitudes. We know, from research that has been accumulating for a decade and that is now at the level of reproducible megastudies, that there are specific things that work. The work of Volume III will be to describe them, to set out the institutional architectures in which they can be enacted, and to address the political-economy question of why, given that we know these things, so little of the country's political life is organized around doing them.

***

The temptation, at the end of a chapter like this, is to speak of polarization as a weather system — as something that happens to us, against which we can at best prepare. It is not a weather system. It is a machine, and machines have designers, operators, and power sources. Each of the layers we have traced — the mega-identity, the perception gap, the diploma divide, the platform incentive structure, the elite rhetoric — is an input. None of them is a permanent feature of the American order. Each of them is the current state of a variable that could, under other conditions, take a different value. The machine is being run. Some of those who run it are conscious of running it; most are not; a great many Americans feed the machine daily without realizing they are its fuel. That does not make it a weather system. It makes it a machine whose operations we participate in.

Polarization does not float free of the media and platform environment in which it is reproduced. It is grown in that environment, fed by that environment, and amplified by it. The next chapter turns directly to that environment — to what has happened to the public sphere itself, to the communicative space in which Americans are supposed to form opinions together. If this chapter has shown the heat at which the country's partisan feelings now burn, the next chapter will look at the fuel and the bellows. The puzzle is not merely that Americans dislike the other party. The puzzle is the set of mechanisms by which Americans are now, at every hour of every day, invited to dislike an image of the other party that is not the party they actually share a country with. What those mechanisms are, how they work, and what a citizen who wishes to be more than their fuel is supposed to do about them, is the subject to which we now turn.
