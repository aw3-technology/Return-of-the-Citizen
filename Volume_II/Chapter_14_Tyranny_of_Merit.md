# Chapter 14
## The Tyranny of Merit and the Hollowing of Work

A diagnosis that stops at the social and the psychological — at the lonely neighbor, the vanished bowling league, the platformed congregation, the partisan stacking of identities, the algorithmic prism — is a diagnosis that has not yet reached the ground. The accounts we have worked through in the last six chapters are true and indispensable. Affective polarization is real. Social capital has collapsed. Institutions have been hollowed into stages. The public sphere has been shattered into feeds. Each of these has its own causal story, and each has furnished a literature of its own. But underneath them, and older than most of them, is a transformation of the American economy and the American status order that neither the classical liberal right nor the therapeutic center-left has fully faced. The populist revolt now coursing through American politics — in its right-wing nationalist form and in its left-wing revolutionary form, so often mistaken for opposites — is at bottom a revolt against that transformation. A book that does not engage it is a book for the credentialed about the credentialed. We have tried, so far, not to write that book. This chapter is the place where we must try hardest.

The transformation has three faces, and three thinkers have given us the clearest view of each. Michael Sandel has written the definitive account of what has happened to merit: how the language of earned success, once an instrument of democratic self-respect, has curdled into a license to humiliate those who did not make it. Christopher Lasch, writing thirty years ago and seeing further than almost anyone, described the secession of the American professional class from the common life it once shared with its fellow citizens, and the resentments that secession would eventually unleash. Sohrab Ahmari, writing from the post-liberal right but drawing on a tradition the right has mostly lost, has made the case that the coercion most Americans experience in their daily lives comes not from the government but from concentrated economic power — from the employer who can fire at will, the landlord who can evict, the platform that can deplatform, the insurer that can deny. Read together, these three books map a terrain on which the master diagnoses of the previous chapters rest. The atomized person of Arendt is often also an economically precarious person. The frustrated person of Hoffer is often also a person whose work has been stripped of dignity and whose community has been stripped of jobs. The mass-movement susceptibility we have been tracking is not evenly distributed across the American map. It is highest where economic dispossession and cultural humiliation overlap. We will not understand the present crisis, or the shape the response must take, until we have looked carefully at that overlap.

\*\*\*

Begin with merit. Sandel's central claim in *The Tyranny of Merit*, published in 2020, is that the most cherished civic ideal in American life — that positions should go to those with the talent and the effort to earn them — has a dark side that has only now become fully visible. The ideal itself sounds unimpeachable. It is, for two hundred years, what Americans have told themselves about the kind of country they live in: a place where you are not shackled by the rank of your parents, where hard work lifts the capable regardless of birth, where every child can aspire to any station. The rhetoric has been bipartisan and near-universal. From Horatio Alger through the mid-century consensus to the "you can be anything you want to be" catechism of late-twentieth-century schooling, the promise of merit has been the American faith.

Sandel does not set out to demolish the faith. He sets out to show what happens when it becomes the dominant, perhaps the sole, public language for describing why some people live well and others do not. Under the pure meritocratic picture, a successful person has earned her place by the legitimate exercise of her talents and effort, and by nothing else. A person at the bottom, by the same logic, must be there by the failure or absence of talent and effort, and by nothing else. This is the crucial move. Meritocracy, in its purest form, is not only an account of why the winners win. It is an account of why the losers lose. And the account is moralizing. The loser is not unfortunate, not unlucky, not blameless; he is insufficient. His place in the order reflects, with a completeness that aristocracy at least had the decency to deny, the measure of his worth as a person.

This is not, Sandel is careful to note, merely a philosophical danger. It is not only a potential misreading of a doctrine that, properly understood, would not entail it. The empirical evidence that Americans have absorbed exactly this moralizing account is abundant and grim. Surveys going back decades show that Americans are unusually willing, among citizens of rich democracies, to attribute poverty to personal failing and wealth to personal virtue. The attribution has grown stronger, not weaker, during the period in which actual mobility has stagnated. The divergence between the inherited rhetoric of opportunity and the measured reality of an increasingly hereditary elite has produced not disillusionment with the rhetoric but an intensification of its moralizing. If the system says that winners deserve to win, and if winners and losers increasingly reproduce themselves across generations, then the moralized conclusion is that some families have figured out how to deserve, and others have not.

The humiliation this inflicts on the bottom two-thirds of the American educational distribution is Sandel's central theme. Americans without four-year college degrees — which is to say, most Americans — have become progressively more alienated from the institutions, the language, and the politics of the credentialed class over the past forty years. They experience themselves as treated with contempt, and the evidence is that they are not imagining it. Listen to how the credentialed speak, in public and in private, about the non-credentialed. Listen to the affect of the evening news anchor describing a rural county; the easy scorn of the late-night comedian; the therapeutic condescension of the professional class explaining, to each other, what the other America is really upset about. The rhetoric of "just go to college" as universal advice — the implicit, and sometimes explicit, suggestion that anyone not holding a credential has failed to accept the basic responsibility of modern life — is a language of moral ranking. It lands harder on its targets than its speakers realize.

Sandel's argument here is not that elite condescension is ill-mannered. It is that it is the visible surface of a transformation in how Americans think about each other. In the pre-meritocratic democratic ideal, a citizen was a citizen — a person of standing in the public life of a republic, whose dignity was not a function of his occupation or his credential but of his membership in the political community. The industrial worker and the accountant shared the same standing in the polity and, often, the same pew, the same bleachers, the same union hall or lodge or PTA. Under the meritocratic regime, standing has been decoupled from citizenship and rebound to achievement. The accountant and the industrial worker no longer share a public. They share, at best, a consumer market. And the accountant has been instructed, by every cultural signal available to him, that his credential confers on him a kind of moral distance from the worker. The language he has been taught to use about the worker's politics, the worker's religion, the worker's diet, the worker's entertainments, is a language of clinical concern laced with derision. This is the diversity-but-not-class professional class, whose inclusiveness along the axes it recognizes is matched by its blindness to the axis that most shapes the lives of those it claims to champion.

Sandel's proposal at the end of the book is modest in tone and radical in implication. It is not a scheme to end meritocracy by fiat. It is a call to rehabilitate the dignity of non-elite work; to reform higher education so that it is no longer the sorting mechanism by which an American becomes eligible or ineligible for public respect; to recover a politics of the common good that does not treat upward mobility as the single, universal metric of a successful life. Behind these proposals lies a deeper claim: that the loss of the pre-meritocratic civic dignity — the standing a person held as a citizen irrespective of his achievements — is the wound around which much of our current politics is organized, and that recovering any version of that dignity will require dismantling the parts of the meritocratic rhetoric that have been used, for forty years, to justify treating fellow citizens as lesser.

We would add one thing to Sandel's account, which is that the humiliation is not confined to the bottom. It travels up. The children of the credentialed are not, by any measure the data can capture, happier than the children of the non-credentialed; their rates of anxiety, depression, and despair are if anything higher. A system that makes the winners prove, over and over, that they deserve their winning is a system that makes the winners miserable. The striving becomes permanent; the resume, infinite; the self, an ongoing audit. This is the joyless, vigilant, self-branding soul that Yuval Levin and others have described so well from another angle. Sandel's point is that it is the same soul, in its upper register, that produces — in its lower register — the person who experiences his life as an unjust verdict. The regime that humiliates the loser also exhausts the winner. Neither half is free.

Lasch saw the structural precondition of all of this before most observers were ready to believe it was happening. *The Revolt of the Elites and the Betrayal of Democracy*, published in 1995 from the thin edge of Lasch's remaining years, is one of the prescient books of the American twentieth century, and its prescience is now almost embarrassing to recover. Lasch was a man of the left. He had spent his intellectual life criticizing consumer capitalism, the therapeutic state, and the narcissistic self that the two produced. He was not, in any sense, a partisan of the people against the experts. But he came to believe, by the early 1990s, that the most important political fact of his generation was the secession of the American professional class from the common life of the republic, and that the consequences of that secession — whenever they arrived — would be severe.

The argument is worth restating in its plainest form. Democracy as a form of life, Lasch held, rests on something more than the periodic exercise of the franchise. It rests on elites — managerial, professional, cultural — participating in a shared common life with non-elites. It rests on the doctor and the patient, the journalist and the reader, the professor and the neighbor, the general and the recruit, having lived in the same neighborhoods, attended the same schools, fought the same wars, worshipped in the same churches, belonged to the same civic organizations. Not always, not perfectly, not without grave exclusions that a more just country would correct. But enough that the elites of a republic knew their fellow citizens by face and by first name, and that the non-elites had continuous, unmediated experience of the people making the decisions that shaped their lives.

By the last quarter of the twentieth century, Lasch argued, that condition was dissolving. The late-twentieth-century professional class — in finance, in law, in technology, in media, in higher education, in the federal and state bureaucracies — was withdrawing from the shared life into a set of enclaves of its own making. These enclaves were geographical (the affluent suburbs, and later the self-consciously urban neighborhoods, of the credentialed coasts); educational (the increasingly stratified public school system and the parallel private school system that the credentialed opted into for their children); cultural (the coherent bundle of entertainments, diets, parenting practices, and moral vocabularies by which the professional class recognized itself and distinguished itself from those outside); and political (the steady capture of party apparatuses by the college-educated wings of both coalitions). The net result was a class that lived among its own, educated its children among its own, met its spouses among its own, worked among its own, and governed, from a distance, a country whose daily life it no longer shared.

Lasch's critique of this class is what is hardest, and most necessary, to reproduce accurately. He did not argue that the professional class was too generous with the poor, or that it was insufficiently patriotic, or that it despised traditional values. He argued that it was performing a form of concern for Americans it no longer actually knew. The therapeutic vocabulary by which the credentialed spoke about the working class — as traumatized, as needing education, as suffering from false consciousness, as requiring expert intervention — was, Lasch held, a mode of distancing. It replaced the older democratic habit of regarding fellow citizens as interlocutors, capable of speaking for themselves and of being argued with, with a newer professional habit of regarding them as patients, cases, or constituencies. The effect on those so described was corrosive. They could feel themselves being studied rather than addressed. They could feel that the concern shown them was real in some sense, but was not the concern of a neighbor.

Lasch predicted — and this is the part that now reads as prophecy — that the populist reaction to this condition would be fierce, would arrive within a generation or two, and would take forms the secessionist class would find both incomprehensible and morally outrageous. He predicted that the professional class's response to the populist reaction would be to double down on the therapeutic frame: to describe the populism as a pathology, to look for its causes in the deficient education or moral formation of those caught up in it, to propose corrective interventions designed and administered by the same class against whose secession the populism was in fact a response. He did not live to see this cycle play out. But anyone reading him now can see it in the page's margin. The mutual incomprehension that is the leading feature of American politics in the third decade of the twenty-first century is, in significant part, the late return on the social check that the professional class wrote against its own country in the final decades of the twentieth.

It is important to say what Lasch was not saying, because his argument has been hijacked by writers who do not share his premises. He was not saying that populist prescriptions were correct. He was not saying that the instinct to cashier the experts was a sound one, or that the institutions of the modern state could be dispensed with, or that democracy was better served by people who read fewer books. His point was civic and relational. Democracy requires elites who share a life with non-elites; a republic whose elites have seceded from the common life of their fellow citizens is a republic in which the legitimacy of the elites has already begun to erode. Recovering the legitimacy requires not better messaging or more skilled therapeutic outreach, but the partial undoing of the secession itself. That is not a problem policy can solve, and it is not a problem technique can solve. It is a problem about where one lives, where one sends one's children, what civic organizations one joins, and whom one considers to be one's fellow in the work of common life.

If Sandel has described the moral humiliation produced by meritocracy and Lasch has described the civic abandonment produced by the secession of the elites, Sohrab Ahmari has done something the American right had, for a generation, been reluctant to do: he has taken concentrated economic power seriously as a source of unfreedom. *Tyranny, Inc.*, published in 2023, is in some respects a difficult book for us to make use of. Ahmari is a post-liberal and a cultural conservative, and he stands against several of the positions we defend elsewhere in this book. We will not pretend otherwise. But we cannot steelman the diagnosis we need to steelman without admitting that on the question of where coercion most often comes from in ordinary American life, Ahmari has seen more clearly than much of the tradition to which he belongs — and more clearly, on this question, than much of the tradition to which we belong.

The central claim of *Tyranny, Inc.* is that for most Americans most of the time, the actor whose decisions most directly circumscribe their freedom is not the state. It is the employer. It is the landlord. It is the creditor. It is the health insurance company. It is the platform monopoly that owns the interface between the worker and his customers, or between the citizen and his neighbors. These actors wield the kind of arbitrary power over the persons subject to them that, in the classical republican vocabulary we worked through in Volume I, is the paradigm of unfreedom. Their decisions can end a paycheck, end a tenancy, end a treatment, end an account, end a career. The person on the receiving end of those decisions has, in most cases, no effective recourse. He has the theoretical right to walk away — to find another employer, another landlord, another insurer, another platform — but the asymmetry of information, the concentration of the market, the dependency of his livelihood on the relationship that controls him, ensures that the walking-away is available to him only at severe cost. He lives, in short, at the mercy of another's will. That is what the classical republicans called domination, and what contemporary classical liberals have had the hardest time seeing.

Ahmari's analytical move, which distinguishes him from standard conservative commentary and from standard libertarian commentary alike, is to insist that contract law — the mechanism by which most of these relationships are formally governed — presupposes a symmetry of bargaining power that no longer obtains in most of the cases it is asked to regulate. The "freedom of contract" story, foundational to the classical-liberal account of market relations, imagines two parties bargaining more or less as equals over the terms under which they will exchange value. In contemporary America, Ahmari argues, this story is a fiction when applied to most workers, most tenants, most consumers of essential services, most borrowers, and most users of platform monopolies. The worker does not negotiate his terms of employment with his employer; he accepts the terms the employer offers, because the alternative to acceptance is the loss of his income, and because the employer can replace him much more easily than he can replace the employer. The tenant does not negotiate his rent; he accepts the rent the landlord demands, because the cost and disruption of moving make the theoretical option of refusal practically unavailable. The user of the platform monopoly does not negotiate its terms of service; he clicks through them unread, because he has no alternative that would reach his customers, his friends, his audience, or his employment.

This is not the language in which the American right, outside a few dissident corners, has trained itself to speak about economic life. For several generations, the right's master story has been that the state is the threat to freedom and the market is its guarantor. There is a truth in that story — the state has an instrument of last resort, the monopoly on legal violence, that private actors do not. But as a complete account of where the threats to a given American's freedom actually come from in a given week of his life, the story is radically incomplete. For most Americans most of the time, the decisions that most directly constrain their lives are made not in Washington but in the HR office, the leasing office, the claims department, the compliance committee of a distant corporation. Ahmari's achievement is to have said this in a voice the right can hear. Whether he is heard is another matter.

The prescriptions he draws from this diagnosis would, forty years ago, have been described as left-wing. He endorses sectoral bargaining on the European model, an expansion of labor rights that would give workers real collective voice in the terms of their employment. He endorses a more aggressive antitrust stance against the concentrated platforms and the consolidated industries, drawing on the trust-busting tradition American progressives and Republicans once shared. He endorses stronger legal protections for tenants and debtors, limiting the arbitrary power of landlords and creditors to disrupt ordinary lives. We will not, in this volume, adjudicate these proposals one by one. Some we find persuasive; some we find too blunt; some belong to debates that are beyond the scope of this book. Our purpose here is different. Our purpose is to take the diagnosis — that most Americans experience their unfreedom as arising from economic rather than governmental actors, that the symmetry-of-contract fiction is a poor description of their bargaining position, and that the condition this produces is a kind of domination the older republican vocabulary was built to name — and to set it alongside Sandel's diagnosis of merit and Lasch's diagnosis of secession. The three together describe something none of them alone describes, and it is that composite picture we now need to see.

\*\*\*

The composite is uglier than any of its parts. It is the condition in which the merit rhetoric tells the bottom of the distribution that their failure is their own fault; in which the market's actual distribution of rewards has, over the same four decades, skewed so steeply that being at the bottom of the distribution has become materially harsher than it was within living memory; and in which the class that has pulled away from the rest has simultaneously retreated from the common life in which it might once have been called to account for the condition of its neighbors. Merit plus market plus secession equals humiliation — a specific, distinctive, historically novel form of humiliation in which economic decline is accompanied by moral blame and by the mannered condescension of a class that is doing fine.

This is not the condition of the medieval peasant. It is not the condition of the nineteenth-century industrial worker. Those earlier forms of hardship were accompanied by a public doctrine that explained, to the person at the bottom, why he was at the bottom: inheritance, providence, fortune, the structure of the world. Whatever else one thinks of those doctrines, they did not require the person suffering under them to regard his suffering as the verdict of his own insufficiency. The modern form is different. The modern form tells the suffering person that he is suffering because he has been weighed on a fair scale and found wanting. It then adds insult to injury by having the weighers — the people whose favored station was delivered to them by the same scale — speak about the weighed in the clinical, euphemistic, slightly pained voice of professionals describing a difficult case.

It is this condition, more than any of the conditions we have described in previous chapters, that supplies the reservoir from which mass movements in contemporary America draw their supporters. Arendt's lonely atomized individual is not, in twenty-first-century America, merely a person who lives by himself in a studio apartment and has no one to call. He is often a person who is also materially precarious — working in a gig job that provides no health insurance and no retirement contribution, living in a community whose economic base has been hollowed out over three decades, receiving medical care through an insurer whose logic is opaque to him and whose customer service function is designed to exhaust his will to appeal. Hoffer's "frustrated" — the person who experiences his life as irredeemably spoiled, who is therefore ready to trade his individual identity for membership in a collective that will give him one — is not a random type who shows up with roughly equal frequency across the American landscape. He is concentrated, in specific places and in specific demographics. The concentration is not random. It tracks deindustrialization. It tracks educational stratification. It tracks the patterns of wealth concentration of the past forty years, which have moved resources and opportunity from broad geographies and demographics into narrow ones. The mass-movement susceptibility is highest where the economic and the cultural humiliations overlap — where a person lives in a place that has been stripped of its work and told, in the same breath, that the stripping was the unfortunate but necessary consequence of the working of a fair system.

The populists of the current moment have understood this, if unevenly and often in demagogic form. The right-populist understands that the people he speaks for have been told they are losers on the meritocratic scale and that the losing is their fault, and he offers them a counter-story: you are not losers, the scale is rigged, your loss is the consequence of a betrayal and the betrayers can be named. The left-populist understands that the workers she speaks for are being squeezed by economic actors whose power is not adequately checked, and she offers them a counter-story: you are not insufficient, the system is predatory, and the predators must be broken. Both of these counter-stories contain a real diagnostic kernel. Each is also partial; each is susceptible to the demagogic temptation that mass movements always hold out to leaders who discover a reservoir of the frustrated; each can curdle, and in several places on the contemporary scene is curdling, into the familiar Hofferian shape in which the movement promises the individual that he can cease to be himself if he will only lose himself in the collective. The populist prescriptions may be wrong in their specifics and dangerous in their drift. The grievance they are organized around is real. Mistaking the one for the other — treating the grievance as pathology because the prescription is pathology, or treating the prescription as justified because the grievance is justified — is the characteristic error of commentary that will not stay on the terrain long enough to tell the two apart.

We have not yet said what we most need to say about work. The economic dimension of the crisis is not only a story about distribution, though it is also a story about distribution. It is a story about what has happened to the idea that work is a calling, a form of service, a contribution to a shared life, a fit object for a person's pride and the basis for his standing in his community. The decline of that idea — the vocational ethic, in the older religious vocabulary, or the craft ethic, in the older secular one — has been steep, and it has been steepest in the jobs that used to carry the heaviest vocational weight and have since been "rationalized" into platform-gig relationships.

Consider what has happened to driving for a living over the past fifteen years. The taxi driver, the trucker, the delivery driver of the pre-platform era had a job. It was often difficult and sometimes dangerous; it paid modestly; it was not, in most cases, glamorous. But it was in its form a job. The worker had an employer. The employer had a duty, backed by law and often by union contract, to provide him with certain conditions of labor. The worker had a relationship with a dispatcher or a regional manager whom he could, in principle, speak to about his working life. He had coworkers he saw regularly. He had a recognizable place in the economic ecology of his city or region, and that place was visible to his neighbors, who could name him and his work. He could teach his son or daughter what he did and why, and the son or daughter could see a path by which they might do something similar, or better. The work was, in the most ordinary sense of the word, dignified.

The platformed successor of this work is not dignified in the same sense, or often in any sense. The worker is not an employee but an "independent contractor" — a category designed specifically to relieve the platform of the duties an employer would owe an employee. There is no dispatcher he can speak to, no manager whose office he can walk into. The algorithm that assigns him work is invisible to him, as is the algorithm that rates him and decides whether to keep giving him work. The relationship has been engineered to extract as much of his labor as possible at as low a marginal cost as the system can sustain, while giving him as little purchase on the relationship as the law can be made to tolerate. He can, in theory, leave. He cannot, in practice, change the terms. He does not know his coworkers; there are no coworkers; there is only an expanding and contracting pool of strangers, each also driving, each also rated. He has difficulty teaching his children what he does, because what he does is barely a what. He is a node in a network whose purposes lie elsewhere. His neighbors cannot easily name his work because the work has been engineered to be named by nothing but its platform. The vocational substance of the older job has been drained from the newer one, and what has been left in its place is thin.

This is one example, a vivid one. It is not unique. It is a particular case of a general transformation that has hollowed many middle-skill occupations — retail, clerical, warehousing, many forms of service — into thinner, more surveilled, more algorithmically managed, less relational, less narratively coherent forms of labor than the jobs they replaced. It is also the particular case that David Graeber, from a different angle, was describing in his book on what he called "bullshit jobs." Graeber's phenomenon — the experience, reported in his survey data at disturbingly high rates, that one's work is pointless, ritualized, or actively harmful — was, interestingly, not confined to the bottom of the occupational distribution. It was in fact most intense among white-collar workers with ostensibly good jobs: compliance officers whose compliance accomplished nothing, middle managers whose management managed nothing, consultants whose consultations were unread. The vocational hollowing, in other words, is not only a problem for those at the bottom. It has crept up the ladder. The credentialed class who are, on the meritocratic story, the winners are in large numbers reporting that they suspect their winning has no content.

Behind this is a story the political economy of the past forty years has told itself, and told the rest of us, with great confidence. The story is that the disappearance of the middle-skill jobs of the older industrial economy was unfortunate but necessary, that the displaced workers should retrain for the better jobs the new economy would create, that the retraining infrastructure would be there, and that the new jobs would be better — more interesting, better paid, more flexibly scheduled — than the ones they replaced. We are now forty years into this story, and it is time to notice that large portions of it have not come true. The retraining infrastructure is in most places rudimentary or absent. The jobs that have been created for the non-credentialed are, on several dimensions that matter to the people working them, worse than the ones they replaced: less stable, less relational, less visible, less amenable to collective voice, less tied to a place, less teachable to children. There are exceptions; there are individual workers whose lives have improved; there are sectors where the story has worked roughly as promised. But there are many more places where it has not, and the aggregate effect on American civic life of a generation of unkept promises on this scale is not small. We should not be surprised that people whose lives were restructured on the basis of a story are unhappy when the story turns out to have been partly untrue.

We should say, because this book is built to take counterarguments seriously, what the strongest versions of the response to the argument we have been developing look like. Two are worth naming.

The first is the argument from the numbers: that things are in fact not so bad as the argument implies, that real wages are up on most measures, that the rhetoric of economic despair outstrips the data, that this is another case of vibes overtaking evidence. There is real force in this objection. On several standard measures, median real incomes in the United States have risen over the past thirty years, in some periods faster than before. Consumption of many goods has expanded. Technology has brought new capacities to ordinary lives that older generations could not have dreamed of. It would be dishonest to claim that the American worker of today is materially worse off across the board than his grandfather was. He is not.

But the standard measures do not capture the relevant transformation. The composition of consumption has shifted in ways that make the traditional pathways to adult stability — a house one can afford on a median income, a family one can raise on that house, a community one can afford to stay in because one's work and one's neighbors and one's congregation are all within reach — systematically harder to attain, even as nominal incomes rise. Housing, healthcare, education, and childcare — the four major expenditures that stand between the median worker and the stable adult life the older economy made accessible to him — have risen much faster than the prices of consumer electronics and inexpensive clothing. One can own a cheaper television and a more expensive life. The argument that real wages are up, taken alone, counts the television and misses the life. It also misses the civic point. The complaint is not only that the numbers have gone the wrong way. It is that the structure of the lived economy — the path from a first job to a household, from a household to a community, from a community to a stable adulthood — has been disrupted even where the numbers look acceptable, and that the disruption is what the grievance is actually about.

The second objection is the charge of nostalgia. It runs roughly this way: the argument we have been making is a dressed-up yearning for a lost mid-century industrial America, for factory jobs with union cards and pensions and wives at home. The yearning, the objection continues, is both politically unavailable — that America is not coming back, nor could it be made to — and morally compromised, given the sex-, race-, and class-based exclusions on which the stability of that earlier arrangement rested. To argue for its return is to argue, perhaps unwittingly, for the return of its injustices.

We take this objection seriously, and we want to say clearly what we are not arguing and what we are. We are not arguing for a return to mid-century industrial America. We are not arguing for a return to the coverture-shaped household, the racially segregated workplace, the closed shop whose closure was in part a function of whom it excluded. The injustices of that arrangement were real, and their partial dismantling in the second half of the twentieth century was moral progress. What we are arguing — and we think Sandel, Lasch, and Ahmari in their different voices are each arguing — is something more limited and more future-oriented. It is that whatever economy we build next must include forms of work that offer dignity, voice, and security to the non-credentialed majority of Americans. No economic arrangement that leaves most of its participants feeling disposable is politically sustainable in a democracy, whatever its numbers look like on aggregate measures. The task is not to recover the forms of the mid-century economy but to recover its best features — its broadly shared dignity of labor, its accessible pathway to adult stability, its local rootedness — in a form that does not require the exclusions on which its historical instance partly rested. That is hard. It is not impossible. It is, in any case, the task the present demands. The alternative — a frictionless labor market topped by a credentialed winners' class presiding, at a distance, over a managed servitoriat — is a political arrangement no self-respecting republic can long endure. The populism now eating our creators is the first visible symptom of what it will not endure.

We can now return to the master argument and see where this chapter has brought us. We said in the opening of Volume II that the disease afflicting the American citizen has multiple faces. We promised to examine each of the leading accounts — the mass-movement diagnosis, the social-capital diagnosis, the institutional-decay diagnosis, the polarization diagnosis, the public-sphere diagnosis — and to add, at the end, the one the first five leave underplayed. That is what this chapter has done. The material-cultural dimension has to be put on the table and its relation to the others has to be specified.

The relation is not competition. It is composition. Atomization, mass formation, institutional decay, polarization, the shattered public sphere, and the merit-and-market humiliation we have examined here are not six separate diseases afflicting the same patient. They are the symptoms of a civic order that has been eaten from several directions at once by a common logic — the logic that reduces persons to individuals-as-consumers, and then blames the individuals when the lives they end up with do not satisfy. The lonely person and the humiliated person are often the same person. The platformed citizen and the precarious worker are often the same citizen. The member of the affectively polarized tribe is often someone whose tribe has, among its other functions, the function of answering the humiliation with pride — of telling him, in a vocabulary of resentment or revolution, that the verdict rendered on him by the merit regime is false. Each of these aspects of the crisis feeds every other. They are not sequential. They are simultaneous, and they reinforce.

This is why the responses we have seen offered in recent years — each picking up a single strand of the composite disease, each pulling on it as if pulling on it alone would untangle the whole — have fallen short. The response that targets social capital alone is real but insufficient; the response that targets the algorithms alone is real but insufficient; the response that targets economic inequality alone is real but insufficient; the response that targets cultural decadence alone is real but insufficient. The disease is composite. The response must be composite as well.

We must also say what this chapter does not, by itself, tell us how to do. We are diagnostic writers in Volume II; we are not yet prescriptive. It would be the final irony of a book about the pathologies of the present to end its diagnostic volume by offering a policy paper. That is not the shape of the response we believe is required, and it is not the shape Volume III will take. The managerial temptation — the idea that we simply need better policies designed by better experts and we will be fine — is precisely one of the temptations Lasch warned against; it is the therapeutic reflex of the class whose secession produced the crisis. The revolutionary temptation — the idea that the system is beyond repair and must be broken so that something better can be built on the wreckage — is precisely the mass-movement reflex Arendt and Hoffer warned against. Both temptations are wrong for reasons this book has been developing from its first pages. We will not offer either in Volume III.

What we will offer, instead, is a program that operates at several scales at once — personal, local, institutional, and national — and on all the faces of the composite disease simultaneously. We will begin from the recognition that this is a task for citizens, which is what the book has been arguing throughout. The merit-and-market humiliation cannot be dispelled by a gift from the humiliators; it can only be dispelled by the reconstitution of a civic life in which persons are regarded as fellow citizens before they are regarded as economic achievers. The secession of the elites cannot be reversed by a declaration; it can only be reversed, if it can be reversed, by deliberate choices of where to live, how to educate one's children, what associations to join, whom to regard as a fellow. The economic domination of ordinary Americans cannot be ended by a speech; it requires the slow rebuilding of countervailing powers — labor, community, local government, mutual-aid associations, real political parties — that the atomizing logic of the past half-century has drained. The atomization cannot be reversed by the mere publication of a book; the recovery of a public life requires citizens willing to undertake the practices of one. And the mass-movement susceptibility that follows from all of the above cannot be dispelled by denouncing the cults; it can only be dispelled by rebuilding the local, associational, vocational, and civic goods whose absence makes the cults attractive in the first place.

That program is what Volume III sketches. It will not be a single policy. It will not be a single movement. It will not be a single cultural turn. It will be a set of interlocking practices, some personal, some local, some institutional, some political, each modest on its own, each pointing toward a common direction, all informed by the composite diagnosis we have now completed. We will draw on the growing empirical literature on what actually works to depolarize and to rebuild civic life; we will draw on the philosophical foundations Volume I laid down; and we will draw on the long American tradition of civic renewal from below, of which the Progressive Era, the civil rights movement, and the mutualist and cooperative traditions are only the most visible instances. None of these was begun by the humiliators. All were begun by citizens, under conditions worse than ours, who believed that the republic was worth the labor of its restoration.

We close Volume II on the hardest of the diagnostic chapters because the dignity of the non-credentialed American is the place where our current civic failure is most visible, most undeniable, and most politically consequential. No recovery of the citizen that does not address the merit regime, the secession of the elites, and the hollowing of work is a recovery that will reach the Americans who most need to be reached. A book of this kind, addressed to the citizen, cannot afford to speak over their heads. It must speak to them as fellows, as equals in the work of the common thing. That is the register in which the final volume will be written, and it is the register in which this book has tried, from the first page, to earn the right to be read.

We began the diagnostic volume with a puzzle: why, in the wealthiest and safest moment in human history, has American public life turned hollow, hostile, and unserious. We have, over seven chapters, tried to show that the puzzle has a composite answer, and that the final piece of the answer is the transformation of work and merit and status that the previous chapters underplayed. We have argued that the lonely, the furious, the platformed, the polarized, and the humiliated are often the same person. We have argued that the mass-movement susceptibility the book has tracked is not a distant threat but a condition already acting on the American polity, concentrated in the places where the composite disease has done its worst work. And we have argued that any response adequate to the situation must be as multi-dimensional as the situation itself — must work at multiple scales, on multiple faces, over multiple years, by the hands of actual citizens.

That response is the subject of the book's third and final volume. It is a response that will be neither managerial nor revolutionary. It will ask more of the reader than either, and it will offer, we think, more in return. The citizen whose return the book has been arguing for is not a figure of nostalgia. The citizen is the only figure under whose aegis the public life of a modern republic can be put back together. Everything the first two volumes have tried to establish has been a clearing of the ground for the argument that begins in the pages that follow. The diagnosis ends here. The work of recovery begins.
