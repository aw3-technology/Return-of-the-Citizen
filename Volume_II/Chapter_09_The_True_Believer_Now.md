# Chapter 9
## The True Believer, Now

In 1951, in a country that had just finished winning a war against one totalitarianism and was beginning a long cold one against another, two books appeared within months of each other. The first was written by a self-taught longshoreman in San Francisco who had spent the previous decade loading ships on the waterfront and reading Montaigne in his boardinghouse at night. The second was written by a German-Jewish philosopher, stateless for eighteen years, who had escaped Gurs and Marseille and arrived in New York in 1941 with almost nothing and had since made herself, against her own temperament, into an American. Eric Hoffer's *The True Believer: Thoughts on the Nature of Mass Movements* and Hannah Arendt's *The Origins of Totalitarianism* were, on the surface, radically different projects. Hoffer's book was short, aphoristic, addressed to the common reader, allergic to jargon, and almost entirely free of footnotes. Arendt's was long, historical, architecturally ambitious, densely annotated, and relentlessly philosophical. They drew on different sources and had almost no overlap in their bibliographies. Their authors never, so far as we can tell, read one another with any seriousness. And yet, in the same year, on different coasts of the same country, looking back at what the twentieth century had just finished doing to Europe, Hoffer and Arendt independently arrived at the same core thesis.

That thesis is the subject of this chapter, because we think it is one of the most important things the twentieth century ever learned and one of the things our own moment has most successfully managed to forget. We want to state it plainly, at the beginning, and then walk it back to its sources, because the argument is more important than the authorities behind it and because the authorities will matter more once we have the argument firmly in mind.

The thesis is this. Mass movements — fascist, communist, nationalist, religious, revolutionary, identitarian, whatever their local content — recruit from a particular kind of person, and they produce a particular kind of person in turn, and the particular kind of person is the one whose structural condition is aloneness. Not the lonely person in the everyday sense — not the bored, the sad, the unpartnered — but the person who has fallen out of the associations that used to tell him who he was: family, congregation, neighborhood, vocation, class, the handful of contexts in which a human being is known by his own name and held to account by others who know him. When those contexts are stripped away, the person who remains is not really an individual in any rich sense; he is raw material. He will seek, with the force of a drowning man seeking air, a world he can belong to. If the world offered to him is a cult — and in our moment the world offered to him is almost always some form of cult — he will take it, because the alternative is to continue to belong to nothing. Mass movements are made from atomized persons. The lonely individual and the totalitarian mob are not opposites; they are the two sides of a single sheet of paper.

This is the connective argument of the book, and it is going to take this chapter and the next five to lay out properly. But it starts here, with Hoffer and Arendt, in the year they both published. We want to begin with Hoffer, because his idiom is the more accessible, and then walk slowly into Arendt, whose analysis goes a layer deeper.

***

Eric Hoffer was fifty-one when *The True Believer* came out. He had spent the Great Depression as a migratory agricultural laborer, riding freight cars up and down California, and the war years as a longshoreman in San Francisco, a job he kept for more than twenty years after his book became famous. He claimed, probably truthfully, that his entire formal education had ended in childhood; his philosophy he had acquired from public libraries. His prose reflects all of this. It is short-sentenced, physical, contemptuous of abstraction, and fond of the kind of compressed statement that gets passed from reader to reader as a quotation. He was not a systematic thinker, and his book does not pretend to be a system. It is a sequence of numbered observations, 125 of them, organized into parts, which together add up to a picture of what mass movements are and what kind of human being they require.

The picture begins with a claim that, when he made it, was genuinely shocking and that the ensuing seventy-five years have done nothing to dislodge: all mass movements, whatever their surface doctrines, are psychologically continuous with one another. "All mass movements," Hoffer wrote, "generate in their adherents a readiness to die and a proclivity for united action; all of them, irrespective of the doctrine they preach and the program they project, breed fanaticism, enthusiasm, fervent hope, hatred and intolerance; all of them are capable of releasing a powerful flow of activity in certain departments of life; all of them demand blind faith and single-hearted allegiance." And then, more pointedly: "All mass movements draw their adherents from the same types of humanity and appeal to the same types of mind."

One has to pause over that sentence, because it cuts against almost every intuition a person trained in modern political analysis has been taught to bring to such questions. The fascist and the communist were, in the Europe of Hoffer's childhood, the two poles of everything; they killed one another in the streets; each defined himself by his opposition to the other. Hoffer's claim is that if you strip away the content and look at the function the movement was performing in the recruit's psyche, you find the same operation in both cases. The content is interchangeable. "A mass movement attracts and holds a following," he wrote, "not because it can satisfy the desire for self-advancement, but because it can satisfy the passion for self-renunciation." The recruit is not joining because he believes the doctrine; he is joining because the movement allows him to stop being himself. The doctrine is the vehicle for the self-renunciation, and any sufficiently absolute doctrine will do.

This is where Hoffer introduces the figure who gives the book its title: the true believer. The true believer, in Hoffer's usage, is not simply someone who believes strongly. He is someone for whom belief is a means of escape. "Faith in a holy cause," Hoffer wrote, "is to a considerable extent a substitute for the lost faith in ourselves." The true believer is fleeing himself. His own life, as he experiences it, is spoiled beyond repair — not necessarily in the material sense, though material ruin will do the job efficiently, but in the sense that he cannot bear to inhabit it any longer as himself. The movement offers him the possibility of putting the self down. "Losing one's individual distinctness in a compact collective whole" is Hoffer's phrase, and it is worth holding in mind, because it is the exact operation every mass movement performs and offers. The cult member becomes the cult. The partisan becomes the party. The convert dissolves into the doctrine. This dissolution is not experienced as loss; it is experienced as relief.

What follows from this account is a series of consequences that will look, to anyone who has watched a modern political movement radicalize, immediately familiar. First, mass movements need a devil more than they need a God. "Mass movements can rise and spread without belief in a God," Hoffer wrote, "but never without belief in a devil." The enemy is constitutive; without him there is no movement. The devil must be ubiquitous, omnipotent, and unmistakably evil, because the intensity of the movement's cohesion is proportional to the clarity of the enemy it defines itself against. A movement that cannot identify its devil will dissolve; a movement with a sufficiently vivid devil can survive the failure of its every positive doctrine. Second, the true believer is not in any meaningful sense attached to the specific movement he happens to be in. He is attached to the operation of self-renunciation. He can and will, when the opportunity presents itself, switch movements — from communism to fascism, from religious zealotry to revolutionary politics, from one identity crusade to another — with a fluency that baffles observers who think he was loyal to the content rather than the form. Hoffer observed this repeatedly in the European exiles of his generation. The fascist and the Stalinist were, at the psychological level, often the same man; the Trotskyist who became a Cold Warrior was not converting, he was continuing. The self-renunciation remained; the vehicle changed.

Third, and most important for our purposes, Hoffer located the reservoir of potential true believers with precision. The reservoir is not the already-engaged — not the politically committed, the religiously devout, the ideologically sophisticated. The reservoir is the frustrated: the men and women who experience their lives as irredeemably spoiled and who therefore crave the opportunity to put those lives down. "The less justified a man is in claiming excellence for his own self," Hoffer wrote, "the more ready is he to claim all excellence for his nation, his religion, his race or his holy cause." The frustrated is not always the poor, though poverty can produce him; he is not always the uneducated, though education alone will not save him; he is the person whose life has become, to himself, unbearable, and who therefore wants out of it by whatever door is offered. Modernity, Hoffer suspected, produces this kind of person at industrial scale, because modernity continuously detaches people from the old sources of meaning — craft, faith, kin, place — and does not reliably replace them with new ones.

And then, buried in the middle of his short book, Hoffer offered a sentence we want to quote at length because it will be the structural hinge of everything we try to do in the third volume: "Any arrangement which either discourages atomistic individualism or facilitates self-forgetting or offers chances for action and new beginnings tends to counteract the rise and spread of mass movements." Three prescriptions, in one line. Discourage atomistic individualism — meaning, give people a world to belong to that is more than the sum of their consumer preferences. Facilitate self-forgetting — but here note the word Hoffer uses, and the implication. He does not say: abolish self-forgetting. He knows, as any honest observer of human beings knows, that we all need, sometimes, to forget ourselves. The question is what we do it *for* and in what company. Prayer is self-forgetting; so is craft; so is friendship; so is serious work. The cult is the pathological form of a genuine need. Offer chances for action and new beginnings — meaning, let people do things. A person who can act in the world, who can see that his action has effect, who can begin something and watch it grow, is a person who does not need to put himself down to feel real.

Those three prescriptions, together, describe something close to what we used to mean by a citizen. A citizen is someone who has a world to belong to, who has legitimate forms of self-transcendence — friendship, work, membership, faith — available to him, and who can act in public to real effect. The cult's offer, Hoffer's deepest insight, is a counterfeit of citizenship. It offers belonging without participation, transcendence without effort, action without consequence. The antidote to the cult, then, is not anti-cult propaganda. The antidote is the thing the cult is counterfeiting.

Hoffer never worked this out systematically; it was not his temperament. But the outline is in his book, if one knows how to read for it, and it is the outline our own book is trying to fill in. The first volume made the philosophical argument for what a citizen is. This volume is describing, through six different diagnostic lenses, the conditions under which the citizen has been replaced by the true believer. The third volume will argue for the restoration of the thing the cult is counterfeiting. Hoffer, the longshoreman, saw most of the argument in 1951.

***

Arendt saw a different part of it, and saw it more deeply.

*The Origins of Totalitarianism* is a much longer and more difficult book than Hoffer's, and the part of it we want to focus on here is only one section of one part — the final chapter, "Ideology and Terror," and the "totalitarianism" section's climactic analysis of what she called "the masses." But this section is, we think, one of the two or three most important sustained pieces of political thinking the twentieth century produced, and it is the deepest account in the literature of what it actually means for a person to be available for totalitarian capture.

Arendt's starting observation is empirical and disconcerting. Totalitarian movements, she noticed — the Nazi movement in Germany, the Bolshevik movement as it consolidated under Stalin — had not recruited from among the politically engaged. They had not recruited from the workers' parties, where class consciousness produced its own rival forms of organization; they had not recruited from the bourgeois liberal parties, or from the professional associations, or from the churches, or from the organized trade unions, or from the established civic bodies. Where did they recruit from? From what Arendt called "the masses," meaning not the lower classes in the old Marxist sense but something newer and, in her view, more ominous: the politically unattached. The atomized. The people who had, for one reason or another, fallen out of every prior form of affiliation and now belonged to nothing. The "neutral, politically indifferent people who never join a party and hardly ever go to the polls" — these were the reservoir.

This was a staggering claim at the time it was made and it has become less staggering only because we have had seventy-five years to absorb it. It means, for one thing, that the received liberal image of the totalitarian — the fanatic, the committed ideologue, the lifelong party cadre — has the causal direction exactly backwards. The fanatic is not the raw material of the movement; the fanatic is the product of the movement. The raw material is the man or woman who had no politics before the movement came for them. The ideological intensity that we associate with totalitarianism is, in Arendt's account, manufactured by the movement out of human material that was previously characterized by the opposite quality — by a kind of listless disengagement, a sense that nothing connected to anything else, a feeling that public life was a theater in which they had no part.

This observation is not merely historical. It is structural. Arendt's point is that the condition of being unattached, of belonging to no class and no party and no association and no faith and no place, is itself a political condition, and it is the condition that makes totalitarian capture possible. She gave that condition a name, and the name she gave it is the one that has come to define her legacy: *loneliness*. "What prepares men for totalitarian domination in the non-totalitarian world," she wrote, "is the fact that loneliness, once a borderline experience usually suffered in certain marginal social conditions like old age, has become an everyday experience of the ever-growing masses of our century."

The word loneliness, in Arendt's use, is a technical term and has to be heard as one. It does not mean what it means in ordinary speech. In ordinary speech, loneliness is a mood, an affect, the pang a person feels on a Friday night without anyone to call. Arendt distinguished that ordinary loneliness, which she called solitude (being alone with oneself, which is the condition of thinking) and isolation (being cut off from political action, which is the condition of tyranny) from the deeper thing she was naming. Loneliness, in her technical sense, is the experience of not belonging to a world at all — the experience of having nowhere that one's reality is confirmed by others, no "place" in the political-existential sense, no ground one can stand on among equals. The lonely person, for Arendt, is not someone who has no friends for the evening. The lonely person is someone who cannot trust the evidence of her own senses because there is no one around her who shares the world in which those senses make meaning.

That is a dark thought and we want to sit with it for a moment, because it is the center of Arendt's diagnosis. A human being, for Arendt, is fundamentally plural. We are what we are in the presence of others; our reality is confirmed by their reality; we think, in the full sense of the word, only when we have other minds to think with, and we act, in the full sense of the word, only in a public where our action appears to others and is taken up by them. Strip a person of that plural context — the family she grew up in, the neighborhood she could walk through and be greeted in, the church that told her who she was in the eyes of God, the workplace that told her who she was in the eyes of her peers, the political community in which she had standing as a citizen — and you have done something to her more profound than loneliness in the colloquial sense. You have severed her from the world. She can no longer trust what she sees, because no one sees it with her; she can no longer think, because she has no one to think with; she can no longer act, because her action falls into a void and produces nothing. At that point she is, in Arendt's phrase, ready. The movement that comes to her next is not a movement she will rationally evaluate. It will be a movement she will fall into, because the alternative is to remain in the void.

"Loneliness," Arendt wrote, in the sentence that ought to be engraved over every doorway in a twenty-first-century civic revival, "is the common ground for terror, the essence of totalitarian government." Not the cause, note — she is careful. Totalitarianism has many causes, some structural, some historical, some contingent on particular leaders and particular moments. Loneliness is the *common ground*: the soil in which terror grows, the human substrate that has to be present for the political disaster to happen. A society of thickly embedded persons — persons with a place in a world — can resist totalitarian movements in ways a society of the lonely cannot. A society of the lonely will fall to the first movement that offers it a world, and the movement it offers will be, of necessity, fictional, because a real world is what has been lost and a fictional one is what remains available.

What the movement offers the lonely person, in Arendt's account, is not simply belonging. It is a *coherent fictional reality* — an ideology, in her strict sense of the word, meaning a system of thought that proceeds from a single premise by inexorable logical necessity to a complete account of everything that has happened, is happening, and will happen. The beauty of an ideology, for someone whose sense of reality has been eroded, is that it explains everything. Nothing is contingent; nothing is merely confusing; nothing is up for interpretation. Every event confirms the system. Every objection to the system is itself predicted by the system and classified in advance. The ideology gives the lonely person what the lost world used to give her: a stable sense of what is real. The cost is that the new sense of reality is a fantasy, and a fantasy with an enemy, because fantasies of this kind always have enemies. But the cost, for the lonely person, is worth paying. The cost of the alternative — of staying lonely, of continuing not to have a world — is higher.

This analysis has an uncomfortable implication, and Arendt was unflinching about it. If the susceptibility to totalitarian capture is rooted in loneliness in her technical sense, then it is not a moral failing on the part of the captured. It is not, centrally, that the true believer is stupid, or evil, or insufficiently critical, or poorly educated. The true believer is, in most cases, a person who has been placed in a structural condition under which thinking, in the full Arendtian sense, has become impossible, because thinking requires a world and the world has been taken from her. You cannot fix loneliness by exhortation. You cannot tell a drowning person to swim better. The lonely cannot think their way out of loneliness, because the capacity to think is itself one of the things loneliness takes.

We want to pause on this, because it is where Arendt goes further than Hoffer and where the argument becomes genuinely uncomfortable for a certain kind of modern liberal temperament — a temperament we admit to sharing. The liberal response to political extremism is almost always pedagogical: teach people to think critically, expose them to better arguments, improve the quality of public information, invest in media literacy, strengthen civic education. Everything on that list is good; much of it is necessary; some of it is what the third volume of this book will argue for. But none of it touches the ground Arendt identified. You cannot critical-think your way out of the absence of a world. The absence of a world is prior to, and deeper than, any cognitive intervention you can make against it. Which means that any serious response to mass politics has to be, at bottom, not epistemic but ontological — it has to restore the conditions under which thinking becomes possible again, which means restoring the plural contexts in which persons are real to one another. That, in one sentence, is the argument of the third volume of this book.

***

Hoffer and Arendt are not quite the same, and we want to mark the difference before we bring them together. Hoffer's register is psychological; he is interested in the interior state of the true believer, the dynamics of self-renunciation and self-loss. Arendt's register is structural and political; she is interested in the conditions under which the interior state becomes available and what kind of world the movement is built to offer in response. Hoffer works from the inside out; Arendt from the outside in. Hoffer's prescriptions, insofar as he offers them, are about offering persons legitimate channels for self-forgetting. Arendt's prescription, insofar as she offers one, is the restoration of the public realm in which persons can act as equals. Hoffer writes like a man who has watched his friends disappear into ideological movements one by one and wants to understand what happened to them. Arendt writes like a woman who has watched her civilization collapse and wants to understand what made the collapse possible.

But when you put the two accounts next to each other, what emerges is a picture with more power than either account on its own. The psychological state Hoffer describes — the spoiled life, the readiness for self-renunciation, the hunger for a cause — is exactly what Arendt's structural condition produces. The structural condition Arendt describes — the loss of the plural world, the descent into loneliness, the availability for ideological capture — is exactly what makes Hoffer's psychological state widespread. Neither account is complete without the other. The mass movement is the joint product of a psychological hunger and a structural void. Fix only the psychology and the void remains, and new hungers will arise. Fix only the structure and the hungers go unmet, and the next void that opens will be filled the same way. Both have to be addressed, which means the diagnosis has to be seen as a single thing even though the authors worked in different idioms.

The single thing it amounts to is what we have been calling, since the introduction, the connective argument of this book. The modern individual, stripped of the associations in which he used to be formed, is the raw material of the mass. The lonely person and the cult member are not opposites; they are successive conditions of the same person. The movement does not oppose atomization; it completes it. Both Hoffer and Arendt, working independently, saw this clearly in the same year. It is the single most important diagnostic insight their century produced, and our century has largely mislaid it.

***

What happened after 1951 is that their argument was taken up, operationalized, and extended, primarily by a small group of clinicians and researchers who began, in the 1950s and 1960s, to study the techniques by which specific movements and groups had captured specific individuals. This is the modern cult literature, and it is the indispensable supplement to Hoffer and Arendt, because it shows in concrete and replicable detail what Hoffer and Arendt had described in general terms.

The founding text is Robert Jay Lifton's *Thought Reform and the Psychology of Totalism*, published in 1961. Lifton was a psychiatrist who had studied the returning Americans and Chinese who had been subjected to "re-education" in Maoist prisons and re-education camps in the late 1940s and early 1950s. His book laid out eight criteria — he called them, deliberately flatly, the eight criteria of a thought-reform environment — and they have held up with remarkable durability in the half-century since. We will name them because the names are useful, and because a reader who knows the names can begin to recognize the operations in environments he encounters. *Milieu control*: the regulation of all information entering and exiting the group, so that the member's informational world is entirely composed of the group's messages. *Mystical manipulation*: the engineering of experiences — "coincidences," emotional peaks, moments of apparent supernatural confirmation — that the member interprets as evidence of the group's special status. *Demand for purity*: the establishment of an absolute binary between the pure (the group, the doctrine, the in-group) and the impure (the world, the skeptical, the out-group), with constant pressure to identify and purge impurity. *Cult of confession*: the practice of continual self-disclosure to group authorities, by which the member's interiority is opened to the group's inspection and shaped by the group's response. *Sacred science*: the presentation of the group's doctrine as both absolute moral truth and rigorously scientific explanation, such that no evidence could count against it and no ethical consideration could stand above it. *Loading the language*: the development of an in-group vocabulary that replaces ordinary words with special terms, compresses complex matters into thought-terminating clichés, and makes communication with outsiders progressively more difficult. *Doctrine over person*: the subordination of lived human experience to the group's teaching, such that when a member's experience contradicts the doctrine, it is the experience that must be wrong. *Dispensing of existence*: the claim, made explicit or implicit, that the group has the right to decide who is fully human, who deserves to exist within the moral universe the group recognizes, and who has forfeited that standing by disagreement or departure.

Those eight criteria, taken together, describe an environment in which the Hoffer/Arendt diagnosis has been operationalized: an environment engineered to absorb the lonely individual and render her no longer capable of the independent thought that would allow her to leave. Every one of Lifton's criteria is, on inspection, a technique for doing to a person what Hoffer and Arendt had described being done to whole populations in the totalitarian twentieth century. The continuity is not accidental. Lifton was explicitly building on the same underlying insight.

Margaret Singer, a clinical psychologist who had worked with Lifton on some of the same material, took the next step. Over a forty-year career at Berkeley and in private clinical practice, she worked directly with former cult members, documented the damage their affiliations had done, and translated Lifton's criteria into tools clinicians could use to identify high-demand groups and to help people extricate themselves. *Cults in Our Midst*, published in 1995, was her summary work. Singer's achievement was to make the Lifton framework operational — to show, with case after case, what the criteria looked like in practice and how the practitioner could distinguish between an intense but healthy commitment and a captured one. She was also, and this matters, careful about the distinction. Not every religious order is a cult. Not every political movement is a cult. Not every family system is a cult. The framework applies when a specific constellation of coercive features is present, and its seriousness comes precisely from its specificity. A framework that treats every passionate affiliation as captivity is useless; a framework that names the specific mechanisms of captivity is a diagnostic instrument.

Steven Hassan, one of Singer's students and a former member of the Unification Church who had been extracted from it in the 1970s, extended the work in two directions. The first was a refinement of the diagnostic: his BITE Model identified four domains of control — Behavior, Information, Thought, Emotion — and laid out specific indicators under each. A group that controls a member's behavior (what she does, where she goes, whom she sees, what she eats and when she sleeps), her information (what she reads, what she is told, what is kept from her), her thought (what she is allowed to think, what vocabulary she is permitted, what conclusions are forbidden), and her emotion (what she is allowed to feel, what she must feel, how she processes guilt and fear and love) is exerting the kind of totalistic influence the Hoffer/Arendt analysis predicts. The BITE Model is not a lie detector and cannot replace judgment; but it is a usable tool for sorting the intense from the captive.

The second contribution was ethical and methodological, and we think it is the more important of the two. The early literature on cult extraction — the 1970s and 1980s "deprogramming" movement — had been willing to use coercion: people were literally kidnapped and held against their will while "deprogrammers" tried to break their attachment to the group. The methods were sometimes abusive and were occasionally criminal; the underlying premise was that since the cult had taken the person's agency, the rescuer was entitled to take it back by force. Hassan broke with this. His method, which he called *exit counseling* or *strategic interactive approach*, was explicitly voluntary, relational, and ethical. The recognition he built on was that the cult's deepest wound was the erosion of the member's relational world — family, old friends, non-cult attachments — and the path out was the careful, patient, non-coercive restoration of those relationships. You do not rescue a person from a cult by overpowering her. You rescue her, if she can be rescued, by being present to her as the cult cannot be: by treating her, persistently and without force, as the full person the cult has tried to replace.

That distinction — between coercive deprogramming and relational exit counseling — is a distinction this book will return to, because it is the distinction between two radically different models of how one meets a person in captivity. It matters a great deal which one you choose.

In the years since January 2021, Hassan and others have applied this framework beyond traditional religious cults to political radicalization: to the online conspiracist communities whose members' families began calling exit counselors in numbers the field had not seen before, to the militant identity movements whose members show many of the same signs, to the radicalized members of one's own extended family whose behavior had changed in ways that the old language of "political disagreement" could not account for. The application is not a metaphor. The structural features the frameworks were built to detect are in fact present, in varying degrees, in a wide range of contemporary political formations. Which brings us to the move this chapter most needs to make, and the move we want to make as carefully as we know how.

***

The move is symmetry. Hoffer and Arendt and Lifton and Singer and Hassan were not partisan figures; their frameworks are not left-coded or right-coded; the diagnostic applies to any movement that satisfies the criteria, regardless of where it sits on the ordinary political spectrum. The point of making the symmetry explicit is not to equate specific contemporary movements as though they were interchangeable in their content or their likely political consequences. They are not. The point is to note that they share a *structure*, the structure of mass formation, and that each of them attracts and produces exactly the kind of person Hoffer and Arendt were writing about — the lonely individual seeking a coherent world, willing to forget himself in the company of the saved.

Consider, then, a brief and necessarily partial set of examples.

On the far right: the constellation of movements that has formed in the last decade around apocalyptic conspiracism — the quasi-religious belief in a hidden elite, the expectation of imminent reckoning, the identification of enemies as literally diabolical rather than merely mistaken. The demand for purity is explicit; the sacred science is total (one explanation accounts for all events); the loading of language is immediate (every movement of this kind rapidly develops its own vocabulary, its own thought-terminating clichés, its own set of in-group signals); the enemy is cosmic. Milieu control is often achieved algorithmically — the member is pulled into a media environment in which every input confirms the doctrine and every outside source is flagged as hostile. The structure is a textbook instance of the frameworks we have just walked through.

On the far left: the more extreme currents of certain identity-political formations, where the features are often harder to see because the participants are frequently highly educated and fluent in the language of critical theory. But the features are there. Milieu control is enforced by social and professional consequence; dispensing of existence operates as social and sometimes economic exile (the member who deviates is not merely argued with but excluded, often permanently, from the relational and professional world in which she used to live); the loading of language is unusually developed, with a rapidly shifting vocabulary that functions both as moral vocabulary and as in-group marker, such that ignorance of the current terminology is taken as evidence of impurity; doctrine over person is explicit, with a member's lived experience subordinated to the theoretical account of what that experience must be; sacred science is total in its own idiom. The content is radically different from the far right's; the structure is, on close examination, the same structure.

Religious fundamentalisms of various kinds — and here we want to be careful to include the ones we think of as "ours," whichever "ours" we mean. Certain currents of prosperity-gospel evangelicalism; certain aggressively traditionalist currents within Catholicism; certain militant strains of Jewish and Muslim affiliation in our own moment; fundamentalisms are not the property of any single religion, and they are not opposed to their religions as such. They are a specific operation performed on a religion: demand for purity, sacred science, enemy-centric theology, a loading of language that marks insiders and outsiders, an ethics that is fully subordinated to doctrine. The religion without the operation is a source of meaning, belonging, and moral formation of exactly the kind this book will argue for. The religion with the operation is a cult that happens to use religious vocabulary. The distinction matters, and it is available to a careful reader.

Then there are the newer and less obvious examples. The tech eschatologies — the singulatarian communities, the "effective altruism" formations in their more totalizing phases, the e/acc and longtermist subcultures, the communities gathered around the belief that we stand at the hinge of cosmic history and that an imminent transformation will either redeem or destroy the human species. These are unusually interesting cases, because their members tend to be extremely well educated and to think of themselves as the most rigorously rational people in the world. The Hoffer/Arendt/Lifton frameworks apply to them too, and in some ways with particular clarity. Sacred science is vivid — the doctrine is presented as both morally absolute and maximally rigorous. Loading the language is extensive (FOOM, p(doom), coherent extrapolated volition, a whole vocabulary that shapes the member's thought from the inside); doctrine over person is common (a member's ethical intuitions are routinely dismissed when they conflict with the theory of value the community has derived); a truth so urgent is posited — the fate of all future beings, say — that ordinary ethical constraints are presented as obstacles rather than obligations. Milieu control emerges naturally from the community's distinctive vocabulary and social world. The fact that the members are brilliant does not exempt them; it changes the register, but the structure is the same structure.

And finally, the phenomenon that researchers have begun to call *conspirituality* — the migration of certain New Age and wellness subcultures into political extremism, often by way of conspiratorial explanation of illness, medicine, and power. The movement of a certain kind of yoga studio, a certain kind of alternative-health community, a certain kind of spiritual influencer, into the orbit of overtly far-right politics has been well documented in the last five years. The mechanism, on close inspection, is precisely the one we have been describing. A community built around personal transformation and a hunger for hidden meaning is, without quite noticing, retrofitted with the apparatus of conspiratorial cosmology, and becomes available for political capture because the underlying psychological operation — the offer of a coherent hidden world for a person whose ordinary world feels inadequate — was always there.

We could keep going. The point is not to be exhaustive; the point is to establish that the phenomenon is not rare, not confined to one political pole, not confined to the uneducated, not confined to any particular demographic or age bracket. It is the characteristic political pathology of our moment, and it has the structure Hoffer and Arendt described in 1951 because it is made out of the same human material they saw then: atomized individuals seeking a world.

***

A chapter like this runs a specific set of risks and we want to be honest about them, because a diagnostic tool that cannot see itself becomes an idol.

The first risk is the one the preface flagged. Calling something a cult is a rhetorical weapon, and it is often deployed in bad faith. "Cult" in careless usage is simply a term of abuse for any movement one disagrees with. Used that way, the analysis is worse than useless; it is itself a form of the thing it claims to diagnose, a thought-terminating cliché that allows the user to refuse engagement with whatever he has labeled. The whole point of building out the Lifton/Singer/Hassan framework with specific, observable criteria is precisely to prevent this slippage. The diagnosis applies when the criteria are met. It does not apply when one merely dislikes the movement's politics. A movement can be bad, dangerous, foolish, wrong about every important question it takes a position on, and still not be a cult in the technical sense we are using. A movement can be sympathetic in its broad aims and unambiguous in its cult structure. These are orthogonal axes, and treating them as the same axis is exactly the error the serious literature is designed to help us avoid.

The second risk is subtler, and it is one we have to flag against ourselves. A person can become a true believer in the thesis that everyone else is a true believer. The Hoffer/Arendt framework, wielded obsessively, becomes its own kind of closed system — every counterexample is explained, every disagreement is diagnosed, every rival view is re-described as a symptom rather than engaged as an argument. At that point the user of the framework has stopped thinking with it and started hiding behind it. The framework is a tool; it is not the whole of political analysis; it does not explain every political phenomenon; sometimes people disagree because they have different values or different information, not because one of them is captured and the other is free. A writer who cannot see this, who cannot distinguish ordinary political commitment from the technical thing Lifton and Singer named, has become the thing he was trying to describe. We have tried, in the pages above, to name the phenomenon precisely enough that this slippage is harder to perform. The reader is entitled to hold us to that standard.

The third risk is the one More in Common's research throws most sharply into relief. The perception-gap work that we will discuss at length in the polarization chapter documents a striking finding: both sides of American politics, in large numbers, view the other side as a cult. Both sides are, in this judgment, partly right and largely wrong. The structural features are present, in some corners, on both sides; but the structural features are not present at the scale each side believes the other's are. The use of the Hoffer/Arendt lens by one partisan against his opponents, without the corresponding application of the lens to his own side, is itself a symptom of the same affective polarization the lens should be helping him see. Any honest user of this framework has to begin with the recognition that the framework is also being weaponized against her, and by users who have every bit as much claim to it as she does. The framework cuts in every direction at once, or it is not being used honestly.

We will say finally that calling a movement a cult, in the sense we are using the term, is not a way of writing its members off. It is closer to the opposite. A committed partisan is a person with a political identity; a cult member, in the technical sense, is a person who has been captured by an ideology that treats her as a means. The point of seeing the capture clearly is not to condemn the captured. It is to take seriously the fact that something has been done to her that is not her fault, and to understand that the path out, for her, is a path that has to be offered by people who refuse to treat her the way the cult has trained her to expect. The exit counseling tradition has things to teach a civic culture here; we will return to it in the third volume.

***

What, then, follows from all of this for the book's larger thesis?

If Hoffer and Arendt and Lifton and Singer and Hassan are right — and we have argued in this chapter that, in their core claim, they are — then the response to the mass politics of our moment cannot be, centrally, a counter-ideology. Counter-ideology tries to defeat a movement on its own terrain; it assumes the terrain is debate, when in fact the terrain is loneliness. A person who has been captured because she had no world to belong to will not be released by a better argument. She might, in some cases, be released by exit counseling; she will be prevented from being captured in the first place by the existence of a world she already belongs to.

Which means, as we have been saying since the introduction, that the solution to the true-believer problem is not an argument aimed at the true believers. It is the restoration of the conditions — the thick local associations, the meaningful work, the face-to-face communities, the institutions that form rather than perform — under which persons do not become available as raw material. That is the Volume III argument, and this chapter has been laying its load-bearing wall.

We want to end with a sentence that is as modest and as ambitious as we can make it. This book does not promise to talk anyone out of his cult. We do not believe such a book is possible. What it does promise is that *preventing* mass formation is possible, because mass formation has knowable causes, and the causes can be addressed. The citizen is the name of the person who cannot be captured, because he is already held — by his relationships, by his memberships, by his work, by his standing in a public he recognizes and that recognizes him. The chapters that follow will describe, in detail, what has happened to the conditions under which citizens are possible. The volume after that will describe what we can do, now, about it.

Hoffer, at the end of *The True Believer*, allowed himself a quiet sentence about what the end of a mass movement actually looks like. "A movement is pioneered by men of words," he wrote, "materialized by fanatics, and consolidated by men of action." The frame of the book is tragic; the sentence is, at minimum, realistic. Our project is to write a different kind of book, about a different kind of movement: one pioneered by citizens, materialized by citizens, and consolidated by citizens — in which the operative word is, in each case, the same, and means what it used to mean. Whether such a movement is available to us is a question we will take up in the third volume. Whether the movements on offer to us now are the kind Hoffer and Arendt were writing about is a question we think, after this chapter, we can consider settled.
