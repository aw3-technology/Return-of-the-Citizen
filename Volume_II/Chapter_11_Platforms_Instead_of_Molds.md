# Chapter 11
## Platforms Instead of Molds

For about half a century now, Americans have been losing faith in their institutions. The Gallup numbers are the most familiar version of the story and they are, by any standard, dramatic. Confidence in doctors and hospitals has slid from around eighty percent in the early 1970s to thirty-six percent by the late 2010s. Confidence in organized religion has fallen from sixty-five percent to thirty-eight. The public schools have gone from sixty percent to twenty-nine. The press has collapsed in much the same way; Congress has collapsed further, from forty-two percent to the low double digits and sometimes lower. Nothing about the curve is ambiguous. Whatever else is true about American life in this period, the people who live inside it no longer believe in the arrangements that are supposed to hold it together.

The standard explanations for this are external. They locate the cause of the collapse outside the institution in question: bad leaders, bad press coverage, bad actors. The doctors are caught in a documentary; the church is caught in a scandal; the newspaper is caught in a lie; the senator is caught on tape. The collapse is presented, in effect, as a series of discoveries — as a public slowly learning what had been hidden from it. On this account the remedy is to fix whichever part of the institution produced the discovery and to reassure the public that the rest of the machine is still working.

There is some truth in this. Scandals are real. Bad leaders are real. Media that magnify both are real. But the explanation is too small for the phenomenon it is trying to explain. Fifty years of nearly continuous decline across almost every major institution in American life is not a story about the occasional rotten apple. Something about the barrel is different. And the most useful recent account of how the barrel is different is the one given by Yuval Levin in *A Time to Build*, published in 2020 — an argument that has become, for us and for a growing number of writers who share our worry about the citizen, the most productive way into the institutional problem.

Levin's central move is to reframe the question. The problem of institutional decay, he argues, is not primarily a problem of how institutions appear from outside. It is a problem of how the people inside the institutions behave. The loss of trust is not a perceptual accident or a narrative failure or a public-relations breakdown. It is the accurate report of an accurate change. The institutions are being used differently than they used to be, and they have lost, in the using, the thing that made them trustworthy in the first place.

To describe that change Levin offers a pair of terms that has become, in the few years since, something close to a piece of common vocabulary among serious readers of institutions. An institution can be used as a *mold* or as a *platform*. The distinction is simple and, once noticed, nearly everywhere.

***

An institution is a mold when it forms the people who pass through it. The medical school forms a doctor. The law firm forms a lawyer. The parish church forms a Christian and forms, over time, a pastor. The newspaper forms a reporter. The committee, the campus, the bar, the academy, the regiment, the seminary — each is a set of norms and habits and constraints and duties, applied over time, to a person who consents in some serious way to being shaped by them. The medical student learns to think diagnostically because her professors think diagnostically in front of her and require her to do the same. The young associate learns to write a brief by writing one badly and having it corrected by a partner who has written three thousand of them. The seminarian learns to preach not primarily from a book on preaching but from standing, week after week, in a congregation whose collective life he is being asked to speak into. The institution pours a set of expectations around a person; the expectations harden; the person comes out recognizable as an example of the kind the institution was trying to produce. Not identical to every other example — molds have some give — but recognizable. You meet a doctor and you know, within a minute or two, that a doctor is what you are meeting. That is the mold at work.

An institution is a platform, by contrast, when the person uses it to project herself. On the platform model the doctor acquires the medical degree and then uses the credential to build a wellness brand, or a podcast, or a supplement line, or a public profile on which her actual clinical practice is a small and shrinking component. The legislator arrives in Washington and uses the committee seat as a studio, the hearing as a stage, the vote as a prop in a content strategy. The professor accumulates a tenure case and then uses the university as a platform from which to issue commentary, appear on cable, collect speaking fees, and accrete followers. The pastor treats the pulpit as a venue for the cultivation of an authenticity brand, and the congregation as an audience. In each case the direction of formation has reversed. The institution is no longer shaping the person; the person is using the institution to shape her audience.

The distinction is not a caricature. Almost everyone who lives inside one of these institutions feels both pulls and in some proportion gives in to both. Few of us are the pure mold-submissive and few of us are the pure platform-performer. The question is which of the two logics is organizing the institution's work — which one the younger members are catching from their elders, which one the incentives point toward, which one the reward system rewards. And the honest answer, across an extraordinary range of American institutions, is that over the last several decades the platform logic has been winning.

***

Why does it matter? One can imagine a perfectly reasonable reply: so what if the doctor has a podcast on the side. So what if the senator makes good clips. This is the age of personal branding. People have always had reputations; now they have reputations at scale. Let them.

It matters because institutions used as platforms lose the capacity to form anyone. That is the whole of the argument, and it is worth sitting with. A medical school whose faculty members primarily use their positions to build personal followings does not produce the same kind of doctor that a medical school whose faculty members primarily use their positions to teach medicine produces. The difference does not lie in the curriculum, or not only there. It lies in what the student is watching. She is watching the grown-ups. She is learning, from what they do rather than from what they say, what it means to be a doctor. If what they do is perform, then she learns that medicine is a performance. If what they do is submit themselves to the constraints of the role and do the boring, demanding, often unrewarded work of diagnosis and care, she learns that medicine is a craft and that she is apprenticed to it. The same is true everywhere. Institutions are not principally teaching mechanisms; they are principally imitative communities. People inside them become, over time, the kind of people the senior members already are.

This is why the public's loss of trust is not a misperception. The doctor who treats her practice as a personal brand cannot be trusted as a doctor in the way her grandmother's doctor could, because the part of her attention that used to be occupied by the patient in front of her is now occupied by the audience watching her treat the patient in front of her. The legislator who treats his seat as a performance vehicle cannot be trusted as a legislator, because the performance logic is not compatible with the quiet, grinding, compromise-heavy work of actually writing a law that functions. The journalist who tweets his feelings in real time and whose personal following is larger than his paper's circulation cannot be trusted to report, because the economy of his attention has shifted away from the things the mold of journalism exists to produce — verification, fairness, thoroughness, the patient refusal to say more than you know — and toward the things the platform rewards, which is the opposite of those things. The public is not being paranoid. The institutions have, in real ways, changed their output, and the public can feel it.

Trust collapses, then, not mostly because people are lying, though some are. Trust collapses because people are using the institutions in a way that makes the institutions unworthy of trust. This is an entirely different diagnosis than the scandal-and-media-coverage explanation. It does not require a villain. It does not require a conspiracy. It simply requires that, on the inside of the institution, the center of gravity has shifted from the mold to the platform, and that the public — which deals with the outputs — has correctly picked up on the shift.

***

Against this general pattern, one American institution continues to hold something like its historic level of public confidence. The military has sagged a little from its peak but remains, alone among major institutions, broadly trusted across the parties and the generations. It is worth asking why, because the answer is instructive — and because the answer is not, we should be clear at the outset, that the military is simply good and the other institutions simply bad.

The military remains, to an unusual degree in contemporary American life, an institution-as-mold. A young person who joins enters a formative structure that is essentially uninterested in her as a brand. Her uniform is identical to the uniforms of a million others. Her title comes from her role. Her habits are corrected by her superiors. She is socialized, over years, into a set of norms — chain of command, duty, mission, the subordination of personal ambition to a collective task — that are explicitly not her own invention and are explicitly not there to serve her self-expression. The institution sanctions, often heavily, attempts to use the role for personal brand-building; the officer who goes on television without authorization to advance her own profile is not promoted, she is disciplined. Whatever else one thinks about the contemporary American military, its internal culture still operates on the logic of formation. It forms people. It is recognizable as an institution that does that.

This is not a blanket endorsement. We are not writing a paean to the armed forces. The military has its own pathologies — covered up, recurring, sometimes grave. Its strategic record in the last three decades is genuinely contested and the book takes no side in that contest here. Its proper role in the polity is a real question for a democratic republic and one that our forebears, from Washington to Eisenhower, were right to worry about. The unusual trust Americans place in the military cuts in more than one direction; it can become the kind of trust that bypasses democratic scrutiny rather than grounds it, and we are wary of that. None of those complications goes away. What we are asking of the reader is something narrower. The military is a useful datum because it is the large American institution in which the mold logic is most visibly still operating, and the public's response to it is the response the public tends to give to institutions that still form the people inside them. The lesson is not "be the military." The lesson is that when an institution forms its members — when it imposes roles and sanctions deviation from them and subordinates the personal to the mission — the public notices, and extends to the institution something the public no longer extends to institutions that have abandoned that work. The trust does not come from charisma or from marketing. It comes from formation.

***

The platform pathology shows up differently in different institutions but the underlying shape is the same. Consider the congressional committee. The committee system is, among other things, the core formative apparatus of the modern American Congress. It is where junior members learn to legislate, where they apprentice under senior members, where substantive disagreement gets hammered into compromise language, where policy gets built by people who have done nothing else for a long time. This is slow work and boring work and mostly invisible work. Until the last couple of decades the mold of Congress — living in Washington part of the week, raising one's children alongside the children of the other party's members, being on committees for years rather than months, having to face the same colleagues tomorrow whose vote you need on something else — bore down on the member in the specific way an institution bears down, and the member was in some degree shaped by it.

The modern pathology of Congress is that its members increasingly do not experience the committee this way. The hearing is not primarily an occasion for the collection of testimony and the clarification of a legislative problem; it is an occasion for the production of clips. A question is not primarily designed to elicit information from the witness; it is designed, often quite precisely, to produce thirty seconds of footage that will travel. The floor speech is not for the record; the record is for the speech. One sees this even in the cadence, the camera placement, the moment at which a member holds up a prop. None of this is because the members are worse people than their predecessors, and we want to be careful to insist on that. Most of them, in our experience, entered public life for recognizable reasons and retain recognizable convictions. The point is structural. The institution no longer forms them to do the work it was designed to do, because the institution no longer bears down on them in the old way. They are, increasingly, performing on a platform that happens to be called Congress.

Consider the university. The tenure-track professor is, in principle, being formed by a guild. She enters as a graduate student. She is trained in methods and in the habits of a discipline. She is reviewed by peers. She is gradually granted authority over other students, then over her own junior colleagues, then over the direction of the department, then over the governance of the institution. Each of these steps is, in its classical form, a mold; each makes certain kinds of demands on her and imposes certain kinds of constraints. The modern pathology is that a growing share of her professional reward has migrated away from that guild and toward a public audience. Her attention is pulled toward the op-ed, the substack, the keynote, the trade book, the cable commentary, the platform following. These are not illegitimate activities; many fine scholars have done some of them well. The question is which is primary. If the audience of strangers is primary, then the department, the committee, the graduate seminar, the faculty meeting, the undergraduate lecture, and the long slog of reading one's students' drafts become costs rather than the core of the vocation. They become the inconvenient overhead of the platform. And students catch this. A university in which the teachers experience teaching as overhead produces students who experience their degree as overhead — not a formation but a credential, not a community of inquiry but a checkpoint on the way to something they can do elsewhere. The university is being used, by both sides of the desk, as a platform; and its capacity to form anyone, teacher or student, erodes accordingly.

Consider the church and the religious community. Here the platform pathology acquires a specifically modern cast, because the thing being performed is often, precisely, authenticity — which is to say, the quality that cannot be performed. A pastor who cultivates his own brand builds a congregation around a persona, which then becomes the product the congregation is there to consume. The congregation is no longer a body knit by worship and sacrament and mutual obligation; it is an audience, and when the persona fails — as personas, in the end, all do — the community that gathered around it has no structure independent of the failure. The older understanding of the pastor, which survives in pockets of American religious life and is visible still in many traditions, is almost exactly the opposite. The pastor is a servant of an institution older than he is; he submits to its liturgy, its canon, its discipline, its elders; his personality is something he subordinates, gladly, to the role he has accepted. He is formed by the pulpit more than he uses the pulpit to form an image of himself. The congregations led by such figures are visibly different from the congregations led by personal brands, and the difference is not a matter of style; it is a matter of whether there is, in the room, anyone who is being formed.

Consider journalism. The trade has always had its stars and always will, and this is not an argument against reputation. It is an argument about what the reputation is for. The institutional mold of journalism consists in a set of boring, demanding habits that journalists used to absorb from older journalists and from the constraints of working at a paper. You verify. You give the other side a chance to respond. You try not to say more than you know. You are willing to be wrong in the first draft in order to be right in the final one. You accept that on any given day your story will be turned down or held by an editor whose judgment you respect and whose authority you acknowledge. These habits are the craft. The modern pathology is that they have become inconvenient to the platform. The reporter whose personal following is larger than her paper's circulation, whose career advances with her audience rather than with her newsroom, whose livelihood increasingly depends on the speed and the heat with which she can metabolize public events into posts — this reporter has, whether she wishes to or not, absorbed the platform's logic. The mold has been replaced by a stage. And the public, which once trusted journalism in numbers now difficult to imagine, correctly observes that the output is different.

And consider, finally, ordinary professional life. The platform logic has migrated from the unusual professions to all of them, along the wires of LinkedIn and its analogues. The employee is no longer primarily formed by her firm; she performs on it. Her profile is a product. Her tenure at a company is a chapter in a personal narrative rather than an apprenticeship inside a community of practice. She is encouraged, by the ambient norms of professional life, to treat every role as content and every colleague as a potential node in a network. There are real virtues in this — mobility, in particular, is a liberation from the older, constraining loyalties of firm and town — and we do not wish to romanticize the version that came before. But there is a cost, and the cost is mostly hidden, and the cost is that the mold of even the ordinary vocation has weakened. People bring to their work, increasingly, the attitude of the performer who is passing through, and they bring to their communities, increasingly, the same attitude. A society of performers has a hard time staffing the roles that require formation.

***

The causal story Levin tells has two layers. The first is cultural: the master value of modern life has shifted, over the course of the last century, from something like duty to something like self-expression. This is a long arc with many authors, from Charles Taylor's account of the ethics of authenticity to Christopher Lasch's bleaker rendition in *The Culture of Narcissism* and the late Robert Bellah's *Habits of the Heart*. However one reads the arc, the upshot for our purposes is that a civilization that regards self-expression as the highest good is going to be a civilization in which institutions-as-molds feel, on the inside, like violations of the self. The young associate's formation at the hands of the partner will be experienced as an insult rather than a gift. The junior member's apprenticeship to the senior member will be experienced as a denial of her voice. The seminarian's submission to the liturgy will be experienced as inauthentic. In each case the very thing the mold is designed to do — the quiet, durable shaping of a self to the demands of a role — runs against the grain of a culture that treats the unshaped self as the valuable one.

The second layer is institutional. The incentive structures of modern media, politics, and higher education have changed in ways that make platform behavior rational and mold behavior costly. The committee chair who refuses to play for clips loses to the colleague who does not. The tenured professor who devotes himself entirely to teaching and the department loses, in salary and in status, to the colleague who trades on a public reputation. The pastor who attends to the hundred elderly members of his aging congregation is outcompeted, in the attention of his denomination and the size of his flock, by the pastor building a personal brand. The reporter who spends three months on a story and kills it because she cannot verify it loses, in career terms, to the colleague who publishes the half-story and the subsequent apology. None of this is inevitable. Other incentive structures are possible, and institutions that deliberately design against the platform dynamic — as some still do — get different outputs. But the drift is real, and it is, at the margin, decisive.

We accept both of Levin's layers. We want to add a third, because we think the argument of this book asks for it.

The platform pathology is also, and we think inextricably, a symptom of the atomization we described in the last two chapters. A person who belongs to fewer thick associations — fewer families, fewer neighborhoods, fewer congregations, fewer guilds, fewer clubs — experiences every institution she encounters as a potential stage, because she has no audience for her personhood anywhere else. In a life thick with non-performative relationships, the office is one place among many, and the impulse to perform on it can be absorbed by the fact that one is already a person elsewhere — a son, a neighbor, a friend, a congregant, a coach. In a life thin with such relationships, the office is the only lit space. Of course one performs on it. Where else is one going to be seen? The lonely person has nowhere to be a person except in performance, and the institutions she enters pay the price for her loneliness. Social media, which is often singled out as the cause of the platform pathology, is better understood as the amplifier of a dynamic that was already underway. It gives the atomized modern person a cheap, endlessly available stage on which to stand. If she had somewhere else to be, she would not need the stage so much. This is partly why, as we will argue in the final volume, the repair of institutional life and the repair of associational life are the same project. You cannot ask the young professional to submit to the mold of her firm if her firm is the only community she has. You have to give her a life in which the firm can go back to being one thing among many.

***

Congress is the paradigm case, and it deserves its own paragraph, because the story of what has happened to it tracks the argument so precisely that it can function as the argument's portable demonstration.

Madison designed Congress, as the *Federalist* tells us clearly enough, to be a deliberative body in which factional conflict would be channeled into compromise. The mechanisms by which the channeling was supposed to occur were institutional: rules of debate, committee structures, norms of seniority, long terms in the Senate, the internal socialization of new members by old ones. These mechanisms are what we are calling, collectively, the mold. They did not work by persuading a member to give up his faction; they worked by bearing down on his factional energy with the weight of a shared workplace, a shared calendar, a shared set of colleagues who knew him and whom he knew, a shared language of deliberation in which he had to operate. The historic Congress — the one you can read about in, say, the careers of its twentieth-century chairmen — was not an assembly of saints. It was an assembly of partisans who had been molded, by the institution, into something a partisan is not naturally: a legislator.

A lot of things accelerated the shift from mold to platform. Some of them are famous: the Gingrich-era reforms in the 1990s that moved members back home on weekends and dissolved the local social fabric that bound them to one another; the growth of cable news and then of social media, which restructured the incentives of political communication around national-audience performance; the rise of the primary as the main electoral threat to a member, which made it more important to brand oneself nationally than to legislate well locally; the centralization of party power in the leadership offices, which hollowed out the committees. None of these caused the platform pathology on its own; together they added up to a change in what the institution was for. A member who wants to legislate, still, in the older sense can still find ways. But the institution no longer forms her to. The reward system forms her for something else.

The result is the Congress we have, which is the Congress that cannot legislate. Not because its members are worse people than their predecessors — again, we insist on this, and it matters — but because the institution that was supposed to turn partisans into legislators has lost the capacity to do so. The mold is gone. The platform remains. The clips get made. The laws do not.

The presidency presents a related but distinct pathology: the long, slow personalization of executive power through the ever-thickening apparatus of mass media. The modern president is a performer at a scale no constitutional designer imagined, and every new communications technology — radio, television, direct cable access, the social feed — has deepened the degree to which the office is experienced, by officeholder and by public alike, as the country's principal stage. The presidency, more than any other American institution, has been a platform almost since the country acquired a national media. But the platform dimension has continued to grow relative to every other dimension of the office, and the constitutional mold — the cabinet, the departments, the structured rhythms of policy-making, the restraining counsel of institutional advisors — has had an increasingly hard time competing with the glare.

The courts, finally, are undergoing their own version of the same story. The confirmation hearing was once, not so long ago, an occasion for at least the performance of deliberation; now it is, visibly, a content event. The opinion — the brief, the concurrence, the dissent — increasingly has the rhythm and the voice of a product designed to travel. One can read certain recent opinions as one reads a viral post: the quotable line, the barbed aside, the appeal to an audience that is not the bar. We do not say every such opinion is written that way; most are not. But the drift is visible, and to a profession whose mold depends on the patient, impersonal voice of judicial craft, the drift is corrosive.

***

What repairs this? Levin's answer, which we endorse and will extend in Volume III, is that the repair of institutions cannot be done from outside. You cannot force a platform back into a mold by legislation; you cannot shame it back with op-eds; you cannot audit it back. The repair, if it happens, has to happen from inside, by the people who inhabit the institution reasserting, in their own practice, the molding function. The core move — and this is Levin's most useful sentence on the subject, in our view — is a single question that every person inside an institution has to learn to ask: *given my position here, how should I behave?*

The question is plain. It is also, in the platform logic, almost impermissible. It presupposes that the position has a content — that it makes demands, imposes constraints, comes with duties, rules out certain conduct and requires other conduct, and does so independent of what the person currently occupying the role happens to want. It presupposes that the right answer to "how should I behave" is not "whatever is most expressive of my authentic self" but "whatever this role requires of me." It presupposes, in other words, that the self is something to be partly surrendered, not something to be projected; and it presupposes that the surrender is not a loss but the condition of a particular kind of personhood — the personhood of someone capable of doing the work the institution exists to do.

This is not a small ask in a culture organized around self-expression. It is, in fact, the hard ask of this book. The citizen we are trying to recover is the person who, standing inside the roles her life has handed her, is willing to ask the question and abide by the answer. She is willing to be a good juror, even when the trial is dull and the case is not the one she would have designed. She is willing to be a good legislator, which means declining some of the performance opportunities her position makes available. She is willing to be a good doctor, which means putting down the podcast. She is willing to be a good reporter, which means holding the story. She is willing to be a good pastor, which means attending the bedside. She is willing, in short, to let her institution do to her what institutions are for: shape her into someone capable of doing its work. And she is willing to extend that acceptance across the many, many institutions a full life includes — the family, the neighborhood association, the volunteer board, the condo meeting, the parish council, the school committee, the state bar — not as grudging concessions to bureaucracy but as the arenas in which the kind of person she wants to be will, if it happens at all, be made.

***

Put all of this in the terms of the book's larger argument. The platform pathology is another face of the master disease: the simultaneous hollowing of the person and the institutions that were supposed to form her. These are not two different phenomena. They are the same phenomenon seen from two sides. The atomized individual — the one who belongs to nothing, who is the raw material of the tribe — has no mold to submit to. The institution used as a platform — the one that forms no one — has no one it is forming. The first produces the second; the second produces more of the first. The young person enters the university expecting formation and finds performance; she absorbs the performance; she goes on to her firm, her committee, her paper, her pulpit, and performs there; the people who come after her absorb, in turn, the performance from her; and another institution finishes its quiet passage from mold to platform, and another generation of its inhabitants is unformed by it.

The Arendt and Hoffer thesis we took up in the chapter on mass movements extends directly into this institutional case. Mass movements, they said, recruit from atomized individuals — from the people who have been stripped of every thick association and are therefore available to be taken in by a fictional world. If institutions no longer form their own members, then institutions no longer produce thickly associated persons; they produce unformed performers who are, just like the atomized individual of the mass-movement literature, available. Available for capture. Available as audience. Available as the congregation of whatever larger tribe happens to be offering a stage. The hollowed institution does not merely fail to produce citizens; it produces raw material for the movements that feed on the absence of citizens. That is how the platform pathology of our institutional life and the cult pathology of our political life come to feed each other. The institutions that cannot form their members become stages on which mass formations perform, and the mass formations recruit, with no resistance, from the unformed members the institutions have failed to shape.

***

If institutions are no longer doing the formative work, then what is forming modern Americans? Something has to be. Character does not go un-shaped; it is shaped by whatever is around it, and in the absence of the old molds the shaping passes to whatever can fill the vacuum. One of the leading candidates, and the one the empirical literature of the last two decades has most thoroughly documented, is partisan identity itself. The next chapter turns to the polarization research, which tells us, with uncomfortable precision, what begins to form a person when the institutions that used to form her have given up the job.
