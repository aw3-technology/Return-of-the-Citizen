# Chapter 8
## The Paradox of Plenty

Consider where we are.

A person reading this sentence in the spring of 2026 is living, by almost every measure human beings have ever used to evaluate the goodness of a time and place, inside the most fortunate society in the history of our species. We do not say this to be provocative or to pick a fight with the mood of the age. We say it because it is true, and because no honest diagnosis of what has happened to the American citizen can begin anywhere else. The paradox that organizes this volume depends on taking the goodness of our moment seriously. Without that first fact the second fact, which is much darker, becomes incomprehensible.

Start with the most basic indicator a political economy can produce, which is the number of human beings alive in conditions of severe material deprivation. A generation ago roughly two billion people lived in what the World Bank classifies as extreme poverty. That number today is closer to seven hundred million, in a world population substantially larger than the one that produced the earlier figure. More than a billion human beings, by the cleanest measure we have, have crossed a line that separates a life of subsistence terror from a life in which food and shelter are not in daily question. This is, in any plausible accounting of moral history, the largest single improvement in the material conditions of humankind that has ever been recorded. It happened in our lifetimes. Most of us were not paying attention.

The next indicator is the one that most closely tracks the deep sentimental meaning of the word *progress*, which is the rate at which children die. For most of recorded history, between a quarter and a third of all human beings born did not reach their fifth birthday. This was the condition of life under the pharaohs, under the Caesars, under the medieval kings, and in the villages our great-grandparents came from. Today, globally, that figure is under four percent and falling. In the wealthy countries it is under one percent. The small coffins that filled the graveyards of every premodern community we have records of have largely disappeared. The parents in our own neighborhoods mostly do not bury their children. A century and a half ago, they often did.

Violence, the other great shortener of human lives, has been in retreat over the long arc and in the recent arc together. Violent crime in most rich countries is a fraction of what it was in the late twentieth century; homicide rates in the United States, even after the pandemic-era surge and the subsequent correction, remain well below their early-1990s peak. The great killers of the twentieth century — famine, smallpox, the wars between great powers that burned entire generations — are either extinct or, for now, in remission. A person born in 1926 was four times as likely to die in a war as a person born in 2006. The most destructive disease ever encountered by the human species was eradicated by a coordinated public health effort in our parents' lifetimes and exists today only in two secure laboratories.

Information, the raw material of every human endeavor that is not purely physical, is free. A medieval monarch with the largest private library in Christendom had access to perhaps a thousand volumes, hand-copied, chained to desks, inaccessible to the unlettered. A twelve-year-old with a phone in a trailer park in Kentucky has access, at no marginal cost, to essentially the entire documentary inheritance of the species: the Vatican archives, the Library of Congress, the arXiv preprint server, the complete works of Shakespeare and Dostoevsky, every major religious canon in every major translation, every peer-reviewed paper ever published on a topic, the recorded performances of every major musician of the last century, and the living lectures of the best professors in the world. That this fact has been so thoroughly absorbed that it no longer strikes us as astonishing is itself a datum about how we live.

We could extend the list. Life expectancy at birth has nearly doubled over a century. The proportion of women who die in childbirth, once a standard feature of married life, is in the low single digits per hundred thousand in the developed world. Hunger in the wealthy countries is no longer a biological condition but a policy failure; the food stores are full. The burden of physical labor that crushed our ancestors' bodies has been mostly offloaded onto machines. The range of vocations open to an American woman today is incomparably greater than the range open to her grandmother. The range open to an American of color is incomparably greater than the range open to a generation earlier. Most of us, most days, can reach most of the people we love by tapping a glass rectangle. We all know this. We are all, in some sense, ungrateful for it.

***

And yet.

The citizen reading this paragraph is also living inside a society that, by every measurable indicator of civic and psychological health, is unraveling in ways that cannot easily be reconciled with the picture just drawn. The two pictures are both true. The paradox is not that one of them is false. The paradox is that they describe the same country.

The suicide rate among American adolescents has risen relentlessly for two decades, after falling for most of the twentieth century. The reversal is large enough and consistent enough across demographic groups that it cannot be explained away as measurement artifact. Rates of depression and anxiety among young people, on every instrument that has tracked them, have climbed over the same period, with a particularly sharp inflection in the early 2010s. In 2023 the United States Surgeon General, Vivek Murthy, issued an advisory declaring loneliness and social disconnection a public health crisis in their own right, with mortality effects comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. The fact that it was felt necessary to use the vocabulary of public health to describe the problem of Americans not having enough other Americans in their lives is itself a kind of indicator.

Trust in institutions has cratered over the past fifty years along a slope so steep that to see it laid out in a single table is to understand why it is hard to govern this country. The Gallup organization has been asking the same questions about institutional confidence in roughly the same language for half a century, which gives us a rare longitudinal view. The proportion of Americans expressing high confidence in doctors and the medical system has fallen from around eighty percent in the early 1970s to something closer to thirty-six percent in recent readings. Confidence in organized religion has fallen from around sixty-five percent to under forty. Confidence in the public schools has fallen from around sixty percent to under thirty. Confidence in Congress has fallen from about forty-two percent to around eleven — a number that, in any normal accounting of consent to be governed, should be treated as a constitutional emergency. Newspapers, banks, the criminal justice system, big business, the presidency: all have tracked the same descent, in most cases losing half or more of the public confidence they once enjoyed. The military is the lone major exception, which is an interesting datum we will return to in Chapter Eleven.

Civic behavior has tracked civic sentiment. The voluntary associations that Alexis de Tocqueville identified in the 1830s as the distinguishing feature of the American regime — the lodges, the unions, the parent-teacher organizations, the mainline congregations, the bowling leagues, the Lions and Elks and Rotarians — have hemorrhaged members across two generations. Americans sign fewer petitions than they used to. They attend fewer meetings. They know fewer of their neighbors' names. They have dinner in other people's houses less often. Robert Putnam's *Bowling Alone*, whose empirical architecture we will work through in Chapter Ten, is the chief document of this long subsidence, and the figures in its 2020 revision are, if anything, worse than the figures in the 2000 original.

Most alarming, because it points most directly at the political future, is the erosion of democratic commitment itself. The World Values Survey has for decades asked respondents in mature democracies how essential they regard living in a democracy to be. Roberto Stefan Foa and Yascha Mounk, working with this data, found a generational collapse that ought to stop anyone who reads it. Among Americans born in the 1930s, roughly seventy percent call it essential to live in a democracy. Among Americans born in the 1980s, the figure falls to roughly thirty percent. A regime cannot indefinitely run on the consent of a population that is losing the conviction that the regime is worth having. We are in the early decades of that loss, and the question of whether it can be reversed is the question under every other question in the chapters that follow.

The two largest political coalitions in the country now describe each other, in survey after survey, not as opponents whose policies are wrong but as existential threats whose victory would end the country. Large supermajorities of Americans in each party report that they would be upset if their child married someone from the other party, a figure that was in the single digits as recently as the 1960s. A non-trivial and rising share — the exact number varies with how the question is worded, and the methodology is contested — tells pollsters that violence against the other side would, in certain circumstances, be justified. The people telling us this are, mostly, our neighbors. They live in the same country we do. They drive to work on the same roads and shop at the same stores. They were not asked, in the Gallup studies of the 1960s, whether they would countenance political violence, because the question would have seemed absurd. It no longer seems absurd.

And in the space opened up by this loss of civic confidence, movements have flourished that define themselves precisely by their rejection of the regime that produced our abundance. On the illiberal right, a politics of grievance and restoration that treats the institutions of the liberal order as enemies to be captured or destroyed. On the illiberal left, a politics of revolutionary transformation that treats the same institutions as expressions of an irredeemable oppression. In the technological subcultures, a politics of secession — whether toward the seasteads or the neoreactionary enclaves or the accelerationist imaginary — that treats democratic life itself as a failed experiment to be superseded. In the identity movements, a politics of group sorting that treats deliberation across difference as a category error, since the groups do not share a world to deliberate about. The common feature across these movements, which otherwise share almost nothing, is the belief that the existing order is beyond ordinary repair and that its transformation requires measures ordinary politics would once have ruled out.

So: the wealthiest, safest, healthiest society in human history, producing a citizenry that is anxious, isolated, furious, and drawn in large numbers to political movements whose foundational claim is that the society is irredeemable. This is the paradox. It is not a rhetorical setup. It is a real puzzle that has to be explained if the word *citizen* in this book's title is to mean anything serious. Yuval Levin opens *A Time to Build* with it, and we open Volume II with it for the same reason. A diagnosis that does not sit honestly with both halves of this picture — the genuine abundance and the genuine disintegration — is not yet a diagnosis.

***

Before we offer our own account, we want to clear away the easy answers. The reason to spend a moment on this is not that the easy answers are stupid. It is that they are tempting. Any sufficiently serious diagnosis will, at first, feel harder than one of the available slogans, and the available slogans will continue to be offered to us, on cable and in campaign speeches and at family dinners, for as long as we have to think about any of this. It is worth saying, once and plainly, why each of them is insufficient.

The first easy answer is that we just need more growth. The country is anxious because the middle class is under economic pressure, and the middle class is under pressure because the growth rate has slowed from its postwar peak; accelerate the growth rate and the anxiety will ease. We have some sympathy with the economic component of this claim — the hollowing of work and the stagnation of wages for non-credentialed Americans is a real datum and a real grievance, and Michael Sandel's diagnosis in Chapter Fourteen takes it seriously. But as an explanation of the paradox of plenty, the growth answer is disqualified by its own terms. The paradox *is* that the growth has happened. Real per-capita GDP in the United States today is roughly double what it was in 1970, a period across which institutional trust collapsed, civic participation collapsed, and the suicide rate among the young reversed course and began to climb. A variable that has been moving in the right direction for half a century cannot be the primary cause of outcomes that have been moving in the wrong direction for the same half-century. The growth answer is addressing an earlier problem, not this one.

The second easy answer is that we just need more democracy. If people feel that the regime does not represent them, the cure is more voice, more participation, more direct expression of the popular will against the machinery that has sidelined it. This is the answer of an honorable progressive tradition and of a less honorable populist one, and it has been offered in one form or another for most of the last century. The difficulty is that the regimes experiencing the crisis most acutely are, by every ordinary measure, the most democratic regimes on earth. Affective polarization is sharpest in the United States, the United Kingdom, France, and the other long-standing Western democracies. The countries turning most decisively toward illiberal strongmen are doing so through their democratic machinery, not around it. If the problem were simply a deficit of democratic voice, the cure would be visible in the places where voice is loudest, and the pattern of evidence runs the other way. The democracy answer also, notoriously, begs the question of which democracy we mean. Deliberative democracy, plebiscitary democracy, identity-based democracy, and constitutional democracy have different relationships to our current disorder, and saying "more democracy" without specifying which is not yet an answer.

The third easy answer is that we just need to go back to the 1950s, or to some similarly gauzy earlier decade in which civic life was thicker and trust was higher and the young were less anxious and families were whole. There is a selective truth in the observation that some of the indicators were better seventy years ago. There is also, in the observation's usual form, a set of occlusions so severe that the comfort it offers is unearned. The America of 1955 was an America in which Black citizens lived under a legal apartheid that most white readers of this book have no living memory of, in which women's professional lives were circumscribed in ways that would be recognized today as actionable, in which gay Americans were routinely imprisoned for existing, in which the psychiatric diagnosis of homosexuality was still seven years from being written down in the DSM and twenty-five years from being quietly deleted. The civic thickness of the period was real; it was also purchased, in part, by the exclusion of whole categories of persons from civic life. Most of the readers of this book, ourselves included, would not actually want to live there. A diagnosis that depends on a return to a world we would not endorse if we saw it clearly is not a usable diagnosis. We have to build forward.

The fourth easy answer is that we just need more science. The scientific method, applied sufficiently to the human problems before us, will yield solutions in the way it has yielded vaccines and semiconductors. We find this the least persuasive of the easy answers, though it is the one most likely to be held by our most educated readers. Science has delivered, in our lifetimes, most of what it promised. It has not failed us. The crisis in front of us is not, in the relevant sense, scientific. Whether a person regards his neighbor as a fellow citizen or as an enemy is not a question that will be resolved by additional data about his neighbor. Whether a young person experiences her life as meaningful or as absurd is not a question the randomized controlled trial can answer, though there is useful social-scientific work to be done at the edges. The crisis is a crisis of meaning, membership, and the public life that frames both, and the tools that will matter most are older than the scientific revolution — moral, philosophical, institutional, practical. We will draw on the empirical literature throughout this volume, and heavily in Volume III, because where evidence can inform practice we intend to use it. But the thought that better measurement will resolve the paradox of plenty mistakes the kind of problem we are facing.

None of these easy answers is wholly without merit. Each of them has gotten some honest thinker somewhere, and some honest reader, most of the way to a real insight. Our claim is only that, taken alone, each of them fails the test of explaining the phenomenon we have to explain — the simultaneous, coincident, apparently contradictory increase in objective flourishing and subjective disintegration that is the American condition in the 2020s. We need a bigger map.

***

The rest of this volume is that map. Over the six chapters that follow, we will work through what we take to be the six most serious diagnoses of the contemporary crisis, each associated with a major contemporary thinker or tradition, each with real empirical weight, each capable of generating its own book and in many cases having done so. We want to introduce them now, one by one, so that when we return to them at length in their own chapters the shape of the whole argument is already in view.

The first diagnosis is the one this book takes most seriously, because it is the oldest and the one that connects all the others. Call it the mass-movement diagnosis. It was first articulated, with full clarity, by Eric Hoffer and Hannah Arendt, writing independently and publishing in the same year, 1951, in the shadow of the totalitarian catastrophes of the middle twentieth century. Their insight, roughly speaking, was that totalitarian movements do not recruit primarily from the politically engaged and the associationally thick. They recruit from the atomized — from the population of modern persons who have lost, or never had, a secure place in a world of other persons. Arendt's phrase was that loneliness is "the common ground for terror." Hoffer's was that the true believer is fleeing himself as much as he is pursuing a cause, and that he is interchangeable in his fundamental psychology across whatever ideological flag he happens to be marching under. The contemporary cult literature — Robert Lifton, Margaret Singer, Steven Hassan — has extended and operationalized this insight into a body of work on how individuals get captured by totalizing ideological formations and how, with difficulty, they get out. Chapter Nine will work this material through and apply it symmetrically to the movements of the contemporary left and right, to religious fundamentalisms and secular ones, to identity crusades and to technological eschatologies. The analysis is not polemical. It is structural. The conditions of modern life produce the kind of person who is susceptible to mass formations, and that susceptibility is the common property of everyone reading this book.

The second diagnosis is the social-capital diagnosis, associated above all with Robert Putnam. Chapter Ten will walk through the empirical architecture of *Bowling Alone* and its 2020 revision. The headline finding is that Americans participate in the institutions of common life — the unions, the clubs, the churches, the leagues, the political parties, the informal sociability of neighborhoods — at a fraction of the rates their grandparents did. Putnam's causal analysis attributes roughly half of the decline to generational turnover: a long civic cohort, formed by the Great Depression and the Second World War, aging out of the population and being replaced by cohorts with much weaker associational habits. Other drivers include suburbanization, the pressures of dual-earner households, and, centrally, the displacement of communal time by electronic entertainment, first television and now the smartphone. The distinction Putnam draws between bonding capital (inward, among people like ourselves) and bridging capital (outward, across lines of difference) is critical: both are eroding, but bridging capital is eroding faster, and the political consequences are severe, because it is the ties across difference that produce moderation. The social-capital diagnosis is necessary; we will argue in Chapter Ten that it is not, by itself, sufficient.

The third diagnosis is the institutional-decay diagnosis, whose sharpest recent statement is Yuval Levin's *A Time to Build*. Levin's move, which we find genuinely illuminating, is to distinguish two different ways a person can inhabit an institution. One can inhabit it as a *mold* — as a structure that forms character by imposing the constraints and disciplines of a role. One can inhabit it as a *platform* — as a stage on which to perform a personal brand before an external audience. A doctor, a journalist, a legislator, a pastor, and a professor all used to occupy their institutional roles primarily in the first mode and increasingly occupy them in the second. When the role-bearer is using the institution to project himself rather than allowing it to form him, the institution loses the capacity to form anyone, including its members. The collapse in public trust that we sketched earlier in this chapter is, on Levin's account, the predictable downstream consequence of an elite that has, across domains, converted its institutions from molds into platforms. Chapter Eleven takes this argument seriously, examines the military as an instructive exception, and asks what the re-moralization of institutional roles would concretely look like.

The fourth diagnosis is the polarization diagnosis, and it is in some ways the most empirically robust of the six. The finding at its center, developed by Shanto Iyengar, Lilliana Mason, Ezra Klein, and the researchers at More in Common, is that Americans have become affectively polarized — they increasingly dislike, distrust, and dehumanize members of the other political party — at a rate that significantly outruns any actual divergence in policy preference. Partisan identity has in many cases swallowed other identities, such that to belong to a party is to belong to a bundle of cultural, religious, geographic, and psychological commitments that may or may not follow the party's official platform. The "perception gap" finding — that each side believes the other side holds more extreme views than it actually does, and that the most politically engaged citizens have the *least* accurate perceptions — is among the most counterintuitive and important results in the literature. It implies that the remedy cannot be more political engagement of the usual kind, because the usual kind of political engagement appears to be part of the disease. Chapter Twelve examines this body of evidence, engages the contested literature on asymmetric and "diploma-divide" polarization honestly, and draws out what it implies about the moral shape of public disagreement.

The fifth diagnosis is the public-sphere diagnosis, which in one form or another has been made by Jürgen Habermas across six decades and is now being made, in different registers, by Chris Bail, Jonathan Haidt, and a growing empirical literature on the political effects of digital media. The claim is that the communicative space democracy requires — the space in which citizens reason together toward considered judgments, in something other than a shouting match — has been fragmented past the point of adequately performing that function. The popular form of this claim emphasizes echo chambers. We will argue, following Bail, that echo chambers are actually the less damaging part of the story. The more damaging part is a distorting prism: the algorithmic amplification of extreme voices and the corresponding invisibility of moderate ones, such that each side sees a caricature of the other and, over time, a caricature of itself. Chapter Thirteen also engages what is probably the most live empirical controversy in our material, the question of whether the smartphone and social media are causally responsible for the adolescent mental-health collapse that began in the early 2010s. Jonathan Haidt's *The Anxious Generation* and the related work of Jean Twenge argue, yes, decisively. A rigorous counterliterature led by Amy Orben and Andrew Przybylski argues that the statistical effects, though real, are smaller than the popular framing implies and that alternative explanations for the inflection cannot be ruled out. We will not pretend this dispute is settled. We will say what we think is defensible and what remains contested, and we will say why the question matters for what citizens can reasonably ask of the platforms and of themselves.

The sixth diagnosis is the merit diagnosis, the argument most philosophically developed by Michael Sandel in *The Tyranny of Merit* and echoed from different directions by Sohrab Ahmari, by the late Christopher Lasch, and by a larger body of work on the hollowing of non-college work. The claim is that we have built a regime, over roughly the past two generations, that tells the economic losers their loss is deserved — that they have failed because they did not apply themselves or pick the right field, that mobility is available to anyone who earns it, and that the labor market allocates rewards in rough proportion to contribution. The first and third of these claims, in our economy, are at best half-true. The second is a cruelty when the instrument of mobility — the credentialed degree — has been made structurally more expensive and more exclusive even as the premium attached to it has grown. A regime that humiliates its losers while delivering real goods to its winners generates, more or less mechanically, the populist revolt that is now eating its creators. Chapter Fourteen develops this argument with the economic evidence and locates it inside the larger frame of this book.

Those are the six diagnoses. We want to say now, because the point is easily missed, that they are not in competition. They are different angles on the same underlying phenomenon.

***

Here is the connective argument, which we will develop across the volume and which is, in some sense, the thesis of the book restated for the diagnostic register.

The mass-movement diagnosis and the social-capital diagnosis are the same diagnosis. The lonely individual Arendt described is the individual Putnam measures. Totalizing movements flourish in exactly the soil Putnam has been watching thin for fifty years. One writer is describing the symptom, the other is measuring the ground from which it grows.

The social-capital diagnosis and the institutional-decay diagnosis are the same diagnosis. An institution ceases to form members when the members have stopped treating it as an institution and started treating it as a stage. That shift is itself a consequence of the collapse of the local, communal, associational contexts in which institutional roles used to be disciplined by the presence of people one actually knew. The platform pastor is performing for a feed because the congregation that would have checked him has dispersed. The platform legislator is performing for cable because the party organization that would have socialized him has dissolved.

The institutional-decay diagnosis and the polarization diagnosis are the same diagnosis. Partisan identity expands to swallow every other identity in direct proportion to the hollowing of the other identities. When the church, the union, the fraternal order, the trade, the town, and the family cease to provide durable frames for the self, the one remaining source of tribal meaning in public life is the political team. The politics gets hotter because it has more work to do.

The polarization diagnosis and the public-sphere diagnosis are the same diagnosis. The prism Bail describes is a machine for manufacturing affective polarization out of people who, in their actual beliefs, are much less polarized than the machine represents them as being. Affective polarization in turn generates the engagement the machine rewards. The loop is self-reinforcing, and the population inside it loses, gradually, the ability to imagine that the other side is made of ordinary persons.

The public-sphere diagnosis and the merit diagnosis are the same diagnosis. A regime that humiliates its losers while making the markers of winning conspicuously public manufactures exactly the resentments the prism then amplifies. The affective charge the algorithms find and boost is not invented by the algorithms; it is the residue of a thousand unspoken class humiliations that modern economic and cultural sorting has produced. Shut the algorithms off tomorrow and the charge would still be there, waiting for the next medium.

And every one of these diagnoses terminates, if you follow it patiently enough, back at the mass-movement diagnosis. The hollowed person, the atomized neighborhood, the platform institution, the polarized partisan, the shattered public, the humiliated laborer — each is describing one facet of a single modern condition. The condition produces lonely individuals; lonely individuals are the raw material of mass formations; mass formations offer enemies, certainty, and belonging at the price of the self; and the self, yielded to the mass, becomes the instrument through which the condition propagates itself. Arendt and Hoffer saw this in 1951. We have largely forgotten it. The point of this volume is to remember it.

The six diagnoses, in other words, are not six causes competing to be named the real one. They are six aspects of a single disease. Each of the authors we will be engaging has a clearer view of one facet than of the others, and each is worth reading for that clear view. Our contribution, such as it is, is to insist that the facets belong to one object, and that a response aimed at only one of them will fail.

***

Before we proceed we want to state, explicitly, the methodological commitments that will govern this volume. These are not abstract scruples. They are what we think we owe the reader who has agreed to follow us this far.

First, we will steelman each diagnosis in turn. We do not intend to weaken the positions we ultimately subordinate by caricaturing them. The mass-movement reading, the social-capital reading, the institutional-decay reading, the polarization reading, the public-sphere reading, and the merit reading have all been made by serious people who have earned, by long labor, the right to be taken at their strongest. We will try to present each in the strongest form we can, including the form each author would recognize, before we situate it in the larger argument.

Second, we will treat the evidence as evidence. Where the empirical literature is clean — the Gallup confidence series, the core findings on affective polarization, the basic shape of Putnam's associational decline — we will say so and cite it plainly. Where the literature is contested — the smartphone thesis and adolescent mental health, the causal weight of algorithmic ranking on political outcomes, the exact mechanisms of the perception gap — we will say so and describe the terms of the dispute. We will not cherry-pick studies. We will not retreat behind the "research shows" formulation when the research shows several things. We will flag the places where our own conclusions are contingent on contested evidence, and we will try to hold those conclusions lightly enough that a change in the evidence would change our minds.

Third, we will apply the mass-movement analysis symmetrically. The Hoffer–Arendt framework is not a weapon against one political tribe. It applies to the far left and to the far right, to religious fundamentalisms and secular ones, to the revolutionary tech utopianisms and to the identity ideologies, to the online subcultures that dream of monarchs and to those that dream of collectivization. The test of our seriousness about this framework is whether we apply it evenhandedly to the movements our readers are sympathetic to as well as to the movements they are not. If the analysis only finds cults in other people's houses, it is not actually the Arendt–Hoffer analysis. It is a partisan maneuver wearing the analysis as a costume.

Fourth, we will not name contemporary partisans for polemical effect. This is a rule we set for ourselves in the introduction and we intend to keep it. The names that recur in these pages are mostly the names of the thinkers whose arguments we engage, not the names of the politicians or commentators whose movements illustrate them. Examples are chosen for what they illuminate, not for whom they embarrass. A book that wins small rhetorical victories over named figures tends to fail at the larger task of saying something durable about the structure of the problem. We have tried, throughout, to pick the larger target.

***

A last word, before we proceed to the first diagnosis.

This is the overture. We are not, in this chapter, resolving anything. The seven chapters that follow — the six diagnostic chapters and the short transitional reflection that ends the volume — will work through the evidence and the argument in the detail each deserves. If the reader finishes this chapter with a sharper sense of the terrain and a firmer conviction that it is worth walking, we have done what the chapter is for.

We want, however, to foreshadow the argument of Volume III, because the diagnostic work we are about to undertake is organized toward it. A correct diagnosis, if the diagnosis is genuinely correct, implies a practice. And the diagnosis we are offering — that atomization and mass movement are not two problems but one, that the hollowing of the person and the rise of the mass are aspects of a single condition — implies a practice of a particular shape.

It implies that any program of repair aimed at only one half of the condition will fail. A program to liberate the individual from the cult, offered to a person who has no world to return to, does not reach him. A program to rebuild the civic world, offered to a person who has been hollowed to the point of being incapable of a free relationship with any other person, does not reach her either. The citizen we are trying to recover is not an individual *or* a member; the citizen is an individual *who belongs*, and the two conditions are not separable. The practices that will matter are the ones that restore the person and the polity at the same time, in the same acts, in the same small institutions where the two are refounded together.

That is the ground we will build on in Volume III. For now, we turn to the six aspects of the disease, beginning with the oldest and deepest of them: the reappearance, in our own time, of the pattern Hoffer and Arendt first named in 1951. That pattern goes by several names in the contemporary literature. We will call it, for reasons the next chapter will make clear, the true believer.
