# Chapter 16
## What Actually Works: The Evidence Base

We begin this volume with an advantage that Hannah Arendt and Eric Hoffer did not have. They wrote in 1951 from within the wreckage of the mass movements they were trying to understand, with almost no empirical literature to draw on and no experimental research to test their intuitions against. The claims they made — that loneliness was the common ground of terror, that mass movements recruited from the politically indifferent rather than the politically engaged, that the remedy lay somewhere in the recovery of a non-totalitarian form of belonging — were arguments from observation, from history, from the testimony of men and women who had lived through what they were describing. We owe those arguments an enormous intellectual debt. They are among the most important things written in the twentieth century. But they were hypotheses. They had to be.

Three-quarters of a century on, we can do better than hypothesize. A real empirical science of political polarization and its reduction has emerged in the last two decades, with large samples, preregistered designs, competing interventions tested head-to-head, and replication attempts that either confirm or (just as importantly) fail to confirm headline findings. The findings of this literature are sobering, surprising, and genuinely useful. They do not say that polarization can be fixed by a clever trick. They do say that some interventions work, some do not, and some that sound appealing in principle make things worse. A solutions chapter written as though this research did not exist has failed before it begins. A solutions chapter that engages with it honestly — naming what it supports and what it does not — can tell the reader something true about what we have to work with.

What we want to do in this chapter is walk the reader through the evidence as we have come to understand it. We will name specific studies, specific researchers, specific organizations, and specific findings. We will be careful about the difference between what has been shown at scale and what has been shown only in controlled settings, between attitude change and behavior change, between short-term and durable effects. We will end with what we take to be the meta-finding — the pattern that emerges when you look at the literature as a whole — and with what that meta-finding implies for the chapters to come and for the reader's own life.

\*\*\*

The single most important piece of work in this literature is the Strengthening Democracy Challenge, organized by Robb Willer at Stanford together with the Civic Health Project, and reported most fully in a paper in *Science* in 2024 with Jan Voelkel as lead author and a large team of collaborators. The design was what researchers now call a megastudy. The team issued an open call for proposed interventions aimed at reducing anti-democratic attitudes, partisan animosity, and support for political violence, and they tested twenty-five of them head-to-head on a representative sample of tens of thousands of Americans, using the same outcome measures and the same follow-up windows. This is a different thing from the usual approach, which is to report a single intervention in a single study, inevitably framed in terms that favor the intervention tested. The megastudy forces interventions to compete. It shows you not only which ones work but which ones work compared to the others.

The broad findings can be stated plainly. Several interventions produced statistically significant reductions in anti-democratic attitudes, partisan animosity, and support for political violence, and several of those effects persisted at the follow-up window two weeks later. The winners were not the interventions most people would have predicted. They were not the generic calls to moderation, not the abstract appeals to democratic values, not the simple civics lessons, and not the "expose yourself to the other side's content" interventions which, in Chris Bail's earlier work, had been shown to backfire. The interventions that did best were more specific than any of those.

First, and most importantly, *corrective information* about the other party's actual views performed well across all three outcomes. An intervention in which Democrats and Republicans were shown accurate data about what the other side actually believed — on immigration, on racial issues, on support for political violence itself — reduced animosity toward the other side and, notably, reduced one's own willingness to endorse undemocratic measures. The mechanism the researchers inferred, and which is consistent with a large body of supporting work, is that Americans have been operating on a mental picture of the other side that is substantially worse than the actual other side. When confronted with the real distribution of opinion, they recalibrate. They stop treating the opposing party as the caricature and start treating it as a somewhat-different population of fellow citizens, which, statistically, is what it is.

Second, *elite cues from within one's own party* performed surprisingly well. Here the design was clever. Republican respondents saw a prominent Republican — a senator, a former governor, a well-known commentator — endorsing democratic norms and condemning political violence. Democratic respondents saw the same kind of cue from a prominent Democrat. The intervention was not asking anyone to listen to the other side. It was asking respondents to hear their own side's elites saying that the rules of the game matter and that certain tactics are off-limits. This worked. The effects on anti-democratic attitudes were among the largest and most durable in the study. The research literature has begun to call this strategy "depolarizing within the comfort of your party," and a separate experimental program by Blair, Gottlieb, Schenk, and Woods has replicated the core finding in several settings. It turns out that one of the most powerful depolarizing interventions is simply hearing your own tribe's authorities reaffirm that the opposing tribe is still composed of fellow citizens.

Third, interventions that *humanized* out-group members through shared-identity framing also performed well. These interventions emphasized common ground — being an American, being a parent, being someone who has lost a loved one, being someone who worries about one's children's future — before engaging with partisan difference. The structure of the intervention mattered. Simple exposure to out-group members, without the shared-identity scaffolding, did not produce the same effects.

The interventions that performed poorly are as interesting as the ones that performed well, because they map onto the standard playbook of American civic reform. Generic civics information, without corrective framing, did not move the needle. Abstract appeals to democratic values — "we should all defend democracy" — did not move the needle. Unstructured exposure to the other side's political content, without facilitation or ground rules, moved the needle slightly in the wrong direction, consistent with the Bail findings we discuss in Volume II. Call-your-representative exercises, without accompanying structure, did very little. Most sobering: many of the interventions beloved of reformers — the ones funded, the ones in civic-education curricula, the ones that turn up at every foundation-sponsored conference on depolarization — did not perform as well as the three specific mechanisms we have just named.

What this tells us is that generic bipartisanship appeals do not work, and specific corrections of specific misperceptions do work. It is a result with teeth. It implies that a solutions program built around "let us all just try to get along" will fail, while a solutions program built around concrete informational and relational structures has a real chance of moving people. This is a more demanding prescription than the standard one, but it is also a more respectful one. It takes the citizen seriously as someone who will respond to evidence, not to exhortation.

The perception-gap finding deserves its own treatment, because it has turned out to be one of the most replicable and scalable results in the entire literature. The work was done largely by the organization More in Common, and the key report, "The Perception Gap," appeared in 2019 and has been updated several times since. The researchers asked Americans of both parties to estimate the percentage of the other party who held various views — about immigration, about race, about policing, about whether a woman should be able to work outside the home, about whether Americans should be proud of their country. They compared those estimates to the actual distribution of opinion on the other side. The gap between perception and reality was striking. Democrats estimated that a majority of Republicans held extreme positions that only a small minority actually endorsed. Republicans estimated the same about Democrats. On a typical question, each side's perception of the other was off by twenty to thirty percentage points.

The single most important secondary finding — and the one Volume II drew on heavily — is that the size of the perception gap correlates with political engagement. The most engaged partisans, the ones who consume the most political media, who talk about politics most often, who vote most reliably, are the ones with the *least* accurate picture of the other side. This is not what one would expect if exposure to political information were producing understanding. It is what one would expect if the dominant sources of political information — cable news, partisan websites, algorithmically sorted feeds — were functioning as distortion rather than illumination. The citizen who watches the most cable news is the citizen most likely to think that the other side is madder, more extreme, and more dangerous than the other side actually is.

The intervention that follows is almost embarrassingly simple. Show people accurate data about what the other side believes. Tell a Democrat what percentage of Republicans actually support a path to citizenship for long-settled undocumented immigrants, actually believe that racism exists as a real problem, actually reject the more extreme positions the Democrat has been told to fear. Tell a Republican what percentage of Democrats actually support secure borders, actually believe that free speech on campus is valuable, actually reject the more extreme positions the Republican has been told to fear. The effects are real and replicable. They are modest in magnitude — we should be honest about this — but they are consistent across samples, they are cheap to deploy, and they persist. A perception-gap intervention costs roughly nothing compared to the cost of producing the misperception in the first place, and it can be delivered at scale through almost any channel: a quiz, a newsletter, a classroom exercise, a social-media prompt. It is the single most promising low-cost intervention in the literature.

We dwell on this because it matters for the way we think about the larger problem. The polarization crisis is not, or is not primarily, a crisis of actual disagreement. Americans disagree about policy, of course, as they always have. The distinctive feature of the current moment is that the disagreement has been superimposed on a field of mutual caricature, in which each side believes the other to be worse than it is. Reduce the caricature and you have not solved the disagreement, but you have returned it to the scale at which disagreement among fellow citizens is tolerable. That is a real thing to do.

The most important American civic organization working in this space is Braver Angels, and the detail of its founding is worth dwelling on because it contains the conceptual innovation that has made the work effective. Braver Angels was founded in 2016 by three people: David Blankenhorn, who came out of the political left; David Lapp, who came out of the political right; and Bill Doherty, who was not a political operative at all but a professor of family therapy at the University of Minnesota with decades of clinical experience running couples therapy. The organization's methodology was drawn, explicitly and deliberately, from couples therapy rather than from the standard playbook of political advocacy. This is not a rhetorical flourish. It is the operative design choice that has made the work different from what came before it.

The reasoning was Doherty's. When a married couple comes into a therapist's office at the edge of divorce, the therapist does not try to convince them to agree on the contested issues. That would fail, because the contested issues are not the real problem. The real problem is that the couple has lost the capacity to hear each other, to feel heard, to imagine the other as a good-faith actor rather than as an adversary. What the therapist does is restore the conditions of hearing. The therapist imposes structure. One person talks while the other listens without interrupting. Then they reverse roles. Each is asked to say back what the other said, in the other's terms, before responding. Common ground is named before difference is named. The session ends at a designated time, and the couple is given a small, specific assignment for the week. Over weeks and months, the couple rebuilds the capacity to argue productively without the argument being an existential threat to the relationship. The therapist has not made them agree. The therapist has taught them how to disagree.

Doherty's insight was that affective polarization looks much more like marital conflict than it looks like ordinary policy disagreement. Two groups of Americans have been living together in a single country, arguing about contested issues, for generations without dissolving into mutual contempt. Something has broken in the last twenty years that is less about the issues than about the felt experience of the other side. Couples in failing marriages also can recite the contested issues at length, and the contested issues are also not the real problem. If the analogy holds, the techniques that work in couples therapy might work, with adjustments, on partisan divides. Braver Angels set out to test this.

The organization's core product is the Red/Blue Workshop. An equal number of self-identified conservatives and self-identified liberals gather in a room, usually a community space in a small city, with a trained facilitator. The workshop runs about three and a half hours and follows a tight structured sequence. In the opening exercise, each side writes down the stereotypes of themselves that they believe the other side holds, and then each side reads those stereotypes aloud. This disarms both sides immediately, because it turns out that each side knows perfectly well what the other side thinks of them, and the acknowledgment is received as a kind of gift. In the centerpiece exercise, which Braver Angels calls the "fishbowl," one side sits in a circle and discusses among themselves, in the hearing of the other side but without interruption from them, a set of structured questions: What are your side's core concerns? What do you wish the other side understood about you? What are the reservations you have about your own side? Then the sides switch. The other group sits in the inner circle, speaks among themselves, and is listened to in silence. The structure makes it impossible for the interaction to become a debate. Debate is the thing the participants have been doing their whole lives, and it has been the problem; the structure forbids the problem.

After the fishbowls, the workshop moves to small, mixed, one-on-one conversations built around specific prompts. The day ends with a group exercise on common ground. Nobody is asked to change his or her mind. Nobody is told that the issues they care about do not matter. Nobody is asked to meet in some bland imaginary middle. What they are asked to do is practice, for three and a half hours, the experience of hearing and being heard across the line.

The numbers are meaningful. Since 2016, Braver Angels has run roughly 5,600 events of various formats, engaged about 70,000 direct participants, and established 125 local alliances across the country. External evaluations, including studies by Francesca Gino and Julia Minson at Harvard and by independent researchers engaged by the organization itself, show measurable reductions in affective polarization among participants, with some persistence at follow-up. This is not the kind of result that generates newspaper headlines. It is the kind of result that suggests the method is doing something real.

The caveats, which we owe the reader, are substantial. Participants in Red/Blue Workshops are self-selected, and they skew older, whiter, more educated, and more civically engaged than the American population as a whole. The people in the most polarized positions, the ones who would not sit for three hours in a room with members of the opposing tribe, are the ones least likely to show up. The effects at the population scale, if such a program were somehow made universal, are unknown and would likely be smaller than the effects on the self-selecting sample. Braver Angels does not solve America's polarization problem, and it does not claim to. What it shows is that a method exists, that the method can be taught and replicated, and that when people do engage with it, something measurable changes in them. This is a piece of good news that should not be overstated and should not be ignored.

The larger conceptual contribution is the one we want to carry forward. Polarization, at the level of the individual citizen, is less like a disagreement to be resolved and more like a relational injury to be repaired. The repair follows the pattern that has been worked out over half a century in the clinical literature on conflict. Listening, acknowledgment, structure, common ground, small practical actions over time. These are old techniques in the therapeutic tradition. They are being reapplied, with adjustments, to a larger-scale form of the same underlying problem. The reapplication is, in its modest way, one of the more hopeful developments in recent American civic life.

A recent academic paper extends this logic into a formal experimental setting. Hannes Baron and colleagues published "Couples Therapy for a Divided America: Assessing the Effects of Reciprocal Group Reflection on Partisan Polarization" in *Political Behavior* in 2024. The design pairs Democratic and Republican respondents in structured exercises in which each partisan articulates his or her views and is then heard, without interruption, by the other — with specific prompts that track the couples-therapy protocol. The researchers measured affective polarization before, immediately after, and at a follow-up several weeks later. The finding: reciprocal group reflection produced statistically significant reductions in affective polarization, with effects that were still detectable at follow-up. The magnitude was modest — roughly the size of the effect produced by the corrective-information interventions in the Strengthening Democracy Challenge — but it was real, and it replicated an earlier pilot. The paper's title is deliberate: the authors are explicitly connecting their experimental protocol to the Braver Angels methodology and arguing that the couples-therapy analogy, which could easily have been taken as a rhetorical conceit, is doing actual work. The structured practice of articulating and being heard, even in a brief artificial setting with strangers, reduces the affective hostility that the research literature has identified as the central polarization problem.

Braver Angels does not exist alone. It is part of an ecosystem, larger and more sophisticated than most readers realize, and one of the things we want this chapter to do is introduce that ecosystem to readers who have never heard of it. The umbrella network is the #ListenFirst Coalition, which at last count includes roughly four hundred depolarization, bridging, and civic-renewal organizations working across the country.

Several members of the network are worth naming. More in Common, whose Perception Gap research we have already discussed, is also responsible for the "Hidden Tribes" study, an empirical taxonomy of the American political population that identified a large, exhausted, non-aligned majority whose views are consistently misrepresented by the most active wings of each party. Essential Partners, founded at a Massachusetts nonprofit in the 1990s, runs a structured-dialogue protocol derived from family-systems theory — another case of therapeutic techniques migrating into civic work. Urban Rural Action specializes in bridging the geographic divide that Bill Bishop diagnosed in *The Big Sort*, pairing participants across the county lines that American politics has turned into a near-hostile frontier. Living Room Conversations offers a simple, scalable format for small-group conversation across difference, designed to be held in people's homes without professional facilitation, using published conversation guides. StoryCorps' "One Small Step" initiative extends the organization's well-known oral-history format to pair strangers across the political divide for recorded conversations that become part of the Library of Congress archive — a beautiful piece of civic craftsmanship that treats each conversation as worthy of institutional preservation. Civity organizes cross-difference relationships in specific cities, built on the premise that the unit of repair is the particular place rather than the national conversation. BridgeUSA is the campus arm of the movement, now operating on more than a hundred college campuses, where it runs moderated discussions across political lines in the population that the polarization research suggests is most at risk of ossifying into lifelong partisan identity. And the Civic Health Project, which co-organized the Strengthening Democracy Challenge, maintains a public database of the full network and supports much of the evaluation work that lets us say anything empirical about what is working.

The existence of this ecosystem is itself a piece of good news. Books in this genre tend not to report it, partly because the organizations are not glamorous, partly because the work is not dramatic, and partly because the reporter's instinct is to cover conflict rather than the painstaking work of conflict repair. But it is a real thing. Four hundred organizations, many of them staffed by small teams on small budgets, running tens of thousands of events a year, producing measurable effects. It is not a movement yet — the constituencies do not reach across the full political spectrum in representative numbers, and the participants still skew toward the civically engaged professional class — but it is the infrastructure from which a movement could be built. We will have more to say about how to build on it in later chapters.

If we step back from the individual studies and ask what the literature as a whole is telling us, a short list of reasonably well-supported findings emerges. It is worth stating them together, because it is this convergence, not any single study, that carries the evidential weight.

First, correcting the perception gap works. Showing people accurate data about what the other side actually believes reduces affective polarization reliably, modestly, and cheaply. It is the most scalable intervention in the literature. It does not depend on a facilitator, a workshop, or a relationship. It can be built into a curriculum, a quiz, a newsletter, or a social-media prompt. If one were to prioritize a single intervention for mass deployment, this would be the one.

Second, structured small-group, face-to-face contact works. Unstructured exposure does not, and frequently makes things worse; this is the Bail finding from *Breaking the Social Media Prism* and a corroborating result from multiple smaller studies. What works is contact with ground rules — who speaks when, in what order, on what topics, under what moderation. The ground rules do the work, not the exposure as such. This is the core insight Braver Angels operationalizes and Baron and colleagues formalize.

Third, cross-partisan signals from within one's own party work. Hearing one's own tribe's elites reaffirm democratic norms and denounce political violence is among the most powerful depolarizing interventions available, more powerful than hearing the other side's elites do the same thing, which typically fails. The research phrase is "depolarizing within the comfort of your party" — an ugly phrase but a real finding. The political implication is substantial: it suggests that a great deal of the work of democratic repair has to be done inside each party's own camp, by voices the camp recognizes as its own, rather than across the partisan line.

Fourth, reciprocal group reflection modeled on couples therapy works. Articulating one's views and being heard in return, in a structured format, reduces affective polarization with durable though modest effects. The Braver Angels program and the Baron et al. experimental work converge on this.

Fifth, institution-building and the thickening of local civic life work — more slowly than any of the above, but probably more durably. This is the Putnam and Levin message, and it is where the depolarization literature meets the social-capital literature. You can run a workshop and produce a measurable effect over four weeks. You cannot, at that timescale, rebuild a congregation, a neighborhood association, a union local, a PTA, a bowling league, a volunteer fire company. But the slower work is the work that sustains the fast work. Without a civic substrate, the workshop's effects decay; with one, they compound. We will take this up in detail in Chapter 20.

Each of these five findings is independently supported. But we want to underline that the more interesting observation is their convergence. If a single method had emerged from the literature as the answer, one would reasonably suspect overfitting, publication bias, or the effect of a charismatic research program around a fashionable idea. What has actually emerged is a cluster of methods, developed by different research teams using different theoretical frameworks, that nevertheless share a set of structural features — concrete rather than abstract, specific rather than generic, relational rather than informational, facilitated rather than passive. When independent lines of research converge on a pattern of features rather than on a single favored intervention, the finding is more likely to be tracking something real about the underlying problem. That is what we think is happening here.

It would be dishonest to close out a survey of the evidence without acknowledging its limits, and we want to do that now rather than leave it for a reviewer. The literature is real, but it is young, and its reach is bounded in at least five important ways.

First, most studies measure attitudes, not behavior. An intervention that reduces a respondent's rating of the other party on a feeling thermometer is producing a real psychological effect, but it is not, or at least not directly, producing a change in voting behavior, civic participation, or willingness to live next to someone from the other party. The chain from attitude change to durable behavioral change is not well-mapped in this literature. Some of it is presumably there — attitudes constrain behavior at the margin — but the strongest version of the findings we have reported is: these interventions change what people say when asked, over some period of time, and that matters less than changing what people do. A more mature version of this research program would measure behavioral outcomes directly. We are not there yet.

Second, the problem of self-selection is everywhere in this work. Red/Blue Workshop participants are not a random sample of Americans. Survey-experiment respondents who sit through a perception-gap correction have, at minimum, consented to sit through it; the most polarized Americans, the ones whose radicalization most concerns us, are the least likely to consent. A finding that an intervention works on a cooperative sample does not entail that it will work, or be accessible, to the non-cooperative. At population scale, effect sizes would likely be smaller and the distribution of effects more uneven.

Third, effect sizes are typically modest. We are not reporting interventions that turn partisans into post-partisans. We are reporting interventions that nudge feeling thermometers down by a few points, that move endorsements of antidemocratic measures from one part of a scale to a slightly less bad part, that reduce but do not dissolve the experienced hostility toward the out-group. The most honest framing is that these interventions chip at a problem; they do not solve it. This is not a criticism of the interventions. It is a caution against the kind of solutionism that would take a modest finding and announce a cure. There is no cure.

Fourth, the research base is overwhelmingly American. Almost all of the studies we have named were conducted in the United States on American samples with American political categories. Cross-cultural generalization is limited. Some of the core findings — the perception gap, the efficacy of structured dialogue — show up in smaller studies in other political systems and appear to travel, but the specific techniques and the specific effect sizes should not be assumed to be universal. Polarization is not the same phenomenon in a multiparty parliamentary democracy as it is in a two-party winner-take-all system, and the interventions may need different designs.

Fifth, most interventions have been tested at short-to-medium time horizons. The follow-up windows in the typical study are weeks to a few months. A handful of studies have extended follow-ups to a year, and the effects tend to persist at reduced magnitude. But we do not have good evidence about what happens over years. The citizen who completes a Red/Blue Workshop in 2024 and feels measurably less hostile to the other party in 2025 may or may not feel the same way in 2035, after a decade of continued immersion in the political environment that produced the hostility in the first place. One would expect decay, but we do not know how much or how fast. Durable civic repair at the individual level is not something the literature, at present, can confirm.

These caveats are real. We want the reader to hold them. But they do not support the stronger claim that "nothing works." Many interventions produce replicable, measurable reductions in polarization. The research has given us real tools. They are modest, they are bounded, they are not magic. They are, nevertheless, ours to use.

There is an institutional question that sits on top of all of this, and we want to name it here because the rest of the volume will be working inside it. Most of the interventions we have described are aimed at individuals. A Red/Blue Workshop changes individual participants. A perception-gap correction changes individual respondents. A Baron-style reciprocal-group-reflection protocol changes individual partisans in small groups. The larger question is how interventions of this kind can be integrated into the institutions that actually shape people's lives — schools, workplaces, religious communities, municipal government, media organizations, political parties themselves.

Where this integration has happened, the evidence is promising. Some school districts have built perception-gap exercises and structured-dialogue protocols into civics curricula, and the preliminary evaluations suggest the effects compound across repeated exposure in a way that a one-off workshop cannot. BridgeUSA's campus work operates inside the institutional structure of higher education, which gives it continuity that a freestanding workshop program does not have. Certain community foundations in certain cities have built local alliances across political lines that persist across election cycles because they are institutionally rooted rather than event-based. In each case, the individual-level interventions are being used as inputs to institutional practice, rather than as substitutes for it.

Where integration has not happened, individual-level interventions remain useful but small-scale. A workshop that reaches seventy thousand Americans in a decade is a remarkable thing, and we celebrate it, but seventy thousand is one in four thousand adults. Scaling depolarization is not something a nonprofit can do alone. It requires the cooperation of the institutions that already, inescapably, structure most of American civic life. The question of how those institutions might be persuaded to take depolarization seriously — how the school board, the workplace human-resources department, the synagogue, the veterans' post, the city council might each come to see this work as part of what they do — is the institutional question we will take up in Chapter 18.

We want to close this chapter by naming what we take to be the most important meta-finding of the entire literature, the pattern that emerges once you have read enough of these studies to see what they have in common. The pattern is this. Intention beats algorithm.

Nearly everything that has been shown to reduce polarization is relationally structured, intentionally facilitated, and face-to-face or close to it. Nearly everything that has been shown to worsen polarization is passive, algorithmically structured, and mediated. You can say this about the Braver Angels workshop versus the cable-news hour; about the reciprocal-group-reflection protocol versus the algorithmically sorted feed; about the perception-gap exercise in a classroom versus the partisan meme delivered to a phone; about the deliberative poll versus the outrage notification. The former in each pair produces modest, real, durable reductions in hostility. The latter in each pair produces large, real, durable increases in hostility. The asymmetry is one of the central empirical facts about civic life in our moment.

This is not a counsel of despair about technology, and we want to be careful with it. The mediated forms of public communication are not going away, and the work of depolarization cannot be confined to small rooms forever. But the asymmetry is telling us something about the shape of a serious civic-renewal strategy. We have to privilege the forms of communication that have been shown to heal, and we have to resist the forms that have been shown to harm, and we have to do both at the level of personal practice, of institutional design, and of policy. A citizen who makes the individual-scale version of this choice — who spends more of her week in a room with other citizens and less in the feed — is doing something that, according to the evidence, matters. A school that makes the institutional version of this choice is doing something that matters more. A platform that made the structural version of this choice would be doing something that matters most, though we are not holding our breath.

There is one further observation about the personal-practice version of this that we want to carry into Chapter 20, which is the chapter on the citizen's own practice. The evaluations of programs like Braver Angels consistently turn up a finding that is almost never emphasized in the popular discussion. The workshop changes the participants. But the participants also change themselves by participating. The act of showing up — of accepting the discipline of the structure, of speaking when it is one's turn, of listening when it is someone else's turn, of spending the afternoon on this particular work instead of something else — has effects on the person doing it that are as significant as its effects on the people he engages with. Civic action is civic formation. You do not show up to a workshop because you are already a citizen; you show up because showing up is how you become one. This is, in the old republican vocabulary, what a practice is: an activity whose purpose is partly to produce a particular kind of person in those who undertake it. The research is catching up to what the old republican thinkers knew by intuition. The citizen is made by what the citizen does.

Two previews before we close. The next chapter, Chapter 17, takes up the question of critical thinking as a civic discipline. The literature on actively open-minded thinking, on the THINKING PRO curriculum, on Project Citizen, on the scandalous numbers about civic knowledge among American high school students, is a different strand of the same evidence base we have been walking through here. Depolarization and critical thinking are not the same intervention, but they share a structure: both require a practice, both resist the lure of the quick fix, both produce modest but real effects when taken seriously as civic disciplines. Chapter 19 will take up deliberative democracy in practice — the citizens' assemblies and deliberative polls that extend the logic of the small-group work we have described in this chapter to the scale of actual public-policy decision-making. These are different strands of the same evidence base, and together they form the empirical floor on which the rest of the volume's arguments stand.

What we have tried to do in this chapter is clear the ground for those arguments. The cynic's claim that nothing works is empirically wrong. The utopian's claim that the right program will fix America is empirically wrong too. Between those claims is a larger, more useful territory: a body of research that has given us real, modest, replicable tools for the work of civic repair, and a set of organizations already using those tools, and a method for doing more of what works and less of what does not. We do not have a cure for what has happened to the American citizen. We have a practice. The rest of this volume is about how to live inside it.
