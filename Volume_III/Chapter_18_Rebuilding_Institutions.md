# Chapter 18
## Rebuilding Institutions from the Inside

Somewhere in the middle of *A Time to Build*, Yuval Levin puts his whole constructive argument into a single sentence that can be read past in a hurry and which, we think, contains more of a program than most books of three hundred pages manage. The sentence is a question. The person who inhabits an institution, he says, should ask herself: *given my position here, how shall I act?*

It does not look like much on the page. Read it again. The sentence contains, in compressed form, an entire theory of moral life, a diagnosis of the pathology we have been describing, and the seed of a program for reversing it. It presupposes that the position has content — that it is not a role one writes from scratch, but a role one enters, and that the role makes demands, imposes constraints, and, if one is honest, forms the person who accepts them. It presupposes that the person asking it is not at the center of her own moral universe, but is instead located — somewhere specific, among particular others, with particular obligations she did not author — and that locating oneself honestly is the first act of serious moral attention. It presupposes, above all, that the good life is not the life one constructs against the grain of one's positions, but the life one practices within them, with such attentiveness that the positions themselves are formed by the attention.

We think this question is the foundational question of a usable virtue ethics for the modern role-bearing person. We think that asking it, across institutions, with real seriousness, by enough of the people who inhabit those institutions, is the primary mechanism by which institutional decay is reversed. And we think — this is the hardest claim of the chapter — that the question is also the most practical answer we have to one of the most persistent and most fair objections to civic-republican thought, which is that "virtue" is a concept without usable content in a modern, pluralist society, and that a book proposing civic virtue as an answer is really proposing a return to a world we cannot rebuild and should not want.

That objection has force. The reader who is skeptical of virtue talk is not a philistine. She has seen virtue invoked, in the last two decades of American public life, as a cudgel — deployed by purity movements on the left and the right to silence dissenters, to weaponize private conduct, to convert politics into an endless tribunal of personal morality. She is rightly allergic. The last thing she wants to read, at the end of a book about the collapse of the public sphere, is that the answer is for everyone to be more virtuous.

We agree with the allergy. We want to describe a different kind of virtue entirely, and precisely enough that the reader can tell what we mean and what we do not.

Virtue in the classical sense — what Aristotle meant by *arete*, what Cicero meant by *virtus*, what the medieval moralists meant when they inherited both — was never a matter of abstract rules applied to atomized persons. It was always attached to *roles*. The virtues of a good soldier were not the virtues of a good judge. The virtues of a good citizen were not the virtues of a good father. Each role had its own excellences, and the task of the person was not to become virtuous in general but to become excellent in the specific ways that the roles he actually occupied required. Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* is often read as a treatise on the virtues of the human as such. Read it alongside the *Politics* and it becomes clear that the virtues of the human are, for Aristotle, in large part the virtues of a human in a particular kind of community performing particular kinds of roles within it. Cicero, writing *De Officiis* as a letter to his son, is more explicit still. The book is organized around *officia* — duties — and the duties are the duties of the roles one occupies: of a son, of a citizen, of a friend, of a magistrate. The excellences of a life, for Cicero, are discovered by attending to the obligations of one's positions with a seriousness that eventually forms the person behind them.

This is virtue in the older and, we think, the more usable sense. It is local, specific, role-bound. It does not demand that a person become purified or exemplary in the abstract. It demands that she attend, with seriousness, to what her positions actually require of her, and that she let those requirements form her over time. The good citizen in Aristotle's sense is not a moral superhero; she is someone who has been formed by a particular kind of participation in a particular kind of political community into the dispositions that kind of community requires. The good judge is not a saint; he is someone who has let the practice of judging — its deliberative demands, its requirement of impartiality, its slow accumulation of case-wisdom — shape him into a person who can judge well. Virtue, in this register, is what happens when a person accepts her positions as formative rather than using them as stages.

The objection to civic-republican thought — that virtue is empty — assumes that modernity has eliminated roles and left only free-floating individuals in need of abstract moral codes. This is wrong. Modernity has not eliminated roles; it has multiplied them, scattered their formation, and severed many of them from the institutions that used to teach them. We have more roles now than any previous society: we are parents, children, spouses, siblings, friends, neighbors, employees, managers, citizens, voters, jurors, patients, practitioners, members of congregations, members of associations, users of platforms, participants in a dozen small publics we hardly have names for. The task of a usable virtue ethics in our moment is not to invent new virtues from scratch, nor to resurrect an ancient moral code. It is to recover the integrity of role as the vehicle of character, and to do that recovery one role at a time.

This is what Levin's question, taken seriously, asks us to do. *Given my position here, how shall I act?* The phrase "given my position" is the whole move. It tells the person that her moral life is not a general project of self-improvement but a local practice of fidelity. It tells her that the place to begin is not a theory but a location. And it tells her, by implication, that the great modern temptation — to treat every position as a platform for personal expression, every office as a stage, every role as an interruption of the real self — is not just a professional pathology. It is a moral failure of a particular kind, and there is a tradition that has known its shape for two and a half millennia.

***

What does the question look like, in practice, across the range of positions that a modern life actually contains? We want to be concrete, because the abstractions are easy and the specifics are where the chapter either earns its argument or does not. We are going to walk, slowly, through a series of institutional roles. In each case the question is the same — *given my position here, how shall I act?* — and in each case the answer has a recognizable shape. The reader will not occupy all of these roles, but she will occupy some of them, and the family resemblance between them is part of the point.

Begin with the legislator. Volume II, in its chapter on the institutional decay of Congress, described with some specificity the pathology that transforms a deliberative body into a platform. The member who treats her office as a social media presence rather than a legislative seat. The hearing conducted not as an exercise in shared inquiry but as an occasion for the harvesting of viral clips. The bill unread before the vote. The committee meeting attended by staff because the member is on television. The vote cast reflexively along party lines because the member has long since stopped forming an independent judgment about the substance. We described these patterns; we did not sneer at them; we tried to explain the incentive structure that produces them. It is not our place to moralize at the people trapped in that structure. Most of them did not set out to be what they have become.

But ask the question. *Given my position here, how shall I act?* The position is that of a legislator in a constitutional republic. The position has content. The legislator reads the bill. This sounds like a small thing; in present conditions it is a revolutionary thing. The legislator treats the committee hearing as an occasion for shared deliberation — for genuine questions whose answers she does not already know, for the airing of factual disagreements in a form that might let a reasonable listener decide between them — rather than as video content to be clipped and distributed. The legislator specializes. She learns the work of a particular policy area deeply, over years, in the way a craftsman learns a trade; she does not float above all subjects as a pundit does, opining on everything and understanding nothing. The legislator disagrees in private with her party on specific matters, and votes her considered judgment on some of them, knowing she will pay for it in the next primary and the next committee assignment and the next round of donor calls. This last is not heroism. It is the job. A legislator who has never disagreed with her party on a vote that mattered has not been performing the job; she has been performing the party. And the party, in turn, has been performing a national tribe. Somewhere in the chain, the legislative function has dropped out.

The legislator formed by her role is a different kind of person from the legislator formed by her platform. She has a subject matter. She has colleagues she works with across the aisle because the work, not the performance, is what she is trying to do. She is, in many cases, less famous than her platform-oriented colleague. She is also more useful to the republic, and she knows it, and the knowledge is part of what sustains her.

Consider the judge. A judge sits in a role whose entire content is impartial adjudication under law. The judge who asks the question finds that the role demands something specific of her prose. Her opinions explain the reasoning of the law in terms that a generalist reader could follow, and that the losing party could at least understand the logic of her loss. The opinions do not, whatever the temperature of the cultural moment, function as partisan signals, as dog-whistles to a political coalition, as content for the weekend talk shows. She recuses herself when recusal is appropriate; the question is answered by reference to the dignity of the office, not the preferences of the officeholder. She resists, patiently and without drama, the transformation of the judiciary into a content factory for partisan media. She knows that every opinion is a kind of teaching — that the republic learns, over the long arc of a judicial career, what the law is by reading the opinions its judges write — and she writes with the care appropriate to a teacher of citizens.

Both the judge formed by the practice of judging and the judge who uses the bench as a platform sit in the same chair and wear the same robe. Only one is doing the job.

Consider the professor. The professor occupies a position whose classical content is three-fold: the formation of the young, the advancement of knowledge, and the maintenance of a community of scholars across generations. The professor who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* finds that the role demands something specific of her time. She treats teaching as sacred rather than as the burdensome administrative tax on her research career. She prepares — not because she needs to review material she knows, but because teaching is an occasion for thinking afresh, and the students deserve the thought. She reads broadly outside her specialization, because a scholar who is only in command of her specialty has, in some deep sense, forgotten what the university is for. She is willing to be changed by her students — to have her framings disturbed, her assumptions tested, her certainties softened — because the encounter that leaves both parties unchanged was not really a teaching encounter. She does not perform intellectual authority on social media as if the authority were a costume, detachable from the institutional practice that actually confers it. She is formed by the community of scholars rather than using her position to build a personal brand. She understands that a professor who has become, essentially, her own media outlet has left the profession even if she has kept the job.

This is not about fame or obscurity. There are famous professors who are still, recognizably, professors; there are obscure professors who have long since become something else. The question is whether the work is formed by the practice of the role or by the logic of the platform. The answer is visible to anyone paying attention, including, usually, the professor herself.

Consider the journalist. The position of the journalist — the reporter, in particular — has content the craft has been elaborating for a century. The reporter who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* verifies. She acknowledges errors publicly and with specificity; she does not launder them into silent corrections or into the permanent amnesia of the feed. She protects her sources, at cost to herself if necessary, because the protection is what makes sources possible in the first place, and because the integrity of sourcing is the integrity of the craft. She writes against her own political sympathies when the facts demand it. This last is the discipline that distinguishes the reporter from the pamphleteer, and it is the discipline the current incentive structure of the press most relentlessly erodes. She resists the temptation to turn her Twitter following into her career ladder. She knows, or she learns, that the platform is not the profession — that a reporter who has accumulated a large social audience by performing her opinions has, whatever her official employment, ceased to be a reporter in any sense the craft has ever understood. She may be something else worth being. She is not that.

We are aware, writing these sentences in 2026, that this description of the reporter will strike many readers as antiquated. We are also aware that it describes a practice we used to have and could have again, and that the people who still attempt it in the diminished conditions of the contemporary press are among the most important civic practitioners in American life.

Consider the doctor. The physician occupies a role with one of the deepest and most carefully elaborated practice-traditions in Western civilization: the Hippocratic tradition, the long apprenticeship of medicine, the rounds, the morbidity-and-mortality conference, the consultation, the teaching hospital. The doctor who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* continues to be formed by that tradition even after her training is officially complete. She participates in rounds; she consults colleagues when her knowledge reaches its limits; she attends the M-and-M conference not as a punitive ritual but as a practice of communal learning from error. She resists the brand temptation — the clinic that becomes a lifestyle outfit, the practice that becomes a social media channel, the treatment protocol that becomes a content strategy. She treats the physician-patient relationship as a vocation rather than a transaction, which is to say she is aware that the encounter imposes obligations on her that exceed the billable hour and are not negotiable.

The doctor so formed and the doctor who has allowed her practice to become a personal brand occupy the same profession in name. In practice they are doing different things, and the patients know it.

Consider the clergyperson. The pastor, the rabbi, the imam — each occupies a role whose content has been worked out over centuries, by traditions that have reflected on the relationship between office and person more carefully than almost any other institution in the civilizations they inhabit. The clergyperson who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* serves the congregation rather than using it. She holds, with care and with humility, to the substantive claims of her tradition, rather than translating those claims into the therapeutic vernacular so that the congregation can hear them without being disturbed. She is willing to correct, gently and lovingly and persistently, because the pastoral role is not the role of an affirmer but of a shepherd, and the shepherd who never corrects has mistaken the job. She resists the temptation — acute in an age of platforms — to let the pulpit become a channel for her own politics or her own personality. The role is older than she is and is meant to outlast her.

This is not easy to hear for clergy formed in the last fifty years of American religion, which has, in many of its strands, drifted toward the therapeutic and the entrepreneurial. We are not scolding. We are naming a practice that the traditions themselves, at their best, have always known how to describe.

Consider the teacher. The K-12 teacher, in the American public school, sits in a role whose content is the formation of the young into persons capable of a shared life. The teacher who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* regards the profession as that — formation — rather than as instruction-delivery, or as content curation, or as a front in the culture wars. She resists both the left-coded and the right-coded instrumentalizations of the classroom, because the classroom is not the place where one side wins. She is formed by a community of teachers across generations, by mentors and by the more experienced colleagues down the hall, rather than by the feeds in which teachers perform teaching for other teachers who will never meet them. She knows that the integrity of her practice is the integrity of her students' civic future, and she carries the weight of that knowledge into rooms where no one is watching her carry it.

Consider the parent. The parent occupies a role whose classical content is staggering and which our culture, for understandable reasons, has spent the last generation attempting to soften. The parent who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* accepts that the role is binding — that it constrains her life, her choices, her time, her attention — and that the acceptance of the binding is itself a condition of doing the job well. She accepts that the work of parenting includes forming a child into a person capable of life in a free society: a person capable of self-government, of friendship across difference, of patience, of attention, of argument, of care. She is not primarily the child's friend, and she is not the child's brand manager. She is something older and harder: the adult the child is given so that the child can become an adult too. In our moment, this framing is countercultural. It used to be the default description of the role in every civilization we know of that endured long enough to write about it.

Consider, finally, the citizen. This is the role every reader of this book occupies, regardless of what else she does. The citizen who asks *given my position here, how shall I act?* votes informed, which is not a matter of voting more but of voting with some actual acquaintance with the choices in front of her. She serves on juries with gravity, treating the occasion not as an inconvenience to be evaded but as one of the few remaining institutions in American life that directly asks ordinary people to exercise judgment together. She attends a school board meeting, or a zoning meeting, or a county commission meeting, at least occasionally, because the small publics in which American self-government still happens at human scale do not staff themselves. She signs a petition after she has read it. She is willing to be a dissenter in her own camp when the case demands it — to say to the people who agree with her on most things that on this particular thing they are wrong — which is the specific discipline without which a free polity becomes a collection of tribes.

The citizen so formed is not a saint. She is, in a certain precise sense, a professional. She has taken the position seriously. She has allowed it to form her. That is the job.

***

It is important, having walked through this catalogue, to describe carefully what we are *not* saying. Two confusions are easy here, and we want to take a moment with each before the chapter proceeds, because the chapter will misfire badly if either is allowed to attach.

We are not preaching moralism. The person who asks "how should I act given my position" is not being told to feel guilty, to purify herself, to conduct a perpetual examination of her conscience, or to judge others in the language of the role she happens to occupy. Moralism is the tendency to convert every description of excellence into an accusation against those who fall short of it, and to convert every attention to one's own conduct into a form of performance. It is one of the recognizable diseases of the contemporary American public sphere, and it is present on both the left and the right, in different vocabularies. What we are describing is different from moralism in at least three ways. First, the demands of the role are specific and limited. The doctor is asked to be a good doctor, not a good person in the abstract; the parent is asked to parent well, not to achieve moral perfection; the citizen is asked to inhabit her civic role, not to become a theologian. Outside the specific demands of the specific positions one occupies, the person is free. Second, the attention is primarily directed at the self, not at others. The question is *how shall I act*, not *how should they act*. Third, the point of the attention is formation, not judgment. The work is slow, local, imperfect, and largely unobserved. It does not produce a satisfying affective payoff of the kind moralism thrives on. It produces, over time, a person who can do the job.

This is virtue ethics in the classical sense — attentive, local, role-bound, practiced — not in the sentimentalized contemporary sense that reduces virtue to good feelings and moral seriousness to the public expression of one's correctness. The difference is not small. The sentimentalized version produces the cultural climate we have. The classical version, recovered one role at a time, produces something else.

We are also not preaching submission. The role has content, but the person is not dissolved into it. Alasdair MacIntyre's image of the *practice*, worked out in *After Virtue*, is useful here, and we think it is worth pausing on. A practice, MacIntyre writes, is a coherent and complex shared activity with standards of excellence internal to it. To enter the practice is to accept the authority of those standards, and excellence within the practice — in medicine, in teaching, in journalism, in the law, in farming, in fishing, in any practice that has such internal standards — forms the practitioner into a certain kind of person. This is the core of our argument. But MacIntyre is clear, and so are we, that a practice can itself be criticized, reformed, extended. The virtues of the practitioner include the virtues necessary to see when the practice has drifted, and to intervene in its reform. Role-fidelity is not role-captivity. The good journalist who sees her craft being hollowed out by a new set of incentives has an obligation arising *from the craft itself* to name the hollowing and to work against it. The good doctor who sees medicine drifting toward brand-management has an obligation that arises from medicine. The good citizen who sees the civic role being redefined in ways inimical to the republic has an obligation that arises from citizenship. The role forms the person; the person, so formed, is among the people capable of reforming the role. This is how practices stay alive, and how, when they die, they are sometimes resurrected.

The two confusions — moralism and submission — are, in our experience, the main barriers to a contemporary reader actually hearing a virtue-ethical argument. We have tried to set them aside. What remains, we think, is an ethic a modern person can use.

***

But an individual, asking Levin's question with all the seriousness in the world, cannot succeed alone in an institution whose structures punish role-integrity. This has to be said, and said hard, because the rest of the chapter collapses into exhortation if it is not. The chapter is not an invitation to a personal piety. It is an invitation to a kind of practice that will only work if the practice is shared, and whose sharing depends on institutional conditions we have to be honest about.

Consider first the question of incentives. Institutions transmit signals about what behavior pays, and most Americans, most of the time, respond to those signals. A legislature whose incentive structure rewards viral moments and punishes quiet substance will, on average, produce legislators who produce viral moments; the exceptions will be exceptional, and their number will be small. A media economy that rewards outrage clicks and punishes careful corrections will, on average, produce reporters who maximize the first and minimize the second. A medical system that rewards high patient throughput and penalizes the unbillable conversation will, on average, produce physicians who have stopped having the conversation. The platform pathology Volume II described is not a mystery. It is an incentive structure operating on persons who, by and large, are neither especially good nor especially bad. Changing behavior requires changing the incentives, and changing the incentives is the slow structural work that neither side of our politics is particularly interested in, because both sides benefit, in different ways, from the incentives as they stand.

Consider second the question of formation. The virtues a role requires are not native to the persons who enter the role. They are transmitted by apprenticeship — by the relationship between the senior practitioner and the junior one, in which the norms of the practice are taught, not abstractly but through the thousand small corrections that accumulate over years of work done alongside. Medicine still has something of this, at its best, in residency. The law has it in the relationship between junior associates and senior partners in the firms that still do it properly. The academy has it in the dissertation advisor-student relationship. Journalism once had it in the editor-reporter relationship. The clergy, in most traditions, formally enshrines it. In each case, the relationship has eroded over the last generation. The senior practitioner, stretched thin and incentivized toward personal productivity, has less time for the junior. The junior, in turn, is less formed, and transmits less to those who follow her. Over two or three generations, a practice can lose the memory of its own standards without anyone noticing until the absence becomes catastrophic. Rebuilding apprenticeship is not glamorous. It is one of the most important forms of institutional repair available to us.

Consider third the question of social standing. The standing of a role depends on the role being seen, by the broader public, to do its work with integrity. When a profession ceases to do its work with integrity, its social standing erodes, and the erosion in turn degrades the incentives for entering the profession, and the degradation produces a worse cohort of entrants. The spiral is real. The doctor who treats her clinic as a personal brand does not only fail, personally, to do her job. She contributes to the public erosion of the social meaning of doctoring. The journalist who uses his reporting as a platform for his tribe's politics makes it harder for the journalist in the next cubicle over, who is still trying, to be trusted by readers. The legislator who treats the office as content teaches the public that legislating is not really what legislators do. Each individual defection is a tiny contribution to the ambient distrust, and the ambient distrust is one of the conditions under which future entrants to the profession cannot sustain the practice even if they want to. This is why the question of role-integrity is not merely a personal question. It is a civic one. A single person's fidelity to her role is a small public good. Enough single fidelities, visibly sustained over time, can reverse the spiral.

***

What, then, can be done at the structural level? We said in Chapter 15 that this volume would be honest about what is hard, and this is the chapter where that promise has to be kept. There is no policy lever that produces role-integrity in the abstract. But there are structural conditions that make role-integrity easier or harder to practice, and there are things that could be done, at various scales, to tilt those conditions.

Professional associations are among the most important and most neglected levers. The American Bar Association, the American Medical Association, the American Historical Association, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, their peers in every profession — these are, at their origin and in their best form, *molding* institutions. They exist to maintain the substantive content of the professions they represent: to write and revise codes of ethics, to set standards of training, to articulate what it means to do the work well, to discipline the members who fall conspicuously short, and to represent the profession to the broader public with some measure of authority. Over the last two decades, many have been subject to the same pathologies we have described elsewhere: political capture, internal polarization, organizational drift, the substitution of public positioning for substantive practice-maintenance. Their rehabilitation is not a small matter. An organized profession is among the most powerful structural allies role-integrity has; a disorganized or captured one is among its most effective enemies. The reader who is a member of such an association and has long since stopped going to the meetings might consider that the meetings are now worth her time in a way they were not when she last attended. Internal reform, led by the members who actually practice the professions, is among the most available structural interventions we have.

Educational institutions are the second lever. Schools from K-12 through graduate professional training exist, in their classical self-understanding, to form persons. They do not only credential, and they do not only instruct; they form. In the current political environment, the formative mission of education is contested from all sides, and under contestation it has tended, defensively, to contract. Schools justify themselves in terms of measurable outcomes — test scores, credentials, employment rates — that are easier to defend than the older, harder claim that they are forming the persons who will inhabit a free polity. We understand the defensive contraction. We think the contraction is also a disaster, because if the educational institutions will not own the formative mission, no other institution in a secular, pluralist society is well-positioned to own it instead. The rebuilding of the formative mission of education is not a project any single actor can accomplish; it requires a long, unglamorous, cross-ideological coalition willing to defend the idea that the classroom is, among other things, a place where persons are made. The coalition does not yet exist. It could.

Regulatory and accountability structures are the third lever. Many of the structural features that protect role-performance against platform-performance are older institutional forms whose function is not always well understood. Peer review in science is one. Editorial structures in journalism — the editor, the fact-checker, the standards desk, the corrections policy — are another. Committee structures in legislatures, with their specialization, their institutional memory, their apprenticeship of junior members to senior ones, are another. Each of these forms, in its different way, imposes friction between the practitioner and the public performance of her practice, and the friction is what allows the practice to be itself. Each of them has been eroded, in the institutions we have been discussing, by the logics of speed, publicity, and platform exposure. Re-engineering these structures — protecting peer review from political capture, re-funding editorial infrastructure, rebuilding committee systems that have been hollowed out by leadership-office centralization — is technical work that does not produce viral content. It is also the kind of work without which the roles we have been describing cannot be practiced well.

The fourth lever is the one we have undersold to this point in the book, and it requires its own paragraph. Religious institutions, in the United States, are among the most robust remaining *molding* institutions in American civic life. They are, at their best, exactly the kind of practice MacIntyre describes: shared activities with internal standards of excellence, sustained across generations by apprenticeship, anchored in traditions older than any individual member, organized to form persons rather than to credential or entertain them. We have not foregrounded this in earlier chapters, partly because we are not writing a theological book, and partly because we are writing for a readership that contains many members of traditions and many members of none. We think the omission was a mistake, and we want to correct it now. The preservation and renewal of religious institutions in this country is not only a theological matter. It is a civic matter. A secular polity that treats its religious institutions as hostile territory — as embarrassments to be tolerated or as adversaries to be constrained — is discarding, without quite meaning to, one of the most durable forms of character formation its members are likely ever to encounter. Friends of civic life on both the right and the left should take this seriously. The right's version of taking it seriously cannot be the capture of the state by one tradition's theology; the left's version cannot be the bureaucratic pressure against traditions whose substantive claims are out of step with the current progressive consensus. Both of those moves damage the formative institutions they are trying, respectively, to weaponize or to manage. What is needed instead is something harder and older: a civic recognition that the synagogue, the church, the mosque, the temple, the meeting-house, at their best, are among the places where the kind of person a republic needs is still being made, and that their maintenance is therefore a matter of common interest, whatever one's personal relationship to the traditions involved.

We make this argument without theological prejudice. The believer will read into it her own tradition's understanding of its mission. The reader who is not a believer at all is being asked to see something about the civic ecology of the country in which she lives, which depends in part on institutions whose members hold commitments she does not share. Pluralism is supposed to describe exactly this situation. We think it is time to mean it.

***

The structural levers are real, and some of the readers of this book will be in positions to pull them, and we hope those readers will. But the majority of readers will not be board members of professional associations, university presidents, media executives, or national religious leaders. The question for the majority is more modest, and more immediate: *what can I, in the positions I actually occupy, do?*

The answer begins with the question itself. Ask it, seriously and regularly. *Given my position here, how should I act?* Given that I am a manager, how do I treat my direct reports? Given that I am a sibling, how do I act in my family's conflicts? Given that I am an employee, what do I owe my employer beyond what is contractually required, and what do I owe my colleagues that my employer cannot reach? Given that I am a member of a congregation, how do I participate in its life in a way that serves the congregation rather than my own self-image? Given that I am a neighbor, what is the neighborhood owed from me this week, this month, this year? Given that I am a citizen, what is the small public within which I actually practice citizenship, and when am I next going to show up to it?

The questions are not dramatic. They do not lend themselves to social media posts. They are the questions that, asked and answered over a life, make a person into the kind of person a free society requires.

Treat your roles as sources of content for the moral life, not as interruptions of it. This is a specific inversion, and it is worth stating directly. The contemporary default framing — reinforced by almost every vector of contemporary culture — is that the "real self" is the self standing outside of roles, that roles are masks or obligations or constraints imposed on the authentic person by external circumstance, and that the task of the moral life is to realize the authentic self despite the roles. We think this framing is close to the reverse of true. The authentic self, in our experience, is almost always found in the careful inhabiting of the roles one actually occupies. The question "what kind of person should I be" is most often answered well not by constructing an abstract project of self-realization but by attending, seriously, to the positions one is in. The parent who attends carefully to the work of parenting discovers, over years, the kind of person she has become in doing it. The doctor who attends to medicine becomes a physician in a way no self-conception project could have produced. The citizen who shows up to the zoning meeting becomes, by the showing up, a citizen in a way no declaration of identity could have accomplished. Role-attention is not the enemy of self-realization. It is, for most people, its only reliable vehicle.

Resist the platform temptation within your own role. This is specific too. Do not use your workplace as a stage for an external audience. Do not use your family to perform authenticity online. Do not use your civic engagement as a source of content. The temptation is present in every role, because the platforms are present in every role; the discipline of not yielding to the temptation is one of the core moral disciplines of our moment. The discipline is not an ascetic rejection of all self-expression. It is the distinction between the role as a practice, which belongs to the persons and institutions it serves, and the role as a platform, which belongs to the performer and her audience. A parent who is still present to her actual children even while parenting is also documented is a different thing from a parent whose parenting is subordinated to its documentation. A professional who mentions her work on social media is a different thing from a professional whose work has been reorganized around what will play well on social media. The line between these is not always sharp, but the direction of drift is usually clear, and each of us, in each of our roles, has a sense of which side of the line we have been living on lately. The discipline is to notice and to correct.

Apprentice yourself, in some form. Find a more senior practitioner in your line of work or your calling, and learn from her. Cultivate the relationship. Ask her questions. Let yourself be corrected by her. Do not do this as a networking strategy; do it as a practice of formation, because formation is what apprenticeship is for. And, as you grow in the role, make yourself available to the juniors coming up behind you. Pass what you received. This is how practices stay alive across generations. It is also how, in the many roles where the formal apprenticeship structures have decayed, the informal ones can be rebuilt from the bottom up. We know of no more important form of voluntary civic work available to the ordinary reader of a book like this, and we know of almost no attention to it in the contemporary literature of civic renewal. It deserves more.

***

The person who is formed by her roles is the opposite of the atomized individual who is raw material for the mass movement. This is the connection, between this chapter and the book's larger thesis, that we have been building toward. It is worth stating now in its bare form.

Volume II, following Arendt and Hoffer, argued that the conditions of modern life produce a certain kind of person — hollow, unformed, unmoored from the particular associations that used to give a human life its texture — and that this person is the reservoir from which mass movements recruit. The atomized individual is not, as the nineteenth century sometimes hoped, a free person standing on his own reason. He is, in Arendt's harsh description, lonely raw material. His loneliness is itself the door through which the movement enters. He has no content of his own, so he is available to be filled by someone else's content. This is why cults, as Hoffer saw, are interchangeable at their psychological core. The content can be almost anything. The hollowness is the condition.

Role-formation is the reversal of this. A doctor formed by medicine over twenty years of practice is not a hollow vessel waiting for a tribal identity. She is too full of content to be captured. A teacher formed by teaching, a citizen formed by civic practice, a parent formed by the long work of parenting, a craftsman formed by the craft — each carries an identity, a set of commitments, a texture of practice that the movement cannot simply overwrite. The movement offers a total explanation and a total belonging; the role-formed person already has partial explanations and partial belongings, and the partial ones are more than sufficient for the needs of a human life, because they are what a human life is. Role-formation is not perfect protection against mass formation. Nothing is perfect protection. But it is real protection. The person who has been formed by the practices she inhabits is, in the specific psychological sense, much harder to capture.

This is why the recovery of role-integrity is not merely a matter of professional ethics or civic manners. It is the primary mechanism, at the personal scale, by which the pathologies of mass formation are resisted. A country of people formed by their roles is a country that does not easily cohere into a mass. A country of people who have been hollowed out of their roles — by incentives, by platforms, by the general corrosion of the institutions that form them — is a country that can be blown, like dust, in any direction the dominant mood happens to be blowing. This is why the chapter matters. It is why the question *given my position here, how shall I act?* is, in our view, a question on which more of the republic's future depends than most readers would initially expect.

***

There remains one limit on the argument we have just made, and it is the limit that launches the next chapter. Role-formation happens within institutions. Institutions — the family, the workplace, the congregation, the professional association, the school, the small public of a neighborhood or a town — can form persons one practice at a time. But the largest institution most Americans inhabit is the polity itself, and the role the polity asks us to occupy is the role of the citizen at the national scale, where there is no congregation, no office, no clinic, no classroom to do the forming. How does a person get formed into her *political* role when the institutional infrastructure of national politics is precisely the infrastructure that has broken down?

This is the question of Chapter 19. The answer, we will argue, lies in the specific practice of deliberative democracy — in the deliberative polls pioneered by James Fishkin, in the citizens' assemblies Ireland has used to settle its hardest questions, in the theoretical work of Hélène Landemore and others on open democracy, and in a small but growing body of American experiments that are attempting to create, at local and state and federal scale, the institutional conditions under which ordinary citizens can practice the specific role-discipline of political deliberation with one another. The argument of this chapter has been that roles form persons. The argument of the next will be that political deliberation is itself a role, with its own practice, its own excellences, and its own institutions, and that the institutions can be built. The question *given my position here, how shall I act?* extends, at the end, into the largest position any of us occupy. The citizen, too, is something one learns to be.
