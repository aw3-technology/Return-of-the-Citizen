# Chapter 15
## Why Most Solutions Fail

We have arrived at the volume that is hardest to write. The two volumes behind us asked what the citizen is and what has happened to him. The volume in front of us has to say what can be done. This is the volume in which books like ours almost always fail, and we think the reader deserves to know, before we start, why they fail and how we are going to try not to.

The failure is a genre failure, and it has a recognizable shape. An author spends three hundred careful pages demonstrating that the problem is deep, structural, civilizational, tangled in the weave of modern life. She convinces us. She earns our assent by the weight and specificity of her diagnosis. Then, in the last chapter, a different voice takes over. The sentences get shorter. The examples get smaller. The confidence goes up and the texture goes out. We are told to talk to our neighbors, to form a reading group, to pass a campaign finance bill, to log off of one app and log on to another, to flee to a farmhouse with the like-minded. The book ends on a note of either frantic optimism or displaced piety, and we put it down with the uncomfortable feeling that the author ran out of the thing she had plenty of two hundred pages ago — namely, the willingness to look the situation in the face.

We have put down a number of those books. We intend not to be one.

The first honest step is to name the three stock moves the genre tends to make in its closing act, and to say plainly why none of them is sufficient.

The first is the platitude. "Have a civil conversation with someone who disagrees with you." "Listen to understand rather than to reply." "Recognize the humanity of those on the other side." We want to be careful here, because each of these sentences is true, and each of them describes something a renewed citizen would in fact do. They are not wrong. They are inadequate. They are inadequate because they ask of an individual what is in fact the result of institutional conditions, and because they do not tell her how to do the thing they are asking her to do, or why, with overwhelming probability, her first few attempts will fail. Most Americans, given a weekend and a well-meaning book, cannot have a civil conversation across the partisan divide, and not because they are bad people. They cannot because they have not been formed by institutions that teach the specific disciplines such a conversation requires — the patience to let a sentence finish, the willingness to grant the opposing claim its strongest version, the habit of treating one's own certainty as itself a datum to be examined. These disciplines are not personality traits. They are practices. And practices, as every serious tradition has known, require structures — apprenticeships, role models, rituals, iteration, correction. A book that ends with "be more open-minded" has skipped over the question of where open-mindedness as a practice is supposed to come from in a society whose dominant institutions reward the opposite. The platitude is not false. It is a sermon delivered to people who have no church.

The second stock move is the policy wishlist. Every author of a book like this has, by the final chapter, a drawer full of favored reforms. Change the voting system. Break up the platforms. Ban the targeted ad. Mandate the civics curriculum. Overhaul the primary process. Pass the disclosure rule. Reinstate the fairness doctrine. Require, forbid, restructure, subsidize. We have our own drawer; every reader does. Some of these proposals are good. Some would almost certainly help. None of them is a solution the reader of the book can implement, and that is the first problem. The second problem is worse. As Volume II argued at some length, the political system that would have to adopt these reforms is the political system whose incentives produced the pathology in the first place. A legislature dominated by the logic of the platform is not going to pass a reform that lessens the pay-off to platform behavior. A media economy that depends on outrage is not going to enact rules against its own dependency. A party whose coalition is built on affective polarization is not going to legislate against it. To say "here is what Congress should do" is, under present conditions, roughly to say "here is what Congress will not do." It is an intellectually respectable exercise, but it is not a solution. It is a bet on conditions that do not obtain.

There is a further problem with the policy-wishlist ending, which is that it tends, by a strange coincidence, to recommend the exact set of reforms that would advantage the author's own side. The neutral observer notices that the author of the progressive book about polarization thinks the answer is proportional representation, the conservative book thinks the answer is subsidiarity and federalism, and the populist book thinks the answer is antitrust enforcement against the other guy's industry. Each policy set is, of course, defensible. Each, as presented, is also indistinguishable from a platform plank. The reader who is not already in the author's camp is not persuaded. The reader who is already in the author's camp does not need the book. The wishlist ending is, in practice, a way of selling a partisan agenda under a bipartisan cover, and thoughtful readers can tell. It poisons the project of civic renewal by making it look like one more weapon in the war that civic renewal is supposed to end.

The third stock move is the retreat. This is the most intellectually serious of the three, and therefore the one that requires the most care to criticize. It is associated, in its various forms, with a number of thinkers we respect: Rod Dreher's Benedict Option, the more ascetic strains of Wendell Berry, certain lines of Alasdair MacIntyre, the post-liberal longing for a return to particular communities with particular goods. The argument is that the polity is too far gone, that the work of maintaining a decent public life under present conditions is not worth doing, and that the honorable response is to withdraw — to the family, to the congregation, to the small intentional community, to the plot of land — and to keep the flame lit there while the darkness does its work outside.

There is something in this that we honor. There is an honesty in admitting that one cannot fix everything, and a dignity in tending carefully what one can tend. The person who quietly raises a good family, keeps faithful company with a congregation, does honest work, and does not look to politics for meaning is not failing at citizenship. She is, in some deep sense, practicing it. A polity of such people would be one we would be glad to live in.

But retreat cannot be the program of a polity. It can be a choice within one. A civilization that retreats collectively from its public life does not survive long enough to see whether the retreat succeeded, because the public life, abandoned, is inherited by the worst elements willing to occupy it. The monasteries that preserved a civilization during the collapse of Rome did so because a new civilization eventually absorbed them; the individual monks were not running a political program, and their survival as a tradition depended on something other than the retreat itself. A contemporary version of the Benedict Option, proposed as *the* answer, cannot be that. It implicitly relies on the continuing existence of the polity it is fleeing, and it has nothing to say to the fact that the polity it is fleeing contains almost everyone the retreater loves, including the members of his own family who will not retreat with him. As a personal vocation, retreat is sometimes wise. As a civic program, it is a surrender dressed up as a virtue.

Those are the three stock moves, and those are the three reasons each of them, on its own, is not enough. We have taken time on them because we want the reader to see that we have read the books she has read, been disappointed by them in the same ways she has, and are not going to repeat their moves. If this chapter does nothing else, we want it to set an expectation. The rest of Volume III will not close with a platitude; it will not close with a policy wishlist; it will not close with a call to head for the hills.

***

What, then, is left?

The harder, more honest answer is that there are really only two categories of proposal that take the diagnosis of Volume II seriously, and that any serious program has to include both.

The first is what we will call localist renewal. The thinkers here are Robert Putnam, Yuval Levin, the thoughtful communitarians, a generation of practitioners whose names do not appear on bestseller lists because they run Little League programs and neighborhood councils and congregational committees. Their claim, amply supported by Putnam's data and by the whole Tocquevillian tradition, is that the bedrock of a free polity is a dense fabric of small, local, non-political, face-to-face associations — families, congregations, clubs, teams, guilds, professional associations, volunteer organizations, neighborhoods with knowable sidewalks — and that where this fabric is thick, people develop the dispositions a republic needs, and where it is thin, they do not. The prescription that follows is to rebuild the fabric. Join something. Start something. Show up. Take a seat at the table of an institution that forms people. The work is slow, small, and largely invisible. It does not photograph well. It does not score partisan points. It is what the Progressive Era, Putnam argues in *The Upswing*, was in fact built upon — three or four decades of civic invention beginning in the 1870s that laid the social groundwork for every political achievement that came later. The people who started it did not live to see its fruition.

This is a real program. It is not a platitude, because it is operationalized in specific institutions that have track records, and because it attends to the conditions — not just the exhortations — under which civic virtue gets formed. It takes the diagnosis of atomization seriously, because it rebuilds, at human scale, the associations whose disappearance the diagnosis mourns. We will draw on it heavily in the chapters that follow.

But localist renewal, by itself, is not enough. This has to be said, because a certain kind of communitarian writing suggests that it is, and the suggestion will not survive contact with the structural forces arrayed against the local. A neighborhood of kind, churchgoing, civically engaged families is still a neighborhood whose teenagers spend six hours a day on platforms engineered to maximize engagement through outrage. A congregation full of decent people is still a congregation whose members consume their politics from a media economy that profits from their mutual hatred. A Little League run by thoughtful parents is still a Little League in a country whose electoral system rewards candidates who perform grievance for a national audience. The local can be rebuilt, and still be swamped, if the structural conditions that are hollowing it out continue to operate at a larger scale. Putnam is right. He is not enough.

The second category we will call structural reform. The thinkers here include a somewhat different group: the institutionalist voices on the left who have been writing about platform capitalism, antitrust, labor law, and media concentration; Sohrab Ahmari in a different register on economic unfreedom; certain of the populist critics of the merit regime; a range of democratic reformers interested in the machinery of elections and deliberation. Their claim is that the structural incentives of late-modern American life — in the platform economy, the media economy, the campaign-finance system, the labor market, the electoral system, the monopoly structure of key industries — do so much of the work of producing the pathology we have diagnosed that no amount of local rebuilding will outrun them unless the structures themselves are altered. A civic renaissance in a country whose public discourse is organized by outrage-optimizing algorithms, whose political careers are funded by concentrated donor classes, whose primaries select for maximal partisan intensity, whose workers are pressed into unfreedom by concentrated employer power, is a civic renaissance being run uphill against a waterfall.

This, too, is a real program. It is closer to the scale of the problem than localist renewal alone. It takes seriously that there are causal structures, not just cultural habits, behind the atrophy of civic life. And it opens the possibility, at least in principle, of changes large enough to alter the gradient the localist has been running against.

But structural reform, by itself, also fails, and for a reason that mirrors the failure of localism. The structural reforms that would actually help require a political coalition capable of enacting them. That coalition does not currently exist, because the citizens who would have to form it have been formed — by the very conditions the reforms would address — into partisans incapable of recognizing common interests with one another. You cannot build the coalition for the structural fix without the civic substrate that the structural fix is supposed to create the conditions for. The structural reformer and the localist are each, separately, attempting to step onto a ladder whose other end the other is holding. Neither alone can climb out.

The honest answer, therefore, is both. It is not a compromise between the two, nor a split-the-difference synthesis. It is the recognition that they are not rival programs but complementary ones, and that a book which proposes only one is leaving out half of what is required. Putnam was right. Ahmari, on the points where he is right, is also right. The localist is right that no reform will take in a country whose citizens have been hollowed out, and the structural reformer is right that no amount of local rebuilding will withstand structural forces engineered in the opposite direction. The program we are going to propose, across the remaining chapters of this volume, will attempt to hold both, not because we are trying to please both camps but because neither camp's program, on its own, will work.

***

That is the high-level commitment. It produces a set of criteria for everything that comes after it, and we want to name those criteria here so that the reader can hold us accountable to them as we go.

The first criterion is that our proposals will be evidence-based where evidence is available. The single most exciting development in the literature of civic renewal over the last decade is that there is now an actual literature — randomized trials, megastudies, program evaluations — on what interventions actually reduce affective polarization and build civic capacity. The Strengthening Democracy Challenge, the Braver Angels evaluations, the perception-gap interventions, the deliberative-polling studies, the work of the Civic Health Project and the #ListenFirst coalition — these are real bodies of empirical work with measurable findings. Where a proposal of ours has been tested, we will cite the tests, including the null results. Where a proposal has not been tested but is consistent with the theoretical literature, we will say so. Where a proposal is frankly speculative, we will say that too. We have no interest in making our own intuitions sound more validated than they are.

The second criterion is that the program will be multi-scale. We will propose things an individual reader can do tomorrow, things she can organize or advocate for in her community this year, things that would require institutional buy-in over a decade, and things that would require political coalitions over a generation. Any book that proposes only one scale of action has, by that restriction, admitted its own inadequacy. The individualist book that tells you to think more critically and read more widely is not taking seriously the scale of the structural forces pressing against the individual. The national-policy book that tells you to pass a reform is not taking seriously that the reform will not pass and, even if it did, would not form a single citizen on its own. The citizen is formed and deformed at every scale simultaneously. The program has to operate at every scale simultaneously. We will say, at each proposal, what scale it addresses and why.

The third criterion is accessibility. Most of our proposals will be things a reader can in some fashion begin. Some will be things she can advocate for. A few will be addressed to those in positions of institutional leadership or political power, because some things genuinely cannot be done at any smaller scale. But we have tried to weight the program toward the actionable. We have tried not to put the center of gravity on what the government should do, both because the reader is mostly not the government and because a book whose main recommendation is what someone else should do is not really a book for the reader.

The fourth criterion is that the program will be non-utopian. We are not going to propose a perfect polity. We are not going to claim that if the reforms we describe were all adopted, America would become a republic of virtue. The proposals we will make are directional. They attempt to describe the direction in which things would have to move to become more habitable, without pretending to know the exact destination or the precise shape of the road. A program that promises the perfect answer is selling something. A program that describes a direction one can defend and the concrete next steps on it is offering something. We mean to offer.

The fifth criterion, and the one we think is most important and most often violated by books like this, is that our program cannot be partisan-coded. If the sum of the proposals, read together, sounds like the platform of one of the two American parties, we will have written the book wrong. The test is specific. A program of civic renewal that only progressives can endorse is not a program of civic renewal; it is a progressive policy agenda with civic-renewal branding. A program that only conservatives can endorse has the same problem in the other direction. The actual work the book is trying to do — the rebuilding of a shared civic life across the people who currently cannot live together — is definitionally impossible to do from inside one camp. We expect to recommend some things that will make readers on the left uncomfortable and some that will make readers on the right uncomfortable, and this will be the sign that we are trying. It is not our job to produce a comfortable book. It is our job to produce a usable one.

There is a corollary to this criterion we should name. The symmetry that makes the book non-partisan-coded also makes it impossible for us to be ideologically satisfying to either side. A reader who wants the book to be a weapon in her own faction's war will not find it. A reader who wants the book to validate the judgment that the other side is the cult and her side is the reasonable one will not find it, because that judgment is itself one of the symptoms we have spent Volume II diagnosing. We know this will cost us some readers. We believe it is the cost of the thing being true.

***

There is one more commitment we want to state plainly before the positive program begins. It is the commitment to resist despair.

Despair is the mood of our moment. It is the substrate on which both the populist right and the revolutionary left grow. It is the structure of feeling that makes platform capitalism profitable, because the platforms feed on the conviction that something is horribly wrong and someone deserves to be punished for it. It is the mood the mass-movement diagnosis of Volume II was trying to describe: the sense that the ordinary institutions of a decent life have failed and that only something drastic, cathartic, total can restore meaning. A person in the grip of this mood believes that nothing short of revolution or restoration will do. Reform is for the naive; participation is for the complicit; the slow work of rebuilding is for people who have not understood how bad it is.

We want to say, calmly and without rhetorical flourish, that this mood is itself a symptom of the disease we have been trying to describe, and that its grip is the single most important obstacle to any program of renewal. A population convinced that nothing can be done will, by a self-fulfilling logic, do nothing. The belief that the polity is beyond repair is the condition under which it becomes beyond repair. The first political act, for any citizen who wants to be one, is the refusal of this belief.

We are not asking the reader to be naive. We are not asking her to believe that the problems we have described are minor, or that they will resolve themselves, or that an election or a policy or a cultural vibe shift will restore the public sphere to health. They will not. The diagnosis of Volume II is the diagnosis we believe. The situation is serious. What we are asking is that the reader distinguish between the seriousness of a situation and the impossibility of acting well within it. These are different things, and confusing them is exactly the confusion the conditions of our moment are engineered to produce.

Putnam's *The Upswing* — we will return to it repeatedly — makes an argument that is the empirical counterweight to our moment's despair. The turn of the twentieth century in America looked, from the inside, very much the way our moment looks to us now. Inequality was obscene; institutional trust had collapsed; the party system had degenerated into machine politics; a few men of vast wealth exercised powers incommensurate with democratic self-government; immigration, technology, and urbanization had dissolved the old local associations; the public sphere was fragmented between yellow journalists and muckrakers; politics was coarse, violent, and performed for entertainment. Thoughtful people at the time concluded that the American experiment was over. They were wrong. Over the following four decades, through a vast, uncoordinated, bottom-up civic invention — the settlement houses, the women's clubs, the Boy Scouts, the Rotary, the PTA, the unions, the trade associations, the good-government leagues, a thousand smaller things — a different America was built on top of the wreckage of the old one. By the middle of the twentieth century, the country had more bridging social capital, more civic participation, and more mutual trust than at any point before or since.

We are not saying that America will automatically climb out of its current trough the way it climbed out of that one. Historical analogies do not guarantee outcomes. We are saying that there is no law of nature that says this trough is bottomless. The people who started the climb last time did not know they were starting it. They did not have a book telling them the sociological literature predicted they would succeed. They did not have any guarantee. They did have, as far as we can tell, a certain refusal — a refusal of the idea that because the situation was bad, the work of making it better was not worth doing. That refusal is the premise of Volume III.

***

The rest of this volume is an attempt to take that premise seriously by filling it in. Six chapters remain after this one, and we want to preview them briefly so the reader can see the shape of what we are attempting.

Chapter 16 turns to the empirical literature we have mentioned. It describes what has actually been tested and shown to work to reduce affective polarization and rebuild capacity for civic reasoning. The Strengthening Democracy Challenge megastudy; the Braver Angels Red/Blue workshops and the couples-therapy methodology behind them; the perception-gap interventions from More in Common; the specific findings on structured contact, cross-partisan signaling, and reciprocal group reflection. The goal of the chapter is to give the reader an empirical floor she can stand on when the rest of the conversation around her is hortatory or cynical.

Chapter 17 takes up the question of critical thinking as a civic discipline. This is the terrain of Jonathan Baron's and Keith Stanovich's work on actively open-minded thinking, the AIR THINKING PRO curriculum, the Center for Civic Education's Project Citizen, and the broader question of what critical thinking actually is when it is taught well and why so little of what is called "critical thinking" in American schools is worthy of the name. Critical thinking in our account is not a cognitive style and not a personality trait. It is a practice, a discipline, and an obligation one owes fellow citizens.

Chapter 18 turns to the question of institutional renewal from the inside. The chapter takes up Yuval Levin's question — "given my position here, how shall I act?" — as the core of a usable virtue ethics for modern professional life, and argues that the platform pathology Volume II described can only be answered by people who refuse to use their institutions as platforms. The chapter attends to the ethics of role, the discipline of formation, and the cases in which the institution's demand on its members is at odds with what its members would prefer personally. This is the slow, structural chapter of Volume III: what it would mean for enough people, across enough institutions, to decide that the institution's work was worth more than the personal performance.

Chapter 19 turns to deliberative democracy in practice. This is the empirical literature on citizens' assemblies — James Fishkin's deliberative polling, the Irish assemblies on abortion and same-sex marriage, the French citizens' convention on climate, Hélène Landemore's broader argument for open democracy. The chapter makes the case that there is a real-world alternative to cable-news-as-a-model-of-public-discussion, that the alternative has been road-tested in serious democracies, and that it ought to be a far larger part of the American civic imagination than it currently is.

Chapter 20 turns to the citizen's own practice — the personal disciplines of a public life. Friendship across lines. Membership in a real association that meets. Service in an office small enough that the reader can plausibly occupy it. The refusal of the outrage loop. The recovery of attention. The discipline of sources. These are the things the reader can begin this week, and the chapter takes them seriously as the individual-scale component of a program whose other components operate at larger scales.

Chapter 21 is the final chapter of the book proper, and it is the one most at risk of the stock-move ending we have spent this chapter trying to disavow. We will try to earn it. The chapter attempts to describe what a polity of renewed citizens would actually look like, and to make the argument against despair one more time — not as a mood but as a choice. A country can climb out of a trough like ours. It has done so before. Whether it does so this time is a function of what its citizens, in the aggregate, decide to attempt.

***

A final note before we proceed. We want the reader to hold in mind the question of scale and time, because almost everything we are going to propose depends on getting it right.

The interventions of Chapter 16 can be adopted in a weekend. The personal practices of Chapter 20 can be begun tomorrow. The institutional renewal of Chapter 18 takes a career. The structural reforms implied throughout take decades and political coalitions that do not yet exist. The civic rebuilding that Putnam describes, in *The Upswing*, as the foundation of the Progressive Era took roughly forty years of patient, small-scale, mostly invisible work, begun by people who did not live to see the polity their work produced. It is possible — it is likely — that the work this book describes will be that kind of work. The reader who begins it may not see its completion. Her grandchildren may.

This is not a reason not to begin. It is, in some ways, the reason to begin. The civic work of a free people has always been this kind of work. It is the kind of work that is its own reward before it is its own success, because it is the work civic life was always supposed to consist of. The citizens of a republic — this has been the thesis of the whole book — are people who belong to a common thing, and belonging to a common thing means taking some responsibility for its maintenance, even when its completion is outside one's lifetime. The medieval cathedral-builders have become a cliché on this point, but the cliché is earned. They knew what they were building even though none of them would see it finished. So did the generation that launched the settlement houses and the women's clubs and the Progressive civic associations. So, we suspect, will the generation that begins the climb out of the current trough, if there is one.

We do not know, and will not pretend to know, exactly which of the interventions we are about to describe will work. We do not know the precise shape of the polity on the other side. We do not know how long the climb will take. What we believe — what the following six chapters are an attempt to make plausible — is that the direction is recoverable, that the first steps are identifiable, that the work can be begun this week by any reader willing to begin it, and that the belief that it cannot be begun is itself the principal obstacle to its being begun. We mean to refuse the two most common closings of books like ours. We will not offer the five easy steps that will fix everything, because there are no such steps, and every book that offers them is a form of soft despair masquerading as hope. And we will not offer the monastic retreat, because there is nowhere to retreat to that is not already inside the polity whose fate is the book's subject.

The honest closing is harder than either of those. It sounds like this: here are things that would actually help. Here are the limits of each. Here is what a single reader can do this week. Here is what will take thirty years. Here is how to tell the difference. And here is why beginning — even in uncertainty about whether one will see the finish — is the thing that makes the difference between a society that climbs out of its trough and one that does not.

We believe ours can climb out. We believe the climb starts not in Washington, and not in one's own head, but in the patient co-presence of both — the individual with the inner discipline to think, and the polity with the institutional substance to make thinking worth something. Volume III is about that co-presence. It is about what it would mean to begin.

Let us begin.
