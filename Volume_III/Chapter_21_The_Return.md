# Chapter 21
## The Return

Picture a small city in the Midwest on a Tuesday evening in the autumn of 2032. The city has maybe forty thousand people in it. There is a shuttered tile plant on the edge of town that will not reopen, and a hospital that will, and a main street with four restaurants, two of them good. It is the kind of place the national press visits once every four years to take the temperature of the country and then leaves.

In the basement of the public library, thirty-some people are sitting in folding chairs arranged in a rough circle. A sign on the door reads, *Citizens' Panel on the Water Utility — Second Session*. The panel was selected by lottery, stratified for age and geography and income, and paid a modest stipend for its time. Its members include a retired nurse, a middle-school teacher, a foreman at the paving company, a second-year law student home for the summer, a pastor, an engineer who worked on the original plant, a single mother who drives for a delivery company, and about two dozen others one would describe, if pressed, as ordinary. A facilitator stands at a whiteboard. An expert from the state environmental agency has just finished presenting the technical options. The panel is now arguing — politely, sometimes sharply — about which set of trade-offs it can recommend to the city council. Some are wearing the hats of sports teams. Some are wearing nothing that says anything about them. None of them is performing for a camera. There is no camera. A local reporter is in the back, taking notes by hand.

Two blocks away, in a building that was once a Moose lodge and is now something the members have not quite named, there is a meeting of a group that meets on the first and third Tuesdays. About fifty people. They came together three years ago around a specific project — tutoring at the high school — and they have added two more since, and what they call themselves among themselves is the Tuesday Group, because any more ambitious name would be a lie they were not prepared to tell. They are not all of one party. They are not all of one church. They are not all of one color. When their meeting ends at nine, most of them will go home, and a few will go across the street to a diner, and at the diner a couple of them will keep arguing — about the school board, about the tutoring curriculum, about a kid they are both worried about — with the unforced frankness of people who have been in a room with each other enough times that they do not have to put on armor first.

Across the river, in a mid-sized Pentecostal church, the men's group is setting up tables for a potluck. The potluck is not a men's event; it is a whole-parish event, and the women are coming in from the parking lot with the casseroles. The pastor's brother-in-law is a loud progressive who has made the drive from the city to argue with him about something for twenty years. He is there tonight, eating a piece of the pastor's wife's pie, arguing about exactly the thing he always argues about, and the pastor is listening, and when the pastor speaks, his brother-in-law is listening too. An outsider watching this would notice, if she watched long enough, that neither of them seems to be performing discomfort at the other's presence. They are not friends in the social-media sense, where the word means *people who agree with me*. They are something closer to the older meaning. They are in a common life together, because they are in a family together, and they have decided — without ever having decided it, because the decision is made by showing up — that the family will not be sacrificed to the politics.

None of this is a fantasy. None of it requires the abolition of the Constitution or the conversion of the nation. It does not require any particular party to be in power. It does not require the platforms to redesign themselves, though it would be helped if they did. It does not require Congress to pass any specific bill. What it requires is already within the capacity of the people in these rooms, and the people in these rooms are not saints. They are the people we know. They are, on a good day, who we are.

The question this chapter asks is: what would have to be true, in the country as a whole, for scenes like these to become ordinary? Not universal. Not uncontested. Just *ordinary* — the kind of thing one could expect to find, without seeking it, in an average county on an average Tuesday.

The book's answer, in its shortest form, is that these scenes become ordinary when citizens become ordinary. A country of renewed citizens is not a country in which the hard questions have been resolved. The hard questions are not the kind of thing that gets resolved. A country of renewed citizens is a country in which the hard questions are being worked on by persons who have the dispositions and the practices this book has been describing. The dispositions and the practices make the scenes possible. The scenes, accumulated over time, make the country possible. There is no shortcut.

We want, in the last chapter of a long book, to say what that country would look like if the work went well. Not to promise it. Not to pretend we know how to deliver it. To describe it, so that a reader who has followed us this far can see the shape of the thing she might be working on.

\*\*\*

Begin with trust. Trust does not come back the way it left. It did not collapse evenly, and it will not be rebuilt evenly. It will not be rebuilt by the national figures who sit atop the institutions the surveys ask about. Those figures are downstream. They resemble, at a lag, the population that produces them. The repair of the tops of institutions begins with the repair of the civic soil in which the institutions grow, and the soil is local.

What one would see, in the country we are describing, is that Americans have begun to trust again — but not in the order one would expect from the nightly news. They trust their school board, because they know three of its members personally and have sat through meetings run by them. They trust their doctor, because the doctor is still a person who has time to look at them. They trust the man who runs the hardware store and the woman who is the principal at the elementary and the deacon at their church and the retired machinist who has taken over the neighborhood watch and the reporter at the local paper, whom they have met at the Rotary and whose kids go to the same schools as theirs. This kind of trust, once recovered in the small places, has a way of leaking upward. The legislator who represents such a district, if she wants to keep representing it, begins to behave more like the people who elect her. Not because she has become virtuous, but because they have become watchful. Trust in the nation is a rumor until trust in the neighborhood is a fact.

Alongside trust, the civic associations thicken. The thickening is not heroic. It is not the project of any single organization or any visionary leader. It is the slow accumulation of what happens when millions of people, individually and quietly, make the decisions Chapter 20 asked them to make. They show up. They join something real. They stay in it past the first year, which is the year most voluntary efforts die. The bowling leagues do not come back; the bowling leagues are gone. But something takes their place, and the something is not an app. It is a room. It is a book club that has met for fourteen years and now numbers thirty and whose members are each other's emergency contacts. It is a volunteer fire department in an exurb that three years ago was going to be shut down and is instead newly staffed by people in their thirties. It is a neighborhood association that has acquired a vacant lot and turned it into something the neighborhood uses. The country we are describing is a country in which these rooms have multiplied to the point that an ordinary person encounters two or three of them in the course of ordinary life, the way she encounters parking lots, and in which the encountering has become the texture of her week.

Polarization falls. Not to zero. Democracies do not have zero polarization, and the ones that do are not democracies. Real disagreement is the oxygen of self-government; we argued for it in Volume I, and we mean it. What recedes is the specific dysfunction of affective polarization — the feeling that the fellow-citizen on the other side of the line is not a rival but an existential threat, not an interlocutor but a symptom of the disease eating the country. That feeling recedes because the conditions that produced it have begun to recede. The sorting is less severe, because more Americans have friends across the line, because more of them belong to rooms that cross the line, because the algorithmic amplification of outrage is being met by a thousand small countervailing practices that reintroduce the human face of the person one was being taught to hate. The perception gap narrows because people are no longer getting their picture of the other side entirely from their own side's descriptions of it. The merit humiliations that drove the anger are being addressed, imperfectly and slowly, by a culture that has started to remember that not every human life is measured in credentials.

This does not produce consensus. It produces, instead, the condition that makes disagreement tolerable: the recognition that the person one is disagreeing with is a person one could imagine sitting next to on the panel at the library, listening to the state's expert, arguing about the water utility. It is hard to regard a fellow-member of the panel as an enemy. That is not a sentimental claim. It is an observation about the behavior of human beings in rooms with other human beings, and it has been confirmed, in small and serious ways, by the research we looked at earlier.

The institutions recover, in pockets first, their molding function. A committee in a legislature remembers how a committee is supposed to work, because its chair has decided that the work matters and has insisted on the norms that make it possible. A newsroom remembers verification, because an editor has refused to let the paper chase a story that has not yet been confirmed, and the refusal has cost the paper a news cycle and won it a reputation. A church remembers its tradition, because a pastor has stopped competing with the Internet for the attention of his congregation and started offering them something the Internet cannot offer. A school remembers that it exists to form minds, because a principal has quietly stopped treating standardized-test scores as the measure of everything and has hired the kind of teachers who will not reduce to the scores. Each of these recoveries is the work of a single role-bearer, asking Levin's question — *given my position here, how shall I act?* — and answering it with something more demanding than what the surrounding culture expects.

These role-bearers do not, at first, change the aggregate statistics. You will not see, in the first decade of the work, a survey that shows the average American suddenly trusting journalism again, or Congress, or the university. You will see, if you look closely, a widening zone of places within those institutions where the molding function has been restored, and a growing number of practitioners within those zones who know about each other and are learning from each other. Eventually, if the work is sustained, the zones become the institutions, because the old institutions cannot hold against the pressures of the platform era without the recovery, and the practitioners who have done the recovery inherit by default what the platform-compromised could not keep.

Deliberative mini-publics become ordinary. This is, we think, one of the most recoverable pieces of the picture, because the tools are already invented and the early adopters already exist. Not as a replacement for the legislature; we said this in Chapter 19 and we mean it. As a supplement. On a dozen or two of the most fraught questions of national and state politics — the ones the ordinary political machinery cannot handle because the ordinary political machinery requires politicians to campaign, and campaigning on these questions punishes candor — citizens' assemblies have deliberated, produced recommendations, and been listened to. A redistricting reform, a fiscal constraint, a bioethical question, an immigration dispute. Not every recommendation has been adopted. That is not how the mechanism works. But enough of them have been seriously considered, and enough of the ones that were adopted have aged well, that the institution is no longer exotic. A city council that wants to address a hard question can now call a panel without anyone thinking the move is strange. A governor who wants to give a stalled question a fresh hearing has a tool she did not have before. The tool spreads because it works.

Education is rethought. The civic education numbers we cited in Chapter 17 were, if we are honest, scandalous. A republic that cannot tell its children what it is does not stay a republic long. In the country we are describing, the scandal has begun to lift — not by federal fiat, because federal fiat does not work for this, but because state by state and district by district, teachers and curricula have begun to do the serious work again. Children leave high school knowing the basic architecture of the government they live under. They have been taught to argue a position they disagree with before they are allowed to argue one they agree with. They have participated in something like Project Citizen, in which they picked a problem in their town and had to analyze it and propose a solution and defend the proposal in front of adults who took them seriously. By the time they vote for the first time, they have had practice.

The universities, more slowly and with more resistance, have begun to recover the sense that they exist to form minds rather than to credential them. The recovery is uneven; the credential function is worth too much money to surrender quickly. But within that uneven landscape, some institutions have begun to remember what they are for, and the faculty members who have been doing civic formation all along are no longer the eccentrics in the department; they are the ones the students seek out.

Mass-movement susceptibility declines. This is perhaps the deepest of the changes we are describing, and also the hardest to observe directly, because one observes its absence. The movements that in the early twenty-first century could recruit so ferociously from among the lonely, the humiliated, and the role-less begin to find their recruiting grounds less fertile. They still exist. There are still cults, on the left and the right, in politics and in religion and in technology; there always are, and there always will be. But the reservoir of available converts is smaller, because there are more people in the country who have a place to go on Tuesday night and someone who will notice if they do not come, and a fellow-member who will call them if they seem to be drifting, and a sense of who they are that does not require them to borrow one from the movement. Arendt's insight still holds. Loneliness is the common ground for terror. Make loneliness less common, and the ground recedes.

And political violence, correspondingly, ebbs. It has never been zero in America, and it is never zero; we do not promise what we cannot promise. But it returns to the margins, where it belongs, because the mainstream has become inhospitable to the rhetoric that licenses it. The public figure who in 2024 could gesture toward violence and be cheered is, in the country we are describing, gently and firmly disinvited from the rooms where influence accumulates. Not censored. Disinvited, which is the older and more republican sanction. The rooms have standards, and the standards are enforced by the members, and the members know their rights but also their responsibilities to each other.

\*\*\*

We have to be careful now. The picture we have been painting is one a certain kind of reader will want to read as utopian, and it is not utopian, and pretending otherwise would forfeit the trust the book has been trying to build.

A country of renewed citizens would still have inequality, and inequality would still be a serious problem. The merit regime's victims are still its victims. The man who did not go to college is not going to be retroactively given the social esteem our culture took from him. The structural reforms that might address his condition — the ones Sandel and Deneen and a growing coalition of economists have argued for, each in their own idiom — are slow, politically contested, and uncertain. Civic renewal alone does not close the wound. It does, however, make it less infected. A polity that can listen to its losers, and hold them in its common life, has the capacity to address the inequality it has. A polity that cannot hear them has no such capacity, regardless of the policy wishlists it publishes.

Climate change would still demand hard decisions that no deliberative body will find easy. The physics of the problem is not going to be softened by the rebuilding of civic life. What rebuilding does, if it does anything, is make it possible for a democratic polity to make the decisions at all — to sustain the long-horizon sacrifices the problem requires without the political system shattering under the strain. That is not a small thing. It is the precondition for any response at scale. But it is not the response.

International strategic competition does not go away. A renewed America still faces the rise of China, the pressures on its alliance system, the revisionist powers along its periphery, the proliferation of technologies that outstrip the wisdom of the institutions asked to govern them. These challenges are real. Some of them will sharpen. A citizenry that is less hostage to its own dysfunction is, we believe, a citizenry better able to respond, but *better able to respond* is not *the response is easy*. It is not easy.

The information environment would still be contested territory. The platforms are not going to redesign themselves for civic health, because civic health is not their business model. Public pressure, user migration, and in some narrow cases regulation can change the margins of what the platforms do; they will not change the basic fact that our public conversation now takes place on privately owned surfaces optimized for the wrong things. A renewed citizenry will have to keep navigating those surfaces, with the attention-discipline Chapter 20 asked of it, probably for the lifetime of any reader of this book.

Religious and cultural differences would remain, and should. The republic we are describing is a pluralist republic. Its pluralism is not decorative. Real pluralism is irreducible; it is not a problem that can be solved by the assimilation of one group to another. The evangelical Christian and the secular humanist are not going to agree about what a human being is. The conservative Muslim mother and the progressive queer-theory undergraduate are not going to share a worldview. The point of the civic work is not to erase these differences but to make them livable — to ensure that those who hold them can still be in common life together, can still work the water utility panel together, can still be part of the Tuesday Group together, can still eat the pie at the potluck together. The livability is the achievement. Anything more than that is a utopia we are not selling.

And there would still be bad elections, bad leaders, bad mistakes. Self-government is self-government; a people capable of governing itself is also a people capable of getting it wrong. We will still occasionally elect charlatans. We will still occasionally pass bad laws. We will still wake up, some years, to decisions we will regret. The question a republic faces is not whether it will ever be wrong. The question is whether it can learn, correct, and go on. The country we are describing has that capacity. It is not a country without problems. It is a country with *capability to address* its problems, which is the only definition of a healthy polity that has ever held up.

\*\*\*

It is at this point that a certain reader will want to close the book and, not unkindly, shake her head. She has followed the argument. She agrees with more of it than she expected to. But she does not believe it can happen. The country is too big. The drift is too far along. The interests arrayed against renewal are too powerful. The generation that would have to lead the work is too distracted, too demoralized, too online. She feels the despair that has been the defining intellectual mood of her decade, and she feels it with some dignity, because it is not an unserious mood.

We want to argue with her, gently and at length.

The first thing to say is that her despair is historically implausible. Robert Putnam, in *The Upswing*, did the unglamorous work of showing that the country has been through something strikingly like this before. In the last third of the nineteenth century, the United States was in what Putnam calls the "I-we-I curve's" first trough: polarized, economically brutal, civically hollowed out, politically corrupt, and saturated with a public mood of exhaustion not unlike ours. By most of the indicators Putnam tracked — economic equality, political cooperation, social trust, civic engagement, cultural solidarity — the country bottomed out in the 1890s. And then, over the following forty or fifty years, it climbed. The climb was not led, primarily, by national politicians. It was led by a mass of small acts: local reform movements, settlement houses, immigrant self-help organizations, the expansion of the public high school, the founding of thousands of civic associations, religious awakenings both Protestant and Catholic, the rise of investigative journalism, the slow construction of what we now call the Progressive Era. The Americans alive in 1890 had every reason for despair. The ones who resisted it became the architects of what followed. This is not a guarantee; we do not promise a repeat. It is a rebuke to the feeling that our trough is unprecedented or that climbing out is unimaginable. It has been done. It was done by people who looked at a wreckage as dispiriting as ours.

The second thing to say is that despair is morally corrosive. A person who has concluded that nothing can be done does nothing. A population that has concluded it becomes, through its own inaction, the author of its decline. This is the worst feature of the attitude: it is self-fulfilling. It is not merely a misreading of the situation; it is a contribution to the situation. The cynic's clear-eyed realism is a failure of practical imagination pretending to be insight. We say this not to scold the cynic — we have both spent our own hours in that chair — but to name what the posture actually costs.

The third thing to say is that despair is empirically wrong. The diagnoses of Volume II are real; we stand by them. But they are not static. Affective polarization is high and rising, and also the interventions of Chapter 16 reduce it, repeatedly and in well-designed studies, by nontrivial amounts. Civic capital is low, and also the practices of Chapter 20 rebuild it, one room and one year at a time. The institutions are decayed, and also the work of Chapter 18 renews them, wherever someone is willing to do it. Each diagnosis was accompanied, if the reader was paying attention, by a corresponding lever. The description of what is wrong is not a prophecy. It is, as we argued in Volume I, already a step toward a description of what might be right.

The fourth thing to say is that despair is contrary to the evidence of Americans' own stated values. Both the left-coded surveys and the right-coded ones — the Hidden Tribes data, the More in Common work, the Pew longitudinals, the local surveys cities commission when they want to know what their residents actually think — keep finding the same substrate. Most Americans, across partisan lines, want something different from what they currently have. They are ashamed of the tone of our public life. They are exhausted by the performance. They remember civic life, even when they have not recently practiced it. They say, when asked plainly, that they would welcome a return. The tribes shouting on the networks do not speak for them. The algorithms amplifying the tribes do not speak for them. The polls, taken seriously, show a country with much more common ground than its public life suggests, waiting for something to give it permission to show itself again.

A book like this cannot be the thing that gives it permission. A book cannot do that. Only the readers, acting together without a center, can. But a book can name the permission that is there to be taken.

\*\*\*

The work has, in fact, already begun. We want to name some of it, without overselling any of it, because a reader who has come this far deserves specifics rather than more argument.

The depolarization ecosystem we described in Chapter 16 is real and growing. Braver Angels has chapters in every state; the #ListenFirst coalition counts hundreds of affiliated organizations; the Strengthening Democracy Challenge has produced a library of evidence-backed interventions that are being picked up by school districts and workplaces and universities and civic clubs around the country. None of these, alone, is the answer. Together they are a serious infrastructure that did not exist ten years ago.

A set of cities is experimenting, in public, with participatory budgeting and deliberative panels — in Arizona, in Oregon, in the Midwest, in New England. The experiments are uneven. Some have produced more theater than substance. Others have produced genuine deliberation and genuine recommendations that have been implemented. The practice, even where it has failed, has taught the practitioners something. The practitioners are writing it up. The next city gets the benefit.

A handful of universities are recovering civic formation as a core mission. We are not going to name them here, because naming produces a backlash that hurts the naming. But one can find them if one looks; they know each other; they are hiring each other's graduates. They are producing a generation of students who can argue in the older sense.

Certain religious communities — we mean specific congregations, not denominations — are doing serious bridging work. The Pentecostal church with the potluck in our opening scene is not invented; there are hundreds like it. There are synagogues, mosques, parishes, and meetings doing the same work. Their members do not advertise the work, because advertising would break the work. It is happening anyway.

Certain professional associations have quietly reclaimed their formative function. A state bar that has started taking its disciplinary role seriously again. A medical society that has rewritten its ethics training in a form that bites. A faculty senate that has begun to behave, after a long silence, as though tenure entails obligations as well as protections. One finds these renewals by walking into the institutions and asking. They are not in the headlines.

And the generation of young Americans who have grown up inside the social-media distortion is, in some of its segments, also the most thoughtful critic of it the country has produced. The twenty-five-year-olds who have seen what the platforms did to them know, with a clarity their parents cannot quite match, that the arrangement is not normal and not acceptable and not permanent. Some of them are leaving the platforms. Some of them are building alternatives. Some of them are in divinity schools and teaching colleges and city halls, starting on the thirty-year work that the Progressive Era generation began in the 1890s. We know some of them. We trust them with what happens next.

\*\*\*

Which brings us to what the book has to ask of the reader.

The argument we have made across three volumes is that the citizen can return. We have meant that seriously; we are not rhetoricians. But the return is not something that will happen *to* Americans. It is something Americans will have to *do*. There is no other mechanism. A nation of citizens is not produced by any institution, any party, any leader, any book. It is produced by the citizens, one at a time, deciding to be citizens. If enough readers of this book take the practices of Chapter 20 seriously — the friendship across lines, the membership in a real association, the service in a local office, the refusal of the outrage loop, the recovery of attention — the work will be done. If they do not, it will not. This is the whole of the argument for taking those practices seriously. They are not private virtues with public side effects. They are the civic mechanism. Without them, no institutional reform is durable. With them, durable reform is eventually achievable.

A word about the time horizon. The reader should not expect to see a country of renewed citizens in five years, or in ten. The Progressive Era took decades to build, and the people who built it mostly died before it was complete. This is the condition of most work that matters. A citizen who plants a tree she will not sit under is closer to the republican ideal than a consumer who expects every order to arrive tomorrow. If the reader is looking for a project that will reward her in the short run, she will have to look somewhere else. If she is looking for the kind of project that has historically been the only one worth devoting a life to, she has already found it. It has been here the whole time. We have only been describing it.

We do not know, honestly, whether the work will succeed. We do not know whether the trough will be climbed out of in thirty years or seventy or at all. Nobody in 1890 knew, either. The people who did the work did it without knowing. That is what it means to be a republican in the older sense: to act on behalf of a common good whose final fate one cannot guarantee, because the common good is what one is for.

\*\*\*

A last thing, more personal.

The book has argued — steelmanning in every direction we could reach, engaging every camp we could find honestly, trying to avoid the partisan claims that disfigure most writing in this genre — that the citizen can and must return. The argument, at its end, does not depend on the reader's agreement with every step. A reader who agrees with three-quarters of what we have said, or half, can still do the thing we are asking. A reader who has finished the book still suspicious of half of it, but willing to try some of the practices and see, is exactly the reader we hoped for. What the argument depends on, at its end, is the reader's willingness to act as if some version of it might be true.

The cynic who turns out to have been right about the situation has, in being right, cost herself nothing; she has also added nothing to the side of the ledger that might have saved it. The hopeful person who turns out to have been right has, in being right, made the future. This is not a gamble between equal options. It is the oldest decision human beings have ever faced in political life, and the ones who have chosen poorly have not been remembered well. The citizen who acts, even in an age of collective insanity, even in the teeth of a diagnosis that says she cannot, is not doing something quixotic. She is doing the only thing that has ever worked.

We do not want to end with a trumpet. The book has not been interested in trumpets. The argument we have wanted to make is more patient than that, and more affectionate, and more ordinary.

If you have read this far, you are the reader we wrote for. You are the person we had in mind when we started arguing about this years ago. You are, we suspect, already doing some of what we have been describing, because you would not have read a book like this otherwise. The thing we are asking you to do is not different in kind from what you are already doing. It is an invitation to do a little more of it, more deliberately, in the company of other people who are doing the same. That is all. That is the whole of it.

The citizen is not a nostalgic figure. The citizen is a figure of the future, or of nothing at all.

Let us begin.
