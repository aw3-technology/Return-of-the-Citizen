# Chapter 20
## The Citizen's Practice

We have spent most of this book at altitudes the ordinary reader cannot directly affect. We have written about traditions and their quarrels, about institutions and their decay, about platforms and polarizations and merit regimes, about deliberative forums and civic curricula and the internal life of professions. These are the proper concerns of a serious book about the citizen. They are also, for most readers, well beyond the scope of what any one person can do on a Tuesday afternoon. A book that ended there would be a book that failed the reader at the moment it was most needed.

So we want to speak to you directly now. Not to the polity. Not to the profession. Not to the movement. To you, reading this, in the life you already have. The argument of this book does not survive — it does not become anything — unless it lands in practices that can be begun without permission, in circumstances that will not change to accommodate them, by someone who has no special access to levers of power and no unusual store of free time. That is almost every reader of every serious book, including this one. It was us, for most of the years we were writing. If there is a move from the diagnostic chapters of Volume II and the institutional chapters of Volume III down to the single life, this is where it happens.

What follows is a small set of practices — we have held ourselves to ten, deliberately, because fifteen would be a syllabus and five would be a slogan. They are not original to us. Most of them have been named, in various idioms, for a very long time. The novelty, if there is one, is in the argument that taken together they constitute something like the disciplines of a public life in our moment, and that a reader who takes them seriously will, over the span of a year or two, become a measurably different kind of citizen than she was when she started. That last claim is not therapeutic. It is empirical. The evidence for each of these practices, drawn from the chapters that preceded this one, is already on the table.

Before we name them, a word about the frame. We want you to understand these as *practices* in the classical sense, not as resolutions and not as lifestyle choices. A resolution is what you announce on a certain day of the year. A lifestyle is what you signal to a peer group that is also signaling back. A practice is something different and older. A practice is a repeated activity with standards of excellence internal to it, into which one enters as an apprentice and in which one matures slowly, through attention, repetition, failure, and the correction of others. A practice changes who you are over time, whether you intended to be changed or not. Learning to play an instrument is a practice. Raising a child is a practice. Prayer, where it is real, is a practice. Citizenship, understood the way we have argued it should be understood, is a practice, or it is nothing. It is not a feeling about the country. It is not a voting history. It is a way of living a life in public among equals. That way of living has to be learned and maintained, and it is learned and maintained by doing specific things on specific days, not by agreeing with specific propositions in one's head.

So. Ten practices. Begin this week.

***

The first, and by a considerable distance the most important, is *membership*. Join something real. We mean this concretely. Not a mailing list. Not a follower count. Not a digital community. Not a podcast feed that refers to its listeners as a family. A real organization, meeting in physical space, made of other specific human beings who will come to know your name and will notice when you are not there. The range of acceptable answers is enormous. A church, a synagogue, a mosque, a temple. A union, if you are eligible for one, or the professional association of your field if you are not. A civic club — Rotary, Kiwanis, Lions, Elks, Masons, whatever the local version is. A volunteer fire department. A community choir or a community orchestra or a community theater. A veterans' organization. A neighborhood association or a block club. A political party committee at the precinct or ward level. A parent-teacher association. A sports league, adult or youth, and coaching counts. A book group that actually meets rather than one that exists on an app. An amateur astronomy club. A garden cooperative. A hiking group. A dojo. A contra dance. A knitting circle. The form is almost infinitely negotiable. The substance is not.

What matters is that the membership be costly in the small but real ways that matter. You have to show up at a time you did not set, among people you did not choose, and do something that has standards you did not write. You have to be missed when you are absent. You have to be counted on. You have to occasionally be disappointed by the others in the room and have them occasionally be disappointed by you, and you have to keep coming back anyway. That is what "real" means here. A membership without the possibility of mutual disappointment is not a membership; it is a consumption pattern.

The evidence for this practice is one of the most robust findings in the social sciences of the last half-century. We walked through it in Chapter 10. Putnam's thirty-year research program, and the work that has elaborated on it, converges on a single conclusion: membership of this face-to-face kind is correlated with nearly every measure of civic health one can define — trust, tolerance, reciprocity, volunteerism, turnout, reported happiness, even physical longevity. The correlations are not trivial in size, and they appear to survive the usual controls for income, education, and selection. It is not that healthy and trusting people happen to join things; it is that joining things, over time, seems to produce healthier and more trusting people. The depolarization literature we summarized in Chapter 16 tells a version of the same story from the other direction. Face-to-face interaction with people who are not exactly like oneself, under conditions of shared purpose, reliably softens the most corrosive forms of political hostility. It is the rare finding in this field that crosses every methodological style and replicates even when researchers try hard to break it.

We want to emphasize something that is slightly counterintuitive. The civic effect of membership does not depend much on the civic mission of the organization. A church committee that argues for three hours about the color of the carpet does almost as much civic work as a committee solving a homelessness crisis, because the work being done in either case, at the relevant level, is the work of learning to sit with other human beings whose preferences diverge from yours and to arrive at a common answer anyway. Putnam's finding that bowling leagues matter is a joke that is not a joke. A bowling league that meets every Tuesday night for ten years has probably done more work of depolarization than most organizations with "civic" in their names, because its members have had to be kind to one another, across whatever lines of division exist between them, in the small and specific ways that kindness actually operates. You do not need to find the right organization. You need to find one and go to it.

***

The second practice is harder, and closer to the bone, because it asks you to make a friend. Specifically, to cultivate at least one deep friendship with someone whose politics differ substantially from your own. Not in the thin sense — not "my uncle votes the other way at Thanksgiving" — but in the thick sense of a friend you talk to about hard things, whom you would call at two in the morning, whom you trust with the parts of your life that are not for public consumption. One such friend, across the line. If you have several, excellent. If you have none, and you are being honest with yourself, this is the most important single piece of work you can do.

We said in Chapter 12 that affective polarization — the escalating contempt of American partisans for one another — is now the dominant finding of American political science, and that it has grown worse in the period during which the objective policy distance between ordinary voters of the two parties has, in many domains, grown smaller. It is not mostly about positions. It is about identities, and the social sorting that has packed more and more of those identities into the partisan box. The perception gap, documented by More in Common and others, shows both sides holding dramatic and empirically wrong beliefs about how extreme the other side is. The political scientists cannot make this finding go away, and neither can the psychologists.

There is one thing that reliably punctures this inflated contempt. A friend. A concrete beloved person whose views on the controverted questions of the day diverge substantially from one's own and whom one nonetheless loves. The contempt does not survive this presence. It cannot. The imagined enemy on the other side of the algorithm has the face of no one; the friend has a face. You know what she has been through. You know the chapters of her life that led her to the views she holds. You know that she is as obviously good a person as you are, and that nothing in her politics is explained by her being stupid or evil, the two hypotheses the algorithm would like you to hold. This knowledge is not a cognitive performance. It is an existential fact, and it radiates outward. Your estimate of the other side, in the aggregate, is revised by the specific person. You do not stop disagreeing. You stop disagreeing in the style that leads to a country that cannot be governed.

We want to be clear about what is and is not being asked here. You are not being asked to agree with your friend. You are not being asked to be persuaded by her. You are not being asked to pretend, in the name of civility, that the things you think are true are not. You are being asked to love her, specifically, and to let the fact of loving her inform the way you hold your political opinions. This is an old teaching, older than democracy. A person is not the sum of her views. The view held by a person whom you love cannot be the view of a monster, even if you still believe the view is wrong. That is a fact about the structure of moral perception, and it is available to anyone who is willing to stay in the relationship long enough to have it.

If you do not currently have such a friend, the practice begins by finding one. Not manufacturing one; nothing is more obvious than being befriended for one's demographic slot. Begin by paying attention, in the memberships you already have, to the people whose politics you have been quietly writing off. Engage them as people. Ask what they do, what they read, what they worry about. Let the friendship, if it takes, take. Most friendships that survive the partisan line were planted before either party noticed that the line had moved.

***

The third practice is attention. You have to reclaim it, because it has been extracted from you in small increments for two decades by systems engineered for that purpose, and the capacity for sustained self-directed thought is the precondition for every other practice on this list.

We argued in Chapter 13 that the platform public sphere has restructured the conditions under which modern people think about anything, not just politics. The design of the systems through which information now reaches most of us is a design optimized for capturing and holding attention in short bursts, because short bursts are what the ad-serving business model monetizes most efficiently. Nothing about this design is accidental, and nothing about its effect on the user's inner life is in serious dispute among the people who built it. Your attention is a resource being mined. It is being mined from you whether you consent or not, and the capacity it was mined out of is the capacity to hold one thought across many minutes, to follow one argument across many pages, to sit with one idea long enough to let it reveal whether it is true. That capacity is not optional equipment for a free person. It is the equipment.

The disciplines here are not a secret, and they are not beyond reach. Set boundaries on your social media use. There is no single correct protocol — some people are well served by deleting the apps from the phone, some by a daily time limit, some by a full detox once a week, some by cutting off the platforms that do them the most damage while keeping the one that does them the least. What is not compatible with the practice of citizenship, or of any other practice requiring thought, is the unconstrained baseline. The unconstrained baseline is corrosive. Everyone who has tested this for himself knows it is corrosive. The question is only whether one is willing to act on the knowledge.

Read long-form writing regularly. Books, yes. Substantive articles, yes. Anything in which a single argument is sustained over enough pages that it has room to be complicated, to meet its objections, to change its own mind as it goes. This is a form of exercise for the muscle social media atrophies. It does not much matter whether the books are novels or histories or philosophy or science; what matters is that they are long enough to retrain you.

Walk without a phone. This sounds small. It is not small. A walk without a phone is a half-hour in which no notification can enter your consciousness, no feed can present itself, no urgency other than the one in your own head can arise. Very little of serious thought has ever been done outside this condition. If you cannot spend half an hour walking without a phone, something has happened to you that you should want to reverse.

And cultivate, in whatever frame works for you, a weekly practice of silence. A full Sabbath if your tradition offers one. A long meditation. A hike. A church service at which you actually put the phone away. A morning alone with a book and no screens. Whatever shape it takes, the point is the same: to set aside a recurring period during which the ordinary hum of extracted attention is switched off, and some other quality of mind is allowed to come forward. Without this, none of the rest of the practices on this list will take root, because none of them can take root in soil that has been paved over.

***

Closely related, and worth naming separately, is the discipline of an information diet. What you consume is not only a matter of how much of your attention it takes. It is also a matter of what it does to your perception of your fellow citizens. You have more control here than the architecture of the current media environment would have you believe. Algorithmic pressure is real; it is not fate.

Include, in your regular reading, at least one serious source from the political tradition opposite your own. Not a caricature. Not the excerpted worst tweets of a movement's fringe. A serious writer, a serious publication, treated with the same charity you would want extended to the serious writers on your own side. If you are on the left, this means reading, at some real cadence, the intelligent conservative writers. If you are on the right, it means reading the intelligent progressive ones. The reason is not balance as a virtue in itself. The reason is that no one who has not read the other tradition in its strong version has any idea what she is actually disagreeing with, and a person who does not know what she is disagreeing with is not participating in an argument but in a performance.

Read primary documents when you can. This is a discipline in its own right, and it is the training ground of a free citizen's mind. If a bill is being debated and you can locate the text of the bill, read it, or at least the relevant section. If a Supreme Court opinion is being denounced or celebrated, read the opinion itself before you decide what you think. If a scientific finding is being marshaled into a political argument, try to find the abstract of the paper, and ideally the paper. Journalism at its best is the summary of primary documents by people who have read them; journalism at its worst is the summary of other summaries, through layers of telephone, until the thing reported has no detectable relationship to the thing that actually occurred. The reader who spends a few hours a month with primary documents acquires, over the span of a year, a kind of epistemic ballast that is very hard to describe to a reader who has not done it. Among other things, she becomes much more skeptical of her own side's loudest claims, because many of those claims turn out, on inspection, to be loose at the joints.

Be systematically skeptical of anything that flatters your priors. This one is counterintuitive, because the emotional signal the system is training in you is the opposite: when a piece of news feels emotionally satisfying — when it confirms that the other side is as stupid or malicious or hypocritical as you suspected — the sensation of satisfaction reads, inside the head, as a signal of truth. It is in fact a signal of nothing except that the outrage machinery has found its mark. A trained citizen reverses this. The piece of news that feels most vindicating is the piece to scrutinize first, not to share first. The piece that unsettles your assumptions a little is the piece more likely to reflect how the world is actually put together, because the world is not, in fact, organized for your emotional convenience.

And, as an annual exercise, set yourself the test we set ourselves. Can you state, fairly, in a form the other side would recognize and endorse, the strongest version of the opposing political tradition? Not the form you would use to refute it. The form its best advocates would use to defend it. If the answer is no, something is wrong, and the thing that is wrong is in you, not in them. Fix it. Read until you can do it. The standard is John Stuart Mill's: he who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that.

***

The fifth practice is the one the whole book has been foreshadowing. Show up locally. Attend a local government meeting — school board, zoning commission, planning and development, city council, town meeting, county commission, whatever the relevant body is where you live — at least quarterly. Four times a year. This is not a ceremonial suggestion. It is probably the single highest-leverage civic act available to most American adults.

We said in Chapter 11 that American politics has become nationalized in a way that would have baffled earlier generations, and that the nationalization is itself a pathology. The machinery of national politics is largely beyond any individual's reach; it is a pageant you can follow but barely influence. The machinery of local politics, meanwhile, determines most of what actually affects the texture of your daily life — what your child's school teaches and how it teaches it, what can be built on the corner lot, whether the road gets fixed, whether the police department is trained the way you want it trained, whether the zoning permits the housing that would make the town livable for young families. These things are decided, in almost every American jurisdiction, in public meetings that are attended by a handful of people, most of whom are retired, most of whom are connected to the specific item on the agenda, and few of whom represent the broad interests of the community.

A citizen who shows up to even a few of these meetings a year has influence entirely out of proportion to her numbers. She learns how the local government actually works, which is usually different from what she thought. She learns the names of her council members and commissioners, who turn out mostly to be ordinary neighbors rather than the political class as she had imagined it. She learns the issues that are actually being decided, which are rarely the issues making national news. She gets her first concrete antidote to national-politics fixation, because the longer she spends in these rooms the more she notices that government is local before it is anything else, and that her own civic capacity will bear on local outcomes in a way it cannot bear on national ones.

And, crucially, she learns that showing up matters. A civic body whose meetings are attended by three citizens will make different decisions than a civic body whose meetings are attended by thirty. The decisions will not always be better — sometimes thirty angry people make worse decisions than three informed ones — but they will more nearly reflect the actual range of views in the community, which is what democracy, at the local scale, is. The first meeting is intimidating. The second is merely strange. By the third, you know the rhythm. By the fifth, you know the players. By the tenth, you are a person the board recognizes, and a person the board recognizes has, in that room, more power than a million people screaming at their phones.

***

The sixth practice follows naturally. After you have shown up a few times, take on a bounded civic role. One. Not five. This is not about maximizing your contribution until you are burned out. It is about moving from the posture of spectator — even attentive spectator — to the posture of participant.

There are more forms this can take than there are readers of this book. Coach a youth team, of any sport, in any league. Lead a committee in your religious community — the finance committee, the hospitality committee, the committee that runs the youth group. Serve on the board of a local nonprofit — the library foundation, the food bank, the animal shelter, the community land trust. Volunteer for an election as a poll worker. Mentor a young person entering your field; most professional associations have programs for this, and the ones that do not will set one up if you ask. Join the board of the PTA. Run for the neighborhood council. Take the training to be a court-appointed special advocate for children in the family court system. Join the volunteer fire department or the community emergency response team. Teach an adult education class in something you know how to do.

We want to note one thing about these roles. Each of them is bounded. There is a specific commitment — a season, a term, a project — after which you can in good conscience stop, or renew, or move on. This matters. The civic role that has no edges is the role that eats people. The civic role whose edges are clear is the role you can sustain for a working life. The history of American voluntary association is a history of bounded roles, taken up in succession across a lifetime. The person we are describing here is not a saint and not a martyr. She is a person who, at any given time, is doing one or two bounded civic things well, and who has done a dozen different such things across the span of her adult years. That pattern, repeated across a generation, is what a civic culture looks like.

***

The seventh practice is negative in form. Refuse the outrage loop.

By "the outrage loop" we mean the specific cycle by which the architecture of the platform public sphere converts citizens into unpaid amplifiers of content whose emotional signature is grievance. You know this cycle. You have been inside it. The trigger is usually a piece of content, often from an obscure source, often taken out of context, carefully selected and algorithmically promoted because it produces the strongest affective reaction — almost always anger, contempt, or fear — in the people most likely to share it. You feel the reaction. You share the content, or a response to it, or a screenshot of it. Your network feels the reaction. They share. The aggregate result is a feed full of the worst behavior of the other side's fringe, which your side's fringe is responding to in kind, which both sides then perceive as representative of the other. Every person in this cycle is doing unpaid labor for a system that has, as its only measurable product, the further degradation of the country.

The discipline is concrete. Do not post angrily. If you feel the urge to post something in anger, write it down somewhere private and come back to it tomorrow. If you still want to post it tomorrow, come back to it the day after. In our experience — and we have run this experiment on ourselves many times — the number of angry posts that survive this procedure is a small fraction of the number that started it, and the ones that survive are almost always better written than they would have been in the heat of the moment.

Do not amplify the worst version of your opposition. This is the single most consistent mistake made by politically engaged people of good faith on both sides. The research on the perception gap is overwhelming. The most extreme example from the other side that appears in your feed is far less representative of the other side than your emotional reaction is telling you it is. Amplifying it — sharing it with a note of horror, quote-tweeting it with a dunk, forwarding it to your group chat — does not serve your political purposes; it serves the outrage economy, which has no political purposes of its own and will metabolize whatever material you feed it. The fringe of the other side is the fringe of the other side. Most of them are ordinary people who also think their fringe is embarrassing, and who would be much more reachable, in the aggregate, if they did not spend every day seeing their fringe weaponized against them.

Do not engage with arguments made in bad faith. This takes some practice to recognize — there are unflattering positions held in good faith and flattering positions held in bad faith, and experience is required to tell the difference — but the cases where it is clear are worth naming. An argument made by someone who does not care whether you are persuaded, whose goal is to produce a clip or a screenshot or an embarrassing quote, is not an argument in a sense that deserves any of your energy. Every minute spent refuting a caricature is a minute the caricature has won, because the caricature's purpose was never to convince anyone of anything; the caricature's purpose was to occupy the time and psychic energy of people who could otherwise have been doing civic work. Recognize the feeling of being baited, and, when you recognize it, do not take the bait.

Finally, and we want to press this last point because it is uncomfortable: do not confuse the feeling of political engagement with political engagement itself. Eitan Hersh's work on political hobbyism, which we touched on in Chapter 12, is probably the single most clarifying finding in this space. A very large share of what passes for "caring about politics" in contemporary American life is what would be called, in any other context, consumption of political entertainment. A person who has spent four hours doom-scrolling the news has not done four hours of political work; she has done four hours of watching political work being performed by others, often badly. Doom-scrolling is to politics what spectator sports are to athletics. There is nothing wrong with spectator sports. There is something wrong with confusing them for the thing they depict. The practices in this chapter are meant, among other things, to be the real version of the thing that doom-scrolling impersonates.

***

The eighth practice is about language, and it is one of the oldest teachings in every moral tradition we know. Speak of the other side the way you would want to be spoken of.

This sounds like a minor matter. It is not. The language in which citizens refer to one another is, to a much greater extent than most of us notice, the air the polity breathes. A society in which ordinary voters for one party are routinely described by ordinary voters for the other party as evil, stupid, subhuman, traitors, vermin, deplorables, is a society whose political future is already in trouble, regardless of which party is currently using which terms. This is not a statement of etiquette. It is a statement about the conditions of possibility of democratic government. A people cannot self-govern if it has stopped being able to address itself as a people.

Concretely: do not use pejorative labels for ordinary people who vote for the other party. There is nothing to be gained by calling them names and much to be lost. Save harsh language for specific actions that deserve it, and direct the harsh language at the action, not at the category of persons. There is an enormous difference between "what this senator did last Tuesday was contemptible" and "the people who vote for this senator are contemptible." The first is a judgment a citizen is entitled to make and often has a duty to make. The second is an abdication of citizenship on the grounds of the speaker's superior moral rank.

When you describe an opposing position, describe it at its strongest. The strong version of a position is the version someone intelligent might actually believe. The weak version is the version that exists only as a target. A writer, a speaker, or a conversationalist who regularly addresses herself to the weak version of her opponent's position is not engaged in argument; she is engaged in self-congratulation, and the people on the other side of the line can see it, even if her own side cannot. The strong version of the opposing position is the version worth engaging with, because it is the only version that will actually cause you to learn anything. It is also the only version that will cause the other side to believe you have been listening.

When you describe your own position, acknowledge the objections. Every serious position has reasonable objections and concerns someone might raise about it. A speaker who presents her own position as if it had no reasonable objections is a speaker no thoughtful person can trust, because the absence of acknowledgment is a signal either of ignorance or of bad faith. A speaker who presents her position together with its strongest objections and her best answers to them is a speaker a thoughtful opponent can at least consider. Credibility is not the possession of the person who appears the most certain. It is the possession of the person who appears the most aware of the difficulty of her own claims. This is the opposite of the register rewarded by contemporary political media, which is one reason the contemporary political media is an unreliable guide to how to talk about politics well.

***

The ninth practice is the one that most surprises people when we name it. Make and repair things.

We mean this in an almost literal sense. Garden. Cook real food from raw ingredients. Fix what is broken rather than replacing it. Learn a skill that requires practice — an instrument, a craft, a sport, a language, a workshop trade. Raise a child, if that is in your life. Care for a parent, if that becomes necessary. Keep a dog or a cat or a flock of chickens. Tend something over time, and let it push back when you neglect it.

The argument for this practice is one we have been building toward since Chapter 9. Hoffer's account of the true believer, which we took seriously in that chapter, turns centrally on what he called the frustrated mentality — the person who has a relationship with her own life that she cannot bear, who therefore seeks self-forgetting in some compact collective whole that will relieve her of the burden of being herself. Hoffer's insight, and it was confirmed by Arendt from an entirely different direction, is that this mentality is produced by a specific kind of rootlessness. It is not poverty, though poverty can contribute. It is not unemployment, though unemployment can contribute. It is, at root, the absence of a meaningful, daily, attention-rewarding relationship with a concrete piece of the world that responds to how one treats it.

A person who gardens has this relationship with a piece of earth. A person who cooks has it with food. A person who repairs has it with machines and tools and materials. A person who plays an instrument seriously has it with the instrument. A person who raises a child has it most profoundly of all. The relationship is not always pleasant. A garden has slugs. A child has moods. A lathe has tolerances that do not care how you feel. But the relationship talks back, and it rewards attention with mastery, slowly, over years. A person inside such a relationship has less need of a mass movement, because the thing a mass movement offers — the experience of being absorbed into something larger than oneself, in a way that relieves one of the intolerable condition of being oneself — is being offered to her, in smaller and more honest form, by the world she has her hands on.

This is why, across every historical case we know, the revolutionary is almost never a gardener. The revolutionary is almost never a carpenter or a mechanic or a shepherd or a baker or a mother of young children. The revolutionary is, very often, a person with nothing in her life that talks back — a student without a craft, an intellectual without a garden, an ideologue whose daily hours are available to be filled with the content of the movement. This is not a dismissal of intellectual life; one of us for much of his life is a writer, which is a condition with some similar risks. It is an observation about where the totalitarian mentality tends to find purchase, and it is not a joke, though it can be told as one. A society that has forgotten how to make and repair things has become available, in a way it was not previously available, to be captured by people who promise that everything can be torn down and started over.

There is a second reason for this practice, which we will state briefly. A citizen of a polity is a person who is responsible for something. The modern condition of consumerism is the condition in which one is responsible for nothing, because someone else has been paid to be responsible for everything. A person who cooks her own food is responsible for it. A person who repairs her own porch is responsible for it. A person who raises her own child is responsible for that child in a way no institution can be. The practice of responsibility in the small things trains the practice of responsibility in the large ones. The reverse, we notice, is not true. A person who has never been responsible for anything in her daily life does not become responsible in the voting booth merely because the ballot is in her hand.

***

The tenth and last practice is the hardest of all, and we have put it last because it is the one you will need most when the others are difficult, which will be often. Practice hope, as a discipline.

We do not mean optimism. Optimism is a prediction about the future. Hope is something different. Hope is the trained disposition to continue working for goods whose achievement is not guaranteed and whose full realization you may not see. It is hope in this sense that Augustine named the three daughters of charity, that the civil-rights movement kept alive through decades when optimism would have been absurd, that the writers of the Federalist papers clung to through the American winter when they had every rational reason to believe their experiment would end in monarchy or in blood. Hope is what remains after optimism has been reality-tested and found wanting. Hope is not the same as belief that things will turn out well. It is the disposition to work as though the working were worth it regardless of the forecast.

We say hope is a discipline because despair is easier. Despair does not have to be argued for; it will come on its own, particularly in a moment like ours. Despair also has a kind of intellectual cool that hope does not have. The cynic gets to sound sophisticated; the hopeful person has to endure being called naive. The cynic cannot be embarrassed by an unrealized goal, because he predicted it would go unrealized. The hopeful person is on the hook for her hope, in a way the cynic has arranged not to be. These are not trivial asymmetries, and they are why most of the intelligent people around you, if you are paying attention, default to some posture of amused resignation that sounds more adult than hope does.

But cynicism is also, not incidentally, how a population becomes the passive audience of its own decline. A people that has concluded nothing can be done will do nothing, and its conclusion will be confirmed by the absence of its own action, in a loop that is indistinguishable from a prophecy fulfilling itself. The single most reliable way to produce the collapse you fear is to announce, in the tone of informed pessimism, that the collapse is already underway and nothing can stop it. A citizen practices hope because a citizen cannot afford not to, and because the alternative — cynicism announced with confidence — is the disease presenting itself as the diagnosis.

The practice, concretely, means refusing to say things in public that you would not want to turn out to be true. It means declining to participate in the conversations whose main emotional function is to confirm that the country is over. It means being willing to be the person in the room who says, when the room has settled into comfortable defeatism, that it is not clear the defeat has happened, and that acting as though it has will make it so. This will sometimes make you the least sophisticated-sounding person at the dinner. It is fine. The long history of free peoples is in the custody of people who were willing, at crucial moments, to sound less sophisticated than the room.

We know the objection. These are small. The country is large and the problems are structural, and a person cooking her own dinner and going to the zoning meeting is not going to reverse any of the trends we described in Volume II. Small practices will not fix structural problems. What good is any of this against the scale of what we are up against?

We want to take this objection seriously rather than dismiss it, because it is not a foolish objection, and because some version of it will be felt by every reader who has read this far. Our answer is the answer the book has been working toward throughout. The problem is both larger and smaller than usually supposed. It is larger because it is structural — embedded in institutions and incentives and technologies that no single citizen, however devoted, is going to fix alone. It is smaller because the repair, wherever it begins, begins in ordinary lives. There is no magical level above the ordinary lives at which the repair could begin, because every institution is, at bottom, made of people doing things, and every large social change in the history of this country, good or ill, started in the same obscure places: in church basements, in union halls, in neighborhood meetings, in the kitchens of people who had decided something had to change and could not wait for someone more important to say so.

What any one of us does in her own life is a small thing. That is true. What a million readers do, in a million lives, over a decade, is not a small thing. The civic generations that built the country we inherited built it this way. They did not descend from on high. They joined a lodge. They coached the town team. They sat on the school board. They ran the food drive. They showed up at the council meeting. They did these things at a scale that produced, in aggregate, the civic culture that in turn produced, in aggregate, the institutions we have spent the last fifty years consuming without replenishing. If there is going to be a civic recovery on the other side of this long depletion, it will have to be built the same way, because there is no other way to build such a thing. The question for us is whether we are going to be the generation that lets the inheritance run out, or the generation that starts paying it back in.

We do not pretend that the practices in this chapter exhaust what is needed. The institutional work described in Chapter 18, the deliberative work described in Chapter 19, the critical-thinking work described in Chapter 17 — all of it remains on the table, at every scale from the schoolroom to the Senate. What we are arguing, in this chapter, is that none of that other work is available to a person who has not first taken up, in her own life, the practices that form a citizen. The policy cannot produce the citizen. The citizen has to produce herself, by practice, over time, and then together with other citizens produce the institutions that further form her, and on up through every scale at which human beings cooperate with other human beings to do common things. The bottom of the ladder is where you are sitting. There is nowhere else the climb could begin.

In the chapter that follows we will try to describe what a country looks like when enough of its members have taken up enough of these practices for long enough. We will describe it not as a utopia, which is not useful, but as a real place we could plausibly build from here, with features we could test for and markers we could measure. It is a less dramatic picture than either the hopeful manifestos or the catastrophic predictions of our moment, and for that reason, we think, it is a more accurate one.

The question on the table, by the end of the next chapter, will not be whether such a country is possible. It is, as far as we can tell, obviously possible, because it has existed in various forms before and because nothing in the laws of nature prohibits it. The question will be whether we, the people actually here, are willing to become it. That is the question every generation of citizens, in every republic that has ever called itself one, has had to answer for itself. Nothing about being the generation that has to answer it now is unusual, except that it is ours.
