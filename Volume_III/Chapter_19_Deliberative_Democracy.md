# Chapter 19
## Deliberative Democracy in Practice

Cable news is many things. It is a business, and a successful one. It is a set of talented performers working inside a genre that has been refined over forty years to hold attention through the dinner hour and into the late news. It is, at its best, a source of same-day information about events in the world, and, at its worst, a source of same-day confirmation for what the viewer already believed on waking. It is a sorting mechanism, sending each audience to the channel that flatters its priors. It is an arousal mechanism, calibrated to a narrow emotional band between indignation and alarm. It is, in its evening blocks, a kind of civic liturgy, in which the faithful tune in each night to hear the names of the enemy recited and the outrages of the day catalogued. It is most of the things it has been accused of being, and most of the things it has been praised for being, and it is not going away.

What it is not, and has never pretended to be, is a model of public reasoning. If you asked an ordinary American to describe what the discourse of a self-governing people *ought* to look like, she would not, we suspect, describe a cable news panel. She would not describe three people in boxes on a screen shouting over one another while a fourth moderates with the tired patience of a referee who has seen this match before. She would describe, we think, something closer to a town meeting. Ordinary people, in a room. Real information on the table. A question that matters to the community, framed in terms the community recognizes. Time enough to hear each other out. A presumption that the people around the table are not enemies but fellow citizens with different angles on the same common life. The outcome not determined in advance. The meeting ending with some kind of honest judgment about what to do next, even if that judgment is only provisional.

That model is not extinct. It has, in fact, been developed in a surprisingly thick empirical and theoretical tradition over the last thirty years, tested in real applications in real countries on real policy questions, and found to produce outcomes that should interest anyone who cares about the quality of democratic judgment. It is called, in the academic literature, *deliberative democracy*, and in its applied form *deliberative mini-publics* — citizens' assemblies, deliberative polls, citizens' juries, planning cells, and a dozen regional variants that all share a family resemblance. The aim of this chapter is to take the reader through what this tradition is, what it has done, what the evidence shows, and what it has to offer a country whose public reasoning has been delegated almost entirely to the cable panel and the algorithmic feed. The short version of the argument is that deliberative democracy is not a utopian idea. It is a set of practices, and the practices work. Whether they can be scaled to the size of a continental republic is an honest question. That they work at the scale at which they have been tried is not.

\*\*\*

The theoretical tradition begins, for our purposes, with Jürgen Habermas, whose *Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* was the subject of part of our analysis in Volume II. Habermas is not the only parent of deliberative democratic theory, but he is the one who set the framing most sharply. Democratic legitimacy, on the Habermasian account, does not flow from the mere aggregation of preferences. It flows from the *process of forming* preferences under conditions of shared reasoning, with relevant information on the table and diverse perspectives represented in the conversation. A vote is a legitimate decision procedure when it summarizes such a deliberative process. A vote on preferences shaped by partisan media, identity signaling, and manipulated information is still a vote; it is just a much thinner kind of democracy than the theory requires, and its outputs are correspondingly less legitimate — not illegitimate, but thinner.

Joshua Cohen, working in the American tradition, pressed the argument further. Deliberation is not a preliminary to democracy or a decorative flourish on top of it. Deliberation is the thing democracy *is*, or should be. The procedural rules — universal suffrage, majority decision, the secret ballot — are the instruments by which a deliberative outcome is registered. They are not the deliberation itself. Cohen's intervention mattered because it set a normative bar that contemporary democratic practice, once named, could be measured against. Measured against that bar, very little of what happens in a contemporary American election is deliberation in any meaningful sense. What happens is the circulation of partisan signals through media designed to amplify them, a period of private decision by the voter acting on those signals, and a counting of results.

James Fishkin took the next step, which was to ask whether the theoretical model could be operationalized — whether you could actually put a representative sample of ordinary citizens into a room, give them the conditions the theory required, and see what happened. Fishkin's career has been devoted to the answer. John Dryzek, in Australia, built a parallel program of theoretical and empirical work on what he called the deliberative system, the idea that a democratic society does not need every citizen deliberating about every question but needs an ecology of deliberative sites — some legislative, some advisory, some educational — whose reasoning feeds into the political system as a whole. Hélène Landemore, at Yale, has in recent years pushed the argument further than anyone else, proposing that randomly selected deliberative bodies should be constitutive institutions of democracy, sharing power with elected ones. We will come back to her argument. The theoretical tradition is not unanimous on every question. It is remarkably unanimous on the core claim: that democracy's legitimacy depends on a quality of reasoning among its citizens that contemporary practice has largely abandoned, and that this quality of reasoning can be recovered by institutional design.

\*\*\*

Fishkin's innovation — the empirical one that makes this chapter possible — is called the *deliberative poll*, and the design has been refined over more than twenty years at the Stanford Center for Deliberative Democracy. The basic procedure is simple enough to state in a paragraph. A scientifically random, representative sample of the population is selected, the way a high-quality survey firm selects respondents. The sample is polled on its initial views on a given policy question. The sample is then brought together, in person or, more recently, in structured online platforms, for a weekend or sometimes longer. The participants are provided with balanced briefing materials prepared by a committee of experts whose charge is to present the strongest versions of the competing positions. They are assigned to small groups, stratified for demographic and ideological diversity, and moderated by trained facilitators whose job is to enforce norms of listening and prevent any single participant from dominating. They meet in plenary sessions with experts and with policymakers representing the competing positions, and they are free to question those experts directly. They deliberate among themselves in small groups between plenary sessions. At the end of the weekend, they are re-polled on the same questions. The researchers measure how their views changed, what they now know that they did not know before, and whether the distribution of opinion on the question has converged or diverged.

The design has been applied in dozens of countries on hundreds of topics. There is now a substantial literature reporting the results, the outcome measures are well-understood, and the findings replicate. We want to walk the reader through what the evidence actually shows, because the findings are specific and they are striking, and because anyone who is skeptical that this kind of work can produce serious outcomes should be met with the evidence rather than with exhortation.

The first finding, which is the least surprising, is that participants' *knowledge* on the topic rises substantially. This is what one would expect from a weekend of intensive engagement with briefing materials and direct questioning of experts. It is worth stating plainly because ordinary democratic practice does not produce informed citizens; it produces citizens with impressions, slogans, and partisan affiliations on most questions. A deliberative poll, at the end of the weekend, has produced a sample of ordinary Americans — or Britons, or Australians, or Chinese, or Mongolians, all of whom have been studied — who know more about the issue at hand than the average congressional staffer. This is not a minor fact.

The second finding is the one that should be more interesting to a partisan reader. The participants' *views change*, and they change in a direction that neither side of the original argument could have predicted. The change is not toward the initial majority view of the sample. It is not toward either partisan pole. It is toward a position that integrates the information and perspectives the participants did not start with. What Fishkin and his colleagues have observed repeatedly is that the post-deliberation distribution of views is more considered, more nuanced, and more resistant to restatement in slogan form than the pre-deliberation distribution was. The deliberative process does not move people toward a predetermined position. It moves them toward a position their pre-deliberation selves, asked the same question without the intervening weekend, could not have arrived at.

The third finding is the one that should interest every reader of Volume II of this book. The change in views *crosses partisan and demographic lines*. Republicans and Democrats, placed in controlled deliberative settings with the design described above, end up in considerably closer positions than they started — not identical positions, but positions whose remaining disagreements are recognizable to each side as reasonable rather than monstrous. The effect has been measured on climate policy, on immigration, on foreign policy, on energy, on electoral reform, on health care, across a range of national contexts. The partisan gap narrows. The gap between what each side thinks the other side believes and what the other side actually believes — the perception gap we dwelt on in Chapter 16 — narrows even more, and in many studies collapses almost entirely. Participants end the weekend with a much more accurate picture of the political landscape they share than they started with, and with a much more accurate picture of each other.

The fourth finding is that the effects are *durable*. Follow-up studies at various intervals — weeks, months, in some cases years after the deliberative event — show persistence of both the informational and the attitudinal changes, with erosion over time but at a rate slower than most depolarization interventions. The deliberative experience, in other words, does something to the participants that an ordinary weekend of media consumption does not.

The fifth finding is qualitative, and it is in some ways the most striking. Participants describe the experience, in post-session reports gathered across many studies and many countries, as transformative. They describe it in strikingly consistent language. They say they had never before been *really heard* on a political topic. They say the experience of disagreeing, at length, with other ordinary people who took them seriously — not as partisans, not as representatives of a tribe, but as fellow citizens with something to say — was qualitatively different from anything they had encountered in their political lives. Many report that the weekend was the first time they had been in a room with people who disagreed with them as *people*, rather than as avatars of an enemy team. Many report, weeks later, a continued sense of civic awakening — a feeling, for the first time in adult life, that they have something to contribute to a public conversation and that the conversation can be had. This is an empirical finding, not a piece of sentimentality. It shows up in the transcripts, in the surveys, in the follow-up interviews. It is one of the more consistent reports in the entire literature on civic intervention, and it is, we think, deserving of more serious attention than it has received.

The most important real-world application of the deliberative tradition is in Ireland, and it is a story that anyone writing about the subject should know in some detail. Over the last fifteen years, Ireland has held a series of citizens' assemblies — bodies of roughly ninety-nine randomly selected citizens plus an independent chair — tasked with deliberating on questions that the Irish parliament had proved unable or unwilling to resolve in the ordinary political process. The assemblies have considered same-sex marriage, abortion, the aging population, climate change, and the form of Irish government itself. They meet over a period of months, they hear from experts on every side of the question, they take public submissions, they deliberate among themselves in small groups and in plenary, and they produce recommendations that are then transmitted to the parliament.

The Irish experience is distinctive because the recommendations have actually been acted on. The Assembly's deliberations on same-sex marriage informed the 2015 referendum, which passed with a decisive majority. The Assembly's deliberations on abortion informed the 2018 referendum, which removed the Eighth Amendment and liberalized Irish abortion law, again with a substantial majority. These were not ordinary referendums. They were held after extensive public exposure to the deliberative process that had informed them, and the public could, and did, treat the Assembly's work as a credible signal that ordinary citizens, given the time and the information, had thought the question through with seriousness. The referendums passed not because the parliament had imposed an elite preference on a reluctant public, but because a deliberative process had laid the groundwork for the public itself to take the question seriously.

We do not argue that Ireland has solved democracy. We note that Ireland has demonstrated something specific, which is that a deliberative mini-public, when given real standing in the constitutional process and supported by a parliament willing to take its work seriously, can produce a legitimate resolution to a contested moral-political question, and that the public can, and in these cases did, recognize that resolution as legitimate. The Irish case is the clearest contemporary example in the world of deliberative democracy being mainstreamed into constitutional politics, and it is young — the institutional model is less than a generation old and has already produced constitutional amendments that had eluded the ordinary political process for decades.

France offers a second case, and a more politically complicated one. In 2019, following the Yellow Vest protests, the government convened the Citizens' Climate Convention — 150 randomly selected French citizens, charged with proposing policies to reduce French greenhouse gas emissions by at least forty percent relative to 1990 levels. The Convention deliberated for nine months, with expert testimony, staff support, and a substantial mandate. It produced a final report with 149 specific policy recommendations, ranging from building-code reforms to restrictions on short-haul flights to a proposed crime of "ecocide." The French government committed to transmitting these recommendations to the legislative process "without filter," a promise it subsequently kept only partially, which became a political flashpoint. Many of the recommendations were adopted in some form; others were watered down or rejected; the participants themselves and the public criticized the government sharply for what they took to be bad faith. The controversy, however, is less interesting than the underlying fact. One hundred and fifty ordinary French citizens, randomly selected, given time and information, produced 149 concrete policy proposals on a highly technical subject, most of which were judged by outside experts to be serious and implementable. Whatever one thinks of the political handling afterward, the Convention is an incontestable demonstration that ordinary citizens, properly supported, can perform policy analysis at a level that ordinary political commentary has all but given up on expecting of them.

British Columbia's Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform, convened in 2004, is the third case we want to name. 160 citizens, randomly selected, deliberated for a year on the province's electoral system. They studied electoral systems around the world, heard from experts and from practitioners, held public consultations, and produced a recommendation to replace the province's first-past-the-post system with a form of single-transferable vote. The recommendation was placed before the province's voters in a referendum in 2005. The referendum required a sixty percent supermajority; it received 57.7 percent. By the standards of ordinary political reform this would be an extraordinary result. By the standards of the supermajority threshold it narrowly failed. The Assembly's work was nonetheless preserved in the comparative literature as one of the benchmark demonstrations of the method, and its basic design — a year of serious deliberation by a representative citizen body on a question of constitutional reform — has been imitated in Ontario, in the Netherlands, and in other settings.

In the United States, the applications are more scattered but they are real. There has been a statewide deliberative poll in Georgia on electrical utility regulation, with significant effects on both the participants' views and the subsequent regulatory process. Oregon, in what we take to be one of the most interesting American experiments of the last two decades, has institutionalized the Citizens' Initiative Review, a process in which a randomly selected, demographically balanced panel of citizens deliberates for several days on each statewide ballot measure, hears from the measure's proponents and opponents, and produces a citizen-authored pro/con statement that is then included in the voter information pamphlet sent to every registered voter. The evaluations have been positive. Participants report the same qualitative experience described in the deliberative-poll literature. Voters who read the citizen-authored statements report higher confidence in their own decisions on the measure. The Oregon model is small, cheap, and transferable; a number of other states have begun piloting versions of it. Beyond these, the Deliberative Democracy Institute, the OECD, and other bodies have now catalogued several hundred applications of deliberative methods in municipal, regional, and national governance worldwide, and the catalog grows each year.

It is worth asking, carefully, why this works, because the answer matters for any attempt to extend the method. Our best reading of the literature identifies six features that appear across successful designs, and we want to name them because they are the part of the design a reader, or an institution, could actually implement.

The first is *random selection*. This is not a self-selected group. It is not the activists and the joiners and the letter-writers who show up because they have opinions to defend. It is a representative sample of the population, including the many citizens who do not ordinarily engage with political questions at all. The dynamic inside the room is different from any other political gathering most participants have attended, because the participants know they are there because they are *ordinary*, not because they are special. The signal this sends is surprisingly powerful. Participants stop performing for a tribe, because no tribe has sent them. They begin, instead, to treat the question as a question to be answered.

The second is *duration and depth*. A weekend or longer of sustained engagement with a single topic, with time to digest briefing materials and to argue with other participants, shifts the cognitive frame from tribal signaling to problem-solving. This is not a claim about human perfectibility. It is a claim about the conditions under which human reasoning tends to improve. Rushed judgment under partisan pressure produces one kind of output. Unrushed judgment with time for reconsideration produces another. The deliberative poll creates the second condition.

The third is *facilitation*. Trained moderators enforce norms of listening. They prevent the dynamics — interruption, domination by confident speakers, identity-driven attack — that poison ordinary political discussion. They are not there to steer the group toward a conclusion. They are there to create the conditions under which the group can conduct the kind of conversation its members already, in principle, know how to have but almost never get to have in practice. Those who have never experienced facilitated group deliberation often assume that facilitation is a mild sort of intervention. It is not. It is the difference between a conversation that goes somewhere and one that does not.

The fourth is *balanced information*. The briefing materials used in a well-designed deliberative poll are not neutral — there is no such thing — but they are representative of the strongest versions of the competing responsible positions on the question. Participants become, within the course of a weekend, better informed on the specific question than almost any ordinary citizen, and often better informed than the elected representatives and staff responsible for the policy area. This, too, is a condition of the reasoning. You cannot produce considered judgment on a question without information. Ordinary democratic practice is, for most citizens, largely uninformed by anything more rigorous than a headline and a partisan reaction to it.

The fifth is *social diversity* inside the small groups. Deliberative polls are designed so that every small group contains participants of different demographics, different political orientations, different regional backgrounds, different levels of prior engagement. The argument that happens inside a stratified small group is different from the argument that happens inside a homogeneous one. The small group is, in miniature, a version of the diverse public that the deliberation is meant to inform.

The sixth is what we will call, for lack of a better term, the *random citizen effect*. Being recognized and addressed as a citizen, as distinct from being addressed as a consumer or a voter or a demographic unit, activates something that ordinary political participation does not. The experience of being selected by random draw, flown in, seated at a table, given materials, introduced to experts, and asked one's opinion with the expectation that the opinion will be taken seriously is, for most participants, unlike anything else in their political lives. It is, in a precise sense, a recovery of the citizen role that this book has argued has gone missing from American public life. Participants often report the experience as a kind of civic awakening, a recognition that they are, after all, part of a self-governing people with a stake in the common question. The reports are strikingly consistent. We think they should be taken seriously. They describe, we suspect, what it feels like to be addressed as a citizen for the first time.

The most ambitious contemporary theorist of this tradition is Hélène Landemore, whose *Open Democracy* appeared in 2020 and is, we think, the single most provocative recent book in democratic theory. Landemore's argument, compressed, is this. Deliberative mini-publics should not merely advise elected bodies. They should be *constitutive institutions of democracy itself*. A fully realized "open democracy" would include randomly selected legislative bodies — what the Greek tradition called *sortition*, the selection of public officials by lot — sharing power, on some domains of policy, with the elected bodies we now treat as the sole legitimate representatives of the people.

The argument is older than it sounds. Athenian democracy, in its classical form, selected most of its officials by lot; election was associated with aristocratic rather than democratic government, on the grounds that election tends to select for those already known, already rich, already connected, while lot selects representatively from the whole citizenry. The American founders were aware of this tradition and, on the whole, rejected it, on grounds one can read for oneself in *The Federalist*. Landemore's claim is not that the founders were wrong then. Her claim is that the conditions of modern representative democracy have produced a class of professional politicians whose incentives are so misaligned with the common good — and whose information environment is so degraded by media, donor pressure, and partisan discipline — that a deliberative body selected by lot, operating under the conditions described above, would produce policy judgments of *higher* informational and moral quality than the elected legislatures we now rely on.

This is a radical claim, and most of the readers of this book, we expect, will not be ready to endorse it in its strongest form. We are not sure we endorse it in its strongest form. But it is a serious argument and it deserves serious engagement. What Landemore has done, with Fishkin and Dryzek and the broader tradition, is to force the question of whether elected representation and random selection are genuine rivals in our democratic repertoire, or whether they can be complementary — whether a mature democracy might include both, with different mandates, constraining and informing each other. That question, once raised, does not go away. It will shape democratic design for the rest of our lifetimes, and probably beyond.

\*\*\*

We owe the reader an honest account of the critiques and limits of the deliberative tradition, because the critiques are real and the limits matter.

The first is *scalability*. Ireland is a country of five million; British Columbia is a province of five million; France is sixty-seven million, but the Climate Convention was a one-time effort of nine months by one hundred and fifty people. Scaling deliberative mini-publics into ongoing institutions of a country of three hundred and thirty million — with fifty states, ten thousand school districts, nineteen thousand municipalities, and a federal system that deliberately complicates every reform — is a much harder problem than running a single citizens' assembly on a single question. Fishkin and Landemore would both say, we think correctly, that the answer is federation and specialization: not one national deliberative body on every question, but many deliberative mini-publics on many questions at many scales, feeding into a political system that has learned to take their work seriously. That answer is plausible and, we believe, correct in principle. It is not the same as a worked-out institutional blueprint for the United States, and no one has produced that blueprint yet.

The second is *legitimacy*. What standing, exactly, do the recommendations of a randomly selected body have, in a system whose legitimate representatives are elected? The Irish answer was produced politically: the Assembly had standing because the parliament chose to give it standing, and the public accepted the result because the subsequent referendums validated it. In a country without that parliamentary tradition — and the United States is such a country, because our separation of powers makes the Irish move much harder — the legitimacy question is harder, and any proposal for an American citizens' assembly has to take this question seriously rather than assume it away. Random selection solves one legitimacy problem. It does not solve every one.

The third is *capture*. Random selection is designed to prevent capture by organized interests. It does not prevent capture by the facilitators, the briefing-material authors, or the agenda-setters who choose the question. Any deliberative process has a designer, and any designer has judgments built into the design. The deliberative tradition has developed protocols to mitigate this — external review of briefing materials, diverse expert panels, transparent selection of facilitators — but the problem does not go away, and any proposal for institutionalizing deliberation has to build in serious, continuing oversight of the design choices. Vigilance is not optional.

The fourth is *participant burden*. Serving on a deliberative poll for a weekend is one thing. Serving on the French Climate Convention for nine months is another, and it imposes real costs on participants: time away from work, time away from family, cognitive load, the strain of being asked to produce serious policy analysis on a technical question. Compensation, in the French case, was modest and was supplemented by an expectation of civic duty, and the experience was, for some participants, exhausting. Any scaling of the method has to take seriously the question of what we owe the ordinary citizens we ask to do this work. Compensation, child care, professional leave, and logistical support are not amenities. They are preconditions.

We say these things because we do not want to offer the reader a method that has been varnished. The deliberative tradition is strong enough that it does not need varnish. It is one tool among several. It will not replace elections, and it should not try to; it will not repair an atomized civic fabric single-handedly, and its advocates do not claim otherwise; it has been shown to work at the scales at which it has been tried, and whether it works at much larger scales is an open empirical and institutional question. What we can say with confidence is that the method produces something democracy has been losing, and that what it produces deserves a much larger place in the American civic repertoire than it currently has.

\*\*\*

Which brings us to the American context, which is where the puzzle gets most interesting. Deliberative democracy, in the United States, is weirdly underused relative to what the evidence would support. The reasons are partly structural. Our representative system is strong; the American political class does not often defer to advisory bodies, and certainly not to randomly selected ones. Our direct-democracy traditions vary enormously by state; a method that works cleanly in Oregon, with its long history of citizen initiatives and of civic experimentation, does not transplant automatically to New York or Texas or Alabama. Our political class has little incentive to adopt tools that would embarrass it — and a well-run citizens' assembly is, among other things, an embarrassment to the ordinary political process, because it demonstrates that ordinary citizens, given time and information, can produce better judgment than the elected officials who spent careers on the question. These are real barriers, and they are why the American uptake has been slower than the Irish or the French.

But the barriers are not fundamental. The American civic tradition, at its best, has been unusually hospitable to exactly this kind of innovation. The jury system — a randomly selected body of citizens, deliberating under facilitation, on questions with real consequences, and issuing binding judgments — was itself once an experimental form of citizen involvement in state decisions, and it remains one of the most successful deliberative institutions in the world. The American town meeting, in its New England variant, is a deliberative form more than two centuries old. The progressive-era innovations — the initiative, the referendum, the recall, the direct primary, the commission form of municipal government — were attempts to expand citizen participation in public judgment. The American genius for civic innovation has produced, repeatedly, forms that the rest of the democratic world has adopted. There is nothing in the American tradition that forbids us from recognizing the deliberative mini-public as the next of these innovations, and every reason in the tradition to embrace it. Some states and some municipalities are beginning to. We think more should.

\*\*\*

We want to be concrete about what that means at different scales, because concreteness is what the solutions volume of this book owes the reader.

At the *local* level, the opportunities are immediate and inexpensive. A municipality can convene a deliberative panel on a specific question — the redrawing of school-district boundaries, a zoning change affecting density, the setting of a police-civilian review board's charter, the priorities in next year's capital budget. The investment is modest: a facilitator, a set of briefing materials, a weekend of time for a few dozen participants, a small stipend. The output is a considered citizen recommendation that the city council can take up or not, but that exists on the public record as a demonstration of what the community, deliberating seriously, thinks. The civic-formative effect on participants, we have reason to believe, is substantial. Many of them will become, within a few years, the kind of citizens local institutions have been starving for — informed, attentive, willing to do committee work, capable of disagreeing without contempt.

At the *state* level, Oregon's Citizens' Initiative Review is the model we would most urge on other states. It is cheap, it is unobtrusive, and it supplies voters with something the current voter-information apparatus largely fails to supply: a citizen-drafted account of the strongest arguments for and against each measure, produced by a representative deliberative body. A version of the same method could be adapted to state constitutional amendments, to major regulatory questions, to the design of ballot measures before they reach the ballot. None of this requires constitutional change. It requires legislative will, modest appropriation, and trust in the method.

At the *federal* level, the ambition would have to be more circumspect, for the reasons discussed. We would not, at present, advocate a national citizens' assembly on a contested partisan question, because we do not think the American political system is yet equipped to handle its outputs. What we would advocate is a more modest beginning: Ireland-style citizens' assemblies, commissioned by mixed congressional authority or by a consortium of state governments, on questions of constitutional and democratic significance that the ordinary political process has demonstrably failed to resolve. Supreme Court reform, electoral reform, the long-term structure of immigration law, the reform of congressional procedure itself — these are questions on which a deliberative body could produce, at minimum, a legitimate public alternative to the current spectacle, and on which the public might, over time, come to recognize the deliberative output as a more credible signal of the considered will of the people than the usual partisan crossfire. Whether the political class would allow such a body to exist is a different question. That it would produce something worth listening to, we have reason to believe.

There is a fourth scale, and it is the one most readers of this book are in a position to affect directly. *Employer-based* and *association-based* deliberation does not require any state participation. A large employer can adopt deliberative methods for questions of internal governance — benefit design, return-to-office policy, major strategic pivots affecting the workforce — in place of the usual top-down announcement or the cynical all-hands town hall. A university can convene a deliberative panel of students, staff, and faculty on questions of campus policy. A church or a synagogue or a professional association can do the same. This is the hidden ground of civic formation, and it is substantially underused. Most Americans, for most of their lives, are members of institutions whose internal governance could be made more deliberative without any change to the Constitution. For most Americans, the first sustained experience of considered deliberation could be at work, or in their children's school, or in the association they have joined for reasons that have nothing to do with politics. Those experiences, once had, carry over. We believe they carry over more than the political-science literature has yet managed to measure. The person who has sat through a well-facilitated deliberative process on a meaningful question inside her own workplace has become, in some small but real way, the kind of citizen this book has been trying to describe.

\*\*\*

We want to end with what this chapter means for the argument of the book as a whole. Deliberative democracy, as we have described it, is not a partisan program. It is not a technocratic fix. It is not a piece of utopian wishful thinking. It is, rather, the institutional form of what we have been calling *republican liberalism* — the synthesis of the liberal and civic-republican traditions that has run through this book from the Preface. The deliberative mini-public treats citizens as capable of reasoning together under the right conditions. That is the republican assumption — that persons form their best judgments in participation with equals, not in private isolation. At the same time, the deliberative mini-public treats each citizen as an end in herself, to be addressed with respect, given accurate information, and invited to make up her own mind. That is the liberal assumption — that each person is a sovereign reasoner whose judgment belongs to her alone. The mini-public joins these two assumptions, which the political theory of the last two centuries has too often treated as rivals, in a single institutional design. It is, in miniature, the polity this book argues for. That is the sense in which we think the deliberative tradition is more than a tool. It is an institutional demonstration of what the citizen, in the full sense of the word, can do.

And this is where we have to be honest with the reader about what the chapter has not done. We have told you about an institutional form that works. We have told you that if the country were to adopt it more widely, our public reasoning would be better than it is. But most readers of this book will never be randomly selected for a citizens' assembly. Most will never sit through a deliberative poll. The Oregon voter pamphlet reaches every voter in Oregon, and that is something; but the deliberative moment itself, with its forty-eight or seventy-two hours in a room with ordinary strangers, is an experience that most citizens of this country will not have, even if the movement we are describing succeeds beyond our current expectations. The question a fair reader will ask, at the end of this chapter, is: what is this for me?

Our answer is that the institutions described here depend on, and produce, a kind of citizen — patient, informed, willing to listen, willing to change her mind, willing to treat her opponent as a fellow citizen rather than as an enemy — and that citizen can be cultivated whether or not the institutions are available. Whether the reader participates in a deliberative mini-public or not, she can cultivate the dispositions those institutions assume. She can read the briefing materials on her own. She can enforce her own norms of listening. She can refuse the small, daily opportunities to treat her political opponents as caricatures. She can build, in the associations and workplaces and congregations she already belongs to, small imitations of the deliberative form. She can become, in her ordinary life, the kind of citizen these institutions were designed to address — and, in doing so, she can make those institutions more likely to arise.

That is the material of the next chapter. We turn, in Chapter 20, from the institutional forms of citizenship to its personal practice — to what one reader, beginning this week, can actually do.
