# Chapter 17
## Critical Thinking as Civic Discipline

For more than a decade now, a research group at Stanford has been quietly gathering what is, we think, the most damning single body of evidence about the condition of American civic life. The Stanford History Education Group began in the mid-2010s to ask a question that had until then been asked mostly in the abstract: can young Americans, who have grown up on the open internet, actually tell whether what they are reading online is true? The researchers devised a set of assessments in which students were shown real-world web material — photographs, news stories, sponsored content, advocacy sites, social-media posts — and asked to judge its credibility. The students came from high schools and colleges across the country, from elite institutions and from ordinary ones. The samples ran to thousands.

The results, published in a series of papers and in a 2019 national study of nearly 3,500 high-school students, are the kind of thing a researcher hesitates to write down because writing it down makes it real. Two thirds of the students could not distinguish a sponsored advertisement labeled "sponsored content" from a regular news article on the same page. More than ninety percent accepted a blatantly political advocacy site, which concealed its funding, as a credible source of information on the topic it was advocating about. Shown a photograph that purported to document a real-world event but was in fact unrelated to it, nearly all failed to perform any verification; the overwhelming majority took the image at face value. Asked to evaluate a Twitter post from an organization with a professional-sounding name, the students accepted the professional appearance as sufficient — they confused the aesthetic of authority for the substance of it. Undergraduates, in a parallel study, did slightly better on average and were still, by any reasonable standard, terrible. Stanford's own students, the researchers noted with a pained sort of candor, were not appreciably better than students at open-enrollment community colleges at the basic task of checking who was behind a website before deciding whether to trust it. What the researchers called "lateral reading" — the habit of opening a second tab and looking up the source rather than staying on the source's own page — was essentially absent from the student repertoire. The students clicked, scrolled, stayed, and judged; they did not, on the whole, verify.

We dwell on this because the first thing to say about critical thinking in American life is that, as a mass competence, it is not present. It is not that we have it and are failing to use it. We do not, at scale, have it. And the failure is bipartisan — it is not that students on one side of the political spectrum are taken in by bad sources while students on the other see clearly. The credulity is general. The right-leaning student who believes the advocacy site on immigration and the left-leaning student who believes the advocacy site on climate are performing the same cognitive operation and performing it equally badly. Whatever else we say about the condition of the American public mind, it is not that half of it has been trained well and half has been trained badly. The training is absent. The information environment, which punishes those who have not learned, presses on everyone.

Put this finding next to another one and the civic picture comes into focus. In a 2016 paper in the *Journal of Democracy* that has since become a reference point in political science, Roberto Stefan Foa and Yascha Mounk reported on a trend in the World Values Survey that almost no one had noticed. They asked respondents across established democracies how essential it was to live in a democratically governed country, on a ten-point scale. Among Americans born in the 1930s, about seventy-two percent rated living in a democracy a "ten" — absolutely essential. Among Americans born in the 1980s, the figure was about thirty percent. A roughly forty-point generational collapse in the importance attached to the democratic form itself. Similar declines, though smaller, appeared in most of Europe. Openness to authoritarian alternatives — "a strong leader who doesn't have to bother with parliament and elections" — rose across the same generations. Other researchers have pushed back on the steepness of the slope and on the measurement; even under the gentlest reading, the gap is one of the largest attitudinal gaps ever reported in public-opinion research on democracy, and it points in one direction.

This is not a private failure. It is a structural one. A civic education — here meaning the whole apparatus of schools, media, families, and civic organizations by which a polity transmits itself to the next generation — that produces a population unable to evaluate sources and increasingly willing to entertain the abandonment of the democratic form is a civic education that has failed at the only job it ever had. The question is what we can do about it.

We want to propose an answer. It is not a policy answer; it is closer to a cultural one, though it has policy consequences. The answer begins by reclaiming a phrase that has been ruined by overuse, and restoring it to the meaning it had before it became a slogan.

***

The phrase is *critical thinking*. It is now used, in the cheerful commercial prose of school districts and human-resources seminars and political op-eds, to mean almost anything the writer wants it to mean. A pundit announces that the public needs more critical thinking, by which he means that it needs to agree with him. A teacher tells her class to engage in critical thinking, by which she means that the students should summarize the reading with skepticism toward some parts and credulity toward others according to rules that are never quite specified. A political movement invokes critical thinking as its self-description and applies it with ferocity to the claims of its opponents and not at all to its own. Each use is a small act of laundering. The phrase comes out of these uses meaning, at best, *the kind of thinking I approve of*, and at worst, nothing at all. If we are to make anything of critical thinking as a civic category, we have to earn the concept. We will not use it the way the culture uses it.

The first thing to do is distinguish it from two neighboring things it is often confused with. The first is general cognitive capability — roughly, intelligence. Cognitive capability is measurable. It is partly heritable. It correlates with many good outcomes. It is not, and this is the point, a prophylactic against bad reasoning. The research tradition associated with Daniel Kahneman, Amos Tversky, Keith Stanovich, Dan Kahan, and others has made what ought by now to be a familiar finding: intelligence, on its own, does not protect a person from motivated reasoning. Under conditions of identity-protective cognition — when a question of fact becomes entangled with a question of who the person is — higher cognitive ability is associated with *more* sophisticated rationalization, not less. The clever partisan builds better arguments for what the partisan already believes. Kahan's studies of numerical reasoning on politically charged topics show the pattern directly: given the same mathematical problem dressed as a skin-cream trial or as a gun-control study, high-numeracy respondents produce the right answer when the result is politically neutral and fluff the analysis when the politically neutral answer would embarrass their side. Intelligence, under motivation, is a weapon. It is not a safeguard.

The second thing critical thinking is often confused with is domain knowledge. A person who has studied the history of the Civil War closely will reason about the Civil War better than someone who has not; a person who has built bridges knows more about bridges than a person who has only read about them. This is true and important. Serious reasoning about any particular subject requires serious acquaintance with the subject. But domain knowledge does not generalize. The Civil War historian is no wiser than anyone else about tax policy, and the bridge engineer has no special authority on public health. A good part of the epistemic damage of the past decade has been done by people with real expertise in one field confidently speaking in another and being treated, by themselves and by their audiences, as authoritative. Domain knowledge is necessary; it is not sufficient; and it is not transferable in the way that a civic education needs a capacity to be transferable.

The third thing — the thing we want to claim is the civic heart of the matter — is what psychologists Jonathan Baron and Keith Stanovich, over a long research career, have called *actively open-minded thinking*. The phrase has a technical meaning. It denotes a disposition rather than an ability — a habit of the mind rather than a feature of its horsepower. The actively open-minded thinker is willing to consider evidence that contradicts her prior belief; actively seeks out viewpoints different from her own; distinguishes the content of a claim from the identity of the person making it; tolerates the psychological discomfort of not yet knowing; gives genuine weight, when she assesses an argument, to the possibility that she is wrong. She has, in Baron's formulation, an aversion to premature closure and an appetite for disconfirming information that most of us, left to ourselves, lack. The trait is ordinary-language simple and technically precise. It is what we mean by open-mindedness when we are being serious.

What the psychometric literature has added to the ordinary-language understanding is, for our purposes, decisive. Three findings in particular.

The first is that actively open-minded thinking is the single best-identified cognitive correlate of accurate political reasoning, and that it predicts accuracy *independently* of intelligence and of education. Stanovich's *What Intelligence Tests Miss* (2009) and his subsequent work with Richard West have made a systematic case that the standard IQ tests do not measure this disposition at all; that the disposition and the ability are roughly uncorrelated; and that many of the failures of rationality we associate with the name of Kahneman are failures of the disposition, not of the ability. A brilliant person who does not want to be wrong is not, on the questions that matter to her identity, going to reason well about them. A modestly intelligent person who has cultivated the disposition will. Across a range of studies — on belief in conspiracy theories, on susceptibility to fake news, on the ability to update in response to evidence — actively open-minded thinking is the trait that moves the needle.

The second finding is more disquieting, and we have already gestured at it. Because intelligence under motivation tends to *better* defend the motivated conclusion, and because political engagement tends to intensify motivation, intelligent partisans are often *more* polarized than less-intelligent ones. Dan Kahan's work shows this directly. The most numerate respondents in his studies show the largest politically-driven gaps in numerical reasoning. Ezra Klein, in *Why We're Polarized*, summarizes the point by observing that the most politically informed voters are, in many ways, the most polarized and the most intractable. This is the paradox of the engaged citizen in a polarized age: the very traits we hope a good civic education would cultivate — attentiveness, informedness, political interest — turn out, without the disposition of active open-mindedness, to produce the more sophisticated partisan rather than the better citizen. More in Common's perception-gap research makes the same point from the other direction: the most politically engaged Americans hold the most distorted pictures of what people on the other side actually believe. Engagement, on its own, is not a solution. It is a multiplier. What it multiplies depends on what disposition it is attached to.

The third finding is the hopeful one. Actively open-minded thinking is teachable. It can be assessed, through standard scales that have been validated over decades. It can be measured before and after an intervention. It responds to interventions. The pedagogical literature is clear that you can, through the right kind of deliberate practice, increase the trait in individual learners, and that the increase produces measurable downstream effects on the quality of reasoning about politically charged topics. The trait is not a fixed feature of a person. It is a muscle.

That is the place where the psychological literature meets the civic one, and where our argument about critical thinking starts to become an argument about citizenship.

***

Consider what it means to call something a virtue. We took some care in Volume I to recover the classical meaning of the word, which the moralistic turn of the last two centuries has obscured. A virtue in the Aristotelian and republican tradition is not a feeling; it is not an inclination; it is not, in the first instance, a moral sentiment. A virtue is a trained disposition — a habituated readiness to act rightly under the circumstances of a particular role. Courage is the soldier's virtue not because soldiers feel braver than other people but because the soldier's role places him in situations where the pull of his own fear must be resisted in favor of the demands of the role, and because courage is the disposition that, once trained in, makes that resistance possible. Temperance is the pilgrim's virtue; patience is the teacher's; honesty, in the strong and costly sense, is the witness's. Each is acquired by practice. No one is born with it. One becomes courageous by repeatedly doing the brave thing in small instances until the disposition is set. One becomes honest by speaking the truth against interest, over and over, until it is the default and not the exception.

The civic virtue analogue — the virtue that belongs to the citizen as the courage belongs to the soldier — is, we think, exactly this disposition of actively open-minded thinking. The citizen's role places her in situations where the pull of her own partisan identity, her own group loyalty, her own desire to be right, her own discomfort with disconfirmation, must be resisted in favor of the demands of the role. The role demands that she consider the claims of her political opponents on their merits. It demands that she take seriously the possibility that she has been deceived by her own side. It demands that she distinguish what she wishes were true from what the evidence supports. None of this comes naturally. No human being is, by default, good at it. We are good at it only to the extent that we have practiced.

This is why it is misleading to call critical thinking, in the civic sense, a cognitive style. A style is a preference. A virtue is a discipline. The citizen who holds her beliefs provisionally, who reads writers she expects to disagree with, who tests her convictions against the best counterarguments she can find, who updates — publicly, at cost to her reputation — when the evidence demands it, is not doing something that comes easily to her any more than the soldier who charges the hill is doing something that comes easily. She is doing the hard thing that the role requires, and she is able to do it because she has cultivated the capacity over time. She has practiced. She has been shaped by institutions that drilled the practice into her. She has kept company with others who do the same. She has built, in the old language, a habit of the soul.

The Center for Civic Education, which for four decades has been the steadiest working institution for civic formation in the American schools, lists a set of what it calls civic dispositions that civic education should cultivate. The list, in the various formulations it has taken over the years, includes civility, respect for the worth and dignity of each person, open-mindedness, a willingness to compromise, tolerance of diversity, patience and persistence, compassion, generosity, loyalty to the nation and its principles, and — in the formulation we find most useful — critical thinking. The striking thing about the list, when you sit with it, is that every item on it is a disposition rather than a body of information. It is not that the citizen is supposed to memorize the Bill of Rights, though we hope she will; it is that she is supposed to have become, through her formation, a certain kind of person. The list is a description of that person.

It is also, if we are honest, a list of traits that the incentive structure of our age actively disfavors. Civility is not how you go viral. Open-mindedness is not what the algorithm promotes. Tolerance of diversity, in the demanding sense of actually finding oneself in sustained contact with people whose views one finds repugnant, is the opposite of the sorted media diet most of us consume. Willingness to compromise is coded, in our politics, as weakness. Patience loses to urgency. Respect for each person's worth is incompatible with the hourly practice of reducing strangers on the internet to objects of contempt. The dispositions civic education is supposed to produce are, at the level of habit, the dispositions our daily life subtracts from us. No wonder the output has deteriorated. We have been running the civic machine in reverse.

***

What would running it forward look like? Here the empirical literature begins to give us something concrete to hold on to. Over the past two decades, a cluster of evaluated civic-education programs has accumulated a track record, and the pattern of what works is remarkably consistent across them.

The American Institutes for Research, with U.S. Department of Education funding, developed a curriculum called THINKING PRO, aimed at grades nine through eleven, that integrates critical-thinking instruction with civic-efficacy training. The curriculum has been evaluated; it shows measurable effects on the kinds of outcomes one would hope a civic-thinking curriculum would move — students' ability to evaluate sources, their willingness to consider alternative viewpoints, their sense that their own civic action can matter. It is not a miracle. The effect sizes are modest, as effect sizes in educational research generally are. But it is something. There is a curriculum, tested against controls, that moves the needle on the outcomes we care about. That is not nothing; it is, in fact, the foundation from which scaled improvement could be built, if we decided to build it.

The Center for Civic Education's Project Citizen is an older and better-known example. The method is project-based. Students in a class identify a real, local problem in their community — the school zone without a crosswalk, the public library with diminishing hours, the park that has not been maintained. They research it. They map the stakeholders. They develop a proposed policy response. They present it to the relevant unit of local government — the school board, the city council, the county commission. The longitudinal evidence on Project Citizen and its international variants shows what we would expect it to show: students who go through the program retain civic skills years later, in ways that students taught the same civic content through lecture do not. Something happens in the doing that the telling cannot accomplish.

There are others worth mentioning. iCivics, the civic-education platform founded by Justice Sandra Day O'Connor in her post-Court years, reaches millions of students and has an internal evaluation record that is defensible even if the medium is sometimes lighter than we would want. Generation Citizen runs something close to a Project Citizen model in under-resourced schools in several major cities. The Harvard-affiliated Democratic Knowledge Project has built out deliberative-pedagogy materials that are in use in a growing set of districts. The Institute for Citizens and Scholars, formerly the Woodrow Wilson Foundation, coordinates a great deal of this work nationally. Taken together, these programs constitute a real infrastructure for civic formation. They are staffed by unglamorous people doing serious work, often in conditions that are harder than they should be. They rarely make the news.

What we find striking, looking across the evaluated programs, is that they converge on a small set of pedagogical commitments. These commitments are worth naming, because they are the operational content of the word *critical thinking* in the civic sense.

Authentic, local, participatory work rather than abstract instruction. The students investigate something that matters in the place where they live, with stakes they can feel. The classroom is not the boundary of the lesson; the community is.

Explicit instruction in source evaluation, with particular attention to the online environment. The students are taught to perform lateral reading — to leave the source and look it up elsewhere before deciding what to think of it. They are taught click-restraint — the habit of not clicking the first search result but scanning down the list. They are taught triangulation: confirming important claims against two or more independent sources. They are taught, by exercise rather than by slogan, that the aesthetic of a site has nothing to do with its reliability, and that the professional look of a headline or a tweet is not evidence of anything. The Stanford History Education Group's curriculum — the same group that documented the original scandal — is freely available and is one of the best things on offer anywhere.

Practice in articulating and defending positions one does not hold. Structured debate, Oxford-style or adapted, where students are assigned to argue the side they came in opposing. Role-play of contested policy questions. Deliberative exercises in which students must genuinely represent a view that is not their own. This is, in essence, the ideological Turing test as a classroom discipline: can the student describe the position of the other side well enough that a sincere holder of that position would recognize the description as fair?

Practice in changing one's mind publicly when the evidence demands it. The hardest item on the list, and the most important. Teachers who do this well model it themselves; they tell their students about positions they have abandoned, and why. The students learn that revision of belief is not a defeat to be hidden but a competence to be cultivated. Most of us go through school, and for that matter through adult life, without ever seeing an authority figure visibly change his mind in real time. Those who do see it, and who are invited to practice it in small matters, acquire a capacity others do not.

Mentorship by adults who themselves model the dispositions. This is the intangible but decisive ingredient. A civic disposition is caught as much as it is taught. The teacher who visibly takes seriously a student's objection, even when the teacher is certain the objection is mistaken, communicates something no worksheet can. The teacher who has a particular political view but who can be seen thinking against it, publicly, teaches open-mindedness more completely than the teacher who professes neutrality. Most of the best civic formation in America is being done by particular teachers in particular classrooms, often without institutional support, often without public recognition, simply because they understand that this is the job. We owe them more than we give them.

***

If civic education were the whole story, the argument would stop there — with the schools, and with what we would need to do to support the people already doing the work. But the public whose critical-thinking capacity we need to rebuild is not in school. Most of it has not been in school for decades. Any serious civic-education program has to ask what adult formation looks like, because the adults are the electorate, and the electorate is the polity.

The adult question is harder than the school question, because we cannot compel an adult to take a course. We can, however, notice where critical-thinking formation is happening in adult life, and we can name the institutions that do it well.

The first is serious journalism — what Jonathan Rauch has called, in *The Constitution of Knowledge*, the set of institutions that together constitute the public's epistemic infrastructure. A working newsroom, with an editor who demands sourcing and a reporter who practices triangulation, is a working factory of critical thinking. A reader of such a newsroom is being trained, day by day, by the discipline of its practice. This is not because journalists are cognitively superior to other people; it is because the institutional role of the journalist, when the institution is functioning, demands the dispositions we have been describing, and the habits rub off on the attentive reader. The decline of local journalism and the hollowing of many national outlets is therefore not only a problem for information; it is a problem for formation. When the public loses access to institutions that model good reasoning, it loses a teacher.

The second is the network of structured-dialogue organizations we discussed in Chapter 16 — Braver Angels, Essential Partners, Living Room Conversations, the #ListenFirst Coalition and the hundreds of smaller groups that do adjacent work. What these organizations do, behind the depolarization framing they lead with, is precisely critical-thinking pedagogy for adults. The ground rules of a Braver Angels workshop — speak for yourself, do not interrupt, seek to understand before seeking to be understood, steelman the other side before you critique it — are the operational definition of actively open-minded thinking in a social setting. A participant who completes the workshop has practiced the disposition for several hours under conditions designed to make the practice possible. It is not a course; it is a rehearsal. But rehearsals matter.

The third is the family of deliberative institutions — deliberative polling, citizens' assemblies, the evidence-based structured deliberations we will take up more fully in Chapter 19. A citizen who sits on a randomly selected deliberative body for a weekend, or for a series of weekends, is required to engage with evidence, to hear from experts with opposing views, to articulate and revise her own position, and to reach a considered judgment under conditions that punish bad reasoning and reward good. James Fishkin's twenty years of comparative results show that ordinary people placed in these conditions do, reliably, perform the disposition — they change their minds on the basis of new information; they narrow the gap between themselves and their opposites; they come out knowing more and caring more and often disagreeing less. Deliberation, in the technical sense that Fishkin and his collaborators have operationalized, is the adult-education form of critical-thinking pedagogy.

None of this scales yet. The school programs reach perhaps a few hundred thousand students in their evaluated forms in any given year, against a national school-age population in the tens of millions. The dialogue organizations reach a few hundred thousand adult participants over their histories combined. The deliberative bodies, in the United States, remain an experiment at the state level. What we have is proof of concept rather than proof of scale. But the proof of concept is, for our purposes, the material point. The dispositions can be cultivated. The cultivation can be taught. The teaching works. The only question is whether we will invest at the level the moment demands.

***

We have been writing at the level of institutions and programs. The reader who has gotten this far has a right to ask what we think she should do. We have some suggestions. They cost nothing and can be begun today.

Read, regularly, serious writers you expect to disagree with. Not social-media opponents; serious writers, at book length or long-essay length, on the other side of questions you care about. The point is not to refute them. The point is to understand them well enough that you could summarize the argument in terms the writer would accept. If you are progressive, read Deneen, or Scruton, or Douthat; if you are conservative, read Sandel, or Coates, or Levitsky. Read one every month or two. Keep a list. Do not buy only the ones you end up agreeing with.

Adopt, as a personal discipline, *steelman before critique*. Before you disagree with a position — in conversation, in writing, online — articulate the strongest version of it, in terms that a sympathetic holder of the position would endorse. If you cannot do this, you are not yet entitled to disagree; you do not yet know what you are disagreeing with. The discipline is simple. It is also, in practice, punishing. It is a discipline.

Before you post, before you argue, before you press send, write out — even in a private note — the best case against what you are about to say. If the best case is strong, consider whether you should say what you are about to say at all. If it is weak, you will have sharpened your own position. Either way, you will have practiced the disposition.

Keep a list of times you have changed your mind. This is the specific exercise we find most useful, and the one we recommend most strongly. Actually write it down. What positions did you hold five years ago that you no longer hold? What convinced you? What evidence crossed the threshold, and what argument did the work? A person who cannot produce such a list is not, strictly speaking, thinking. She is reinforcing. She is metabolizing inputs into the confirmation of prior commitments. The person who can produce such a list — ten items, say, from the last decade — has a track record of doing the hardest thing a reasoner ever does, and knows something about herself that she can rely on. If you have not kept the list, start it. You can recover much of it retrospectively.

Take the ideological Turing test, in Bryan Caplan's formulation. Can you state your opponent's position well enough that a sympathizer would recognize it as a fair description? Test yourself. Try to write an op-ed in the voice of someone you oppose — not a parody, a genuine attempt. Show it to someone on that side. See whether they flinch, and where, and why. The exercise is humbling. It is also an almost pure measure of the disposition we have been discussing.

Shift your information diet toward slower, less emotionally reinforcing sources. Newspapers over feeds. Books over clips. Primary documents over summaries. Long-form audio over rage-cycle television. The argument here is not aesthetic. It is neurological. The fast, emotionally laden, algorithmically selected diet exercises the part of us that is most susceptible to motivated reasoning and least susceptible to its correction. The slower diet exercises the part that can hold a proposition without immediately reacting to it. Over months and years, the difference compounds. It may be the single largest adjustment an individual reader can make at zero financial cost.

Find at least one person with whom you can practice honest argument. Not a sparring partner who shares all your premises; a friend whose premises are different, whose arguments you respect, with whom you have agreed — explicitly — to tell each other when you think the other is wrong and to listen when the other tells you. This is the oldest civic technology in existence. It is how Socrates thought, how Lincoln thought, how anyone who has ever thought well has thought. The solitary reasoner is a myth. We reason with others, or we do not reason at all. You need a person. If you do not have one, the search for one is itself an early practice of the discipline.

***

A warning before we close. A reader who absorbs an argument like this one is in a specific kind of danger, and we have seen it happen often enough that we want to name it. The danger is that the reader, inspired to think more critically, uses her new inspiration as a weapon against the other side, while leaving her own side's premises unexamined. She returns from this chapter sharper at finding the flaws in the arguments she already rejected, and no more rigorous about the arguments she already accepts. If that is the effect of what we have written, we have done harm. The reader who has been inspired to be more skeptical in exactly the direction she was already skeptical, and no more, has not become a better citizen; she has acquired a rhetorical accessory. She is more confident, not more open. The world has one more clever partisan in it.

The discipline we are describing cuts the other direction first. The most important application of actively open-minded thinking is not against the claims of the tribe across the aisle; it is against the claims of one's own. The perception-gap research is clear that the most politically engaged Americans are the most misinformed about their opponents. The corollary is that the most practiced skeptics in a given camp are often the ones least inclined to apply the skepticism where it would cost them something — where it would bring them into tension with the people they want to sit with at dinner, with the institutions they want to belong to, with the moral identity they have constructed for themselves. The test of whether a person has actually cultivated the disposition we are describing is not whether she can find the flaws in the arguments of the other camp. Anyone can find those flaws. The test is whether she can find, and acknowledge, the flaws in the arguments of her own. Whether she can hold her own side to the standards she holds the opposition to. Whether she can say, in public, *I was wrong about that*, or *my people are wrong about this*, and bear the cost. The cost is real. The social pressure to be a reliable ally to the people one likes is among the oldest and most powerful forces in the human psychology, and it is currently being amplified by every technology of our public life. To resist it — not to abandon one's commitments, but to hold them accountable to the same reasoning one demands of the other side — is the specifically civic form of the disposition.

We ought to say, plainly, that we find this hard. We have tribes of our own. We have dinner tables and group chats and reading lists and professional associations whose approval we want, and whose displeasure at disagreement we feel. We have written this chapter as much to remind ourselves as to propose anything to the reader. The discipline is not something one arrives at. It is something one practices, imperfectly, across a life.

***

We do not want to oversell what is on offer here. Critical thinking, even in the disciplined sense we have tried to give it, is not a magic bullet. Societies with excellent civic education and widespread actively open-minded thinking would still make mistakes, have factions, produce occasional demagogues, and argue about the things that are worth arguing about. A polity in which the disposition was widely cultivated would not be a utopia. It would be something both more modest and more valuable: a polity whose public disagreements were of higher quality than ours, whose public reasoning moved on the evidence and not only on the team-colors, whose citizens could be addressed as reasoners and not only as loyalists. It would be, approximately, the polity we have had, in patches, at particular times, in particular places — in certain town meetings, in certain newsrooms, in certain classrooms, in certain professional communities, at certain moments when the institutions were functioning well. It is not asking for something we have never done. It is asking for something we have done intermittently and could do more of, if we wanted.

It is worth saying clearly that the task of doing more of it will fall most heavily on people we do not currently honor enough. The American classroom teacher who runs a Project Citizen unit in a district that has cut her department's budget three years running; the community-college instructor who builds a lateral-reading module into her English composition course on her own time; the librarian who teaches a media-literacy workshop in a small-town public library on a Tuesday night; the journalist at a shrinking newspaper who still insists on triangulating before publishing; the volunteer moderator at a Braver Angels workshop who takes a Saturday out of the month to sit in a room with twenty strangers — these are the people doing, today, the work we are describing. Most of them will never be famous. Most of them will never be thanked adequately. Some of the best civic formation in America at this moment is being done by people whose names we will never learn, in rooms we will never see, with students and neighbors we will never meet. We know this is true because we have met some of them, and every time we have, we have come away with more hope than the general tenor of the public conversation would suggest is warranted. The civic body of this country still has the antibodies. They are not active everywhere. They are active in more places than the commentary suggests.

It remains, finally, to say how this chapter connects to the book's larger argument. The thesis of *Return of the Citizen* is that the modern person has been degraded by two linked forces — the atomization that strips her of the associations in which character is formed, and the mass movements that rush in to fill the resulting void. Critical thinking, in the sense we have tried to define, is the specific civic virtue that speaks to the second half of that degradation. It is the trained disposition that makes a person resistant to the offer of certainty that every mass movement extends. The cult — whether the cult is ideological, partisan, religious, technological, or identitarian — promises to lift from the adherent the burden of not yet knowing, in exchange for the adherent's willingness to stop thinking in the specific ways the movement finds inconvenient. The cult offers a world in which the questions are settled and the enemies are named. The adherent is spared the discomfort of open cognition; the movement is spared the inconvenience of dissent.

The person who has practiced holding provisional beliefs in the face of social pressure is the person the cult cannot recruit. It is not that she is immune to persuasion; she can be persuaded, and ought to be, by evidence and argument. It is that she cannot be persuaded by the particular package the mass movement sells, because the package is not evidence and argument; it is the offer of certainty in exchange for the surrender of inquiry, and she has practiced refusing that offer. The practice is not glamorous. It does not feel, day to day, like resistance. It feels like the ordinary intellectual housework of reading the other side, steelmanning the opposing case, keeping a record of one's changes of mind, tolerating the discomfort of open questions. But it is the housework, accumulated over years, that builds the structure that holds when the wind comes. The citizens who held, in the crises of the twentieth century, were the ones who had been practicing.

That is what we are asking of the reader and of ourselves. Not a certificate in critical thinking; not a declared allegiance to open-mindedness; not a posture of reasonableness to be worn in public. A practice. A discipline. A slow and unglamorous training of the mind toward the specific dispositions the role of citizen requires. We will not all become good at it. We will all become better at it if we work at it. And the polity is the sum of those small, private labors — the thousand daily acts of steelmanning, of source-checking, of self-correction, of honest argument with another honest mind — which, accumulated, make the difference between a public world capable of governing itself and one ready to be captured by whoever shouts the loudest.

We have the tools. We know what works. We have the teachers, if we will support them. We have, each of us, the capacity for the discipline, if we will practice it. The only remaining question is whether, in the moment we have been given, we will decide that the civic life is worth the work. The citizen, in the end, is the person who has decided that it is.
